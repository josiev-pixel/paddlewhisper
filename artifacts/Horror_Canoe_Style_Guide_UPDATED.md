# Horror Canoe — Style Guide

## Purpose

This document defines the prose style for *Horror Canoe*. Use it as a project source alongside the Story Bible, Route & Day Map, Character Bible, Mimicry Ledger, and Chapter Outline.

The target style is not imitation of any single writer. It is a controlled blend:

> **Hard plainness with lyrical pressure.**

The prose should be mostly concrete, direct, and matter-of-fact, with occasional lyrical or image-rich flares when the moment earns them: wilderness beauty, fear, exhaustion, grief, uncanny sound, or Joel’s perception sharpening under stress.

The book should feel plain until it needs to become beautiful, and beautiful only when pressure earns it.

---

## Core Style Formula

### Default Mode

Use this for most narration, action, logistics, camp work, paddling, portaging, argument, and ordinary observation.

- Concrete, direct, physical prose
- Short-to-medium sentences
- Strong nouns and verbs
- Minimal abstraction
- Minimal explanation of emotion
- No melodrama
- No ornamental language during ordinary action
- Let physical process carry meaning
- Let silence, gesture, and practical detail reveal feeling

Default prose should feel restrained, muscular, and lived-in.

### Heightened Mode

Use this sparingly, only when the moment earns it.

Appropriate moments:

- river, weather, woods, water, light, fog, cold, insects
- exhaustion and sleep deprivation
- grief after Alex’s death
- uncanny sound or mimicry
- Joel’s fear sharpening into pattern recognition
- the collapse of ordinary explanations
- late-book silence

In heightened mode, allow:

- longer, more musical sentences
- sensory density
- sharper image-making
- subtle wordplay
- strange but grounded metaphors
- beauty that feels pressured, not decorative

Even in heightened mode, stay anchored in physical detail.

---

## Prose Ratio

Aim for roughly:

```text
75% hard, clean, concrete prose
20% sensory literary prose
5% strange or lyrical flashes
```

If the whole book is lush, it loses force. If the whole book is blunt, it loses the eerie beauty of the river. The power comes from contrast.

---

## Sentence-Level Rules

### Prefer

- specific physical detail
- verbs that carry weight
- concrete objects
- practical action
- observed behavior
- controlled rhythm
- sensory facts before interpretation

### Avoid

- explaining feelings too early
- abstract emotional labels
- florid horror language
- generic dread phrases
- repeated “he felt” constructions
- overuse of simile
- poetic language that is not grounded in the scene
- characters thinking in polished literary metaphors during practical danger

### Good Sentence Energy

```text
Joel pulled the canoe higher and turned it sideways against the wind. The hull scraped over stone. Mike told him to lift, not drag, and Joel told him to buy lighter boats if he wanted elegance.
```

This is practical, physical, and character-revealing.

---

## Example: Too Plain

```text
Joel looked at the river. It was dark and cold. He felt afraid. The trees moved in the wind.
```

This is clear but dead. It gives the reader information without pressure, texture, or rhythm.

---

## Example: Too Ornate

```text
The river, a sable ligament of primeval longing, coiled beneath the moon’s indifferent chalice while Joel’s soul trembled in the cathedral of spruce.
```

This is too self-conscious. It pulls attention away from the scene and toward the prose.

---

## Example: Target Style

```text
Joel stood at the landing and watched the river take the last light. It did not shine so much as hold the dark in long strips. The spruce leaned over it, black and close, and every small sound seemed to travel farther than it should.
```

This is the target balance: concrete, readable, restrained, but with enough music to carry dread.

## Dialogue Rules

The men should not talk like the narration.

They are roughly forty. They are old friends. They argue easily. They are funny, inappropriate, tired, defensive, and affectionate without saying so directly.

Dialogue should be:

- natural
- rough
- argumentative
- funny without becoming sitcom-like
- character-specific
- sometimes inappropriate
- full of interruption and pushback
- shaped by old friendship rather than exposition

### Dialogue Should Show

- old rhythms
- old resentments
- practical competence
- male affection expressed sideways
- irritation mixed with loyalty
- debates that are partly real and partly performance

### Dialogue Should Avoid

- polished emotional speeches
- everyone sounding the same
- exposition disguised as conversation
- sentimental declarations
- horror-movie panic too early
- overly clever banter in moments of real danger

### Sample Dialogue Target

```text
“Map says two miles,” Mike said.

“Map also says there’s a cabin,” Will said. “So far the map’s batting about .200.”

Alex snorted. “Listen to Bill Nye over here.”

Joel looked at the water. “Can we maybe argue after we’re not sideways in the wind?”
```

This is the preferred energy: argumentative, specific, lightly funny, and practical.

---

## Character Voice Notes

### Joel

- Quieter than the others
- Dry, rational, understated
- Pushes back against reckless or silly ideas
- Uses short practical phrases
- Humor is reactive, not performative
- Does not over-explain feelings

### Will

- Talkative, geeky, anxious
- Explains, corrects, distinguishes
- Can be inappropriate
- Fills silence when uncomfortable
- Debates as self-soothing
- Gives the entity abundant material

### Mike

- Brash, pessimistic, route-certain
- Inappropriate and blunt
- Uses reassurance as control
- Argues to manage the group
- Often negative about the world
- His confidence should sound useful before it becomes dangerous

### Alex

- Outwardly cheerful, loud, attention-seeking
- Teasing, cajoling, performative
- Uses “aw, come on, guys” energy
- Wants a story and wants response
- Should be vivid before he dies

## Horror Style Rules

The horror should emerge from practical reality first.

### Prefer

- wrong sounds
- repeated phrases
- ambiguous directionality
- ordinary gear noises in impossible places
- weather constraining decisions
- fatigue degrading judgment
- grief making the impossible tempting
- characters rationalizing what they hear
- silence becoming both safety and threat

### Avoid

- explaining the entity
- showing the entity clearly
- jump-scare chapter endings as a habit
- supernatural acceptance too early
- gore as the main source of fear
- characters making stupid choices just to advance the plot
- “the woods watched him” phrasing unless earned and rare

The entity should become more frightening as it becomes more limited. Its rules make it believable.

---

## Sound and Mimicry Prose

Because the entity is sound-based, sound must be written with precision.

### Sound Should Often Be

- directional but uncertain
- familiar before it is frightening
- repeated in altered context
- emotionally tempting
- slightly wrong in timing, placement, or tone

### Good Mimicry Description

```text
The cough came from the trees to Joel’s left. Not loud. Not dramatic. Just Will’s small dry cough, the one he used before correcting someone. But Will was in front of him on the trail, bent under the canoe, his boots planted in the mud.
```

This works because it is specific, ordinary, and impossible.

### Avoid

```text
A terrifying demonic imitation of Will echoed through the cursed forest.
```

Too abstract, too declared, too generic.

---

## Wilderness Detail Rules

Wilderness detail should feel procedural and physical, not decorative.

Use details like:

- wet socks
- blackflies and mosquitoes
- cold hands on aluminum or wood
- mud sucking at boots
- canoe hull scraping stone
- pack straps biting shoulders
- stove flame fluttering
- tarp lines ticking in wind
- fog flattening distance
- water carrying sound too well
- spruce boughs holding rain
- fatigue making simple knots difficult

The river should be beautiful, but not sentimental. It is indifferent.

---

## Emotional Restraint

Do not overstate emotion. Let grief and fear appear through action.

### Instead of

```text
Joel was devastated by Alex’s death and felt guilty for all the times Alex had annoyed him.
```

### Prefer

```text
Joel found himself waiting for Alex to say something stupid about the coffee. Then he remembered the red place in the brush and poured too much water into the grounds.
```

The second version lets behavior carry the feeling.

---

## Chapter Ending Rules

The user prefers clean, decisive endings rather than habitual cliffhangers.

A chapter may end with tension, dread, or an unresolved problem, but it should not feel like a cheap cut-to-black unless specifically intended.

Good endings may close on:

- a completed physical action
- a changed understanding
- a practical decision
- a quiet wrongness
- an emotional turn
- a line of dialogue that lands without winking

Avoid ending every chapter with:

- a scream
- an unseen hand
- “then he heard it”
- a sudden impossible voice
- an obvious teaser

---

## Drafting Prompt Style Block

Use this block in chapter-drafting prompts:

```text
Style requirements:
Use a restrained, forceful literary style: mostly plain, concrete, matter-of-fact prose with strong physical detail and short-to-medium sentences. Allow occasional lyrical, Nabokov-like sensory flashes only when the moment earns them — wilderness beauty, dread, exhaustion, grief, or uncanny sound. Do not imitate any author directly. Do not become purple or ornate. Dialogue should remain natural, rough, funny, argumentative, inappropriate at times, and character-specific. The prose should feel plain until it needs to become beautiful, and beautiful only when pressure earns it.
```

---

## Revision Checklist

When revising a chapter for style, check:

- Are the first three pages too explanatory?
- Is the prose concrete enough?
- Are there too many abstractions?
- Are lyrical moments earned?
- Do the men sound distinct?
- Does dialogue feel like old friends arguing rather than characters delivering information?
- Are wilderness details physical and procedural?
- Does sound have direction, texture, and consequence?
- Is emotion shown through action rather than labeled?
- Does the chapter end cleanly rather than with a cheap cliffhanger?

---


## Project-Specific Continuity Style Rules

Use these in addition to the general style guidance.

- In character dialogue, prefer plain canoe language: “front,” “back,” “left,” “right,” “boat,” and “canoe.” Avoid formal “bow” and “stern” unless a character is being deliberately precise, mocking, or technical.
- The men say “portage,” not “carry,” when referring to the trail between waters. Use “carry” only for the physical act of carrying an object.
- Default water/portage footwear is hiking sandals unless a scene establishes boots, camp shoes, or cold-weather footwear.
- Keep Will from becoming phone-centered. His texture is maps, route distinctions, knots, fishing, gear, and technical argument.
- Mike is more likely to check service, worry over vehicle condition, and use reassurance as control.
- Joel should not become the route planner. He observes, notices systems, steers when needed, and recognizes consequences, but Will and Mike own most planning.
- Alex should be funny and performative, but not broadly competent in canoes. He is strong and game, more of a backpacking/mountain guy, and usually rides in front.
- Dialogue can be sharp and funny, but avoid making every exchange too polished. Let fatigue create missed jokes, unfinished lines, practical interruption, and silence.

## Final Style Summary

*Horror Canoe* should read like a practical wilderness story being slowly poisoned by sound.

Most sentences should be plain, physical, and controlled. The lyricism should rise only when the river, fear, grief, fatigue, or mimicry puts pressure on Joel’s perception.

The book should not sound ornate. It should sound exact.
