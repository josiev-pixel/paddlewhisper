# Outline

## Act I - Before The Rupture

1. The Old Landing - The men gather, load, argue, drive north, and seed the human sound bank.
2. Two Vehicles - They stage Mike's Highlander at North Road Bridge and launch from South Access.
3. First Water - The route begins; first portage, first odd paddle knock, first camp reached.
4. Granite Shelf - Camp routines, food storage, firelight, Alex's performance, and night wrongness.
5. Beaver Work - Day 2 labor, beaver dams, muddy footing, brush crack, and route pressure.
6. The Rough Carry - Wind and rough portage make Alex vivid and seed the old cabin as backup.
7. Second Camp - Bad campsite, bad sightlines, mosquito dusk, and Alex stepping to the edge.
8. Nobody Panic - Alex dies just beyond camp in a bear-plausible but wrong attack.

## Act II - Natural Explanation Fails

9. Bear Work - Morning examination, shock logistics, bear theory, and the first dead-voice wrongness.
10. Windbound - Weather blocks movement; living-habit mimicry begins.
11. The Second Night After - Rain and camp sounds teach Joel that something has learned them.
12. Bad Crossing - A dangerous crossing goes wrong; Mike's route phrases come from impossible places.
13. Emergency Camp - Wet gear, bad shelter, tracking phrases in Joel's notebook.
14. The Old Map - Mike chooses the maintenance cabin as practical hope.
15. The Trace - The old route half exists; voices begin separating them.
16. The Cabin - The cabin is real and useless.
17. Mike's Authority - Mike dies trying to restore control and recover what matters.

## Act III - Silence And Recognition

18. Two Men - Joel and Will survive after Mike; written rules begin.
19. Written Rules - Visual confirmation, hand signals, and silence become necessary.
20. His Own Voice - Joel hears his own rare phrase and understands the thing has heard him too.
21. Bad Boat - Damaged gear narrows options; they push forward.
22. The Channel - Silence on narrow water; the entity uses living Will against visible Will.
23. No, No, No - Gravel-bar camp, dead voices, and Joel stopping Will from answering.
24. The Difference - Will partly understands but wants to test the rule.
25. Will Answers - Will dies after engaging the voices.
26. Alone - Joel strips the problem down to daylight, distance, silence, and notes.
27. The Planted Phrase - Joel gives the entity one controlled phrase and uses its misuse.
28. The Crossing - Final dusk crossing toward the take-out while refusing the dead.
29. North Road Bridge - Joel reaches the vehicle and help; the official story fails.
30. Home Sound - Safe at home, Joel hears Mike's phrase and cannot know what it means.

# Chapter 1 - The Old Landing

Joel reached Will’s house a little after nine, which meant he was late by the version of the morning Mike had imagined and early by the version everyone else would actually live through.

Will’s truck sat in the driveway with the tailgate down and two canoes already on sawhorses in the grass. Packs, paddles, dry bags, fishing rods, a food barrel, and three milk crates of loose gear covered the concrete. Mike was standing over a roll-top dry bag with both hands on his hips, looking at it as if it had lied to him.

“That’s not fitting,” Mike said.

“It fits,” Will said.

“It fits in the sense that a raccoon fits in a chimney.”

“You’re folding it wrong.”

“I’m not folding a dry bag.”

“You’re compressing it emotionally.”

Mike looked up. “Do you hear yourself?”

“Constantly,” Will said. “It’s a burden.”

Joel parked along the curb and shut off his old Acura. For a moment he stayed where he was, hands resting on the wheel. The morning had the clean September look that always made him mistrust the forecast. Blue sky, dry pavement, a small bite under the sun. St. Louis had heat banked in the sidewalks by now, but here at the edge of town, under Will’s maples, the air still felt usable.

He had driven in from Madison the day before and slept badly on Will’s couch, half under a dog blanket Will insisted was clean. The others had started the old argument before coffee. They could argue over rope, fuel, order of departure, whether a cooler counted as a food-management system, whether a seven-day route meant six dinners or seven, whether a man approaching forty should still own a foam sleeping pad from college.

This was how the trip began. Not at the water. Not even on the road. It began in somebody’s driveway with too much gear and too many opinions and the sense that if they could get the first load tied down, the rest of their lives might also become manageable.

Joel got out.

Will turned from the truck. “Madison has arrived.”

Joel looked at the gear spread across the driveway. “Apparently without enough opinions.”

“We have extras,” Will said.

Mike pulled once more at the dry bag and got it cinched. “You bring the camp shoes?”

“Yes.”

“For a canoe trip?”

“For camp.”

“You know what shoes work at camp? The ones already on your feet.”

Joel lifted his pack from the trunk. “You can tell me that again when your socks smell like a drowned mouse.”

Will made his little dry cough that was almost a laugh and almost a defect in the throat. Joel had known that cough since they were boys in church together, back when Will used to draw spaceships and circuit diagrams on the bulletin during sermons. Neither of them went anymore. Joel especially had lost the habit so completely that walking into a church now felt like walking onto the set of something he used to believe was real.

Mike had met Will later, in middle school. They had gone through high school together, old enough in each other’s lives to know the bad haircuts, the first cars, the first stupid jobs, the things men remembered instead of saying they loved each other. Joel knew Mike through Will and through years of trips. That was different. It was sturdy, but it had seams.

A car horn sounded from the street. Not one of theirs. A rideshare sedan stopped behind Joel’s Acura, and Alex got out with a big duffel, a backpacking pack, and the expression of a man entering a room he expected to improve.

“Gentlemen,” Alex said. “Try not to look so relieved.”

Mike looked at the bags. “That all of it?”

“For a week in the woods? Sadly, yes.”

“You fly with bear spray?”

“I look stupid to you?”

Mike said nothing.

“I bought it here,” Alex said. “Jesus.”

Alex lived in Seattle and had for a long time, long enough that he had become the group’s western correspondent on rain, coffee, ferries, and the moral superiority of mountains. But he had grown up in Alaska, and he carried that fact differently than the Seattle part. Seattle was posture and complaint. Alaska was something he mentioned less often, usually when cold or distance or bears came up and he wanted everyone to understand that he had been formed by bigger things than Missouri men with canoes.

He was loud in person. Not just volume. Presence. He made the driveway feel like an audience had gathered.

Will looked at the duffel. “You packed food?”

“I packed food I can eat.”

“That means what?” Mike asked.

“It means I have standards.”

“It means he brought crackers,” Joel said.

Alex pointed at him. “And this is why Joel’s engaged. Cruel but attentive.”

“Mostly available,” Joel said.

Alex laughed, a hard bark of sound that bounced off Will’s garage door. The neighbor’s dog answered from behind a fence.

Mike bent over the duffel and unzipped it before Alex could stop him.

“Hey.”

“Relax.”

“Quit mothering me.”

Mike pulled out a bundle of tortillas, oatmeal packets, two jars of peanut butter, instant rice, protein bars, and a plastic bag of what looked like plain noodles. He held up the noodles.

“What is this?”

“Food.”

“For prisoners?”

Alex took it back. “I have a sensitive palate.”

Will coughed into his coffee. “That’s a generous name for it.”

“I’m branching out.”

“You brought unseasoned noodles.”

“Branching is a process.”

Joel smiled despite himself and went to his trunk for the rest of his gear. He had packed neatly enough, but not obsessively. Will and Mike did the planning. They made the lists, argued routes, watched water levels, compared portage reports, and decided which old map was trustworthy enough to carry and which old map was trustworthy enough to mock. Joel looked at what they sent him, asked a few practical questions, then showed up with what he was told to bring plus what he knew he would want when wet and tired. He was competent on the water, more than casual, less than Will and Mike, but he only got one real trip a year now if he was lucky. Work, travel from Madison, wedding planning, house things, the ordinary undertow.

Will and Mike went several times some years. Small rivers, shoulder-season overnights, route tests, gear shakedowns that sounded to Joel like recreation disguised as logistics. Mike sold and resold canoes and took it personally when anyone used the wrong tie-down. Will fished every piece of water that looked like it might tolerate him.

Alex backpacked mountains. He knew cold nights, wet feet, long climbs, and bad food. Canoes he treated with respect and mild suspicion. He was usually put up front where he could provide power, commentary, and fewer steering decisions.

They started loading Will’s truck.

It was the practical vehicle: crew cab, bed cap, roof rack, enough rust to prove loyalty but not enough to question the frame. The canoes would ride on top. The packs and paddles would go under the cap. Mike’s ancient Toyota Highlander would be staged at the take-out later, a faded gray thing with a rear hatch that sometimes opened only if you insulted it first. Mike loved it because it was paid for and hated it because it kept needing things.

“No, not there,” Mike said.

Will stood in the bed of the truck with a food barrel between his boots. “You cannot say ‘not there’ to every place. Eventually objects occupy space.”

“The barrel goes forward.”

“If it goes forward we have to unload half the truck to get lunch.”

“We’re not eating lunch out of the barrel.”

“We are if you pack lunch in the barrel.”

“I’m not packing lunch in the barrel.”

“You packed lunch in the barrel last year.”

“That was because Alex kept sitting on the tortillas.”

Alex held up both hands. “They were warm and flexible. You’re welcome.”

Joel lifted a paddle bundle and slid it under the cap. The blades knocked softly together, wood and carbon and aluminum making separate notes. Will had three rods rigged in tubes, plus a tackle bag he kept moving as if it were an animal that might bolt.

“You bringing all of those?” Joel asked.

“Yes.”

“You can only use one at a time.”

“That’s a failure of the human body, not my system.”

Mike pointed at the rods. “They better not be in the way.”

“They are spiritually central to the trip.”

“You fish constantly,” Alex said. “It’s like a medical condition.”

“It regulates me.”

“That I believe.”

By ten-thirty they had loaded enough to reveal what would not fit. This produced a second round of decision-making. The cooler was reduced. Alex surrendered a camp chair after accusing everyone of anti-comfort extremism. Mike tried to remove Joel’s extra fleece and was denied. Will argued for a second tarp on grounds of weather, group size, and general human fragility.

Mike said, “Forecast is clean.”

Will said, “Forecasts are confessions of uncertainty.”

“That sounds smart and means nothing.”

“It means bring the tarp.”

Joel said, “Bring the tarp.”

Mike looked at him. “Et tu, Madison?”

“Especially Madison.”

The second tarp went in.

They tied down the canoes with the solemn irritation of men who had done it badly before. Mike took charge of the straps. Will corrected his twist. Mike corrected Will’s correction. Alex tried to help, then mainly stood in the wrong place. Joel held the canoe steady at the rear rack while Mike fed the webbing over the hull.

The buckle clicked against the roof bar.

“Careful,” Mike said to Alex without looking.

“I’m standing still.”

“Do it more carefully.”

“Quit mothering me.”

Joel tightened his side until the canoe settled into the foam block. The hull was scuffed green with a pale scar near the bow. Mike had bought it from a dentist, fixed a cracked seat hanger, used it for two seasons, then decided it was too good to sell and too heavy to love.

Will pulled the strap flat. “Half twist or it hums.”

“That’s not how that works,” Mike said.

“It is exactly how that works.”

Alex looked at Joel. “Are they always married?”

“They’ve had a long engagement.”

“Unlike you,” Alex said. “Actual engagement. Brave man.”

“Someone had to test the institution.”

“Report back from the front.”

Mike cinched the rear strap. “Marriage is people pretending property law is romance.”

Will said, “And there’s Mike, smiling at the children.”

“I’m not wrong.”

“You’re not always useful either.”

That was the tone of it. Mike and Will could move from joke to argument without changing volume. Both of them had done a string of relationships that ended in different weather. Will’s usually lasted three to nine months, long enough for habits to form and short enough for him to describe them later as “educational.” A few had lasted longer and left him quieter afterward. Mike’s came less regularly, sometimes more intensely, sometimes with shared leases or dogs or storage units, and always ended badly enough that his friends learned not to ask until he made the first joke.

Alex kept his own life closer. Or emptier. Joel could never tell which. There were women in stories sometimes, but rarely names that returned. He made singleness sound like freedom when he wanted to and like weather when he did not.

Joel was engaged, which in the group made him both suspect and useful. Someone had to know about venues, contracts, and the price of flowers. The others treated it like a foreign war he had volunteered for.

They finished loading close to noon.

Mike looked at the truck. “We should have left an hour ago.”

Will closed the tailgate. “We said eleven.”

“You said eleven because you knew it meant noon.”

“I understand the group.”

Alex climbed into the back seat with his backpacking pack between his knees. “Wake me when Missouri stops being flat.”

“We’re going north,” Will said. “Missouri has already stopped.”

“Culturally.”

Joel took the other rear seat. Mike rode up front with Will so they could argue over navigation and because neither trusted the other enough to surrender it completely. The truck smelled like dust, old coffee, fishing line, and the faint rubber smell of dry bags warming under the cap.

They pulled away from Will’s house with the canoes tight above them and the remaining day already smaller than planned.

The drive north passed in stages. Suburbs gave way to fields, then to towns built around gas stations and bars with meat raffles advertised on the signs. They stopped for fuel, ice, and the kind of lunch eaten standing beside a truck because sitting would imply delay.

At the first stop, Mike walked around the truck and touched every strap.

“We’re fine,” Will said.

Mike tugged the rear line anyway. “We are fine because I check.”

Alex came out of the gas station with coffee, crackers, and a banana. “You guys have any idea how hard it is to find food with no personality?”

“For you?” Joel said. “Or the food?”

Alex pointed the banana at him. “There he is. Very dry. Very Midwest.”

“I live in Wisconsin.”

“That’s advanced Midwest.”

Will came out with a bag of sour candy, jerky, and two electrolyte drinks.

Mike stared at the candy. “You are forty years old.”

“Nearly.”

“That makes it worse.”

“This is for morale.”

“Your morale has corn syrup in it.”

“My morale has range.”

They ate beside the pumps. Trucks came and went. The canoes rocked slightly when a semi passed too close. One strap made a low hum in the wind and Will reached up to flatten it.

“Told you,” he said.

Mike said, “I’m not giving you that.”

“You don’t have to give it. Reality already did.”

At the edge of the lot Mike unfolded one of the maps on the hood. Will stood beside him with a pen. Joel drank bad coffee and watched the two of them bend over the paper. The route was a line of blue and green and little black warnings. South Access at the bottom. A chain of narrow lakes, a river section, several carries, a larger lake, a constricted channel, another portage. North Road Bridge at the top, where Mike’s Highlander would wait if the old thing agreed to start again after a week alone.

“First day is easy if we get on the water early enough,” Mike said.

“We are already not doing that tomorrow if we keep stopping,” Will said.

“We’ll sleep near the forest road. Get up at seven. Out by eight.”

Alex laughed.

Mike looked up. “What?”

“You said get up at seven like that creates a legal obligation.”

“It creates a plan.”

“Plans are how men like you experience hope.”

Will tapped the map. “The first real issue is here. If wind’s wrong on the second lake, we lose time.”

“Forecast says southwest, light.”

“Forecast says a thing. Weather does a thing.”

Mike made a low sound in his throat. “It opens up after the first tight stretch. We’ll be fine.”

Joel saw Will’s mouth move around an answer and then close on it. Will did not always let arguments go. When he did, Joel noticed.

Alex leaned over the map from the wrong side. “Where’s the cabin?”

Mike’s finger moved to a small square back from the water. “Old maintenance cabin. Probably nothing. It’s on the old map.”

“Cabin,” Alex said. “Now we’re talking.”

“It’s not part of the route.”

“You say that like routes don’t enjoy surprises.”

“They don’t,” Mike said.

Will said, “That cabin could be gone.”

“It could be.”

“Burned, collapsed, stripped, eaten by raccoons.”

“Probably.”

“Then why are we looking at it?”

“Because it’s on the map.”

Joel looked at the square. It was printed with the same confidence as the lakes and roads, but it felt different. Human marks aged badly in wild country. A lake could move at the edges, a channel could choke with beaver work, but it remained itself. Cabins became rumors. Roads became brush. Names stayed longer than things.

Mike folded the map hard. “We’re exactly where we ought to be.”

“There it is,” Alex said.

“What?”

“Your campaign slogan.”

Mike opened the truck door. “Get in.”

They drove until the light began to go sideways through the trees.

The motel was north of the last useful town, set along a two-lane road with a bar across from it and a ditch full of dark water behind it. The office had postcards in a rack, a bowl of mints, and a woman behind the desk who looked at the canoes without interest. Men with canoes were not rare here. Men with too much confidence probably were not rare anywhere.

They took two rooms because the rate was cheap enough after Will argued about cash and Mike made a face at the number. Even so, Mike said he might just sleep in the truck.

“No,” Will said.

“What?”

“You’re not sleeping in the truck to save forty dollars.”

“I sleep fine in the truck.”

“You sleep like a raccoon in a dryer.”

“Then you won’t hear me through the wall.”

Alex said, “Let the man commune with his Highlander.”

“The Highlander is at the take-out tomorrow,” Will said.

“Then he can start saying goodbye tonight.”

Mike took the room key from Will. “I said might.”

They carried in the gear that mattered. Maps, food, electronics, paddles, rods, sleeping bags, anything that would be irritating or expensive to lose. The canoes stayed on Will’s truck under the parking lot light. Mike checked the straps again. Will checked the rods. Alex disappeared into the bathroom and came out saying the towels had the texture of legal documents.

Dinner was across the road.

The bar had a mounted bear near the entrance with dust on its muzzle and one cloudy glass eye. Alex stood beside it and asked Joel to take his picture. Joel did, badly.

“You made me look short,” Alex said.

“You are standing beside a bear.”

“Grew up with bigger.”

“In Alaska,” Will said, with the tone of a man beginning a familiar bit.

Alex put a hand to his chest. “I don’t bring it up.”

“You bring it up every time an animal is mentioned.”

“That’s because you people think deer are wildlife.”

Mike took a table in back where they could see the truck through the window. They ordered burgers, fries, fish, beer, coffee for Will because he claimed beer made him sleepy in a way that wasted the first one. Alex studied the menu with suspicion.

“Do you think the chili is aggressive?” he asked.

Mike stared at him.

“I’m asking a serious question.”

“Order chicken strips,” Will said.

“I’m not a child.”

“You packed plain noodles.”

“Chicken strips,” Alex told the waitress.

The talk loosened with food. Old trips came out. Bad landings. Broken tent poles. A river in Arkansas where Mike had sworn the gravel bar was “right around the bend” for six bends until Will threatened to drown him in ankle-deep water. The time Alex dumped a canoe getting out on the wrong side and then argued that the shore had moved. The time Joel forgot a spoon and ate oatmeal with a tent stake for three mornings.

“I still say that worked,” Joel said.

“It worked in the way prison solutions work,” Will said.

“Efficient and morally clarifying.”

Alex laughed, loud enough to turn heads at the bar. He did not notice, or did not mind. “God, I missed this.”

For once nobody made a joke right away.

Then Mike said, “You missed an audience.”

Alex pointed a fry at him. “And you missed having a soul, but nobody brings that up.”

“I bring it up,” Will said.

“You do,” Mike said. “Constantly.”

Joel drank his beer and let them work. He did not feel outside the group exactly. He felt adjacent to its louder mechanisms. Will and Mike built the plan. Alex built the weather around it. Joel listened, carried, steered when needed, said no when no needed saying, and took his share of the weight. It was enough. Most years it was enough.

After dinner they walked back under a sky that had cleared into hard northern stars. The air was colder now. The ditch behind the motel smelled of mud and wet leaves. Mosquitoes gathered under the lights and vanished in the darker spaces between them.

Mike stopped at Will’s truck and put one hand on the rear strap.

“Still tight,” Will said.

Mike pulled it anyway.

Alex said, “Quit mothering the canoe.”

“The canoe appreciates me.”

“The canoe is planning to leave you.”

“Everything leaves Mike eventually,” Will said.

It came out lightly, but it did not land that way. Mike’s face changed only a little. Enough for Joel to see it. Will saw it too and looked down at the gravel.

Alex, for once, did not fill the space.

Joel said, “We staging the Highlander before or after breakfast?”

Mike let go of the strap. “After. If we say seven, we’ll leave at eight.”

“There’s growth,” Will said.

“Don’t ruin it.”

They sorted the morning load under the motel overhang. The Highlander would go first thing to the North Road Bridge with Mike and Will in it. Joel and Alex would follow in Will’s truck. They would leave the Highlander at the take-out and all come back to South Access in the truck with the boats. It was cleaner than trying to move everything twice and worse than having a shuttle, which made it a normal plan.

No satellite messenger. No emergency beacon. Will mentioned it once, not as panic but as inventory.

“We still not bringing one?” he asked.

Mike shook his head. “Didn’t buy one between lunch and now.”

“Could rent one.”

“From who, the bear?”

Will looked at Joel.

Joel shrugged. “We’ve done without.”

That was true and not an argument, but it passed for one.

“We have two vehicles, paper maps, downloaded maps, first aid, spray, and four functioning adults,” Mike said.

Alex raised his hand. “Three and a half.”

“Fine,” Mike said. “Three and Alex.”

Alex bowed.

Will nodded, not satisfied but finished. “I’m just saying.”

“I know,” Mike said.

The rooms went quiet by eleven, late for the plan and early for them. Joel shared with Will. Alex and Mike took the other room, though Mike left once more to check the truck and came back smelling of cold air.

Will sat on the edge of the bed tying a leader onto a lure by the weak yellow light of the lamp.

“You’re doing that now?” Joel asked.

“If I do it in the morning, Mike will stare at me.”

“He’ll stare anyway.”

“True.”

Joel lay back on the bed without taking off his socks yet. The wall hummed faintly with the next room’s television. Mike and Alex were talking, their words blurred by plaster. Alex’s voice rose, then Mike’s cut across it. Then Alex laughed, that hard bark again, alive and demanding.

Will trimmed the line with nail clippers. The little piece fell somewhere onto the bedspread.

“Find that,” Joel said.

“I will.”

“You won’t.”

“I might.”

“You’ll hook yourself in the ass at two in the morning.”

“That’s not how that works.”

Joel closed his eyes.

Outside, a truck went by on the road and faded north. The canoes rested on Will’s truck under the security light. The Highlander sat beside it, ancient and square and waiting to be abandoned at the far end of the route. In the morning they would put a vehicle in the future and then drive back toward the beginning. It was a practical thing. A normal thing. The sort of thing men did when they trusted maps and weather and one another enough.

Through the wall, Alex laughed once more. After that came Mike’s lower voice, indistinct, then the click of a door latch somewhere down the row, then the building settling into its cheap nighttime silence.

Joel listened until the sounds separated into ordinary parts.

Then he slept.

# Chapter 2 - Two Vehicles

They said seven and left at eight.

That was not failure. It was the trip adjusting itself to the men who had planned it. At seven, Mike knocked on Joel and Will’s door with the flat of his hand and said, “We’re burning daylight,” though the sun was still low and weak over the motel roof. At seven-fifteen, Will was in one sock, tying a leader onto a small spinner because he had dreamed of a broken knot and woken up offended by his own subconscious. At seven-thirty, Alex was standing outside with wet hair and a motel towel around his shoulders, eating crackers from a sleeve and claiming this counted as breakfast.

At seven-forty, Mike decided he would not sleep in the Highlander after all, which meant he had to move two things from the back seat that had been there since spring: a folded camp chair with a torn arm and a cardboard box full of canoe seats, cracked yokes, two old PFDs, and a bilge sponge black at the edges.

“Why do you own garbage professionally?” Alex asked.

Mike set the box on the pavement. “That’s inventory.”

“That sponge has seen death.”

“That sponge has value.”

“To mold.”

Will came out carrying the food barrel. “If that box is coming with us, I’m staying here.”

“It’s not coming.”

“It has been in your car since May.”

“Then it knows how to wait.”

Joel carried his pack out last and set it beside Will’s truck. The morning was cool and bright. Dew stood on the canoes. The motel’s security light had clicked off, and the place looked worse in honest day. The office window was taped at one corner. The ditch behind the building held a skin of fog over black water. Mosquitoes floated in the shadow under the eaves, slow from cold but alive enough.

Will’s truck looked competent under the two canoes. Long bed, cap, roof rack, gear tucked tight beneath. Mike’s Highlander sat beside it, faded gray and square, its rear hatch lifted halfway like a tired jaw. One of the taillights had red tape over a crack.

The plan was simple in the way plans were simple before weather, fatigue, and human nature got involved. Mike would drive the Highlander to the North Road Bridge, the intended take-out. Will would follow in the truck with the boats and most of the gear. They would leave the Highlander at the bridge, pile everyone into Will’s truck, drive back south and east along the forest roads to the old landing, then put in.

Will and Mike had worked it out over three phone calls, two maps, and one argument about whether the logging road between the bridge and the landing was faster than the paved loop. The paved loop had won after Will found a road report from six years earlier and Mike called it “probably still true enough.”

Joel had listened to the summary and said it sounded fine. That had been his role. Fine was not nothing. It meant he did not see the bad assumption yet.

Mike stood with his phone in one hand, looking at the screen.

“Service?” Will asked.

“One bar.”

“That’s generous.”

“It comes and goes up here.”

Alex leaned against the truck and yawned. “I love watching men stare at rectangles in wilderness-adjacent locations.”

“We’re not in the wilderness,” Mike said. “We’re at a motel with vending machines.”

“Exactly. Transitional habitat.”

Mike lifted the phone a little, as if height might improve the truth. “Nothing now.”

“We know there won’t be much,” Will said.

“I’m checking.”

“Great. It’s checked.”

Mike pocketed the phone with visible reluctance. “When something goes wrong, everybody wants the guy who checked.”

“When something goes wrong,” Alex said, “I want the guy who brought crackers.”

“You brought crackers for yourself.”

“I’m not a socialist.”

They ate what passed for breakfast standing in the parking lot. Muffins from Will’s kitchen, bananas bought the day before, instant coffee from the motel room machine that tasted faintly of plastic and old mineral deposits. Alex ate crackers and peanut butter with the seriousness of a man performing a medical routine. Will drank coffee and rearranged the lunch bag three times. Mike drank from his dented thermos and walked around both vehicles, touching straps, tires, hatches, locks.

Joel watched him check the Highlander’s oil even though they had checked it in St. Louis.

“You worried about it?” Joel asked.

“I’m always worried about it.”

“That sounds healthy.”

“It’s old enough to vote and dumb enough to do it wrong.”

Alex looked over. “Is this the vehicle that saves us at the end?”

“It’s the vehicle that is waiting at the end,” Mike said.

“That was not a yes.”

“It starts.”

“Again, not a yes.”

Mike shut the hood. “We’re fine.”

He said it without looking at anyone, as if speaking to the engine.

By eight they were on the road. Mike led in the Highlander. Will followed in the truck, the canoes riding above them, Joel in the passenger seat, Alex in back with his long legs angled around a cooler and a pack he had refused to move because he said it created a useful barrier between him and responsibility.

The town ended quickly. A bait shop, a church, two houses with junked snowmobiles beside the garages, then trees. The road narrowed and rolled north through spruce, jack pine, and birch. The sun came and went through the windshield. The day had the clear feel of good weather before it had been used.

Will drove with one hand low on the wheel, relaxed until he saw a pothole, then personally offended. He had a paper map folded beside him and the downloaded route on a small GPS unit clipped near the dash. He did not look at his phone. Will trusted phones for weather at home and distrusted them everywhere else.

“You think he checked the spare?” Will asked.

“On the Highlander?” Joel said.

“Yeah.”

“Probably.”

“That means no.”

Alex leaned forward between the seats. “Can we not begin the day imagining wheel failures?”

Will said, “We can imagine them now or experience them later.”

“You two grew up together?” Alex asked Joel.

“Church.”

“That explains the optimism.”

“It explains my posture,” Will said.

Joel watched the Highlander ahead of them. It bounced hard over a patched section, recovered, and kept going. Mike drove like he expected the road to get worse and wanted to prove he had known it first.

They reached the North Road Bridge a little after nine.

It was not much of a bridge. Concrete slab, low rails, tannin water moving under it between alder banks. A gravel pullout opened on the far side. There was a post with no sign, two beer cans in the weeds, and a place where someone had cut a sapling and left the pale stump angled toward the road.

Mike pulled the Highlander into the gravel and parked it facing out. Will stopped the truck beside him and left it running.

For a few seconds nobody spoke.

Joel got out and stretched his back. The morning was quieter here than at the motel. No vending-machine hum. No road traffic after their own engines died. The river under the bridge moved steadily, brown and cold-looking, a narrow piece of the route appearing before the route itself. Fog still clung low upstream in torn bands.

This was the future. That was what made it feel wrong. They had not put a canoe in water yet, and already here was the endpoint, waiting with its gravel and weeds and blank signpost. They were leaving a vehicle inside a day they had not earned.

Alex walked to the bridge rail and looked down. “This is where we come out?”

“If everything goes according to plan,” Will said.

“Never say that.”

“It’s literally the plan.”

“Say something more outdoorsy. ‘If the river permits.’”

Mike came around the Highlander with a small dry bag and a coil of paracord. “The river doesn’t care.”

“See?” Alex said. “That’s almost good.”

Mike locked the Highlander, then unlocked it, opened the rear hatch, and checked inside again.

Will said, “What are you looking for?”

“Nothing.”

“You found it?”

Mike shut the hatch. It did not latch. He opened it and slammed it harder. This time it caught.

Alex winced. “She likes it rough.”

“She likes decisive closure,” Mike said.

Joel walked to the edge of the pullout. The river here was too narrow for their canoes to finish dramatically. They would come out tired, probably dirty, probably sick of one another, sliding through alder and road noise after seven days of water and carries. Then there would be the Highlander. A machine made ugly by usefulness. Keys in Mike’s dry pouch. Dry clothes, maybe. A cooler if they had thought of one. The ordinary world restored by a bad suspension and old vinyl seats.

Mike lifted his phone again.

Will saw him. “Still?”

“One bar.”

“Congratulations.”

“Enough maybe for a text.”

“To who?” Alex asked.

Mike did not answer. He typed something, waited, then frowned.

Joel said, “It go?”

“No.”

“Who were you texting?”

“Just checking.”

Will leaned against the truck. “You keep saying checking when you mean worrying.”

“Worrying is checking before it has a task.”

“That’s bleakly accurate.”

Mike stared at the phone another second, then shoved it into his pocket. “No service at the take-out. We knew that.”

“We knew spotty,” Will said.

“No service is a species of spotty.”

Alex laughed, and the sound went under the bridge and came back thinner. Joel heard the first bark, then a second version of it under concrete, lower and farther away. Alex looked pleased with himself, as if he had done that on purpose.

“Nice acoustics,” he said.

Will walked down to the water and crouched, already looking for fish. “There’s current here.”

“That is often a feature of rivers,” Mike said.

“I mean more than I expected.”

“It’s the take-out. We don’t care until next week.”

Joel looked upstream. Alder leaned over the banks. Beyond it, spruce closed the gaps. The fog had the flat look of breath held close to the ground. A raven called from somewhere behind them. Its voice was rough and ordinary and made all four men look up anyway.

Mike checked his watch. “Let’s go. We’re losing the morning.”

They left the Highlander in the gravel, backed Will’s truck around, and drove away.

For a while the empty fact of the staged vehicle sat in the cab with them. Joel looked back once through the rear window and saw the Highlander shrinking between the canoe hulls. Then the road bent, and it was gone.

Alex said, “I hope it doesn’t get lonely.”

Will said, “It has Mike’s old sponge.”

“Companionship comes in many forms.”

Mike had given Will directions for the return to South Access before leaving the bridge, but Will still glanced at the GPS and the paper map when the road forked. He and Mike had argued the fork the night before. Joel remembered enough to know one way was shorter by distance and worse by surface. They took the longer road.

The farther south they went, the rougher the country became. Not higher exactly, but less arranged. Granite showed through the roadside. The ditches deepened. The trees leaned closer. Old logging spurs appeared and vanished, each marked by a gap and two ruts disappearing into brush.

Alex watched through the window. “This reminds me of roads outside Fairbanks, except with more deciduous optimism.”

“Deciduous optimism?” Joel said.

“Leaves. Seasonal delusion.”

Will nodded. “That one I like.”

“Thank you.”

“Don’t make a habit of it.”

The last road to South Access was barely two lanes and then not really one. Branches touched the truck cap. The canoes creaked against the racks. A strap made a short, low hum, then stopped when Will slowed for a hole full of black water.

Mike, now in the back seat beside Alex, leaned forward. “Left here.”

“I know.”

“You almost missed it.”

“I did not almost miss it. I approached it with suspense.”

“That’s not a driving technique.”

Alex said, “It is in Seattle.”

“You drive a Civic,” Mike said.

“I drive an old Civic. There’s heritage.”

“You drove here in a plane.”

“That also had heritage. And a crying baby.”

The old landing appeared without ceremony.

The forest road widened into a muddy clearing sloped toward dark water. A weathered signboard stood at an angle near the brush. Whatever notices had once been pinned there were gone or reduced to pale rectangles under rusted staples. There was a fire ring full of wet ash, a length of old rope in the weeds, and two boot tracks hardened in mud from some earlier party. The lake lay narrow and black-green below the landing, held between spruce and low shelves of granite. Morning fog still lingered over the far shore though the sun had lifted above the trees.

No other vehicles were there.

Will parked facing out, because Mike told him to before he could do it himself. For a moment nobody moved. The truck engine ticked. Somewhere near the signboard, a mosquito whined against the glass.

Then Mike opened his door. “All right.”

The practical work took them.

Packs came out first. Food barrel. Rod tubes. Paddles. PFDs. Rain gear. The spare tarp. Alex’s personal bag of edible beige things, which Mike placed in the wrong pile on purpose and Alex retrieved with dignity. Will checked his tackle bag and then checked it again. Mike took the map case from the front seat and set it on the hood.

Joel stood at the back of the truck and looked over the spread. The gear had been handled so many times already that it seemed less like preparation than rehearsal. Everything had a category. Things that needed to stay dry. Things that could get wet. Things needed at lunch. Things needed only if something went wrong. Things they had brought because last year someone had not brought them.

Mike and Will argued over boat loading.

“You and I should not be in the same canoe all day,” Will said.

“Because of trim?” Alex asked.

“Because of criminal law.”

Mike said, “Joel and I in back makes sense if we split up.”

“You and Joel both in back of different boats,” Will said. “That’s what I’m saying.”

“Alex up front with Joel,” Mike said. “You up front with me.”

Alex raised his hand. “Least skilled guy in front. Tradition honors me.”

“You’re not least skilled,” Will said.

Alex looked touched.

“You’re least skilled in a canoe,” Will added.

“There it is.”

Joel took a PFD from the pile. “I’m fine with Alex up front.”

“Joel likes a challenge,” Alex said.

“Joel likes being able to see the challenge.”

Mike pointed at Will. “You fish from the front if we get time.”

“I fish from wherever God places me.”

“You don’t believe in God.”

“I believe in structure.”

“Then get in the front.”

The canoes came down one at a time. Mike and Joel took the green one first. Will loosened the front strap while Mike handled the rear. Alex stood ready, then stood in the wrong place, then moved when everyone told him to. The buckle clicked against the roof rack.

The hull shifted.

“Wait,” Joel said. “Just wait.”

Mike stopped. “What?”

“Block’s crooked.”

Joel reached up and set the foam block straight under the gunwale before they slid the canoe down. It was a small thing, barely a problem. But if they had pulled hard it would have dropped the wrong way against the door frame, scraped paint, maybe cracked nothing, maybe cracked something, maybe only given Mike a grievance for the first three miles.

“All right,” Joel said.

They lowered the canoe and carried it toward the landing. The hull was cold and damp in Joel’s hand. Mud sucked at the soles of his shoes as they reached the water. The lake moved softly against stone, dark and almost without wave.

Alex watched them set it down. “Ninety-dollar mistake avoided.”

Mike said, “It would not have been ninety dollars.”

“You don’t know that. Spiritually.”

The second canoe came down easier. They laid both near the water and began loading. Heavy packs low. Food barrel near the middle of Mike and Will’s boat because they argued over front and back weight until Joel suggested middle and both accepted it as if they had invented compromise. Rods along the side where Will could reach them if they stopped. Rain gear near the top. First-aid kit in the red bag. Spare paddle lashed. Bear spray clipped where Alex could not bury it in a duffel.

“No,” Mike said, moving the spray.

“I had it,” Alex said.

“You had it clipped to the grab loop of a bag that will be under a pack.”

“Still technically had it.”

“Quit making me mother you.”

“Never.”

Will snapped the food barrel latch shut. The sound was clean and small in the clearing.

Mike looked at the map one more time, though the first leg was not complicated. Narrow lake, north shore, left into the channel after the rock point, short carry. He traced it with his finger. Will looked with him, not because he needed to but because not looking would concede something.

Joel checked his own boat. He would sit in back. Alex in front. That was fine. Alex might paddle lazily once conversation started, but he had strength and would admit when he did not know what he was doing. Joel could work with that. Better than a man who guessed and called it instinct.

The bugs thickened as the sun warmed the brush. Mosquitoes came from the shaded ditch. Blackflies found wrists and the backs of ears. Alex slapped his neck.

“Jesus. They filed paperwork.”

“Could be worse,” Mike said.

Will looked up. “That’s going on your tombstone.”

“It will still be true.”

Alex put on his PFD and tugged at the straps. “How tight is too tight?”

“If you can breathe, you’re fine,” Mike said.

“That’s your whole safety talk?”

“You want a pamphlet?”

Joel stepped closer and pulled the lower strap on Alex’s vest until it sat right.

Alex looked down. “See? Gentle instruction. Joel understands leadership.”

Joel said, “I understand I’m sitting behind you and don’t want to watch you fall out.”

“Quit mothering me.”

“Then stop needing it.”

Will made the cough-laugh again and turned away to hide it badly.

By ten-twenty the truck was locked, the key sealed in a small dry pouch in Will’s pack, and the boats sat half in water. Mike stood at the edge of the landing with the map case under one arm and his paddle in the other hand. The old signboard leaned behind him. The road they had come in on already looked less like an exit than a scar that might heal over.

Mike looked at the boats, the packs, the men.

“We’re exactly where we ought to be,” he said.

Alex groaned. “You’re going to have that printed on something.”

“Maybe your memorial shirt.”

“Bland colors only. Sensitive palate.”

Will stepped into the front of Mike’s canoe first, careful but not graceful, one hand on each side. The boat rocked and steadied. He placed his rod where he could see it.

Mike got in back and settled with one small shift that made the canoe behave. He did not talk while doing it. His body knew the correction before his mouth could improve it.

Joel held the second canoe steady while Alex stepped into the front.

“Middle,” Joel said.

“I know.”

“Low.”

“I know.”

“You’re standing high.”

“I am built high.”

“Sit down.”

Alex sat. The canoe rocked once, then settled. Joel stepped into the back, placed his feet, and lowered himself onto the seat. Damp cold came through his pants. He took the paddle and set the blade flat on the water.

For a moment they floated half a canoe length from shore while Mike adjusted something in his boat and Will freed a lure that had already found a strap.

Joel looked back.

Will’s truck sat alone in the muddy clearing. The old landing showed their boot prints and the two grooves where the canoes had slid. The signboard had nothing to tell them. The road disappeared into spruce and alder so quickly that it seemed improbable a truck had come through it at all.

A paddle knocked once against Will’s gunwale.

“Sorry,” Will said.

“Try water,” Mike said.

“I was checking acoustic properties.”

Alex dipped his paddle too hard and splashed Joel’s boot.

“Testing splash properties.”

Joel pushed them off with one stroke. Gravel whispered under the hull and then was gone. The water deepened from brown to black-green beneath them. A loon called somewhere ahead, long and hollow, widening over the narrow lake until the sound seemed to belong to the water more than the bird.

No one spoke for a few minutes.

This was always the best part. The first silence after all the planning and argument and straps. Paddle in. Pull. Lift. Drip. The canoe sliding forward with a grace men did not deserve and could not keep long. Spruce leaned over the shore. Granite showed in pale shelves at the waterline. Fog lifted in torn strips, and the sun turned the upper branches gold while the trunks stayed black.

Joel felt Alex’s first few strokes strong and uneven in the front. Will and Mike moved ahead, their canoe tracking cleaner. Will turned his head once toward a ring on the surface near shore, already thinking fish. Mike corrected their angle with two quiet strokes from the back.

Joel matched them and let the old rhythm find his shoulders.

Behind them, the landing slipped out of sight. The truck vanished first, then the signboard, then the road. The last thing Joel saw was the pale mud where they had stood arranging gear as if arrangement could save them from disorder.

Then the trees closed the gap, and there was only water ahead.

# Chapter 3 - First Water

The first lake was narrow enough that it never let them forget shore.

Spruce leaned over the right side, black at the waterline and green where the sun struck the higher limbs. To the left, granite shelves came down in pale steps, some dry and white, some dark with old water marks. Small cedars had found cracks in the rock and grown there crookedly, as if the place had tolerated them but not welcomed them. Fog lifted in strips ahead of the canoes and broke apart when the sun touched it.

Joel paddled from the back and let Alex set whatever rhythm he could manage in the front.

Alex was strong. That helped. He was also uneven, which did not. He would paddle hard for thirty strokes, then look off toward a bird or a snag or some imagined thing in the trees and let the blade rest on the gunwale until the canoe began to yaw. Joel corrected from behind without saying much. A quiet draw. A little push on the left. A stroke held longer than it needed. The boat came back.

After ten minutes Alex glanced over his shoulder.

“You’re doing something back there.”

“I am.”

“Feels judgmental.”

“That’s steering.”

“Same vibe.”

Ahead of them, Mike and Will moved cleaner. Mike was in back, compact and economical, doing less than he seemed to. Will sat up front with a rod laid along the side of the canoe and kept turning his head toward shadows near shore. He paddled well when he remembered paddling was the point. Every patch of downed wood, every dark pocket under alder, every ring on the surface took part of his attention.

Mike’s voice came over the water. “Will.”

“I see it.”

“You see what?”

“Nothing. That’s the problem.”

“Paddle.”

“I am paddling.”

“You are browsing.”

Alex smiled. “Fishing is just aquatic browsing.”

Will did not turn. “That’s not terrible.”

“Don’t encourage him,” Mike said.

The sound carried strangely on the lake. Not wrong. Just wider than it should have been. Mike’s words seemed to lie flat over the water. Will’s answer came back thin from the granite. Joel knew enough not to make anything of it. Lakes did that. Shorelines did that. Fog and rock and morning air could take a sound apart and hand it back slightly changed.

He had heard his own paddle drip behind him before. He had heard ducks sound like men coughing. He had once heard a loon call so close it seemed to come from inside the canoe.

The woods were not quiet. They only made you listen harder.

They kept to the right side of the lake because Mike had said the first portage would be easier to find from that shore. The lake ran long and crooked, more like a widened river than open water. Twice they passed narrow cuts that looked like routes until they drew close and saw deadfall and shallow weeds. A beaver lodge rose on the left bank, sticks piled high and silvered at the tips. The water around it held a scum of chewed bark.

Alex pointed with his paddle.

“That occupied?”

Will said, “Probably.”

“Do beavers attack?”

Mike said, “If you annoy them.”

Alex looked back. “Why is that always the answer?”

Joel said, “It’s the honest answer to most animal questions.”

Alex dipped his blade again. “I grew up around animals that could kill you. Beavers feel insulting.”

“Beavers can ruin your day,” Will said.

“Lots of things can ruin my day. Plain oatmeal. Small talk. Men named Todd.”

Mike looked over his shoulder. “Paddle.”

Alex paddled.

The lake narrowed further. The water changed from black-green to brown where a creek entered through grass and alder. Mud smell came through the cold, rich and sour. A kingfisher rattled from a dead branch and crossed in front of them, blue-gray and quick, sounding offended by their presence.

Joel watched the bird go.

For the first hour he felt the old pleasure return in the muscles before it reached the mind. The pull of the blade. The little shift of weight in the hips. The corrected angle. The canoe sliding as if the effort were being translated into something cleaner than effort. In Madison, his days had become too square. Rooms, screens, calendar blocks, errands fitted between calls, wedding decisions that seemed small until there were fifty of them. Here the work was dumb and whole. Push water. Move north. Avoid rock. Keep gear dry. Watch the weather. Eat when possible.

That was why he came. Or part of why. The rest was harder to name and therefore easier to ignore.

Mike raised his paddle ahead and pointed left.

Will said something Joel could not hear. Mike pointed again, sharper this time.

Alex turned. “What’s the verdict, back there?”

“Portage soon.”

“Like portage portage?”

“No,” Joel said. “The decorative kind.”

“I’m asking because backpacking has made me emotionally prepared for carrying things, but not boats.”

“You’ll be fine.”

“I love when people lie gently.”

They followed Mike and Will toward a low opening in the shore that did not look like a portage until they were almost on it. A strip of mud, two rocks placed by human hands, alder pressed back enough to suggest a path. There was an old blaze on a spruce trunk, nearly healed over. The landing smelled of muck and wet leaves.

Mike got out first in calf-deep water and held the canoe. Will stepped into the shallows, slipped once in his hiking sandals, recovered, and swore with unnecessary precision.

“Rock,” he said.

Mike looked down. “Yes.”

“Under silt.”

“Where rocks live.”

Joel brought their canoe in beside them. Alex reached for a branch and missed it. The canoe swung sideways.

“Left,” Joel said.

“My left or boat left?”

“Your actual left.”

“Good. I only brought the one.”

Joel planted his paddle and held them steady. Alex got out with one foot in water and one on rock, paused in a shape no man could hold long, then committed to the water with a splash.

“Dry feet lasted ninety-three minutes,” he said.

Will looked over. “That’s optimistic.”

“Emotionally dry.”

They unloaded enough for the portage. Mike did not want to double back more than necessary. Will did not want to overload the boats on the trail and crack something. This produced ten minutes of argument beside the water while mosquitoes found them in the alder shade.

“We single portage if we’re not stupid about it,” Mike said.

“We double portage if we’re not stupid about it,” Will said.

Alex slapped at his wrist. “Those sound suspiciously similar.”

Joel lifted one pack and weighed it by feel. “How long is it?”

“Quarter mile,” Mike said.

Will made a small sound.

Mike turned. “What?”

“Old report said closer to half.”

“Old report included whining.”

“You can’t correct distance with attitude.”

“Watch me.”

Joel looked at the path. It rose almost immediately from the landing, muddy at first, then root-bound under spruce. He could see ten yards before it bent and disappeared. Quarter mile, half mile. Both were short if the footing was good. Both were long if it was not.

“Let’s not make the first portage heroic,” he said.

Mike gave him a look that asked whether he was taking Will’s side. Joel ignored it and pulled his pack from the canoe.

They decided on a modified double portage, which meant everyone pretended compromise had occurred. First trip: heavy packs, rods, paddles, map case, the food barrel carried between Mike and Alex on a strap because neither trusted the other with it alone. Second trip: canoes and whatever had been left to punish them for optimism.

The trail was worse than it had looked and better than it could have been. Mud at the start, then roots, then a rise over granite where the moss had been scraped by other sandals and boots and never quite recovered. Spruce branches brushed their shoulders. A few birches leaned pale and thin among the darker trees. The air inside the portage held less wind and more insect.

Will walked ahead of Joel with two rods in one hand and a pack that looked too high on his shoulders.

“You good?” Joel asked.

“I’m calibrated.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the answer before the answer.”

Up ahead, Mike said, “Step left here.”

Alex said, “Your left or my left?”

“Trail left.”

“That’s not a thing.”

“It is if you understand trails.”

“I understand mountains. Trails there have dignity.”

“These have water.”

“Water has no dignity.”

The food barrel knocked against a tree with a hollow plastic sound.

Mike said, “Watch it.”

Alex said, “The tree attacked me.”

“Tree had right of way.”

Joel walked with his head slightly down, watching footing. Mud took the tread of his hiking sandals and gave back a sucking sound. The pack straps bit into the muscle above his collarbones. Sweat started under his fleece despite the cool morning. His body had not yet accepted the trip. It was still negotiating.

Halfway through the portage, the trail crossed an open patch of rock. The sun came down clean there. Joel stopped and shifted the pack to ease one shoulder. From the rock he could see a narrow piece of the lake behind them through black trunks, bright where the fog had burned off. Their canoes were hidden by alder at the landing. The water looked empty.

Then something knocked.

Once.

Wood on hollow wood. Or a paddle against a gunwale. A small sound, ordinary and exact.

Joel turned his head.

Will stopped ahead of him. “What?”

“You hear that?”

Will listened. Mike and Alex kept moving ahead, still arguing about whether rice was a side dish or a failure of imagination.

“Hear what?” Will asked.

Joel looked back through the trees. The lake flashed between spruce trunks. No canoe showed. No person. No movement except water.

“Nothing,” Joel said.

Will waited a second longer. “Branch maybe.”

“Probably.”

“You sure?”

Joel smiled faintly. “That’s your job.”

“My job is to introduce uncertainty, not resolve it.”

“Then you’re doing great.”

They moved on.

At the far end, the portage dropped to a second landing, better than the first. Flat rock sloped into a narrower piece of water that ran north between alder and low marsh. The lake they had crossed was behind them now. Ahead, the route tightened into something that might be called a creek if no one wanted to sound grand.

Mike and Alex set the food barrel down. Alex bent with his hands on his knees.

“Quarter mile, my ass.”

Will came in behind Joel and looked at Mike.

Mike said, “It was a quarter mile emotionally.”

“That’s not how distance works.”

Alex pointed at Will without looking up. “There it is.”

They drank water and went back for the boats.

The return trip felt shorter because their bodies knew the path and because nothing heavy sat on their backs. The sound of their own steps changed after they had walked a trail once. Joel noticed this each year. First pass, every root was a question. Second pass, the body had made a rough map. Step here. Duck there. Mud on the left. Rock on the right. Branch at eye level if you stopped paying attention.

At the first landing, the two canoes lay where they had left them, half-hidden in alder and shadow. Water lapped softly against one hull. A painter rope tapped once against the bow, light and quick.

Joel looked at it longer than necessary.

There was your sound, he told himself. Rope. Water. Boat shifting.

Mike took the first canoe with Will. They lifted it, rolled it up, and set the center yoke on Will’s shoulders. Will adjusted under the weight and took a few testing steps.

“You want me to take it?” Mike asked.

“No.”

“You sure?”

“Yes.”

“You’re walking crooked.”

“I have a canoe on my head.”

Alex watched him go. “That looks bad.”

“It is,” Joel said.

“Do we all pretend it’s spiritual?”

“Mostly.”

Joel and Alex carried the second canoe. Alex wanted to try the yoke and lasted thirty yards before admitting that backpacking had not prepared him for carrying a boat upside down while blind to the sky and personally hated by every branch in Canada. Joel took over without comment.

Under the canoe, the world became a strip of ground and feet. Joel saw mud, roots, Alex’s sandals ahead when Alex carried the paddles, Mike’s heels beyond him when the trail straightened. The canoe amplified some sounds and removed others. His breath came back at him. Brush scratched the hull inches from his ears. Once, a branch dragged along the canoe’s side with a dry fingernail rasp.

He thought of the knock again and was annoyed with himself.

There were four of them. Two canoes. Paddles. Ropes. Trees. Water. Any wilderness trip produced a hundred hollow sounds before lunch. The mind sorted some and kept others for no reason but timing.

At the open rock, Alex stopped ahead and looked back.

“You alive?”

Joel shifted under the yoke. “Keep moving.”

“That’s not a yes.”

“It’s canoe for yes.”

At the far landing they set the boats down and loaded again. This took longer than unloading because the order now mattered. Will’s fishing rods had to be accessible without becoming spears. Alex’s food could not be buried under the first-aid kit because he claimed low blood sugar made him philosophical. Rain gear near the top. Map case where Mike could reach it from the back. Joel’s day bag behind his seat. Spare paddle along the left side.

They pushed off into the narrow water just before noon.

The route after the portage was not much wider than two canoes. Alder leaned over both sides, and in places the branches met overhead. The water moved slowly enough that they still had to paddle. It was darker here, stained by leaves and old beaver work. The bottom showed in the shallows as a wavering brown skin over mud and stone.

Will had his rod in his lap within five minutes.

Mike said, “No.”

“I’m not fishing.”

“You are holding a fishing rod.”

“I’m preparing to fish.”

“Prepare to paddle.”

Will looked back over his shoulder. “There is a difference.”

“Not to the boat.”

Alex whispered, loudly enough for everyone, “There’s trouble in the marriage.”

Joel corrected their angle around a half-submerged log. Alex’s strokes had grown lazy after the portage, but he admitted it without shame.

“My arms have filed a complaint.”

“Denied,” Joel said.

“You’re a harsh captain.”

“I’m not a captain.”

“Back guy.”

“Still no.”

“Rear admiral.”

“Absolutely not.”

The channel bent right, then left, then right again. Mike called back directions in ordinary language. “Stay left of that rock.” “Come right.” “Shallow middle.” Will argued with some of them and obeyed most. Joel followed their line when it made sense and chose his own when it did not. He liked being in back because the back let him see the whole boat’s mistake before it arrived.

Alex turned once and saw Joel looking past him.

“You always this serious on water?”

“Yes.”

“Good to know.”

“You always this chatty?”

“Also yes.”

“Good to know.”

Alex smiled and faced forward. For a few minutes he paddled better.

They stopped for lunch on a low gravel bar where the channel widened under a stand of jack pine. The place was not a campsite, just a place to get out. Enough stone to stand on, enough sun to push back the bugs, enough current that the water sounded clean over the shallows.

Will had a line in the water before Mike had opened the food barrel.

“I thought you were hungry,” Mike said.

“I am spiritually hungry for fish.”

“You catch one now, we’re not cooking it.”

“I didn’t ask you to be involved.”

“You’re in my canoe.”

“That’s a logistical arrangement, not a moral framework.”

Alex sat on a rock and opened his private bag of bland food. He pulled out tortillas, peanut butter, and a packet of tuna he regarded with uncertainty.

“Branching out?” Joel asked.

“This is advanced work.”

“That tuna has lemon pepper.”

“I know. I’m not afraid.”

“Okay,” he said. “This part does not suck.”

Joel let the boat glide. “Careful. Mike might hear.”

“He already knows. That’s what makes him insufferable.”

Joel angled the canoe to follow.

Behind them, from somewhere near the channel mouth, came a single hollow knock.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Just wood against wood, or paddle against canoe, or a branch shifting in water.

Joel turned.

The channel behind them lay empty. Alder. Brown water. A few floating leaves. The place they had just come through held still in the afternoon light.

Alex looked back too. “What was that?”

Mike and Will were ahead, their canoe moving toward the point. Will had his paddle across his lap, not in the water. Mike’s blade flashed once on the right.

“Probably a branch,” Joel said.

Alex waited, listening.

The sound did not come again.

“Sure,” Alex said.

He turned forward and took up his paddle.

Joel stayed looking back for one second more. The channel was already closing into distance. Sound carried over water. Boats made noises after you left them. Branches knocked. Ropes tapped. The world had joints and hinges everywhere if you listened long enough.

He dipped his paddle and brought the canoe back on line.

They went on toward the first camp with enough daylight to feel successful.

# Chapter 4 - Granite Shelf

The granite point was empty.

Mike saw it first and lifted his paddle. The gesture was small and certain, blade angled toward the left shore where pale rock came down in layers through jack pine and black spruce. A few red pines stood higher than the rest, long-trunked and clean-limbed, holding the last good light in their tops. Below them, the point sloped into water dark enough to double the trees.

“Camp,” Mike called.

Nobody argued.

They had been paddling long enough that even Alex did not make a joke. The afternoon had gone soft and slanted. The lake was not wide, but the wind had found the length of it and pushed little black wrinkles toward the boats. The work was not hard. It was just constant. Paddle, correct, watch the next point, watch Mike’s boat, keep Alex moving, keep the front from wandering toward shore.

Joel felt the day in his shoulders. Not pain yet. A thickening. His hands had gone soft between trips and were remembering friction. The place between thumb and forefinger burned when he shifted his grip. His hiking sandals were wet, then drying, then wet again, and his feet had the swollen, gritty feel they got after a day in water and mud.

Alex turned halfway around.

“That real camp or Mike camp?”

“What’s Mike camp?”

“Any place Mike says is fine because he no longer wants to look.”

Joel glanced past him toward the point. There was a break in the trees, a flat place above the rock, and a dark line where people had dragged boats before. “Looks real.”

“God exists.”

“You don’t believe that.”

“I believe in flat ground.”

Ahead, Mike and Will angled in first. Will had his paddle across his lap and his fishing rod already in one hand, as if the day might otherwise forget his priorities. Mike said something Joel couldn’t make out. Will said something back. Mike pointed to the landing with more force than the situation required.

Joel brought their canoe around the lee side of the point. The water quieted near shore. Granite showed under it in gray plates with seams of weed between them. He let the boat glide until the front touched stone.

Alex looked down. “Elegant.”

“Out.”

“I’m appreciating.”

“Appreciate from shore.”

“Don’t do a full performance,” Joel said.

“I’m giving the landing what it deserves.”

“You’re giving me a problem.”

He pulled the canoe up higher.

“Boats above the waterline,” he said. “Wind shifts tonight, I don’t want to hear them banging all night.”

“You don’t want to hear anything all night,” Will said.

“Correct.”

Alex dragged a pack onto the rock. “Is this where I ask if we can sit down?”

“No,” Mike said.

“Good. I won’t.”

“You just did.”

“I asked if this was where.”

Joel lifted his day bag and set it near the first flat place above the landing. The campsite was better than he expected. A granite shelf with enough dirt pockets for tents, two good trees back from the fire ring, a pair of red pines on the water side, and a rough bench made from a log dragged against stone. Someone had left a ring of blackened rocks and a half-burned split log. There was a trail leading back into spruce where previous campers had gone for latrine privacy or firewood or both. Someone had cut small limbs and stacked them under a rock overhang. Old, damp, probably smoky, but wood.

There were signs of people and not much else. A twist of blue cord tied to a branch. A square of foil half-buried in ash. Boot prints softened by rain and time. One rusted bottle cap wedged in a crack.

“Not bad,” Will said.

Mike looked around as if he had made the place himself. “It opens up after this.”

Will turned. “What does?”

“Tomorrow. After the next narrow section. More room, better water.”

“You said that about today.”

“And was I wrong?”

Will looked at the lake, the rock, the trees, the reasonable camp they had reached with enough daylight to unload like civilized men. “Annoyingly, not completely.”

Alex raised a finger. “Put that on his memorial shirt.”

“Everyone’s obsessed with my memorial shirt,” Mike said.

“You make people imagine it,” Will said.

They unloaded everything before doing anything comfortable. That was Mike’s rule and, worse, a good one. Packs came out. Rods came out. Paddles went under the pulled-up canoes, tucked out of the way and off the grit. The food barrel was carried to the middle of camp and then moved because Mike said the middle of camp was where idiots put food. Will opened it anyway to get the dinner bag. The latch made its clean plastic snap.

A small thing. A camp sound. Joel heard it and felt the day settle around the noise.

He set his pack near the two trees he liked and looked out over the lake instead of opening it. The far shore was darkening. Sun still held in the pine tops, but the water had lost its brightness and now seemed to store the shade coming off the trees. A loon called from the north, long and hollow. Another answered, or the first call came back broken by distance. The two notes overlapped for a second and left the lake larger than it had been.

“You coming or writing a poem?”

Joel turned.

Alex stood with a bundle of tent poles over one shoulder and the tent bag under his arm, smiling at his own usefulness.

Joel said, “Neither if I can avoid it.”

“You looked deeply employed.”

“I was waiting for you to do something correctly.”

Alex handed him the tent bag. “Then your vigil ends.”

Joel had been camping in a hammock almost exclusively the last few seasons, partly because he slept better off the ground and partly because he liked having a system that was his alone. Straps, suspension, tarp, underquilt if weather wanted to be rude. He liked the order of it. Tree to tree. Line tight. Sag right. Tarp pitched low enough to matter.

Will brought a hammock too, but mostly for lounging. He still slept in the tent more often than not, unless the weather was perfect or he had talked himself into an experiment. Will liked owning options. Joel liked eliminating them.

“Yes.”

“All week?”

“That’s the plan.”

“Plans are how men experience hope,” Alex said.

Will looked over. “That was mine.”

Joel clipped the carabiner and pulled the suspension through. The webbing made a dry scrape against bark.

Mike shook his head. “Ground exists for a reason.”

“So do rocks,” Joel said. “I don’t sleep on those either.”

Will had his own hammock half out of its sack and was looking at the trees behind camp with a fisherman’s version of commitment, which meant one eye was still on the water.

“You contain redundant systems.”

“Door facing water,” Alex said.

“Door out of the wind,” Mike said.

“I want a view.”

“You’ll be unconscious.”

“I dream visually.”

Mike stared at him.

Will, crouched over his hammock sack, said, “Put his side facing the water. If a bear comes, it gets him first.”

Alex nodded. “Finally. Logic.”

Behind him, Mike zipped the tent door open, then closed it, testing it the way men tested zippers in the woods: not because they distrusted the zipper exactly, but because a zipper that failed at night became a moral problem.

Mike looked at Alex. “Don’t yank on it.”

“I have used zippers before.”

“Not always well.”

Alex spread his hands. “Quit mothering me.”

Joel glanced over.

That one had been said enough now. It belonged to Alex fully. He did not have to think before handing it out.

He pitched the tarp above it even though the sky was clear. Early September could change its mind without warning, and the first night of any trip was not the night to trust a blue sky.

Mike saw him. “Water first.”

“I’m at the water.”

“For drinking.”

Will stopped, considered whether he could argue it, and came back for the filter.

Joel handled dinner because he usually did. Not every dinner, not always the cooking, but a lot of the prep. The others made fun of him until they ate what he brought. Then they asked how much was left.

The more ambitious meals could wait: quesadillas with chorizo and red onion, lentils and yams if Will actually caught fish, fish fry if the lake gave them enough, Zatarain’s with sausage when nobody wanted to think, mac and cheese with bacon when morale needed to be purchased cheaply.

“Eventually.”

Mike took another piece. “Don’t change it more.”

Will appeared with the gravity bag, water dripping from one elbow. “Agreed. This is one of your useful traits.”

Joel looked at him. “My useful trait is meat dehydration?”

“Don’t get attached,” Mike said.

Will hung the gravity bag from a low limb. “That limb is sketchy.”

“It’s holding,” Mike said.

The smell came up slowly: tomato, garlic, dried herbs, a faint sweetness from hours in the dehydrator. Not home exactly. Everyone put on long sleeves. Will found a head net and wore it like a man proving a point. Alex mocked him until one mosquito got him on the eyelid.

“Head net me,” Alex said.

Will looked at him through mesh. “No.”

“Too late.”

Alex shook his head. “No. This has discipline.”

Alex took another piece of jerky. “Unless society collapses.”

“Day one,” Joel said.

“Careful,” Joel said. “That one’s mine.”

Alex glanced at it. “I know. It looks judgmental.”

“Everything I own can’t be judgmental.”

“I have evidence.”

After dinner, Will went back to the shore with his rod. Nobody told him not to. Everyone knew by now that Will would fish if there was water, light, and enough motor function left in his arms. It was less a choice than a tide.

The lake darkened by degrees. Across the water, the opposite shore went from green to black. The first stars appeared between the pine tops before the sky was fully night. The chill came up from the stone. It entered through damp sandal straps and wet cuffs and the places where sweat had dried into salt.

Will cast from the edge of the granite shelf. The lure made a small whip in the air and a softer sound when it touched water. He reeled slowly, patiently, his head net pushed back now that the bugs had thinned in the open breeze.

Alex sat near the fire ring with his fleece zipped to his chin. “You actually enjoy that?”

“Yes.”

“You know there are fish at stores.”

“They’re not surprised to see you.”

Will cast again. “Part of it.”

Thumb-thick sticks, pine curls, a few split pieces from the dry stack. The hatchet bit twice into one stubborn piece before Mike gave up and fed it half-burned ends from the old ring. The fire took with a low crackle, then settled into itself.

He could not help it. The day’s fatigue left him, or else the fire gave him a place to spend it. He told the story of a backpacking trip in the Cascades where a friend had packed six pounds of cheese and no rain jacket. He told it too long and too well, with voices, with pauses, with a whole middle section about the cheese sweating through a stuff sack that should have been cut and wasn’t because everyone was tired and the fire was warm and Will, despite himself, kept asking questions.

Mike’s jaw moved once. “I mean people want the story without the part where things suck.”

About jobs, partners, houses, obligations, knees, money, fatigue, the fact that a seven-day route now required negotiation with calendars months in advance. It was about Mike’s life staying loose while everyone else’s had tightened. Or it was about nothing that neat.

The fire burned low. Mike poured a little water over one edge, then stopped because there was still heat enough to sit with. Steam rose and carried the smell of wet ash. The night pressed in around the small ring of men and gear.

“You actually committing?” Joel asked.

“Coward.”

“Strategist.”

Will whispered from inside the tent, “He’s going to push early.”

“Mike?” Joel said.

“No, the loon.”

Joel smiled in the dark.

“He wants to make the big lake before wind,” Will said.

“That bad?”

“No. Maybe. It’s fine if we get there clean.”

“Fine according to you or fine according to Mike?”

Will was quiet a moment. “Exactly.”

Then silence came down, not all at once, but by layers.

Will stopped talking. Mike and Alex put out the fire. Their tent zipper opened and closed. A headlamp beam crossed Joel’s tarp and disappeared. The food barrel latch clicked once more, or Joel thought it did, though it could have been a buckle on Mike’s pack.

The hammock held him in a shallow curve. His shoulders ached. His feet were cold. Somewhere behind him, from the tent, Will breathed with a small dry catch every few breaths.

For a moment Joel did not move.

Outside, the lake was quiet. The camp had the dense stillness of first night, when every object was in the place men had put it and had not yet become familiar enough to ignore.

From the shoreline came a soft knock.

One sound. Hollow. Wood or hull against stone.

Joel opened his eyes fully.

He waited.

Nothing followed. No voice. No footstep. No second knock. Only the lake moving lightly below the granite and the wind worrying the top of one pine.

He pictured the canoes pulled high, angled on rock, paddles tucked beneath them, painter rope loose enough to touch. Wind could do it. Cooling hull could do it. Water lifting a branch could do it. There were a dozen ways for the night to make one ordinary sound.

From the tent, Will breathed in and out.

Joel rolled slightly in the hammock, careful not to shift too hard and wake the trees with strap noise, and closed his eyes again.

The camp held. The lake held. The dark kept its own counsel.

After a while, Joel slept.

# Chapter 5 - Beaver Work

Joel woke to the sound of Will’s zipper.

Not the tent zipper at first. Smaller. A bag, probably. Then the tent door itself, slow enough that whoever opened it thought slowness made less sound. It did not. The teeth came apart one by one in the gray morning, dry and careful and loud.

“I made a tent-based decision.”

The fire ring was gray and cold. Mike and Alex’s tent had not moved except for one side panel sagging where Alex had probably pressed into it. The canoes lay above the shoreline, dark with dew, paddles tucked underneath where they had left them. The loose painter rope touched a hull once in the faint breeze. Tap. Then nothing.

Joel looked at it and then away.

Will had already seen the rods.

“They’re wet,” he said.

“Everything’s wet.”

“Not everything is a precision instrument.”

“Your rod is a stick with jewelry.”

Will looked offended but too tired to answer well. “That’s not even worth correcting.”

Mike’s voice came from the other tent. “If you two are talking about fishing before coffee, I’m leaving you here.”

Alex groaned from inside. “Is the wilderness always this administrative?”

Joel swung his feet out of the hammock and sat up. Cold came through his base layer and made him move faster. He pulled on his fleece, then his wet hiking sandals, grimacing as the straps took hold of his feet. He had camp shoes buried somewhere but did not want to open the pack yet. The first step on granite was sharp and cold.

Morning camp took shape without ceremony. Water first. Stove next. Breakfast by negotiation.

Will wanted pancakes because he had remembered them into existence the night before. Mike wanted bars and coffee because he wanted miles. Alex said he was willing to support pancakes in principle but not if the batter had texture. Joel found instant oats near the top of the food bag and held them up.

“This is the compromise.”

“That’s not compromise,” Will said. “That’s surrender with raisins.”

“No raisins.”

“Worse.”

Mike was already filling the pot. “Oats.”

Alex sat up in the tent with his hair crushed flat on one side. “Do oats have seasoning?”

“They have whatever you put in them,” Joel said.

“So no.”

“Brown sugar.”

“That’s not seasoning. That’s apology.”

They boiled water and made coffee with Folgers packets. The packets always seemed like a bad idea at home and a good idea in camp. Tear, pour, stir, drink something hot enough to correct the body. Joel ate oats from his mug and watched fog lift from the lake in low pieces. It moved like smoke that had forgotten fire.

Mike checked the map while standing. He did not like sitting before the route was decided, even if the route had been decided months ago.

“We want to be moving by eight,” he said.

Will looked at the sky. “That means nine.”

“It means eight.”

Alex, still inside the tent, said, “It means someone will be looking for his headlamp at eight-ten.”

“I know where my headlamp is,” Mike said.

Nobody said anything.

Mike looked up. “I do.”

Joel sipped coffee. “Then we’re ahead of schedule spiritually.”

They broke camp in cold stages. Sleeping bags stuffed. Pads rolled. Tent shaken, fly wet enough to be annoying but not soaked. Joel took down his hammock last because he liked how quickly his whole bedroom became a fist-sized bundle of fabric and straps. Will packed his unused hammock with the expression of a man putting away a tool he had not deserved.

The food barrel latch snapped closed. The stove cooled, clicked once as Mike handled it, and went into its bag. Paddles came out from under the canoes. Wet blades, grit on the shafts. Will wiped his rod guides with a cloth and pretended no one noticed.

“Fishing while we move?” Alex asked.

“In spirit,” Will said.

“No,” Mike said.

“I said in spirit.”

“I heard the part you didn’t say.”

“You often do, and usually badly.”

They loaded the boats. The morning light grew cleaner but not warmer. Mosquitoes were thin near shore, slow enough that they seemed confused by their own hunger. The lake held the canoes without wave. Whatever had knocked in the night had left no sign. No shifted hull. No loose paddle. No branch caught on the rocks. Joel checked because checking was easier than wondering.

By a little after eight-thirty they pushed off.

The first part of the morning was easy water. The lake stretched north in a long reach, then narrowed around a low point where black spruce crowded the shore. Mike and Will led. Joel and Alex followed. Alex paddled better after breakfast than he had near the end of the day before. Maybe sleep helped. Maybe the cold made him want to be useful.

They passed the point and entered a channel not much wider than the one from yesterday, but lower and flatter. Alder came down to the water on both sides. Grass leaned out over mud. The water lost the clean dark of lake depth and turned brown and slow.

“Here we go,” Mike called back.

“Here we go good or here we go bad?” Alex said.

“Beaver work.”

Will turned his head. “Plural.”

“Could be.”

“Great.”

Alex looked back at Joel. “Define beaver work.”

Joel said, “Sometimes you get out and drag.”

“That’s work for us.”

“Correct.”

“Misleading name.”

The first dam came twenty minutes later. It crossed the channel in a low brown ridge of sticks and mud, water shining over the top in thin sheets. On one side a narrow chute had been opened by previous travelers. Mike guided his canoe toward it, stepped out in knee-deep water, and held the boat while Will got out and pulled from the front.

“Looks simple,” Alex said.

“Don’t say that,” Joel said.

“Why?”

“Because the river hears confidence.”

Alex smiled. “That sounds like something from your Wisconsin cult.”

“I’m from St. Louis.”

“Madison changed you.”

They came in on the right. Joel stepped out first. Cold water went up over his sandal straps and around his calves. The mud under the surface accepted one foot, then the other, and did not want to give them back. Alex got out and immediately sank deeper.

“Oh, come on.”

“Don’t fight it straight up,” Joel said. “Rock your foot.”

“I am negotiating with it.”

“You’re losing.”

The canoe slid halfway onto the dam, then stopped. Joel pushed from the back. Alex pulled from the front. The hull rasped over wet sticks. Something under it cracked. Water ran around their legs with more force than it had seemed to have from the boat.

“Hold up,” Alex said.

Joel stopped.

Alex bent and freed the painter rope from a branch woven into the dam. He held it up. “See? Useful.”

“I didn’t say otherwise.”

“You were thinking it.”

“I was thinking many things.”

Mike, already on the far side, called, “You coming?”

Alex looked back at him. “We’re preserving infrastructure.”

“You’re standing in beaver trash.”

Will said, “Technically impressive beaver trash.”

“That’s your review of everything in nature,” Mike said.

They got the boat over. It dropped into the water beyond with a hollow slap and a little roll that sent water against Joel’s thighs. Alex made a sound between a laugh and a curse.

“Refreshing,” he said.

The second dam was worse.

The channel bent left and disappeared behind alder. The dam sat just beyond the bend, higher, newer, still wet with the look of recent labor. Water backed up before it in a shallow pond full of drowned grass. A lodge rose on the left, larger than the one from the day before, sticks piled against a bank where fresh cuts showed pale.

Will said, “Somebody’s active.”

Mike said, “Beavers usually are.”

“I mean recent.”

“Are we filing a complaint?”

Alex lifted his paddle. “I support labor.”

The pull-over took longer. They unloaded a few bags because the boats were catching on the crest. Food barrel out. Day bags out. Rods handed forward like fragile diplomatic gifts. Alex took one rod and held it away from himself.

“This thing makes me feel irresponsible.”

“It should,” Will said.

Joel pushed and slipped. One foot went sideways between two submerged branches. For a second the mud held him and the current leaned against his knees. He caught the gunwale with one hand and steadied.

“You good?” Will called.

“Yeah.”

“Don’t break Madison,” Alex said.

“Madison bends,” Joel said.

Mike was on top of the dam, pulling his canoe by the front line. “Lift, don’t drag.”

“Buy lighter boats,” Joel said.

Mike looked over his shoulder. “That line’s less funny when you’re wrong.”

“It wasn’t meant to be funny.”

“It never is.”

They got the boats over one by one. By the end, everyone was wet to the knee or higher. Alex had mud up one thigh. Will had a leech on his ankle, which he noticed only after he had already described leech behavior in too much detail.

“No, no, no,” he said, more annoyed than scared. “Absolutely not.”

Mike looked down. “It’s tiny.”

“It’s attached.”

“That’s how they work.”

Will flicked at it with a fingernail and missed. “That’s not how I want it to work.”

Alex crouched beside him. “Allow me. I grew up with real parasites.”

“You grew up in Alaska, not a medieval swamp.”

Alex pinched the leech off and held it up for half a second before flinging it into the grass. “Nature defeated.”

Will stared at his ankle. “I hate that I needed you.”

“You’ll heal.”

They moved again.

The morning warmed. The chill lifted from their clothes but left them damp. The sun came through breaks in the alder and lit the water in copper patches. Every few bends the channel opened into a marshy pool where lily pads held yellowing edges and dragonflies stitched the air low over the surface. Then it tightened again and made them work.

Joel felt the route changing the men. Not dramatically. It was too early for drama. But the first-day jokes had less gloss on them. Everyone had slept, but not enough to erase the weight of portage and water from their bodies. Mike’s instructions were shorter. Will still talked, but more of it came between breaths. Alex performed, then forgot to perform, then remembered when silence lasted too long.

At a shallow gravel bar, Mike stopped to check the map. Will cast once while standing in ankle-deep water, because his body had been waiting for the chance before his mind approved it.

Mike said, “Really?”

“One cast.”

“You said that yesterday.”

“And I have grown as a person.”

The lure landed near a sunken branch with a small plip. Something moved in the shadow. Will’s whole body changed.

“Fish?” Alex said.

“Maybe.”

“Useful fish or emotionally symbolic fish?”

Will reeled slowly. “All fish are both.”

The lure came back clean.

Mike gave him five seconds of silence. “Done?”

Will clipped the lure to a guide. “Done.”

“Miracles happen,” Mike said.

They ate lunch out of the boats because the ground was mostly mud and grass. Tuna packets, Triscuits, trail mix, jerky already diminished by theft and democracy. Alex examined a salmon packet as if it might argue with him.

“Just eat the crackers,” Mike said.

“I’m branching.”

“You branch like a dead tree.”

Joel handed him a strip of jerky. “Here. Safe food.”

Alex took it. “Your usefulness continues.”

Will said, “Don’t praise him too much. He’ll dehydrate a whole cow.”

“I’ve considered it,” Joel said.

“Of course you have.”

The channel after lunch became less forgiving. Beaver cuts narrowed the turns. Drowned branches reached up from underwater, invisible until a hull scraped them. Mike called back directions in clipped pieces. Left. Stay right. Shallow. Watch the snag. Joel relayed what mattered to Alex when Alex was looking at the wrong thing.

A branch cracked in the alder to their left.

Not loud. A clean snap, followed by brush moving low and fast.

Alex stopped paddling mid-stroke.

The canoe turned slightly.

“Hold up,” he said.

Joel put his paddle in to correct. Ahead, Mike’s boat kept moving three more strokes before Will turned.

“You guys hear that?”

Mike looked toward the alder. “Animal.”

“What kind of animal?” Alex asked.

“The kind in bushes.”

“That narrows it.”

Will held his paddle across his knees and listened. The brush moved once more, farther back. Leaves shook. Then nothing.

“Beaver?” Joel said.

“Could be,” Mike said.

“Could be bear,” Alex said, but lightly.

Mike turned his head just enough. “Don’t start.”

“I’m not starting. I’m identifying risk factors.”

“That’s Will’s job,” Joel said.

Will, still listening, said, “Bear would probably sound heavier.”

“Probably,” Mike said. “Everybody paddle.”

The word everybody did its work. They moved.

Joel looked once into the alder as they passed. He saw leaves, mud, the dark arch of roots where the bank had undercut, a few fresh chips on a cut sapling. No animal. No shape. No reason to remember it except the way Alex had stopped before anyone else did, his shoulders tightening under the straps of his PFD, paddle held over water.

A person could grow up in Alaska and still become alert to brush. Maybe especially then.

The channel opened after that, though not into easy water. It widened into a marshy run where the current slowed and the wind found them from the side. The sky had changed while they worked the beaver dams. It was still clear overhead, but a smear of cloud sat low in the west. The light had gone flatter. Far ahead, a wider lake showed between low banks.

Mike checked the map without stopping, one hand on the paddle, the case open against his knees.

“Bigger water after this,” he said.

“How big?” Alex asked.

“Big enough.”

Will made a sound.

Mike said, “What?”

“Nothing.”

“Say it.”

“I’m just saying, ‘big enough’ is not a measurement.”

“It’s not the problem lake.”

“That’s not an answer.”

“It’s the lake before the problem lake.”

Alex turned around. “Do the lakes know which one is the problem lake?”

Joel said, “Mike tells them.”

Mike ignored that. “We get across this, short ugly bit, then camp.”

“Second camp?” Alex said.

“No. Lunch two-point-five.”

“Don’t name things.”

“I’ll name whatever I survive.”

They paddled into the wider water.

The wind was mild but steady, enough to press Alex’s lazy strokes into consequence. Joel had to correct more often. Mike and Will angled left toward a low shore where the map showed the next narrow exit. Will kept looking back toward the marsh.

Joel saw it and did not ask.

The branch crack had gone. The brush had stopped moving. Nothing followed them but small waves and the sound of water dripping from lifted paddles.

Still, for a while no one joked.

That did not last. It never did.

Alex finally said, “For the record, if a beaver kills me, lie.”

Will turned in the front of Mike’s canoe. “About which part?”

“All of it.”

“Say bear?”

“Say moose. Say I fought a moose for the honor of the group.”

Mike said, “I’m saying you fell in mud and got unionized.”

Alex laughed, and because the water was wider now the laugh carried out from him and came back from nowhere in particular. It skipped once against the low shore and thinned into the marsh behind them.

Joel paddled and watched the widening lake ahead. He could feel the day going long. Not late yet, but moving that way. The first camp was behind them. The second camp was somewhere past work they had not done.

Mike had rounded the route down. Maybe not by much. Maybe by the exact amount all competent men rounded discomfort down when selling it to themselves first and others second.

They reached the far side later than Mike wanted and earlier than worry required. The exit was a gap in alder, half-hidden by grass. Water moved through it in a narrow brown pull, just enough current to show the way.

Will lifted his rod and looked at the widening water behind them.

Mike said, “No.”

“I didn’t say anything.”

“You thought it loudly.”

“There could be pike.”

“There could be time.”

Alex looked toward the alder gap. “Does it open up after this?”

Mike gave him a hard look.

Alex smiled. “Asking for morale.”

Mike cleared his throat. The sound was small, almost lost under the wind and water.

“After the next stretch,” he said. “Just keep moving.”

Will glanced at Joel. Not alarmed. Just registering the phrase the way he registered all of Mike’s route talk.

Joel dipped his paddle and followed the first canoe into the gap.

The alder closed around them. The light narrowed. Water slid under the boats with a dark, steady pull, and from somewhere ahead came the faint sound of it dropping over stones.

Not danger. Not yet.

Just the route, doing what routes did after men had described them too easily.

# Chapter 6 - The Rough Carry

The alder gap did open, but not into relief. It opened into a run of water broken by stone, then into a lake that took the wind cleanly from the west.

Mike looked at it and made the small throat sound he made before deciding for everyone.

"We cross close to shore," he said. "No hero lines."

"I was saving my hero line for dinner," Alex said.

Will had the map case across his knees. "Camp is after the portage on the north side."

"Correct."

"How bad is the portage?"

"It exists."

"That is not a measurement."

Joel kept his paddle low while the wind worked against the bow. Alex was better when he had something to fight. He leaned into the strokes and stopped performing for five whole minutes at a time. Spray came over the front and darkened his sleeves. He wiped his face with the back of one hand.

"Seattle prepared me emotionally for this," he said.

"Seattle prepared you to complain in layers," Joel said.

Alex laughed, but he was breathing hard.

They made the far shore without drama large enough to name, which was not the same as making it easily. The landing was a slope of slick rock with alder overhanging it. Mike and Will came in first and badly. Will got one foot down, slipped, and sat hard in two inches of water.

"Grace," Mike said.

"Physics," Will said.

Alex stepped out with the painter line and did not make the joke Joel expected. He braced the canoe, took Will's pack when Will came back for it, and helped him tighten a strap that had twisted against his shoulder.

"You good?" Alex asked.

Will looked at him suspiciously. "That was almost useful."

"I contain multitudes."

"You contain crackers."

"Also multitudes of crackers."

The portage began as trail and became negotiation. The tread climbed through spruce and over roots polished by old wet boots. Blowdown forced them into the brush twice. Mosquitoes found the sweat at their collars. Joel carried the canoe on his shoulders and watched his world narrow to mud, root, breath, boot placement, the gray-green underbody of the hull.

Ahead, Mike called, "Step left. Rock's loose."

Will said something Joel could not hear.

Then a cough came from behind Joel.

It was small, dry, familiar. Will's cough, almost a laugh and almost a defect in the throat.

Joel stopped because the trail demanded stopping more than the sound did. The canoe pressed down on his shoulders. Behind him Alex was twenty feet back with a pack, looking at the mud in front of his sandals.

"What?" Alex said.

"Nothing."

Ahead, Will coughed again, real and visible, bent under his own load beside Mike.

Joel started walking.

At the top of the rise they rested in a place where the trees opened enough to show water through black trunks. Mike took out the older map, the one he liked because it had more lines and fewer disclaimers. A small square sat inland beyond the next camp.

"Old maintenance cabin," Alex said, leaning over his shoulder. "Map says there's a cabin."

"Map says there was a cabin when somebody cared enough to print this," Will said.

"Still a cabin," Mike said.

"Could be a square of moss."

"Could be useful if weather comes in."

Joel looked at the printed mark and then at the forest around it. Human things did not feel permanent here. They felt like requests.

They finished the portage late. Second Camp was still somewhere ahead, past another constricted channel and a ragged spit of spruce. The light had started to flatten. Nobody said they were behind schedule. Mike did not have to say it for everyone to know.

Alex shouldered his pack and looked at Joel, who had stopped to watch the lake take the wind in dark strips.

"You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel lifted the canoe again.

"Neither," he said. "I'm suffering plainly."

Alex grinned. "That's the brand."

They went on.

# Chapter 7 - Second Camp

Second Camp was not a campsite so much as a place where other people had once lost the argument.

The landing was mud under grass. The flat ground was too small for two tents and a hammock unless everyone agreed not to move in the night. Spruce crowded the back of it, and alder leaned in from the channel side. There was a fire ring full of old foil and wet ash. Mosquitoes came out of the brush as if somebody had opened a door.

Mike stood in the middle of it and said, "This works."

Will looked around. "For what?"

"For sleeping."

"For malaria research."

"Wrong continent," Alex said.

"Thank you. I feel corrected and still bitten."

Joel set down his pack. His shoulders kept the shape of the straps after the weight was gone. The wind had shifted colder, and sweat cooled against his back. He wanted First Camp's granite shelf, the clean pine space, the water visible on three sides. This place had edges too close to see.

They worked because work was easier than judgment. Tents went where they fit. Joel strung his hammock between two jack pines that bent a little too much and made him mistrust them. Will hung the food rope and cursed the available branches. Mike found a deadfall and broke it down for a small fire. Alex went for water and returned complaining that the shoreline smelled like a wet dog had lost a legal dispute.

"That's just marsh," Will said.

"Then marsh needs counsel."

Dinner was rice, packets of chicken, and a pouch of sauce Alex inspected and rejected.

"You are going to die of blandness," Mike said.

"Not before the rest of you die of trusting pouches."

"Put that on my stone," Will said.

Alex made himself noodles and peanut butter like a man committing a private crime. He ate with satisfaction and received no punishment except conversation.

Dark came early under the trees. The fire was small, more gesture than heat. Beyond it the brush made a wall that seemed to take light without giving any back. Alex told an old story about a trip where Mike had insisted a thunderstorm was "moving north" while everyone watched it arrive from the west.

"Weather has multiple directions," Mike said.

"Weather had your number," Alex said.

Will laughed and coughed into his sleeve. Joel found himself laughing too, not because the story was new but because the old rhythm still worked. That was the cruelty of it. The group was thinner than it had been and still able to become itself around a fire.

Something knocked once from the channel.

No one spoke for a moment.

"Boat settling," Mike said.

"Boat is on shore," Will said.

"Then shore settling."

Alex stood and stretched. "I'm taking a piss. Nobody panic."

"Stay close," Mike said.

Alex waved him off. "Quit mothering me."

"Headlamp," Joel said.

"I have one."

"On your head?"

Alex touched his forehead, found nothing, and came back for it while Will made a theatrical note of the failure.

"See?" Mike said. "Mothering saves lives."

"Mothering is a system of oppression," Alex said, putting on the lamp.

He walked beyond the last clean edge of firelight. The beam moved through alder leaves, white, then broken, then mostly gone.

"Don't piss on anything we're eating," Will called.

"I make no promises."

The fire snapped once. Wind moved in the spruce tops but not at ground level. Joel heard water beyond the brush, or thought he did. Then a small sound came from farther out. Not Alex. Not quite animal. A branch touched another branch and stopped.

Alex's headlamp turned toward it.

"What?" Alex said, amused and irritated, already answering.

# Chapter 8 - Nobody Panic

For a second the camp held its shape.

Mike by the fire. Will kneeling beside the food barrel. Joel half turned toward the brush. Alex's headlamp a small white place beyond the alder.

Then Alex said, "Hold up."

Not loud. Not frightened. The way he had said it all day when rope caught or mud held his foot.

Will stood. "Alex?"

The headlamp jerked once, dipped, and came back up sideways.

"What the hell," Alex said.

Brush moved. A heavy sound followed, not a roar, not anything clean enough to name. It was impact and wet alder and Alex's breath cut short. Mike grabbed the bear spray from beside his pack and ran toward the light. Joel was behind him before he knew he had moved. Will shouted Alex's name and kept shouting it, which made every direction seem like a direction.

The headlamp went out.

"Stop yelling," Mike snapped.

Will did not stop.

Joel's own lamp found alder stems, leaves, mud, the pale side of a broken branch. Something big moved just outside the beam and was gone before the light could decide on it. He saw a color that might have been hide, or wet bark, or his own fear making a body out of dark.

Mike sprayed into brush.

The orange cloud hit leaves and came back at them. Joel choked. His eyes burned. Will doubled over coughing. Mike swore and kept moving anyway.

They found Alex ten yards beyond the first alder screen.

At first Joel's mind refused scale. It gave him pieces. One sandal. One hand curled in mud. The headlamp strap. Blood black under white light. A torn place where the body should have remained ordinary.

"No, no, no," Will said, and now the words had weight.

Mike pushed him back with one arm. "Don't touch him."

"We have to--"

"Don't touch him."

Joel stood with his paddle in his hand because he had somehow brought it instead of anything useful. His lungs hurt from spray. The woods around them were quiet in the exact way quiet becomes a thing with edges.

"Bear," Mike said.

No one answered.

"It was a bear."

Alex's face was turned partly into the mud. Joel could not make himself look long and could not stop looking. Alex had been laughing twenty minutes earlier. He had been complaining about sauce. He had been impossible to ignore, and now the world had made him into evidence.

Will said, "Where is it?"

"Gone."

"How do you know?"

Mike turned on him. "Because we're standing here."

That was not an answer. It was only enough shape to stand inside for one more second.

They backed toward camp with their lights pointed out. Mike kept the bear spray in front of him though the canister was light now. Joel took Will by the pack strap once when Will drifted toward the body again.

"We can't leave him," Will said.

"We're not leaving him," Joel said.

But they did. They left him for the night because darkness made morality smaller than fear. They pulled the canoes closer. They put every pot and paddle within reach. They sat under the tarp with lights off and listened.

After a long time, from the brush beyond Alex, something made his hard bark of laughter.

Once.

Will made a sound like he had been hit.

Mike whispered, "No."

Joel did not know whether Mike meant he had not heard it, or that the world was not allowed to have done it.

# Chapter 9 - Bear Work

Morning did not make Alex easier to see. It only made the details insist on themselves.

Fog sat low in the channel. The campsite smelled of wet ash, bear spray, blood, mud, and the sour human fear that had come out of them in the night. Nobody had slept. They had sat in turns with headlamps off, listening until every drip from a spruce needle sounded chosen.

Mike went first to the body. Joel followed with the hatchet because holding something made movement possible. Will came last, carrying the map case, which made no sense until Joel understood he had needed an object too.

In daylight the scene looked more like a bear attack and less like one.

There was violence enough. Crushed alder. Mud torn by heels. Blood on leaves. A drag mark that stopped too soon. But tracks overlapped in ways Joel could not parse. Some looked broad and animal. Some were broken by roots. Some were simply absent where the mud should have kept them.

"Bear," Mike said again.

Will crouched near one print without touching it. "Maybe."

"Don't maybe this."

"I'm not maybeing it. I'm looking."

"At what?"

"At the fact that I don't know what I'm looking at."

Joel heard the anger under Mike's breath. It was not only anger at Will. It was anger at the scene for refusing to become simple.

They covered what they could of Alex with a groundsheet and stones. It was not enough. Nothing was enough. Will wanted to move him. Mike said they could not safely carry a body through the route. Will asked if Mike had a pamphlet for the correct way to abandon a friend. Mike stepped toward him, then stopped.

Joel said, "We mark it. We get out. We bring people back."

The words sounded responsible. They also sounded like betrayal.

At camp they inventoried with the hard tenderness of men not ready for grief. One tent fly was torn where something had gone through brush near it. A food barrel latch was open though the barrel itself had not been raided. Alex's bear spray lay in the leaves with the safety still in place. His headlamp was cracked.

"Food brought it in," Mike said.

"Food wasn't touched," Will said.

"Animal gets confused."

"That is not a known wildlife principle."

Mike turned away before answering. "Pack."

They packed badly. Hands fumbled buckles. Joel poured too much water into the coffee grounds and watched brown liquid spill over the mug onto granite-colored dirt. He waited for Alex to say something about Midwestern soup coffee. Then he remembered the groundsheet beyond the alder and stopped moving.

From the brush behind the covered body came Alex's voice, soft and annoyed.

"Quit mothering me."

Will dropped the map case.

Mike looked at Joel, and in his face Joel saw the terrible request: say it was a bird, say it was grief, say sound does things over water.

Joel listened to the fog.

"We leave now," Mike said.

No one argued.

# Chapter 10 - Windbound

They did not leave.

By the time the boats were loaded, the lake beyond the channel had gone white at the tips. Wind came over it in steady pushes, laying the near grass flat and lifting spray off the farther chop. Mike stood at the landing with the map in both hands while Will stared past him.

"We can hug the left shore," Mike said.

Joel looked at the water. "Not with one boat half empty and everyone cooked."

"We are not staying here."

"I didn't say we stay."

"Then what are you saying?"

"I'm saying that crossing that because we're scared is still crossing that."

Will made a dry sound that might have been agreement or the beginning of collapse. "Backtrack?"

Mike shook his head. "Worse. More portages. Back through the beaver work. Past him."

Nobody used Alex's name.

They tried to wait the wind down. It rose instead. Low cloud came in from the west, and by noon rain had started, first as a mist and then as a cold insistence that soaked collars and hands. They dragged the canoes higher, tied them separately, and put the tarp back up over the same miserable center of camp.

The body remained beyond the alder.

That was how Joel thought of it now. Not Alex. The body. The covered place. The problem they could not solve without turning their friend into logistics.

Will paced under the tarp until Mike told him to sit or go be useful elsewhere.

"Useful how?" Will said. "Do you have a department? Should I file for bear confusion?"

"Stop."

"No, because if it was a bear, then fine, it was a bear. Horrible, but fine. But bears do not talk."

"Nothing talked."

Will stared at him.

Mike said, "You heard what you heard after not sleeping."

"You heard it too."

"I heard a sound."

Joel watched rain gather on the tarp seam and fall in a steady leak beside the stove. He wanted Mike's answer to be true because it was narrow enough to live in. He also remembered the exact rhythm of Alex's line. The little irritation in it. The way it had belonged to the driveway before it belonged to blood.

Will coughed.

Joel turned toward him.

Will was on Joel's right, hunched under his rain jacket.

The cough had come from the left side of camp, near the food barrel.

Joel looked there and saw nothing but rain ticking on blue plastic.

"What?" Will said.

Joel shook his head.

Mike said, "We're fine."

His voice was flat. It did not reassure anyone. It only closed the conversation.

Rain thickened. The wind kept them pinned to the place where Alex had died, and by evening the camp seemed to have learned them. Zipper. Buckle. Cough. Latch. Paddle knocking once from the shore though the paddles were under the tarp.

Joel lay awake that night and understood nothing except that the thing outside did not need to be seen to get closer.



<!-- ===== manuscript/11-the-second-night-after.md ===== -->

# Chapter 11 - The Second Night After

The second night after was worse because they knew what they were listening for and still did not know what it was.

Rain closed the camp. It flattened distance, put a roof over every sound, and made the tarp into a drum. The men sat close without comfort. Mike had the bear spray across his knees. Will had the map case open though there was no light to read by. Joel had his notebook and did not write in it yet.

They had agreed on watches. Then they had all stayed awake.

"If it is a bear," Will said, too softly, "it may still be nearby."

"It is nearby because we're still here," Mike said.

"That's not a reason."

"It's the only reason that matters."

"No. No, it's not."

Joel said, "Save it."

Both men looked at him. He rarely used that tone. He did not like how easily it came.

The food barrel latch clicked.

It was a small sound. Ordinary. One of the first trip sounds. Plastic, metal, pressure released.

The barrel sat under the tarp between Mike's boots.

Mike put one hand on it. The latch was shut.

From the dark beyond the tarp, a tent zipper moved up, then down.

Will whispered, "Both tents are right here."

"I know," Joel said.

The zipper moved again. Patient. Demonstrating.

Mike stood too fast and hit his shoulder on the tarp line. "Show yourself."

"Don't," Joel said.

"It's an animal."

"Then it doesn't understand English."

Mike froze with his mouth half open. That, more than anything, made Joel afraid. Mike had wanted to answer. The thing had made a situation with only one human shape: challenge it, name it, push back.

Out in the rain, Alex laughed once.

Will covered his face. "Stop."

The laugh came again, farther away.

"Stop," Will said, louder.

Joel grabbed his sleeve. Will tried to pull free and then did not. His breath came in small hard pulls.

Mike sat down. Rain dripped off his hood onto his hands.

"Maybe somebody's out there," he said.

Nobody believed him fully, but they all needed the sentence.

"Then why Alex?" Will said.

Mike did not answer.

Joel opened the notebook. His pen shook once before he steadied it. He made four columns because columns were better than prayer.

Alex.

Mike.

Will.

Camp.

Under Alex he wrote laugh, mothering, hold up. Under Will he wrote cough. Under Camp he wrote latch, zipper, paddle knock.

Mike leaned over. "What is that?"

"What we've heard twice."

"You're making a ghost spreadsheet?"

"I'm making a list."

"Same thing, Madison."

For a second Joel almost laughed. Then something knocked from the shoreline, hollow wood on stone, and nobody laughed at all.



<!-- ===== manuscript/12-bad-crossing.md ===== -->

# Chapter 12 - Bad Crossing

Morning gave them a smaller wind and the same fear. Mike took the change as permission.

"We move now."

Will looked at the lake. "That's still ugly."

"Ugly is not closed."

Joel wanted to object with something stronger than instinct. The map showed the nearest exit forward. Backtracking meant Alex, beaver dams, the first camp, and distance expanding behind them. Staying meant listening until they stopped being men and became ears.

They moved.

Three men in two canoes made every old habit wrong. Mike took one boat alone, loaded heavy in front to keep it from weathercocking. Joel and Will took the other, Will in front because that was the shape the boat still knew. Alex's absence sat in the empty spaces: no complaint when the pack pressed wrong, no laugh, no body in the front of Joel's canoe.

The crossing started controlled and became labor. Wind came in quartering bursts. Mike fought his canoe with short hard strokes. Twice he spun too far and cursed. Joel kept their bow angled toward the black line of shore.

Halfway across, from somewhere ahead and left, Mike's voice called, "It opens up after this."

Mike was behind them and to the right, head down, paddling.

Will stopped.

"Paddle," Joel said.

"That was--"

"Paddle."

Will paddled. His strokes were too fast and shallow. The canoe yawed. A wave slapped the side and came in over the gunwale. Cold water spread around Joel's shoes.

From the trees came Will's own voice: "You guys hear that?"

Will made a noise low in his throat.

"Look at me," Joel said.

Will did not turn. "That was me."

"No."

"That was me."

"The living you is in the boat. Paddle."

They hit the wrong landing because the right one disappeared behind wind and panic. Gravel scraped the hull. Joel jumped out into thigh-deep water and dragged until his hands burned. Will stumbled after him. Mike came in badly, rammed a rock, and cracked something in the front thwart with a sharp wooden sound.

"Damn it," Mike said.

The word was too small for the day.

They unloaded in pieces. One dry bag had not been sealed right. Will's sleeping bag was damp along one side. A map case had taken water. Mike inspected the cracked thwart as if anger could reverse grain.

"It's usable," he said.

"For what?" Will asked.

"For a canoe."

"That's an aspiration."

Mike rounded on him. "Do you want to do this now?"

Will's face changed. "Do what? Point out reality?"

"Reality is we landed."

"Reality is something used your voice while you were twenty yards behind us."

Joel pulled the canoe above the stones and turned it sideways against the wind. His hands were numb. His mind felt too clear and not clear enough.

"Camp," he said.

Mike looked at him.

"We make camp," Joel said. "Then we decide."

The wind pushed through the spruce behind them. Far back across the water, something knocked once, like a paddle against an empty boat.



<!-- ===== manuscript/13-emergency-camp.md ===== -->

# Chapter 13 - Emergency Camp

Emergency Camp had no fire ring and no mercy.

They put the tents among spruce roots because the roots were higher than mud. The tarp went low. Everything wet was hung where it could become differently wet. Will's sleeping bag steamed faintly in the cold air and did not dry.

Mike splinted the cracked thwart with cord, a tent stake, and two strips of aluminum from a repair sleeve he had insisted on bringing. He worked with furious care. It was the best thing about him and the worst. Problems became things to dominate.

Will watched too closely.

"It's a field repair," Mike said without looking up.

"I didn't say anything."

"You were breathing critically."

"There is a difference between skepticism and breathing."

Joel wrote that down later because he knew the thing outside might have heard it. He hated himself for thinking that while Will was still alive and cold beside him.

At dusk, Alex said, "Hold up," from the spruce behind camp.

Will turned before Joel could stop him.

Mike grabbed his shoulder. "No."

The voice came again, softer. "Hold up."

Will whispered, "Alex."

"No," Mike said. "No, it isn't."

That was the first time Mike said it plainly. The words changed the camp more than the voice had. They had lost the bear because Mike had let it go.

Joel took out his notebook and made the columns again. He added arrows. Alex phrase heard after death. Mike voice while Mike visible. Will voice while Will visible. Camp sounds without source.

Will knelt beside him. "It repeats."

"Yes."

"Maybe not only repeats. Maybe recombines."

"Maybe."

"No, that's important. If it can recombine phonemes--"

"Will."

"I'm just saying."

"Don't give it more."

Will shut his mouth. The effort looked physical.

Mike came over. "What are we doing?"

Joel showed him the page.

Mike read badly in the fading light. His jaw moved once. "So we don't talk?"

"We talk less."

"Great plan."

"You have a better one?"

Mike looked toward the water, then inland. "Old cabin."

Will laughed once, mean and tired. "Jesus."

"It's on the old map."

"Map says there's a cabin," Joel said.

Mike looked at him sharply, as if Joel had stolen the sentence.

"It may get us off the water," Mike said.

Nobody wanted the water. Nobody wanted the woods. The route had become a set of wrong doors.

That night Joel wrote four words on a clean page and showed them to both men.

DO NOT ANSWER.

Will read it. Mike read it.

Outside camp, the food barrel latch clicked once in the dark, and all three men kept their mouths shut.



<!-- ===== manuscript/14-the-old-map.md ===== -->

# Chapter 14 - The Old Map

In the morning Mike spread the old map on a wet rock and put stones on the corners.

The cabin square sat inland from a portage they had already missed by landing wrong. A faint line ran from it toward an old fire road. On paper it looked reasonable. Paper was good at that.

"We leave one canoe hidden," Mike said. "Take the repair kit, food for two days, sleeping bags, first aid, map, water filter. Find the cabin. If there's a road, we follow it. If not, we come back."

Will stared at the map. "That's a lot of if."

"Everything is if."

"No. Some things are distances. Some things are contours. Some things are false hope printed in black."

Mike's throat cleared. "You want to paddle the big water with a cracked boat and voices?"

Will had no answer.

Joel wanted to say that voices were in the woods too. He did not because saying it did not improve the world.

They cached the damaged canoe under spruce boughs and tied it off above the high-water line. The other they pulled farther into brush, in case they needed both again and in case whatever followed them understood value. That last thought made Joel stop. Understand value. Did it? Did it know why the boats mattered, or did it only learn the sounds men made around things they feared losing?

He wrote in the notebook:

ONLY WHAT IT HEARD.

Will read over his shoulder and whispered, "Maybe."

The trace began as a path old enough to have humility. Grass grew in the center. Alder narrowed it. Boot prints appeared and vanished in mud. They walked single file: Mike, Will, Joel. Mike insisted on front because Mike insisted on front.

No one spoke for the first half hour. Silence made every practical thing harder. A raised hand meant stop, or maybe watch the branch, or maybe listen. Will kept starting to say something and biting it back. Joel could hear his teeth click once.

From the brush ahead came Mike's throat clear.

Mike stopped walking.

The sound had come from twenty yards in front of him.

Will looked back at Joel. Joel lifted one hand: wait.

Then Mike's voice, from ahead, said, "Just keep moving."

The living Mike turned slowly. His face had lost color under the grime.

Will mouthed something. Joel shook his head hard.

They did not move until the woods resumed being woods: rain ticking from old leaves, a distant raven, the small release of water from moss. Then Mike stepped forward with the careful fury of a man refusing to be handled by his own voice.

By afternoon the trace worsened. Blowdown forced them around a black tangle of trunks. Will fell once and split his palm on a rock. Joel bandaged it while Mike watched the trees.

From somewhere off trail, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel closed the first-aid kit.

"No," he said before he could stop himself.

The woods gave no answer. That was worse.



<!-- ===== manuscript/15-the-trace.md ===== -->

# Chapter 15 - The Trace

The trace disappeared three times.

Each time Mike found it again, which made it harder to argue with him and easier for him to trust himself. He read cut stumps under moss, old drainage, the faint straightness of trees along a grade. He was good at this. That was the problem. Competence kept opening the next door.

Will's bandaged hand throbbed. He kept flexing it and then remembering not to. Joel watched his own feet because the ground wanted ankles. Wet leaves hid holes. Roots crossed the path at bad angles. Packs shifted and knocked against branches with small human sounds.

At a break they ate bars without speaking. Mike checked the Highlander key in the zip pocket of his PFD. He had kept the PFD on after leaving the boats, absurd and practical at once.

Will saw the gesture and raised his eyebrows.

Mike said, "Take-out doesn't help if we lose the key."

It was a reasonable sentence. Spoken aloud, it became material.

Joel wrote: key in Mike PFD.

Will read it and nodded.

The woods made direction strange. A cough came from ahead while Will stood behind Joel drinking water. Mike's voice came from the left saying, "It opens up after this," though the living Mike was silent and visible twenty feet ahead. Alex laughed behind them, bright and wrong.

Will pressed both hands over his ears.

Mike turned. "Don't."

Will lowered them. "Don't what?"

"Don't break."

"That's your advice?"

"Yes."

"Useful."

Mike's mouth tightened, but he let it go. They kept walking.

Late in the afternoon the trees thinned. Joel saw a hard dark line through spruce trunks. Roof. For one minute the whole day changed. Even Will moved faster. The cabin existed. A human thing, square and deliberate, set back from a small clearing where grass grew waist high around rusted cans.

Mike stopped at the edge of the clearing and looked back at them with something like vindication.

Will was too tired to deny him.

The cabin had a sagging roof, one intact shutter, one window boarded from inside, and a door hanging slightly open. No smoke. No antenna. No visible road beyond it, though an opening in the trees suggested where one had once entered.

"Careful," Joel said.

Mike almost smiled. "Now we're careful?"

Nobody answered.

The door groaned when Mike pushed it. The sound moved through the clearing and came back from the trees too late.

Inside smelled of mouse droppings, damp wood, old ash, and something mineral under the rot. Light came through roof gaps in thin white pieces. There was a table, two broken chairs, a rusted stove pipe, an empty shelf, and names cut into the wall by people who had been gone for years.

Will laughed once. Not humor. Exhaustion.

"Okay," he said. "Cabin."

Mike stood in the middle of the room with the map in his hand.

Outside, from the far side of the clearing, Mike's voice said, "Map says there's a cabin."



<!-- ===== manuscript/16-the-cabin.md ===== -->

# Chapter 16 - The Cabin

No one moved toward the voice.

That felt like progress until Joel understood they had also stopped breathing.

The false Mike did not repeat itself. Rainwater dripped through the roof into a black pot on the floor. Somewhere in the wall a mouse worked at paper. The real Mike stood with the old map half folded in both hands, looking more exposed than Joel had ever seen him.

"It heard me say that," Mike said.

Will nodded too fast. "Yes."

"At the gas station."

"Yes."

"Or yesterday."

"Yes."

"Stop saying yes."

Will's face hardened. "I'm agreeing with you, you miserable--"

Joel lifted a hand between them.

The argument died because the cabin was too small to hold it.

They searched anyway. People searched failed hopes the way they pressed elevator buttons already lit. There was no radio. No batteries. No first-aid kit. No road map newer than a decade old. The old fire road beyond the clearing had grown shut with spruce and poplar. A rusted culvert lay collapsed in the ditch. The cabin had been a square on paper longer than it had been help.

Mike went outside and followed the road trace fifty yards, then one hundred. Joel went with him. Will stayed at the doorway because his hand had started bleeding through the bandage.

"This might still connect," Mike said.

Joel looked at the young trees growing in the roadbed. "Not today."

"You don't know that."

"I know we can't walk it in dark."

"Then we stay here."

Joel looked back at the cabin. Its roof had a hole big enough for weather and whatever weather brought with it. "That isn't shelter."

"It's more shelter than spruce roots."

"And farther from the boats."

Mike turned on him. "You have become very confident for a man whose plan is mostly not liking mine."

Joel felt the hit land and chose not to answer it.

From the cabin, Will called, "Mike?"

Mike looked that way.

Will stood in the doorway, visible, one hand wrapped in red-specked gauze.

From the trees behind them, Will's voice called again, "Mike?"

Mike moved before Joel could stop him.

"Visual," Joel said. "Mike."

Mike kept going toward the sound, not running, just striding hard, bear spray in one hand. The brush beyond the old road shook once.

"Mike," Joel said, louder.

Mike stopped. For a second Joel thought he had him.

Then a dry bag buckle clicked from somewhere behind the brush. Once. Then again. The sound of gear being opened wrong.

Mike touched the zip pocket of his PFD where the Highlander key was.

"It may be in the packs," he said.

"What may be?"

"The key pouch. I want to check."

"It's on you."

Mike looked down as if he needed proof. His hand found the pocket. Relief crossed his face and became embarrassment and then anger.

From deeper in the trees, Alex said, "Quit mothering me."

Mike stepped after it.



<!-- ===== manuscript/17-mikes-authority.md ===== -->

# Chapter 17 - Mike's Authority

Mike died because he was almost right.

He did not sprint into darkness. He did not lose his mind and chase a ghost because a dead friend teased him. He moved ten yards into brush to confirm a thing any competent man would confirm: that the packs were where they had left them, that the key was secure, that the route still had objects in it and objects could be managed.

Joel followed.

Will shouted from the cabin doorway and then stopped himself with an audible bite of breath.

The brush thickened after ten yards. Old road vanished into alder and young spruce. Mike's shoulders moved ahead of Joel, close enough to see. Then a branch snapped to the right.

Will's voice said, "No, no, no."

Mike turned right.

"Visual," Joel said. "You don't have him."

Mike stopped again. That was the worst part. He kept stopping. He kept almost understanding.

Then Mike's own voice came from ahead, low and certain. "We're fine."

Mike lifted the bear spray. "Come on, then."

The brush in front of him opened without wind.

Joel saw something pale low to the ground, or high and bent, or neither. He saw alder stems move apart. He smelled river mud and old meat and wet hide. The bear spray hissed. Mike shouted, not a word but a refusal.

Impact took him sideways.

Joel did not see the body of the thing. He saw Mike's feet leave the ground. He saw leaves shake. He saw the bear spray canister hit a stone and spin. He heard Mike say, "Jesus," in a voice that was all surprise.

Then the woods closed.

Joel backed up because every cell in him chose backward. He hated himself while doing it and kept doing it. Will was at the edge of the cabin clearing now, face white, hatchet in his good hand.

"Where is he?"

Joel could not answer.

From the brush came Mike's voice, strained and angry. "Just keep moving."

Will took one step toward it.

Joel caught him across the chest and drove him backward so hard they both almost fell.

"No."

"That's Mike."

"No."

"You don't know."

Joel did know. Not enough for court. Not enough for God, if there had been one. Enough for the body.

They waited until dark at the cabin doorway. They called once, together, because not calling felt like murder. They heard nothing living answer. Later, something dragged through brush beyond the old road. Later still, the Highlander key struck the cabin threshold with a small bright sound.

Will began to cry without making noise.

Joel picked up the key. It was slick with rain and mud. The small Toyota fob looked stupid and domestic in his palm.

From beyond the clearing, Mike cleared his throat.

"It opens up after this," Mike said.

Joel closed his hand around the key until the plastic hurt him.



<!-- ===== manuscript/18-two-men.md ===== -->

# Chapter 18 - Two Men

They spent the night in the cabin because leaving it in darkness would have been another way to die.

Shelter was too generous a word. Rain came through the roof. Wind entered around the door. Mice worked inside the walls with the confidence of owners. Joel and Will sat with their backs to the least rotten wall and their feet braced against the table shoved in front of the door.

Neither slept.

Mike spoke twice from outside. Once he said, "We're fine." Once he said, "Just keep moving." The second time Will put both hands over his mouth and pressed until his eyes watered.

At dawn Joel wrote in the notebook:

DO NOT ANSWER UNLESS SEEN.

Will read it and nodded.

Joel wrote:

IT ONLY USES WHAT IT HEARD.

Will took the pen with his bandaged hand and wrote under it:

HOW DO WE KNOW ONLY?

Joel looked at the sentence for a long time. It was fair. It was Will. Even in ruin he wanted the terms exact.

He wrote:

WE DON'T. ACT LIKE IT.

That got the smallest broken sound from Will. Almost a laugh. Almost not.

They inventoried again. Two men. One key. One damaged hand. One bear spray canister lost with Mike. One hatchet. One cracked repaired canoe at the cache, one better canoe hidden near it. Enough food if they did not count appetite. Too little sleep. Too much route.

Will wanted to talk through the options. Joel made him write them instead. Back to boats. Push forward by water. Try the road trace. Stay and wait. Each option looked worse when written down because writing removed tone. Mike could not say "we're exactly where we ought to be" over ink and make it sound like fact.

The road trace was impossible. Staying was death delayed. Back to the boats was the only option that still touched the planned route.

They left the cabin midmorning.

At the clearing edge Will stopped and looked back.

Joel touched his shoulder.

Will whispered, "That's not how this should work."

Joel closed his eyes.

Will knew as soon as he said it. His face folded with self-disgust.

From inside the cabin, in Will's own voice, came the same words, softer and cleaner.

"That's not how this should work."

They walked.



<!-- ===== manuscript/19-written-rules.md ===== -->

# Chapter 19 - Written Rules

Traveling silently made them clumsy.

On water, men could point. On portages, under packs, in brush, with footing bad and sightlines worse, speech was the tool that kept bodies from breaking. Step left. Duck. Wait. Loose rock. Your strap. Snake, maybe. Don't put your foot there.

Without speech they had hands, eyes, and the notebook.

Joel made rules on a page and tore it out for Will to keep folded in his pocket.

VISUAL BEFORE VOICE.

NO ANSWERING FROM TREES.

NO NAMES.

POINT FIRST.

WRITE IF POSSIBLE.

Will added:

NO THEORIES OUT LOUD.

Then he underlined it until the paper almost tore.

They moved down the trace toward the canoe cache. Mike's voice came and went behind them, sometimes close, sometimes absurdly far. It used whole phrases and small habits. Throat clear. A low curse. "Just keep moving." Once, Alex laughed from the left and Will's knees softened so fast Joel had to grab his pack.

They did not answer.

The entity adjusted.

A boot scraped stone ahead where no trail showed. A pack shifted behind them. A branch cracked exactly when Joel looked down to place his foot. The woods no longer needed words to make them spend attention.

At noon they stopped beside a fallen spruce. Will's hand had bled through again. Joel changed the bandage. Will watched him work and then took the notebook.

It can use sequence, he wrote.

Joel frowned.

Will wrote: cough then phrase. buckle then Mike. It learned associations.

Joel wrote: maybe.

Will wrote: that means it may understand more than repeat.

Joel wrote: don't teach it.

Will looked at the page, then at Joel. Anger moved in him. Not large. Not unfair. He wanted to be useful in the way he knew how to be useful, and Joel was telling him usefulness had become poison.

They went on.

Late in the day, near the first break in the trees where Joel thought the water might be close, his own voice came from the brush behind them.

"Wait. Just wait."

Will stopped so hard Joel nearly hit him.

Joel had said that at the put-in. He remembered the canoe shifting on gravel, Alex laughing, Mike impatient, the truck still visible through black spruce. He had said it once, maybe twice, a practical phrase for a practical moment.

Now it stood behind him in his own mouth.

Will turned slowly toward Joel, not toward the voice.

That saved him.

Joel wrote with a hand that did not feel fully attached:

IT HEARD ME TOO.



<!-- ===== manuscript/20-his-own-voice.md ===== -->

# Chapter 20 - His Own Voice

After Joel heard himself, the route lost its last outside.

Before that, some part of him had believed the thing belonged to Alex's death, to Mike's mistake, to Will's noise, to the brush around Second Camp. His own voice proved otherwise. It had been near from the beginning, or near enough. At the landing. At the first water. At the places they had felt almost safe because nothing had yet happened.

Will kept looking at him as if Joel might explain what selfhood sounded like when taken.

Joel had no explanation. He had only rules.

They found the canoe cache late. The repaired boat was still under spruce boughs, but the boughs had been moved. Not scattered. Moved aside and laid in a neat fan on the mud. The better canoe was turned six feet from where they had left it. The painter rope had been looped twice around an alder stem and tied in a knot neither Joel nor Will recognized.

Will crouched beside it.

Joel touched his shoulder before he could speak.

Will took out the notebook.

Can it tie knots?

Joel looked at the rope. The knot was ugly but effective. It could have been made by a human hand, an animal's accident, or something imitating the result of hands without understanding comfort.

He wrote:

DOESN'T MATTER. CUT IT.

They cut the rope rather than untie it. Small decisions became offerings to speed.

The food barrel was still sealed. One dry bag had been dragged into mud and soaked through. The map case had grit in the seal. Mike's repair on the cracked thwart held when Joel pressed it, but barely.

Two men with two canoes made little sense. One usable canoe was faster but riskier. Two boats meant more gear and more mistakes. They chose one: the better hull, loaded low, with the repaired paddle as spare, the key in Joel's inner pocket, and most nonessential weight left under the spruce.

Will held up his fishing rods.

Joel shook his head.

Will's face changed more than Joel expected. The rods were not just rods. They were habit, hope, a version of Will that existed before voices made habit dangerous.

Will laid them under the canoe they were abandoning and covered them with boughs.

From the water came a hollow knock.

The canoe they had loaded sat still at their feet.

Another knock came from the fog beyond the first bend.

Will wrote:

It wants us to think boat/person.

Joel wrote:

yes.

Will wrote:

Then it has a goal.

Joel wrote:

So do we.

They launched without speaking. The channel ahead was narrow and dark, and the fog took the far bank in pieces.



<!-- ===== manuscript/21-bad-boat.md ===== -->

# Chapter 21 - Bad Boat

The bad boat was not bad enough to abandon and not good enough to trust.

That was the shape of everything now.

Joel sat in back. Will sat in front with his injured hand wrapped in a plastic bag and tape, paddling awkwardly on one side. The canoe rode low but clean. Fog lifted from the channel in slow pieces. Alder brushed the gunwales. Water made almost no sound unless they touched bottom.

They used taps. One tap on the hull meant stop. Two meant look. A hand flat meant wait. Pointing was direction, warning, accusation, prayer.

The first beaver obstruction took fifteen minutes because they would not speak. Joel got out in cold water and steadied the stern. Will pulled from the front, grimacing when his bad hand slipped. The canoe rasped over sticks. The sound went out through the fog.

From downstream, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will froze with both hands on the painter rope.

Joel shook his head.

Alex said it again, farther away.

Will pulled. Joel pushed. They got over the dam and climbed back in with water running off their sleeves.

Later, Mike's voice called from behind, "Just keep moving."

They did.

Joel began to understand that refusal was not dramatic. It was physical and repetitive. Hear the voice. Feel the body answer. Stop the body. Paddle. Hear another voice. Stop again. Paddle. Survival became a set of small withheld motions.

At a narrow bend Will tapped twice. Joel looked.

On the right bank, in mud below alder roots, was a print. Not clear. Not enough. Broad at one end, long at another. Bear if you needed bear. Human if you needed worse. Water had filled it and made the edges soft.

Will pointed to it, then to his eyes, then lifted both hands in a question.

Joel shook his head. No time. No answer.

Will's jaw worked.

The channel widened near evening into a slow reach between black spruce. Fog thinned. The sky beyond it had the yellow-gray color of weather not finished with them. They needed a camp and did not want one. Camps were places that taught the thing.

They found a gravel bar barely high enough for bodies. No tents. No fire. Tarp low against a driftwood snag. Food beside them. Canoe pulled up and tied to Joel's wrist with the shortened painter, because losing the boat in sleep would be the simplest death left.

Will took the notebook.

If it only repeats, it cannot lie about new facts, he wrote.

Joel read the sentence twice.

Will wrote under it:

We can test.

Joel wrote:

No.

Will wrote:

There is a difference between answering and testing.

Joel underlined NO until the page tore.

Across the channel, Will's own voice said, "There is a difference."

Will shut his eyes.



<!-- ===== manuscript/22-the-channel.md ===== -->

# Chapter 22 - The Channel

Morning came with frost on the tarp edge and no sleep worth naming.

They ate cold food from the bag. Will's hands shook badly enough that Joel opened a packet for him. Will hated that and accepted it anyway. The river steamed where first light touched it. The channel ahead bent through alder so tight the canoe would have to be walked in places.

Will wrote:

If I am right, wrong context exposes it.

Joel wrote:

Survival exposes it enough.

Will did not answer on paper.

They pushed off.

The channel was work without space. Gravel shallows. Beaver cuts. Grass mats. Alder stems slapping shoulders. Twice they had to get out and line the canoe around snags. Once Joel slipped and went to one knee, cold water entering over his waistband. He did not swear. The unsaid word burned in his teeth.

From ahead, Will's voice called, "You guys hear that?"

Will was in front of Joel, visible, back bent over the painter line.

Joel tapped twice on the hull. Will turned. His face was gray.

The voice came again from ahead, same tone, same upward hook inviting an answer.

Will pointed to himself, then ahead.

Joel nodded. Yes. It used living voices now in full daylight. The rule was no longer grief. It was acoustics with teeth.

At the next bend, Mike cleared his throat from the left bank.

Will flinched.

Alex laughed from behind.

Joel kept his eyes on Will's shoulders and on the canoe between them. Visual before voice. Object before sound. Water before memory.

By afternoon the channel joined a longer lake, narrow but open enough for wind. The map, damp and soft at the folds, showed a final portage tomorrow if they made enough distance today. North Road Bridge beyond that. The Highlander key in Joel's pocket. Human road. Human stupidity. Human help.

They paddled until Will's strokes failed. At camp, if the gravel shelf could be called camp, Will wrote with hard pressure:

CAN IT LEARN FROM WRITING?

Joel looked at the question and felt real fear, different from voices. He had assumed writing was safe because the thing was sound. Assumptions had killed better men than him.

He wrote:

NO PROOF. DON'T TEACH IT.

Will took back the pen.

If we never test, we never know.

Joel wrote:

Knowing is not the same as leaving.

The woods beyond the gravel bar gave Will's dry cough.

Will turned his head toward the sound before he could stop himself.

Joel tapped once on the notebook.

Will turned back, ashamed and angry and alive.



<!-- ===== manuscript/23-no-no-no.md ===== -->

# Chapter 23 - No, No, No

They did not build a fire.

Smoke would not matter. Light might. Habit certainly would. They made the tarp low and dark, crawled under it before the last light went, and lay shoulder to shoulder in damp clothes that never warmed.

The world became sound.

Water moving over small stones. Tarp ticking in the wind. Will's breath catching when he almost slept. Joel's own pulse in his ears. Somewhere far off, or close, a branch breaking under no visible weight.

Will whispered once, "I'm sorry."

Joel put a hand over his mouth before the second word could come. Not hard. Enough.

Will stared at him with such hatred that Joel almost removed it. Then understanding came back and the hatred changed into something worse. Will nodded. Joel took his hand away.

They wrote by the dim red lamp under Joel's jacket.

Will: Mike is dead because we did not go after him.

Joel: Mike is dead because it took him.

Will: both can be true.

Joel had no answer that improved anything.

From beyond the tarp, Alex said, clear and cheerful, "I'm taking a piss. Nobody panic."

Will's whole body jerked. Joel caught him. Will fought for one second, not against Joel but toward the voice, toward the impossible mercy of an old joke. Joel clamped a hand over his mouth harder this time.

Alex laughed outside.

Then Mike said, "We're fine."

Then Will's own voice, from three different places in the dark, said, "No, no, no."

It was not panic. That was what made it unbearable. The words were even, copied from fear but emptied of fear, spaced like drops from a roof.

"No, no, no."

"No, no, no."

"No, no, no."

Will shook under Joel's hand. Tears ran sideways into his hair. Joel held him because holding was the only answer that did not feed the thing.

After a long time the voices stopped. Not faded. Stopped.

Will took the notebook with hands that barely worked.

It does not know why that hurts, he wrote.

Joel read it.

Will wrote:

That matters.

Joel wanted to write no. He wanted to write sleep. He wanted to write their names and all the stupid ordinary things they had done before the woods got their voices.

Instead he wrote:

Morning. Move.

Will looked at the words for a long time. Then he nodded.

In the dark beside them, outside the tarp but close enough to touch, a tent zipper moved slowly down.

They had not pitched a tent.



<!-- ===== manuscript/24-the-difference.md ===== -->

# Chapter 24 - The Difference

Will was quieter in the morning, which frightened Joel more than the night had.

They packed with hands swollen by cold and lack of sleep. Will's injured palm had gone puffy around the bandage. His eyes were bright and fixed. He moved carefully, saving himself for an argument Joel could feel coming.

The map showed the final portage as a black dash line between two lakes. It looked short. Nothing in the last week had made Joel trust short marks.

They paddled in low cloud. The lake was narrow but the wind still found it. Once, far behind them, Mike said, "We're exactly where we ought to be," and Joel felt the sentence pass through him like cold water.

Will did not turn.

At the portage landing they unloaded in silence. The trail climbed immediately into brush. Too narrow for side-by-side. Too thick for long sightlines. Perfect for a thing that worked in voices and gaps.

Will took the notebook before they started.

I can make it contradict itself.

Joel wrote:

That is answering.

Will wrote:

No. There is a difference.

Joel wrote:

No difference that keeps you alive.

Will read that and smiled a little. Not happily. With pity. "That's not how that works," he said.

Joel closed his eyes.

Will realized it too late.

From the trail ahead, in Will's voice, came: "That's not how that works."

Then from behind: "There is a difference."

Will's face changed. Fear, yes, but also recognition. He had been right about part of it. The thing did not understand the argument. It had the shells, not the animal inside.

He pointed down the trail, then to his ear, then made a small split motion with his fingers. We can use that.

Joel shook his head.

Will took one step close and whispered, almost soundless, "We need to know."

Joel wanted to hit him. He wanted to hug him. Both would have taken time.

They began the portage. Joel carried the canoe first, Will the heavy pack. After fifty yards the trail turned through young spruce and Joel lost sight of him for three seconds.

Three seconds became enough.

Mike's voice called from ahead, "Will. Key's here."

Will stopped. Joel saw his legs below the canoe rim.

Alex said, "Quit mothering me."

Will said, "No."

Joel dropped the canoe. It crashed through brush beside the trail.

Will was standing where the trail bent, facing left into trees.

"That's not Mike," Joel said.

Will looked back at him, and for one instant he was the boy from church basements, the man with too many hobbies, the friend who could not leave a wrong thing uncorrected.

"I know," Will said.

Then he turned toward the voice.



<!-- ===== manuscript/25-will-answers.md ===== -->

# Chapter 25 - Will Answers

Joel reached him before the trees took him, but not before Will spoke again.

"Say something new," Will called into the brush. His voice cracked on new. "Come on. If you understand, say something new."

The woods answered with Mike: "We're fine."

"No," Will said. "That's old."

Joel grabbed his pack. Will twisted free with strength Joel would not have credited to him an hour earlier.

From the left, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will laughed once, wild and broken. "Old."

From ahead, Will's own voice said, "There is a difference."

"Old," Will said, and stepped off the trail.

Joel went after him. Branches whipped his face. The ground dropped, not steeply but enough. Will moved toward a small opening where stones showed through moss. Beyond it the forest was dim and wet and crowded.

"Will," Joel said.

The name was out before he could stop it.

Will froze. So did Joel.

For a second nothing happened.

Then Joel's voice came from behind them. "Will."

Will turned the wrong way.

Something moved in the opening. Not a bear. Not not a bear. A pale angle between trunks, a wet dark shoulder, limbs or branches crossing where no branch should be. Joel's mind refused the whole and kept the fragments as punishment.

Will said, very softly, "Oh."

That was his last living word.

The motion was fast and low. Will went down without a scream. Joel saw his hand strike stone, saw the bandage flash white, saw his body pulled sideways into alder that thrashed once and then stilled.

Joel had the hatchet in his hand. He did not throw it. He did not charge. He backed away, making no sound at all, because the part of him that loved Will was not stronger than the part that understood the rule.

From the brush came Will's voice, panicked now, perfect and empty.

"No, no, no."

Joel backed to the trail.

"No, no, no."

He lifted the canoe because leaving it meant losing the route. His legs shook so badly the hull knocked against a spruce trunk.

"No, no, no."

He walked.

At the place where the trail bent, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel did not answer.

He put one foot down, then the next, and carried the canoe toward the last lake alone.



<!-- ===== manuscript/26-alone.md ===== -->

# Chapter 26 - Alone

Alone simplified the arithmetic and ruined everything else.

Joel had one canoe, one pack cut down to essentials, one map too soft at the folds, one Highlander key, one hatchet, one filter, one wet sleeping bag he did not plan to unroll, and daylight draining through low cloud.

He also had three dead men behind him who would not stop speaking.

Mike told him to just keep moving. Alex asked if he was coming or writing a poem. Will coughed from ahead, behind, once from so close Joel felt it on the back of his neck though there was no breath.

Joel wrote notes and pinned them under the map cord where he could see them while walking.

NO NAMES.

NO ANSWERS.

ONLY SEEN IS REAL.

SOUND IS BAIT.

He abandoned weight in stages. Stove. Extra pot. The damp tarp. Food he could not eat while moving. Will's broken rod tip, which he had taken without remembering taking it. Each thing left behind felt like a failure of loyalty until the next step proved loyalty was too heavy.

The final portage ended at a lake under dusk-colored cloud. The water ran north between low black shores. The map said the bridge was beyond it, at the outlet. The map had said many things.

Wind moved along the lake but not across it. That mattered. Joel could paddle that if his hands kept working.

At the landing he stopped and listened. The woods behind him assembled the group.

Mike cleared his throat.

Will said, "I can make it contradict itself."

Alex laughed.

Joel put the canoe in the water and loaded it without hurry because hurry made noise. Then he took the notebook and wrote one last rule.

GIVE IT ONE THING.

He needed to know direction. He needed to know which voice was a voice and which sound was being thrown to turn him. He needed bait with no grief in it.

He looked in the pack and chose the blue dry bag under the front seat.

He stood facing the water, not the woods, and said clearly, once, "Blue bag under the front."

The sentence went out over the lake and came back smaller.

Behind him, nothing answered.

He waited until waiting became danger. Then he got in the canoe.

As he pushed off, from the trees to his left, Alex's voice said, "Blue bag under the front."

It was perfect and useless.

Joel paddled right.



<!-- ===== manuscript/27-the-planted-phrase.md ===== -->

# Chapter 27 - The Planted Phrase

The planted phrase saved him by being stupid.

No memory hung from it. No old trip. No argument. No dead man's way of handling shame. Blue bag under the front. A fact, and not even an important one now that the bag was in the canoe and Joel could see it.

The entity used it three times in the first twenty minutes.

Alex said it from the left shore, teasing, as if the bag had done something funny.

Will said it from behind him with technical urgency.

Mike said it from ahead, managerial and certain, exactly where Joel most wanted the route to be simple.

Each time Joel chose against the voice.

The lake narrowed. Dusk thickened. His hands hurt around the paddle. Blisters had opened and gone numb. His shoulders no longer belonged to him. The canoe slid through dark water that held the sky in broken strips.

Something knocked from the right. Paddle on gunwale.

Joel looked at the blue bag.

It sat under the front seat, visible, ordinary.

The knock came again.

He paddled left.

The wind dropped near full dark. That should have helped. Instead the silence grew large enough to contain every voice the thing had learned. They came at intervals, never all at once for long. Mike's throat clear. Will's cough. Alex's laugh. Mike saying, "We're exactly where we ought to be." Joel's own voice saying, "Wait. Just wait."

That one nearly turned him.

It was not command that hurt. It was familiarity. His own restraint used against him. The voice sounded careful. Reasonable. Alive in the way he was alive, alone in the back of a canoe trying not to become sound for something else.

He did not answer.

The planted phrase came again from open water to his right, in Will's voice.

"Blue bag under the front."

There could be no speaker there. No shore. No body. Just water, dark, a few pale chips of reflected cloud.

Joel understood then that the thing could throw sound across emptiness or move where he could not see. Understanding did not change the rule. It sharpened it.

He kept the bow toward the black cut in the trees where the map promised an outlet.

Near the narrows, something rose ahead. Low. Pale. A shape of water and wrong joint. It slipped under before the canoe reached it. The lake closed over without ripple enough.

Joel did not stop paddling.

From behind him, Mike said, "Just keep moving."

For once the dead voice gave useful advice.

Joel hated that and obeyed the living need under it, not the sound.

# Chapter 28 - The Crossing

The last crossing was not large on the map.

On the water, in dark, alone, after seven days had been stripped down to one body's remaining heat, it was a country.

Joel found the outlet by sound first: water pulling over stone, a lower note under the wind. He kept to the center because the banks were too close and too full of voices. Branches leaned over the channel. Twice something moved beside him at walking speed. Once a hand, or the idea of a hand, touched the outside of the hull and fell away.

He did not look.

The channel dropped through a short run of stones. The canoe struck once, spun, and almost broached. Joel shoved with the paddle until the blade scraped rock and flexed. Cold water came over the side. The blue bag shifted under the front seat.

"Wait. Just wait," Joel's voice said from the left bank.

He leaned right and drove the canoe through.

After the stones, the water opened into the final lake. The far shore was invisible. The map showed a bridge beyond the north end, but the map did not paddle. Joel did.

He set a rhythm small enough to keep. Dip. Pull. Breathe. Dip. Pull. Breathe. He did not think of Alex under the groundsheet. He did not think of Mike lifted sideways into brush. He did not think of Will saying old as if knowing a thing could hold it still.

The voices thought for him.

Alex: "I'm taking a piss. Nobody panic."

Mike: "We're fine."

Will: "You guys hear that?"

Joel: "Wait. Just wait."

He let each sentence strike and pass. He gave them no new shape.

Halfway across, the wind turned. Small waves slapped the bow. His arms had become tools used by someone else. The canoe yawed. He corrected late. Water came in again. The cold in his feet climbed to his knees.

Then he saw a reflector.

One small human eye in the dark, amber and flat, high above water.

Joel stared until it vanished behind the rise of a wave and came back. A road reflector. A sign. A thing made for machines, not men in canoes. He almost laughed, and the almost was dangerous enough that he bit the inside of his cheek until he tasted blood.

The voices stopped before he reached shore.

That was the worst thing they had done.

He landed under the bridge in black gravel and crawled out of the canoe on hands and knees. The concrete above him held the day's leftover cold. The Highlander sat where Mike had left it, gray and ordinary, behind the guardrail.

Joel took the key from his pocket.

For one full minute he could not make his fingers press the button because the world had become so quiet.



<!-- ===== manuscript/29-north-road-bridge.md ===== -->

# Chapter 29 - North Road Bridge

The Highlander opened with a tired chirp.

Joel nearly dropped the key.

The sound was too domestic, too obedient. A machine answering a signal. He stood beside the driver's door with water running from his clothes onto gravel and waited for the answer to become something else.

Nothing spoke.

The engine started on the second try. Mike would have made a remark about Japanese loyalty or American decline or the false economy of replacing a battery before it failed. Joel sat behind the wheel and listened to the heater fan tick itself awake.

There was no service.

He drove because roads still did what roads did. Slowly at first, then badly faster. The logging road shook the Highlander hard enough that gear rattled in the back. Every rattle sorted itself into the old ledger: food latch, buckle, paddle knock, zipper. Joel kept both hands on the wheel and did not answer any of it.

He got service eleven miles later on a rise cut through spruce.

The emergency operator asked what happened.

Joel said, "Canoe accident. Animal attack. Three men dead or missing."

He did not say voices.

She asked his location. He read the road marker twice because the first time his mouth would not shape numbers correctly. She told him to stay where he was if safe.

Safe was not a place he knew how to identify.

By noon there were deputies, conservation officers, a ranger from another district, an ambulance he did not need until they saw him, and men asking careful questions around the fact that he looked like someone dug out of weather.

"What did you hear?" one of them asked.

Joel looked at him too sharply.

The man softened his voice. "Before the attack."

"Brush," Joel said. "Alex. Then impact."

"Did he call out?"

Joel closed his hands around the blanket. "Yes."

"What did he say?"

Joel could have told the truth. Hold up. What. Maybe nothing. Maybe another voice before him. Instead he said, "I don't know."

The official story began forming while the search teams assembled: bear, panic, separation, exposure, possible drowning, survivor shock, unreliable statements under trauma. It was not malicious. It was what people did with facts too large and strange to carry whole.

They found Alex first because Joel could mark the camp. They found enough of Mike to stop saying missing. They found Will's pack near the final portage and then stopped letting Joel overhear.

A deputy asked if Joel wanted to call someone.

He thought of his fiancee's voice. He thought of giving any machine the right to bring a beloved sound out of nowhere.

"Not yet," he said.

That night in a hospital room two towns south, Joel gave a statement that was true in parts and useless as a whole.

He said weather trapped them.

He said they made bad decisions.

He said sound carried strangely over water.

No one disagreed.

# Chapter 30 - Home Sound

Home had too many sounds.

The refrigerator motor. Pipes in the walls. Cars passing on wet pavement. The phone lighting and darkening on the table. His fiancee breathing in sleep beside him and turning once under the sheet. Floorboards settling after the furnace stopped. Rain in the gutter making small patient ticks that had nothing to do with tarp lines and therefore everything to do with them.

Joel did not sleep much.

People wanted language from him. Police follow-ups. Insurance forms. Families. Services. Messages from men who had known one of the others in college or on a trip or through a job and needed Joel to confirm the shape of loss. He learned to say as little as possible without seeming cruel.

Animal attack.

Bad weather.

We got separated.

I am sorry.

All true enough to pass through a room.

Will's mother asked whether he had been scared. Joel said yes. That was the safest truth and the least complete one.

Mike's sister asked if he suffered. Joel said it was fast. He did not know that. He gave it to her because some lies were doors out of worse rooms.

Alex's brother laughed once during a call, not because anything was funny but because grief sometimes came out wearing the wrong coat. Joel had to put the phone down on the table and walk away until the sound stopped being Alex.

Weeks passed. The world resumed its insulting competence. Emails arrived. Leaves came down. Someone from work asked whether he planned to ease back in. His fiancee moved carefully around him until he asked her not to, and then she moved less carefully and he loved her for trying both ways.

He kept the notebook in a drawer.

He did not open it.

One night rain came hard after midnight. Joel woke on the couch with the lamp still on and the house dark beyond its circle. He had fallen asleep reading an article he did not remember choosing. His mouth tasted of metal. The room smelled like dust, wool blanket, cold coffee.

From the kitchen came a small click.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Plastic against metal. A latch settling, maybe. The refrigerator shifting. A house sound. Houses made sounds because materials changed with temperature and pressure. The world did not need permission to be ordinary.

Joel sat up.

The click came again.

He did not move. He watched the doorway to the hall, the dark rectangle leading to the kitchen.

A man cleared his throat in there.

Mike's throat. The small preparatory sound before a plan, before certainty, before the world got reduced to a route Mike could manage by saying it hard enough.

Joel's hands closed on the blanket.

From the kitchen, Mike said, "We're exactly where we ought to be."

The house held still around the sentence.

Joel did not answer. He did not call out. He did not wake the woman sleeping upstairs. He sat in the lamp's weak circle and listened until the refrigerator motor came on, until rain filled the gutters, until morning began to make the windows gray.

When the sun rose, nothing in the kitchen was out of place.

That did not help.
