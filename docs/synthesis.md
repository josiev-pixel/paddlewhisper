# Synthesis

## Premise

Four longtime male friends take a remote early-September canoe route through a fictional northern river system near Canada. The trip begins as a competent but friction-filled wilderness tradition: two staged vehicles, paper maps, ordinary canoe experience, practical gear, old jokes, and old resentments. On the second night, Alex is killed just outside camp in a scene that can be read as a bear attack until the details begin to fail.

The true horror is sound. Something in the river corridor can imitate voices, phrases, habits, gear noises, and environmental sounds it has heard. It cannot read minds. It cannot invent freely. The men arm it by talking, joking, arguing, correcting, reassuring, panicking, and calling to each other.

## Settled Canon

- Primary POV is close third through Joel.
- Joel is the survivor.
- Death order is Alex first, Mike second, Will third.
- The route is fictionalized but physically plausible: Canadian Shield / Boundary Waters adjacent, with lakes, alder channels, beaver work, gravel bars, spruce, granite, rough portages, old roads, and sparse camps.
- Will's truck is left at South Access / Old Landing; Mike's old Toyota Highlander is staged at North Road Bridge.
- No satellite messenger or emergency beacon is present.
- Will and Mike are the primary planners. Joel is competent but not the route lead.
- Alex lives in Seattle, grew up in Alaska, is least canoe-skilled, and is most performative.
- The abandoned ranger / maintenance cabin is a real but failed exit hope.
- The entity is never clearly explained or fully seen.
- Speech becomes dangerous; silence, writing, visual confirmation, and a planted phrase become survival tactics.
- The ending resolves immediate survival but leaves ordinary sound contaminated.

## Voice And Tone

Target voice is hard plainness with lyrical pressure. Most prose should be concrete, restrained, physical, and procedural. The writing earns beauty through wilderness, fear, grief, fatigue, and uncanny sound, then returns to plainness. Dialogue should sound like old friends arguing and helping each other without sentimental disclosure.

The first five chapters establish the desired cadence: gear work, banter, friction, exact physical process, and brief heightened observations when Joel notices water, light, distance, or wrong sound.

## Core Character Arcs

Joel begins as the quiet observer inside louder group rituals. He survives because he can stop answering and can think in rules under pressure. The cost is that speech and ordinary domestic sound become unsafe for him.

Will is useful, funny, technical, anxious, and verbal. His need to explain and correct helps identify the rule but also kills him.

Mike is competent and controlling. He knows canoes and routes, but his certainty becomes brittle. His death comes from trying to restore control after the route and cabin fail him.

Alex is vitality and performance. His death weaponizes grief. His jokes and ordinary lines become the first dead language the entity uses.

## Main Contradictions Reconciled

- Earlier broad notes allowed alternate endings. The working draft uses Joel reaching help and then a quiet ambiguous home sound.
- The old outline sometimes suggested Joel as a planner. Current canon keeps Will and Mike as planners, with Joel as observer and system recognizer.
- The entity is treated as capable of reproducing exact sounds and emotional tone, but not semantic invention. It may use phrases in wrong contexts.
- The first five chapters already advanced some seeds earlier than the outline did. The locked outline follows the draft, not the older abstractions.
