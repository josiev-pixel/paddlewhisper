# Chapter 8 - Nobody Panic

For a second the camp held its shape.

Mike by the fire with the stick still in his hand. Will half risen from the pack, one knee bent, his headlamp strap loose around his neck. He had taken it off to save battery and forgotten he had done so. Joel standing with his spoon in his hand, the nicked edge pressed into the pad of his thumb. Beyond the alder, Alex's headlamp made a small white wound in the brush.

Alex had just said, "What?"

It had come back in his ordinary voice, irritated more than afraid, the voice he used when someone had interrupted a story or questioned a minor competence. The word should have ended the moment. It should have been followed by a joke, a complaint, a report of a branch or his own stupidity. Alex could restore ordinary weather around himself. A bad landing became a bit. A missed throw became evidence of conspiracy. A mosquito bite became civic failure. The group had relied on that more than Joel wanted to admit.

But the headlamp did not turn fully back toward camp.

It held low and slightly right, aimed into the dark beyond the place where Alex stood. The light touched alder leaves, moved over them, and stopped on nothing Joel could see. The beam looked like fact until it failed to find a body.

A sound came from beyond him. Not a word, not quite. A wet little click in the throat of the channel, like water catching under a root and letting go. Joel held still until it became water again.

"Alex?" Joel said.

His voice went into the little camp and nothing answered.

Then Alex said, "Hold up."

Not loud. Not theatrical. The way he had said it at the beaver dams when mud kept one sandal, the way he had said it on the rough portage when a pack strap caught on blowdown. Practical. Annoyed. Expecting the world to wait because he had found a small problem inside it.

Joel could not tell whether Alex had said it to them or to whatever had made him turn.

Mike stood all at once. "Come back in."

Alex did not answer.

"Alex," Mike said, sharper.

Will fumbled at his headlamp. The plastic housing clicked against his collarbone. "What is it?"

"Shut up," Mike said.

"I'm asking what--"

"Shut up and get your light."

Joel had not moved. He saw that before he felt it. His body remained in camp while his attention had gone through the alder and stopped beside Alex. The spoon was still in his hand. He put it down on the nearest pack too carefully, as if a louder movement might answer for him.

Alex's beam jerked left.

"What the hell," Alex said.

He took one step farther from camp. Not a run. Not panic. The small offended step of a man going to prove the thing was nothing before anyone could make a rule about it.

Brush moved.

At first the sound was small enough to stay ordinary. A branch scraping another branch. A foot in wet grass. Something pushing through alder with less care than a person and more care than wind. Then the brush gave way in a heavy rush, not from one point but along a short line of darkness. Leaves flashed silver in Alex's lamp. Stems bent, snapped back, bent again. For half a blink the beam caught a low dark mass, or only leaves folding over one another. No face. No eyes. By the time the light steadied, there was no animal in it.

Alex shouted once. Not a word. A raw, insulted burst of air.

Mike grabbed for the bear spray.

The canister had been set upright beside his pack after dinner, part of the normal theater of caution. At First Camp it had seemed like an object that proved they were responsible. Here it looked small in Mike's fist. He tore the safety free with his thumb and started toward the channel side.

"Back," he said.

No one went back.

Joel moved because Mike moved. Later he would not know whether he had decided to follow or had been pulled by the sight of another man acting. He took two steps, then stopped and turned for something, anything. His hand closed around the nearest paddle, the bent-shaft one Alex had been using all day because he liked the look of it and because Will said it made no difference for his form.

"Joel," Will said.

"Light," Joel said.

Will had his own lamp on now. The beam shook over the fire, Mike's shoulder, Joel's paddle blade, the wet path to the channel. "Alex!"

"Don't yell," Mike snapped.

"Alex!"

"Will."

The second shout came from beyond the alder.

Alex this time. Or the shape of him under pressure. It started as a word and broke before it reached them. Something hit brush hard. A body hit mud. There was a wet thump, then a tearing sound too brief and physical for Joel's mind to keep. Alder whipped back and slapped itself. The headlamp beam spun upward, showing leaves from below, then a hard piece of sky, then the white undersides of leaves still moving after the violence had passed.

Joel ran.

He struck the first screen of alder shoulder-first. Branches raked his face and arms. His sandal sank past the sole into black muck. The paddle caught across two stems and almost tore from his hand. Mike was ahead of him by two body lengths, broad back hunched, bear spray out, lamp beam jumping. Will came behind making a continuous broken sound, not quite Alex's name anymore, not quite words.

The brush made every distance dishonest. Ten yards became a room with no door. Mike's light opened one narrow piece of it at a time: green leaves turned white, black water between roots, mud polished by movement, a broken stem bright at the fresh split. Joel's own light found Mike's elbow, then lost it. Something crashed to their right. Or behind them. Or across the channel. The alder broke the sound and gave it back from everywhere.

"Stop," Mike said.

Joel stopped because the command had found some old trail in him. Will did not. He pushed into Joel's back and nearly knocked him down.

"Where is he?"

"Quiet."

"Where is he, Mike?"

"Quiet."

Something breathed beyond the light.

That was the only word Joel had for it, and even as he thought it he distrusted it. It might have been wind moving through wet leaves. It might have been Alex's breath. It might have been his own breathing made huge by the cup of his hands around the paddle shaft. It came once, low and close, with a wet catch at the end.

Mike raised the spray.

"Don't," Joel said, too late.

The canister made a hard choking hiss. Orange fog hit the alder and opened in the beam, immediate and chemical, a cloud with weight. It drove back into them as soon as it left the nozzle. Joel inhaled and his throat closed. Fire ran into his eyes. He turned away coughing, blind, spit hanging from his mouth. Will cursed and stumbled into him. Mike kept spraying for another second, then swore hard enough to tear his voice.

"Jesus," Will said. "Jesus, Jesus."

"Move left," Mike said.

"I can't see."

"Then blink."

"That's not--"

"Left."

They moved because standing in one place was worse. Joel wiped at his eyes with the heel of his hand and made the burning worse. His lamp beam became water and color. The alder stems doubled, then slid back into one another. His lungs wanted air and rejected what came.

The smell of spray covered everything for a few seconds. Then another smell came through under it.

Mud. Split green wood. Marsh water. Blood.

Mike stopped so abruptly Joel almost ran into him.

"No," Will said.

He said it once, softly, before he had seen enough. The word had arrived ahead of understanding.

Alex lay ten yards beyond the first screen of alder, in a place that had not looked like a place from camp. There was a narrow shelf of mud between roots, then black water. The alder grew thick around it, trunks no wider than broom handles but multiplied into a wall. Something had cleared a gap through them. Stems were bent flat. Leaves floated in the water with their pale undersides up.

Joel's mind refused Alex at first. It accepted only objects.

One hiking sandal, heel-down in mud. A calf streaked with black muck. A hand open as if asking for something small. The headlamp strap twisted under his cheek, cracked at one corner, its light pointing sideways into the water. A torn sleeve. A red canister half-hidden in grass near his hip, belt clip turned in the weeds, safety still bright, trigger clean. Blood on alder leaves, too dark under the lamps. A wet red place where the body had stopped being only Alex.

"No, no, no," Will said.

Now the words had weight. They came out of him as if something was being unloaded too fast.

Mike put out one arm and blocked him.

"Don't touch him."

"He's--"

"Don't touch him."

"We have to--"

"Back up."

Will tried to go around him. Mike caught him by the front of his PFD and shoved him hard enough that Will slipped in the mud and caught himself on a branch.

"What is wrong with you?" Will said.

"Do not touch him."

"He's right there."

"And whatever did it might also be right there."

The sentence entered the brush and gave the dark a shape. Joel lifted the paddle with both hands. He did not know what he intended to do with it. Hit a bear. Hit the air. Give his hands a task.

His eyes kept returning to Alex's hand. Mud darkened the lines of the palm. One fingernail was torn back. The hand looked separate from the violence because it was still shaped like Alex: broad, careless, capable, the hand that had slapped mosquitoes off his calf and held the filter hose badly and stirred peanut butter noodles with religious seriousness. Joel had spent whole stretches of the day annoyed by the man attached to it. He had measured Alex in performances, in bids for attention, in jokes that asked too much from a tired room. Now the hand lay open and all of Joel's measurements became obscene.

The woods were very quiet.

Not empty. Quiet. There was water moving under the alder. There were mosquitoes, somehow still alive in the spray and blood, whining thinly around their faces. There were the small internal noises of three men trying to breathe through panic. But the larger woods had withdrawn from them. No frog. No night bird. No branch settling after a disturbance.

A small wet sound came from beside Alex's cheek. Will jerked toward it. Joel's light found only current drawing a ribbon of blood under a root.

"Alex," Will said.

Joel almost told him not to say the name. He did not know why. It would have been cruel and useless. He also knew, in some part below reason, that names went farther here than they should.

Mike swept his light past the body and into the brush beyond it. "Hey!"

"Don't," Joel said.

Mike ignored him. "Hey! Get out!"

His voice cracked on the second word. Not fear exactly. Force applied to something that would not take shape.

No bear moved in the beam.

The light showed broken alder, mud gouged into shelves, black water where the channel curled close, a dead spruce branch with old gray lichen on it. It showed one dark smear low on a trunk. It showed something that might have been a print near the waterline and then, when Joel looked longer, only a collapsed place in mud. It showed wet roots scraped pale where bark had come off. It showed no eyes, no retreat, no body large enough to carry the whole explanation.

Then, to their left, brush ticked once.

All three lights went there.

Nothing.

Mike sprayed again, a short burst this time, reckless and furious. The cloud opened and fell. Joel turned his face away before it reached him, but it caught the side of his neck and made the skin burn.

"Stop wasting it," Will said.

Mike rounded on him. "You want to save it?"

"I want to see."

"See what?"

"Anything."

"It's a bear."

The word sounded prepared. Not because Mike had known, but because it was the only box large enough and close enough to put the scene inside. Bear. The route had bears. They had bear spray. They had hung food badly in a bad camp near water and brush. A bear could be reported. A bear could be managed, feared, survived. A bear belonged to the world.

Joel looked again at Alex's body and tried to make the word fit over it.

It fit in places. Violence. Strength. Blood. Mud. The smashed alder. The speed of it. The terrible plain fact that large animals could do more damage to a human body than humans liked to remember.

It did not fit everywhere.

The food barrel was back at camp, still hanging. Alex had been standing. His spray had not been fired. There had been no bawl, no roar, no warning huff, no heavy retreat through brush after Mike came at it with lights. Nothing had gone for the barrel or the trash or the cooking pots. The ground near Alex showed confusion but not the certainty of a large animal leaving. Maybe mud did not keep prints. Maybe alder hid movement. Maybe bears did not follow the little script men carried for them. Maybe Joel knew too little to know what he was seeing. Fear was not expertise.

He did not say the other thing, because it had no useful place in a bear attack: Alex had turned before the rush. Alex had answered something.

"Bear," Mike said again.

Will heard the repetition and flinched from it. "Where is it?"

"Gone."

"How do you know?"

"Because we're standing here."

"That's not how that works."

"Will."

"No, don't Will me. Where did it go? It was just--it was just right here."

Mike lifted the canister toward the dark. "It ran."

"We didn't hear it run."

"We heard everything."

"No. We heard brush. We heard--"

"Stop."

Will's mouth stayed open. His face had gone pale and shiny under the headlamp. The lamp tilted down and lit his own hands, which were shaking hard enough that the beam trembled over the mud at his feet.

Joel stepped closer to Alex.

He did it slowly, not out of courage but because his mind needed one more fact and hated him for needing it. Mud sucked at his sandal. He kept the paddle between himself and the brush as if the shape of defense mattered. Mike made a warning sound but did not stop him.

Alex's face was turned partly into the mud.

Joel saw enough to know he should not try to see more. One eye closed. Cheek streaked. Mouth open as if he were about to object to the inconvenience. Mud had filled the small crease beside his nose. The headlamp had slipped under him and lit the water, where blood moved in a thin ribbon and separated into the black current. The sight had an intimacy that felt like trespass. Joel backed up one step.

He remembered Alex at the channel earlier, crouched beside him while the filter ticked into the bottle.

You ever miss it?

This.

The annual misery project.

Joel had given him half an honest answer. Afterward, yes. He had not said the smaller, meaner truth: that he missed the men more reliably from Madison, when irritation had time to edit itself into affection and Alex became a story told at a reasonable volume. Being with them was harder than loving them from a distance. The trip needed planning, mileage, vacation time, weather windows, stored gear, staged keys, and the old deliberate lie that if they repeated the shape often enough, nothing essential had changed.

Now Alex was ten yards outside camp, and every unsaid thing had become permanent.

"We need to do something," Will said.

Mike rubbed at his eyes with his free wrist. "We are doing something."

"No, we're standing here."

"We're making sure it's gone."

"And then what?"

Mike did not answer.

The question went through Joel with a cold practical edge. Then what. Carry Alex? Where? Through alder, mud, two days of route behind them, harder water ahead, no service, no beacon. Leave him? Stay beside him? Sit in this brush until morning with whatever had done it still close enough to hear them breathe?

Will said, "We can't just--"

"I know," Mike said.

"Do you?"

Mike turned his light on Will, not quite at his face. "I know."

There was no comfort in the words. There was only the beginning of an argument too large for the dark.

Something splashed softly at the waterline.

Joel's body answered before thought. The paddle came up. Mike swung his light. Will made a strangled noise and stepped back into Joel's shoulder.

The beam found water moving around a broken branch. A leaf turned over in the current. A bubble rose and broke.

Nothing else.

"Back to camp," Mike said.

"No," Will said.

"Yes."

"We can't leave him."

"We're not leaving him."

"What do you call this?"

"Surviving the next eight hours."

Will stared at him. "Listen to yourself."

"I am."

"Mike--"

"If that bear is still inside fifty yards, you standing here saying his name is not helping Alex."

The cruelty of it worked because it was practical. Will shut his mouth. His chest rose and fell fast. He looked at Joel as if Joel might have another category available, some third thing between abandonment and suicide.

Joel did not.

"We mark where he is," Joel said. His voice came out thin. He cleared his throat and hated the sound. "We go back. We keep lights out except for checks. We stay together. At first light we come back."

Will shook his head once, hard. "At first light he's still dead."

No one answered.

Mike crouched without turning his back fully to the brush and picked up Alex's headlamp by the strap. Joel almost told him to leave it. Then he saw the light under Alex's cheek, cracked and shining stupidly into the water, and understood that Mike needed one small task that could be completed. Mike switched it off. Darkness took back the water immediately.

"Don't take his stuff," Will said.

"It's a light."

"It's his."

"Will."

"Don't say my name like that."

Mike held the lamp out to Joel, who took it because someone had to. The plastic had split near the battery case. It was slick. He did not look at what made it slick. He clipped it to the front of his own PFD, then unclipped it again because that felt unbearable, and carried it in his fist, away from his body.

They backed out of the alder.

No one wanted to turn first. They made an ugly sideways retreat, lights pointing into too many places, feet slipping in mud. Branches caught on straps and sleeves. Once Will stumbled, and Mike grabbed his arm, and Will yanked away as if the help itself accused him. Joel kept the paddle across his body and listened for something following. Every scrape they made became answerable by another scrape. Every branch they bent sprang back with a small wet tick that sounded, for half a second, deliberate.

When they broke through the alder screen into camp, the fire had settled into red pockets under ash. The tents stood open-mouthed and thin, nylon walls holding the small warmth of men who were no longer inside them. The food barrel hung pale and round in the back of camp, absurd in its correctness. The latch faced the fire exactly as before. The canoes lay high on the bank. The pots were stacked. The water bottles stood where Joel had left them. Their camp looked abandoned by people who had intended to come back in a minute.

Will stopped at the edge of the firelight and bent over with his hands on his knees. He retched once but nothing came up.

Mike went straight to the packs. "Spray. Where's the second can?"

"Out there," Joel said. "Alex had it."

The sentence broke something in the air. He had not meant to say had.

Mike looked toward the alder.

"I'm not going back for it," Will said.

"Nobody asked you."

"I'm saying it."

"Stop saying things."

"Don't tell me to--"

"Both of you," Joel said.

It surprised them enough to work. Mike turned. Will straightened halfway. Joel stood with Alex's dead headlamp in one hand and the paddle in the other and felt ridiculous and old and utterly unqualified.

"We need the boats farther up," he said. "Then we sit where we can see the channel and the back brush. No tents."

"No tents?" Will said.

"If something comes through one, I don't want to unzip my way out."

Will almost laughed. It came out as one hard breath.

Mike nodded once. "Canoes."

Work saved them because work did not ask what it meant. They hauled the canoes another few feet from the mud line, too close to camp now but better than the channel. Joel and Mike turned one sideways near the fire ring as a poor barrier, though all of them knew it would stop nothing determined. Will gathered paddles and laid them within reach, then moved them again because he did not like the first arrangement. He made a row along a log as if order could become defense.

Mike checked the food barrel line. It was still up. The latch was closed. He yanked the rope once and the barrel swung, knocking softly against the trunk.

All three men froze.

The knock seemed to come back from the channel side, smaller.

No one spoke.

Joel could not tell whether it was echo, memory, the barrel hitting twice, or something else answering with the exact vocabulary of their camp. His eyes burned from spray. His throat tasted like metal and pepper. He wanted to say branch, or barrel, or nothing. He did not. Words were beginning to feel less like explanations than offerings.

Mike steadied the barrel until it stopped moving. He tied the rope off again with fingers that missed the first turn.

"It's an echo," Will said.

His voice sounded angry at the mercy he had given them.

"Yeah," Mike said.

The agreement was too fast.

They put on every layer within reach. Fleece over damp shirts. Rain jackets zipped to the throat. Hats pulled low. Joel's hands shook when he tried to work the zipper. He looked at the pull and forgot what order fingers used. Will reached over and did it for him without comment, then turned away before the kindness could become visible.

Mike tried his phone.

He did it facing slightly away from them, as if the old embarrassment still mattered. The screen lit his face. He held it up, turned once, stepped onto a root, raised it higher. No bars. No service. The blue light showed flecks of bear spray on his cheek and the wet shine under his eyes. He put the phone away.

"Nothing," Will said.

Mike did not answer.

"Because of course nothing," Will said. "Because why would we have the one piece of equipment that solves this. A beacon. A messenger. One stupid red button."

"Will."

"No. Don't. This is exactly when people have the thing."

"We don't."

"I am aware."

"Then save it."

Will laughed once, without humor. "Save what?"

Mike sat on a pack near the fire ring and set the partly spent bear spray across his knees. "Your battery."

"Great."

"Your air."

"Good."

"Your whatever keeps you from making this worse."

Will stared at him. For a second Joel thought he would cross the small distance and hit Mike. Instead he crouched by the turned canoe and put both hands over his face. His shoulders moved once. Then again. No sound came out. That made it harder to watch than crying would have been.

Joel looked away and saw Alex's tent.

The second tent, the lumpy ground he had claimed with theatrical martyrdom, stood half zipped. One guyline had pulled loose. The door flap moved when the wind reached camp, lifting and dropping with a small nylon whisper. Inside would be his sleeping bag, his dry clothes, the pouch of bland food he had guarded from Will's spices, the paperback he had been unlikely to read, his spare socks. He had said nobody panic and left all of it arranged for morning.

Joel turned from it.

"We should get his pack," Will said, still crouched.

"No," Mike said.

"Not from out there. From the tent."

Mike looked at the tent. "In the morning."

"It's right there."

"In the morning."

"You think the bear cares about his socks?"

"I think if we start sorting a dead man's gear right now, you're going to come apart."

Will lifted his head. His face was wet and furious. "Don't make me your project."

"Then stop trying to fix the one thing we can't fix in the dark."

"Jesus Christ, Mike."

"Enough." Joel heard his own voice and recognized the thinness in it. "Enough for tonight."

They sat under the tarp because it was the only structure that let them see out. The tarp had been pitched low against possible rain and now made them hunch. Water from old condensation or fog gathered along one seam and fell at intervals onto the dirt. Tick. Tick. Then a longer pause. Then tick again. The sound made time without moving it forward.

They killed the fire after a short argument. Mike wanted light. Joel wanted not to sit in a lit room for whatever stood outside it. Will said both options were bad in different, specific ways until Mike told him to pick one or be quiet. Will picked quiet and made it look like injury.

When the last flame died, the world became depthless.

Headlamps stayed off except for brief checks. In darkness the camp rebuilt itself out of sound. The channel spoke first: water under alder, small and black. Then the food barrel rope, tightening and easing as wind moved above. Then nylon from Alex's tent. Then someone's swallow. Then Mike's thumb, once, on the bear spray safety. The click was tiny and enormous.

Joel sat with the paddle across his lap. He had washed his hands twice from a water bottle and could still smell blood where the cracked plastic of Alex's lamp had marked his palm. Maybe it was not there. Maybe the smell had entered the whole camp. He rubbed his thumb against the seam of his pants.

Will whispered, "What if he was still--"

"No," Mike said.

"You don't know what I'm going to say."

"Yes, I do."

"What if he was still alive when we--"

"No."

"You don't know."

"Will."

"Don't say my name."

"He was gone."

The words sat between them. Joel wished Mike had not said them. He wished someone else would say they were true. He wished truth had a sound you could identify in the dark.

Will's breathing changed. "You can't know that."

Joel looked toward the place where Will was only a darker shape beside the canoe. "He was gone."

It cost him less than it cost Will to hear it. That made it worse.

For a while no one spoke.

Joel tried not to listen. The effort made him listen harder. His mind sorted sound into columns without permission: water, rope, nylon, breath, branch. Ordinary. Ordinary. Ordinary. Maybe ordinary. He thought of the motel wall, the way he had lain awake and matched each noise to a source until he could sleep. Bathroom fan. Door latch. Alex laughing through plaster. Mike's cough or Will's. It had seemed like a way to settle the world. Now it felt like practice for a test that had changed its questions.

A branch cracked beyond the channel.

Will inhaled.

Mike lifted the spray.

Joel held up one hand, though neither man could see the gesture well in the dark. He waited. The crack did not repeat. Wind moved in the spruce tops and arrived at camp a few seconds later in pieces. A dead twig dropped somewhere behind them.

"Branch," Joel whispered.

He hated himself for needing the word and hated the word for being useful.

Later, maybe minutes later or an hour, something moved near Alex's tent. Nylon touched nylon. Joel turned his lamp on for one second. The beam showed the loose flap settling, the guyline slack, no animal, no man. He turned it off before the light could travel farther and find the alder.

"Wind," Mike said.

"I know," Joel said.

Will said nothing.

The night became a series of decisions not to move. Do not check the body. Do not check the sound by the channel. Do not retrieve Alex's spray. Do not open his tent. Do not ask whether anyone else heard the small almost-voice, if it had been a voice at all, that might have been water under a root. Do not say his name more than necessary.

Joel's legs cramped. His back tightened. The cold worked upward from the ground through the soles of his sandals and into his knees. Mosquitoes found the gap between his cuff and glove. The bear spray burn faded from flame to raw skin. He pressed his tongue against the roof of his mouth until the taste of pepper changed to blood from a bitten place there.

Will began talking again near what might have been midnight.

Not loudly. Not even to them at first. He listed facts under his breath, the way he might list gear before a portage, trying to make sequence stand in for sense.

"Bad food hang. Marsh camp. Dusk movement. He stepped away. We heard brush. Spray cloud. Body ten yards out. His spray still safetied. Food untouched. Bear country. This is bear country."

Mike said, "Stop."

"I'm just saying what happened."

"You're making yourself worse."

"I am trying to keep sequence."

"Keep it in your head."

"My head is currently not a trusted environment."

Joel almost laughed. The sound rose in him and stopped at his throat, not because it was funny but because it was Will, impossibly Will, still able to make a sentence that was both precise and unbearable. He looked toward the shape of him in the dark and felt the old church-basement version of Will pass through him: the boy who had corrected adults under his breath, who had made Joel laugh during prayers, who had been impossible to silence even when silence was the point.

"Sequence doesn't save us tonight," Joel said.

Will was quiet for a few breaths. "It might tomorrow."

"Tomorrow," Mike said, with the hard relief of a man taking possession of a word. "At first light we assess. We mark the site. We pack. We leave."

"Leave which way?" Will said.

"Out."

"Thank you. Very detailed route plan."

"Not now."

"It's not a small question."

"I said not now."

Will let out one dry, broken cough. The sound went into the dark above them and broke among the spruce. For a second Joel thought it came again from behind the fire ring, thinner and late.

Joel turned his head.

Nothing.

He looked at Will. Will was still beside the canoe, one hand over his mouth as if ashamed of the cough. Mike had not moved. Maybe neither of them had heard it. Maybe there had been nothing to hear. Maybe Joel's ears, burned and strained and full of blood, had made a double out of one sound.

He said nothing.

The silence after that felt less like restraint than debt.

Near dawn the cold deepened. The black in the trees thinned by degrees without becoming light. The channel showed itself first as a dull strip. Then the turned canoe. Then the hanging food barrel. Then the three packs they had pulled close and never opened. Shapes came back into the world reluctantly, each one bringing its own accusation.

No one had slept. They had leaned, shifted, jerked awake from half-dozes, and returned to listening.

Mike stood when there was enough gray to see his hands. His face had changed in the night. It had not softened. It had set around something. He picked up the bear spray, tested the weight of it, and looked toward the alder screen.

Will remained seated. "Don't."

Mike said, "We have to."

Joel stood too. His knees made small private sounds. He took the hatchet this time instead of the paddle, then looked at it and understood there was no correct object. A hatchet was only a smaller tool in a night that had made tools ridiculous, but it had weight and an edge, and his hands accepted it.

Will looked at both of them as if they had betrayed him by becoming able to move.

"At first light," Mike said.

Joel wanted to tell him this was not light. It was only the end of dark. But the distinction belonged to a world that still cared about precision. He clipped his lamp to his head and did not turn it on.

Will picked up the map case before he followed them. No one asked why. Joel understood anyway. A map was another thing with lines on it. Another thing that promised the world could be reduced to marks and distances if a man held it hard enough.

Together, three men walked back toward the place where the fourth had become a fact they could not yet name.
