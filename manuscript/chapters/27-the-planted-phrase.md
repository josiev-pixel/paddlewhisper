# Chapter 27 - The Planted Phrase

The planted phrase helped him once by being stupid.

No memory hung from it. No old trip. No argument. No dead man's way of handling shame. Blue bag under the front. A fact, and not even an important one now that the bag was in the canoe and Joel could see it. It did not ask anything of him except that he notice where it was.

The canoe was already moving when he understood the first use of it. He had shoved off from the gravel with both hands on the gunwales, one foot in the lake and one on stone, and for a second the hull had tried to leave without him. The emergency paddle lay crosswise over the seat. The small pack slid toward his knees. The map page under the cord lifted and slapped once, then settled. He got in badly, weight too far to one side, and the canoe rolled under him with the offended quickness of something that still remembered two men.

He did not swear. Swearing was a gift. Breath was almost a gift. He sat low, waited for the wobble to pass, and let the landing fall behind without turning to measure it.

The first time the thing used it, Alex said it from the left shore.

"Blue bag under the front."

Teasing. Almost amused. As if the bag had done something funny and Alex had found the smallest possible way to make Joel look at it.

Joel did not look left.

He looked at the bag.

It sat under the front seat, the blue dulled by mud and last light, the rolled mouth buckled twice, one corner still creased where the aluminum tube of the emergency paddle had pinned it before Joel pulled the paddle free. Its presence was not an answer. It was not rescue. It proved only that a voice in the trees could repeat a sentence about an object that gave it nothing back.

That was enough.

Enough had become a smaller word than it used to be. It no longer meant food for the day, daylight for the portage, proof for an explanation, or a route a man could defend on a map. It meant one fact that held for one breath. The bag was there. The voice was not the bag. The shore was not a mouth because Alex's mouth had spoken from it.

The canoe drifted while he made himself hold the difference between enough and safe. Wind pushed along the lake in narrow gusts, lifting small black wrinkles off the water. The right-hand reach opened ahead of him, darker than the shore but less dark than the alder-choked left channel where Alex's voice had placed itself. The map under the thwart cord showed a north-running lake, then a pinch, then water beyond. But the map had already been right in shapes and wrong in mercy. A blue line could be true and still not save anyone.

Joel set the short paddle on the right side and pulled.

The canoe answered late.

Everything about it was wrong now. Too little load in the front, too much human weight in the back, no second paddle correcting small mistakes before they became large ones. With Alex in front, the boat had jerked and argued but had still had another body reading the water. With Will, after Alex, it had been overloaded and miserable and alive with disagreement. One man in the back made the canoe ride light and foolish ahead of him. Each stroke swung the front wider than Joel wanted. Each correction cost more water than it gained.

He tried kneeling and could not hold it. His knees had gone stupid on the last portage, the left one swollen tight under wet fabric, the right one bruised where he had dropped against a root while lifting the boat alone. Sitting raised him too high, kneeling gave him control and then took it away with pain. He compromised in the ugly way survival made men compromise: one knee down, one foot braced, hips twisted, back already complaining. It was not good paddling. It was movement.

He moved anyway.

From the left shore, Alex laughed once.

The sound ran along the reeds, struck the open water, and came back thinner, in the old bridge way. Joel saw, without wanting to, Alex leaning over the rail at North Road Bridge on the first morning, pleased with his own echo, not knowing he had given the woods a rehearsal. The memory arrived bright and useless. He let it pass without giving it his mouth.

The second use came ten strokes later.

Will said it from behind him.

"Blue bag under the front."

Not amused. Urgent. Technical. Will's voice made the phrase into an instruction Joel had failed to obey correctly. He could almost hear the sentence that should have followed: No, no, no, you're shifting the trim, that's not how that works.

Joel's shoulders tightened. The canoe yawed. The left side dipped toward a little slap of chop.

He corrected too hard and the paddle blade fluttered in his hands. Aluminum flexed at the joint. The cheap collar gave a small dry click.

From behind him, the same click answered.

He wanted Will there with a fury so sudden it felt like nausea. He wanted the correction. He wanted the fight. He wanted Will in the front, cold and furious and alive, complaining that Joel's steering was turning them into a physics demonstration. The wanting came before thought and nearly became speech.

He pressed his tongue against the roof of his mouth until it hurt.

The boat steadied by inches.

He wrote no new rule. He could not write while moving, and stopping in open water was another kind of answer. The old rules sat in the notebook under his jacket and on the page pinned at the thwart behind him: NO NAMES. NO ANSWERS. ONLY SEEN IS REAL. SOUND IS BAIT. GIVE IT ONE THING.

He had given it one thing. It had not become his friend. It had not become a map. It was only a mark on the water, a way to see the trap spring.

Will would have hated how little that proved. Joel could feel the shape of the objection without needing the voice to say it. A controlled phrase was not a controlled experiment. No constant held. The lake changed, the light changed, Joel changed by the minute. The thing might repeat the phrase because it heard him, or because it had learned that any new sound from a living mouth mattered, or because all mouths were equal to it once opened.

The uncertainty did not make the test useless. It made it honest.

The lake narrowed by degrees. Low reeds began to show in clumps to the left. The right shore lifted into black spruce and small granite shelves, all of it sliding past too slowly. Dusk thickened under the cloud. The water stopped reflecting sky and began holding it in broken, darker strips. Every paddle stroke put a small wound in that surface and closed it again.

Joel tried to count strokes to keep himself from thinking.

One through twenty on the right.

One through fifteen on the left, because the left shoulder had less left in it.

Switch before the front wandered.

Do not reach. Short stroke. Close to the boat.

Do not listen for meaning.

He counted wrong almost at once. The numbers became men. Four had left the motel. Three had left Second Camp. Two had left the cabin. One had left Will.

He stopped counting.

Something knocked from the right shore. Paddle on gunwale.

Not a voice. Older than the voices. A trip sound. A boat sound. A sound from before the woods had learned grammar.

Joel checked the blue bag.

It sat under the front seat, visible, ordinary.

The knock came again from the right. Once, then twice, the rhythm of a man tapping for attention because shouting across water was rude before it was dangerous.

He paddled left.

Not because left was safe. Because the sound wanted his right side. Because the right shore, which should have been quiet if it was only spruce and stone, had chosen the language of men and gear.

The canoe slid toward the center. Wind took the front and pushed it back. He leaned forward, though leaning forward hurt his stomach and made the key in his inner pocket press hard against his breastbone.

The key was still there.

He checked it with the heel of his free hand between strokes, a small push against the jacket. Metal under cloth. The whole planned exit reduced to that cold tooth. Mike's key. Mike's Highlander. Mike's confidence broken out of his dead pocket and carried by the man who had learned not to believe confidence just because it spoke clearly.

The key had no voice. That was part of its mercy. It did not clear its throat. It did not tell Joel the road would be there, or the battery would turn, or the bridge would be where the map said bridge. It only pressed into him, a hard private fact. If he drowned, it would sink with him. If he reached the road, it might open a door. Between those two uses it knew nothing.

Ahead, Mike cleared his throat.

Joel kept paddling.

The throat-clear came again, from the right-hand reach, then from farther ahead, exactly where the lake seemed to open.

"Blue bag under the front," Mike said.

Managerial. Certain. The phrase became inventory, became route call, became an item checked off before the next instruction. It belonged in Mike's mouth more easily than Joel liked. Mike had been good at making objects into control. Boat. Pack. Key. Map. Cabin. Even dead, his voice tried to put the blue bag into a system Joel could obey.

Joel almost did.

The right-hand reach looked broader. The wind ran there, and running wind meant depth, travel, main water. The left stayed brushy, broken by reeds and a small point that could hide a dead channel. A living Mike would have gone right unless the map said otherwise. A living Mike would have been loud about it. A living Will would have argued him there and maybe been correct. Alex would have asked if this counted as team-building.

Joel held the paddle across his knees and let the canoe glide.

He made himself use his eyes.

The right-hand reach had open water, yes, but its far end rose dark against the surface. No break in tree line. No lower seam where water passed out. The wind made it look like a way because wind preferred open length. The left channel looked worse because the reeds masked it, but beyond the reed point he could see a darker cut, a long narrow absence between banks.

He looked at the map.

The map was nearly useless in the failing light. The page had swollen under its plastic, the ink blurred at old folds, but the north end of this lake narrowed before opening again. The pinch could be hidden behind reeds. The broader right-hand reach could be a bay that map scale made cruel.

Mike's voice came from the right again.

"It opens up after this."

Joel closed his eyes for one breath, then opened them because closed eyes gave sound too much room.

The thing had not used the planted phrase to reveal the correct direction. It had used the phrase the way it used everything else: to make a body move before the mind could finish looking.

That was the last temptation in Mike's voice. Not that it sounded like rescue. That would have been easier to refuse. It sounded like the efficient thing, the reasonable thing, the thing a tired group would have accepted because dissent took strength. Go where the water looked more open. Trust the man who had always been willing to decide. Let certainty do the work sight no longer wanted to do.

He worked the canoe left of center, not into the reeds but toward the visible cut beyond them.

The wind dropped near full dark.

That should have helped. Instead the silence grew large enough to contain every voice the thing had learned. Without wind, the little sounds of the boat separated from one another and became suspicious. Water ticking from the paddle. The hull's quiet rub against a floating stem. His breath catching when the blade found mud instead of depth. The collar of the emergency paddle creaking under his lower hand.

Then the voices came at intervals, never all at once for long.

Mike's throat-clear.

Will's cough.

Alex's laugh.

Mike saying, "We're exactly where we ought to be."

Joel's own voice saying, "Wait. Just wait."

That one nearly turned him.

It was not command that hurt. It was familiarity. His own restraint used against him. The voice sounded careful. Reasonable. Alive in the way he was alive, alone in the back of a canoe trying not to become sound for something else.

He let the paddle rest for half a second. Too long. The canoe lost its line and began to slide sideways toward the reed point. A stem touched the hull with a faint dry scrape.

From the reeds, Joel's voice said, "Wait."

Only the one word.

Less was worse. Less sounded more like him because he had never been a man of big speeches. He had given the woods so little that it could make a meal out of crumbs.

He wondered, with the quick useless cruelty of fatigue, whether the thing had enough of him to imitate silence. Not absence. Silence. The little pause before he answered. The way he let other men spend themselves first. The way his restraint had once felt like patience and had become, under pressure, a kind of coldness. A man could be copied by what he did not say, too, if the people who loved him had built their speech around that vacancy.

He did not answer. He did not wait.

He planted the paddle and shoved away from the reeds. The blade struck bottom. Mud took it and held. For one bad second the paddle would not come free. The canoe kept moving. The shaft bent. He saw himself losing it, saw the emergency paddle sliding out of his hands into black water, saw the remaining route become a joke too stupid to be called fate.

He twisted, pulled straight up, and the blade came loose with a wet pop.

The same pop sounded from behind him.

Then to the right.

Then under the canoe.

Joel froze with the paddle across his lap.

Under the canoe was not a direction. Under the canoe was a thought his body had before he could stop it: something beneath him, pale, close, waiting for the shape of a man to lean too far over the gunwale.

He looked down only as far as the blue bag.

It was still there. The bag. The pack behind him. His knees against the ribs. The dull gleam of water inside the hull, where earlier spray had pooled and carried a spruce needle back and forth with each small rock of the boat. These were facts close enough to see.

The planted phrase came again from open water to his right, in Will's voice.

"Blue bag under the front."

There could be no speaker there. No shore. No body. Just water, dark, a few pale chips of reflected cloud.

Sound did not have to stand where a body would stand. Knowing that did not change the rule. It sharpened it.

Sight had never meant safety. It meant only that sound alone could not vote.

He thought of the notebook under his jacket, wet pages swelling against his ribs. All week he had been making law out of panic. Rules gave the panic edges. They did not make it true. They did not make the lake shallow or the thing stupid or the dead less dead. They only slowed the body's first obedience.

He kept the front toward the black cut in the trees where the map promised an outlet.

The cut did not resolve quickly. It lengthened and shortened with the angle of his approach, sometimes visible between reed stalks, sometimes gone behind the shoulder of the point. The water shallowed. Twice the paddle struck submerged stems. Once the hull slid over something soft that gave under it, not rock, not wood, and Joel's whole back tightened. The canoe slowed as if a hand had taken the back painter.

He turned enough to see behind him.

No hand. No rope. Only the small wake opening in a V, black on black, closing itself as if the canoe had never passed.

From the shore, Alex said, "Hold up."

The old reflex went through Joel's hands. Hold up meant obstacle, pack strap, sandal caught, a body needing ten seconds without having to confess the need. Hold up had been useful before it was fatal. He had answered it for days. He had answered it in spirit before the trip even began, whenever Alex performed helplessness as a joke and someone else paid the small cost.

Joel did not hold up.

He paddled.

The left shore pressed closer. Alder leaned out over the water, black stems combing the surface. The canoe moved inside the smell of mud, cold vegetation, and the sour rot of reeds. Mosquitoes lifted from the bank in a thin late swarm and found his face. He let them. Swatting was sound. Swatting was a hand off the paddle. One landed at the corner of his eye and drank there while tears from the irritation blurred the cut ahead.

He blinked it clear.

The blue bag showed less in the dark now. It was not blue so much as a remembered blue, a slightly different darkness under the front seat. Joel reached forward with the paddle shaft and touched it once. The plastic crinkled.

From the bank, the crinkle answered.

"Blue bag under the front," Alex said again.

This time Alex sounded bored. That was almost worse than teasing. The phrase had already become nothing to the thing. It could use a new sentence until it wore the newness off. It did not care what the sentence meant. It cared what a living body did when a familiar mouth held it.

Joel knew then the tactic would not last.

It had bought him direction once. Maybe twice. It would not carry him to the bridge. It would not protect him from his own voice. It would not make the thing stupid enough to defeat itself.

The thought should have discouraged him. Instead it steadied something small and mean in him. He did not need a solution. He needed the next hundred feet.

That had been the size of survival since Alex died, though Joel had only just admitted it. Not the route. Not the take-out. Not the official category waiting somewhere beyond the bridge, bear or accident or exposure or missing. The next hundred feet, then the next. A distance too short to contain hope and too long to cross by wishing.

The cut opened.

Not wide. Not welcoming. A seam of darker water between reeds, with black spruce crowding both sides beyond. A little current touched the front and changed the feel of the canoe. Not much. Enough that the water seemed to have a preference. Enough that the lake's flat holding became a pull.

Joel let the front take it, then checked the map one last time.

He had to use the headlamp. He hated that. Light made him visible in a way darkness did not. Light also made the near world honest for as far as it reached. The lamp sat in the small pack behind him. Getting it meant twisting, risking the boat, making zipper sound.

He waited until the canoe drifted into a small lee behind the reed point, then set the paddle across both gunwales. He turned slowly. His spine cramped. The pack had shifted; he pulled it forward by one strap. The zipper was gritty and loud in the stillness.

From three places at once, zippers answered.

Tent zipper. Gear shelter zipper. Pack zipper. The old sound of people opening and closing little human weather.

Joel found the headlamp by touch, closed the pack without zipping it, and settled back before the canoe could swing broadside. His hands shook too much to fix the strap around his head. He held the lamp against his chest, cupped the beam with his fingers, and clicked it on.

Weak yellow light leaked between his knuckles.

The map appeared in pieces: blue lake, black line, wet wrinkle, his own dirty thumb, the written phrase on the torn margin. BLUE BAG UNDER THE FRONT. The pencil letters had softened at the edges. They looked older than they were, as if all writing aged quickly here.

The line matched enough. The lake narrowed into a channel. The channel bent north and east before opening into the next water. North Road Bridge beyond. Road beyond. Highlander beyond, unless the road had washed out, unless the vehicle would not start, unless the key had bent, unless help was another human mark the world had let decay.

Enough.

He clicked the lamp off.

Dark returned thicker for having been cut.

The headlamp click went on ringing after the light was gone. It seemed impossible that such a small switch could have so much edge. Plastic against nail. A cheap battery contact closing. The kind of sound a man made in a tent without thinking, half inside sleep, opening a little private circle of yellow. Joel kept the lamp between his knees and waited for the answering click.

It came from the right bank. Then from behind him. Then, too quietly, from the pack at his feet though the lamp lay visible and dark.

He did not touch it again.

Mike said from ahead, very softly, "Just keep moving."

For once the dead voice gave useful advice.

Joel hated that and obeyed the living need under it, not the sound.

He stowed the headlamp loose between his knees and took up the paddle. The current gathered him by inches. He gave two small strokes to keep the front from the reeds.

Something rose ahead.

Low. Pale. A broken piece of reflected sky, except it bent once against the direction of current. Not an animal he could name in the time he had. Not a log. Not a man, because a man would have made the water tell a different story. It broke the surface without a splash and slipped under before the canoe reached it.

The lake closed over with barely a ripple.

Joel's arms stopped.

The canoe did not.

It moved toward the place where the shape had gone under, current and last momentum carrying him, reed shadows sliding along both sides. The paddle lay half raised in his hands. If he dug too hard, he might turn broadside. If he stopped, he would drift over it anyway. If he looked for it, the dark water would become all the places it was not.

From the right bank, Will said, "There's a difference."

Joel put the paddle in.

Small stroke. Close to the boat. Another. Another.

The hull passed over the place where the pale thing had been. Nothing struck. Nothing touched. Water made its ordinary small talk along the ribs. His own breath sawed in and out through his nose because his mouth was closed and would stay closed.

His body wanted proof after the danger passed. It wanted to look back, lean over, shine the weak lamp into the water and make the pale thing become one of the old categories: log, fish, reflection, animal. Explanation was another appetite. It had teeth in it. He kept the paddle moving until the need to know dulled into another ache.

Behind him, in Mike's voice, "Blue bag under the front."

Ahead, in Joel's voice, "Wait. Just wait."

To the left, in Alex's, "You coming or writing a poem?"

The voices no longer tried to be plausible. They tried to be many. They tried to make the world all invitation and correction. They made old friendship into a net, each strand a habit Joel would once have honored without thinking.

There were other strands the thing did not have, or did not use. Mike's hand on a shoulder after a bad landing, too brief to embarrass them both. Will going quiet when he was truly hurt, all correction drained out of him. Alex doing useful work in silence and ruining the kindness later by making a joke of it. Those things had not been sound, or had not been useful sound. The dead were smaller in the thing's mouth than they had been in life, but the smallness did not make them easier to refuse.

Joel looked at the channel.

He looked at the water he could see.

He looked once at the blue bag, dark under the front seat, stupid and faithful only because objects did not answer when called.

Then he chose against the voices again.

He set the front into the black cut and committed to the crossing beyond it.
