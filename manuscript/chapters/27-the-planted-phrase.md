# Chapter 27 - The Planted Phrase

The planted phrase helped him first because it was stupid.

No memory hung from it. No campfire. No old argument. No dead man's way of handling fear. Blue bag under the front. A sentence small enough to fit in the hand. A fact Joel could check without wanting anything from it.

The canoe was already moving when he understood that.

He had shoved off from the gravel with both hands on the gunwales, one foot in the lake and one on stone. Cold went through his sandal and up his shin. For a second the hull tried to leave without him. The emergency paddle lay crosswise over the seat. The small pack slid toward his knees. The torn map margin under the thwart cord lifted and slapped once, then settled. He got in badly, weight too far right, and the canoe rolled under him with the quick offended motion of a boat that still remembered two men.

He did not swear. Swearing was a gift. Breath was nearly a gift.

He sat low and waited for the wobble to pass. The landing went dark behind him. He did not turn to measure it.

The first time the thing used the phrase, Alex said it from the left shore.

"Blue bag under the front."

Teasing. Almost amused. As if the bag had done something funny and Alex had found the smallest possible way to make Joel look.

Joel did not look left.

He looked at the bag.

It sat under the front seat, blue dulled by mud and last light, its rolled mouth buckled twice, one corner creased where the aluminum tube of the emergency paddle had pinned it all day. Not rescue. Only a fact. The voice had moved. The object had not.

That was enough.

Enough had become a narrower word. It no longer meant food for the day, daylight for a portage, proof for an explanation, or a route a man could defend on a map. It meant one fact holding for one breath. The bag was there. Alex was not. The sentence had only moved.

Wind moved along the lake in black wrinkles. The right-hand reach opened ahead, darker than the shore but less dark than the alder-choked left channel where Alex's voice had placed itself. The left looked like a mistake in the shoreline, a reed seam no tired man would choose unless the map forced him to look again. The map showed a north-running lake, then a pinch, then water beyond. The map had been right in shapes and wrong in mercy. A blue line could be true and still let men die.

Joel set the short paddle on the right side and pulled.

The canoe answered late.

Everything about it was wrong. Too little weight ahead, too much human weight behind, no second paddle correcting small mistakes before they spread. With Alex in front, the boat had argued but still had another body reading water. With Will, after Alex, it had been overloaded, miserable, and alive with disagreement. One man in the back made the canoe ride light and foolish ahead of him. Each stroke swung the empty front wider than he wanted. Each correction spent strength he did not have.

He tried kneeling and could not hold it. His left knee had swollen tight under wet fabric. His right had gone deep and hot where he had dropped against a root with the canoe on him. Sitting raised him too high; kneeling gave control and then took it back as pain. He settled into the ugly compromise survival offered: one knee down, one foot braced, hips twisted, back already failing.

It was bad paddling.

It moved him.

Alex laughed once from the left shore.

The sound ran along the reeds, touched open water, and came back thinner. Joel saw, without asking to, Alex leaning over the rail at North Road Bridge on the first morning, pleased with his own echo, not knowing he had given the woods a rehearsal. The memory arrived bright and useless. Joel let it pass without giving it his mouth.

The second use came ten strokes later.

Will said it from behind him.

"Blue bag under the front."

Not amused now. Technical. Urgent. Will made the phrase into an instruction Joel had failed to obey correctly. Joel could hear the next sentence without hearing it: no, no, no, you're shifting the trim, that's not how that works.

His shoulders tightened. The front wandered. The left side dipped toward a small slap of chop.

He corrected too hard. The paddle blade fluttered. Aluminum flexed at the joint. The cheap collar gave a dry click.

From behind him, the click answered.

He wanted Will there with a fury so sudden it felt physical. He wanted the correction. He wanted the fight. He wanted Will in the front, cold and furious and alive, turning Joel's steering into a lecture on leverage and drag. The wanting came before thought and nearly became speech.

He pressed his tongue hard against the roof of his mouth.

The boat steadied by inches.

He could not write while moving. The old rules had to hold without the page: NO NAMES. NO ANSWERS. ONLY SEEN IS REAL. SOUND IS BAIT. GIVE IT ONE THING.

He had given it one thing. It had not become a map. It had not become a shield. It was only a mark on the water, a way to see where sound wanted him.

Will would have hated how little that proved. No constant held. The light changed. The lake changed. Joel changed by the minute.

That weakness kept the test in its place.

The lake narrowed by degrees. This was not yet the final crossing. It was the approach the map had drawn with one careless blue gesture: last water, pinch, outlet, then the blacker lake beyond. Low reeds showed in clumps to the left. The right shore lifted into black spruce and small granite shelves, sliding past too slowly. Dusk thickened under the cloud. The water stopped reflecting sky and began holding it in broken, darker strips. Every paddle stroke cut the surface and then vanished.

Joel tried counting strokes.

Twenty on the right.

Fifteen on the left, because the left shoulder had less in it.

Switch before the front wandered. Short stroke. Close to the boat. Do not listen for meaning.

The count failed almost at once. The numbers became men. Four had left the motel. Three had left Second Camp. Two had left the cabin. One had left Will on the portage and carried the boat to water. One could not keep a canoe straight.

He stopped counting.

Something knocked from the right shore. Paddle on gunwale.

Not a voice. Older than the voices. A trip sound. A boat sound. A sound from before the woods had learned grammar.

Joel checked the blue bag.

It sat under the front seat, visible and ordinary.

The knock came again from the right. Once, then twice, the rhythm of a man tapping for attention because shouting across water used to be rude before it became dangerous.

Joel paddled left.

Not because left was safe. Because the sound wanted his right side. Because the right shore, which should have been only spruce and stone, had chosen the language of men and gear.

Wind took the front and pushed it back. He leaned forward to fight it, and the Highlander key pressed cold against his breastbone.

He touched the pocket with the heel of his free hand. Metal under cloth. The planned exit reduced to that cold tooth: Mike's key, Mike's Highlander, Mike's confidence torn out of a dead pocket and carried by the man who had learned, too late, not to believe confidence just because it spoke clearly.

The key had no voice. That was its mercy. It did not clear its throat. It did not promise the road, the battery, the bridge, or help. It only pressed into him. If he drowned, it would sink. If he reached the road, it might open a door.

Ahead, Mike cleared his throat.

Joel kept paddling.

The throat-clear came again from the right-hand reach, then from farther ahead, exactly where the lake seemed to open.

"Blue bag under the front," Mike said.

Managerial. Certain. The phrase became inventory, became route logic, became one item checked before the next instruction. It belonged in Mike's mouth more easily than Joel liked. Mike had been good at making objects into control. Boat. Pack. Key. Map. Cabin. Even dead, his voice tried to put the bag into a system Joel could obey.

Joel almost did.

The right-hand reach looked broader. Wind ran there, and running wind meant length, depth, main water. The left looked broken and brushy, hidden behind reeds and a low point that might conceal only a pocket of dead water. A living Mike would have gone right unless the map made him stop. A living Will would have argued until the argument became the choice. Alex would have asked if this counted as team-building.

Joel held the paddle across his knees and let the canoe glide.

He made himself look past the voice.

The right-hand reach held open water, but its far end rose dark against the surface. No break in the tree line. No lower seam where water passed out. Wind made it look like a way because wind preferred open length. The left channel looked worse because reeds masked it, but beyond the reed point he could see a darker cut, a narrow absence between banks.

He looked at the map.

The page was nearly useless in the failing light. Water had swollen it under the plastic. Ink blurred at old folds. Still, the north end narrowed before opening again. The pinch could hide behind reeds. The broader right-hand reach could be a bay that map scale had made cruel.

Mike's voice came from the right.

"It opens up after this."

Joel closed his eyes for one breath, then opened them because closed eyes gave sound too much room.

The planted phrase had not revealed the correct direction. It had shown only the pressure: where sound wanted his body before his eyes finished their work.

That was the last temptation in Mike's voice. Not rescue. Not comfort. Efficiency. Go where water looked open. Trust the man who had always been willing to decide.

Joel worked the canoe left of center, not into the reeds but toward the cut beyond them.

The wind dropped near full dark.

It should have helped. Instead the silence grew large enough to hold every voice the thing had learned. The small sounds of the boat separated and became suspect: water ticking from the paddle, hull rubbing a floating stem, breath catching when the blade found mud instead of depth, the collar of the emergency paddle creaking under his lower hand.

Then the voices came at intervals.

Mike's throat-clear.

Will's cough.

Alex's laugh.

Mike saying, "We're exactly where we ought to be."

Joel's own voice saying, "Wait. Just wait."

That one nearly turned him.

It was not a command. It was restraint. His restraint, careful and tired and alive, alone in the back of a canoe trying not to become material for something else.

He let the paddle rest half a second too long. The canoe lost its line and slid toward the reed point. A stem touched the hull with a dry scrape.

From the reeds, Joel's voice said, "Wait."

Only the one word.

Less was worse. Less sounded more like him. He had never been a man of big speeches. He had given the woods so little that it could make a meal from crumbs.

For a moment he wondered whether the thing had enough of him to imitate silence. Not absence. Silence. The pause before answering. The way he let other men spend themselves first. The way restraint had once seemed like patience and had become, under pressure, a kind of coldness.

He did not answer. He did not wait.

He planted the paddle and shoved away from the reeds. The blade struck bottom. Mud took it and held. For one bad second the paddle would not come free. The canoe kept moving. The shaft bent. He saw himself losing it, saw the emergency paddle sliding out of his hands into black water, saw the remaining route become not tragic, not meaningful, only stupid.

He twisted and pulled straight up. The blade came loose with a wet pop.

The same pop sounded behind him.

Then to the right.

Then under the canoe.

Joel froze with the paddle across his lap.

Under the canoe was not a direction. Under the canoe was a thought his body had before he could stop it: something pale beneath him, close, waiting for the shape of a man to lean over the side.

He looked down only as far as the blue bag.

It was still there. The bag. The pack behind him. His knees against the ribs. The dull gleam of water inside the hull, where spray had pooled and carried a spruce needle back and forth with each small rock of the boat. Facts close enough to see.

The planted phrase came again from open water to his right, in Will's voice.

"Blue bag under the front."

There could be no speaker there. No shore. No body. Just dark water and a few pale chips of reflected cloud.

Sound did not have to stand where a body would stand. Knowing that did not change the rule. It sharpened it.

Sight had never meant safety. It meant only that sound alone could not vote.

He thought of the notebook under his jacket, wet pages swelling against his ribs. All week he had been making law out of panic. Rules gave panic edges. They did not make the lake shallow or the thing stupid. They only slowed the body's first obedience.

He kept the front pointed toward the black cut where the map promised an outlet.

The cut did not resolve quickly. It lengthened and shortened with the angle of approach, visible between reed stalks, then gone behind the shoulder of the point. The water shallowed. Twice the paddle struck submerged stems. Once the hull slid over something soft that gave under it, not rock, not wood, and Joel's back tightened. The canoe slowed as if a hand had taken the back rope.

He turned enough to check behind him.

No hand. No rope. Only the small wake opening in a V, black on black, already closing.

From the shore, Alex said, "Hold up."

The old reflex went through Joel's hands. Hold up meant obstacle, pack strap, sandal caught, a body asking for ten seconds without having to confess the need. It had been useful before it was fatal. He had answered it for days.

He did not hold up.

He paddled.

The left shore pressed closer. Alder leaned over the water, black stems combing the surface. The canoe entered the smell of mud, cold vegetation, and sour reed rot. Mosquitoes lifted from the bank in a thin late swarm and found his face. He let them. Swatting was sound. Swatting was a hand off the paddle. One landed at the corner of his eye and drank there while irritation blurred the cut ahead.

He blinked it clear.

The bag showed less in the dark now. Not blue so much as a remembered blue, a different darkness under the front seat. Joel reached forward with the paddle shaft and touched it once. The plastic crinkled.

From the bank, the crinkle answered.

"Blue bag under the front," Alex said again.

This time Alex sounded bored.

That was almost worse than teasing. The phrase was already wearing out. A familiar mouth mattered more than meaning.

Joel knew then the tactic would not last.

It had bought him one direction. Maybe two. It would not carry him to the bridge. It would not make the thing defeat itself.

The limit steadied something small and hard in him. He did not need a solution. He needed the next hundred feet, then the next.

The cut opened.

Not wide. Not welcoming. A seam of darker water between reeds, with black spruce crowding both sides beyond. A little current touched the front and changed the feel of the boat. Not much. Enough that the water seemed to have a preference. Enough that the lake's flat holding became a pull.

Joel let the front take it, then checked the map one last time.

He had to use the headlamp.

Light made him visible in a way darkness did not. It also made the near world honest for as far as it reached. The lamp sat in the small pack behind him. Getting it meant twisting, risking the boat, making zipper sound, taking one hand off the paddle while current nosed him toward reeds.

He waited until the canoe drifted into a small lee behind the reed point, then set the paddle across both gunwales. He turned slowly. His spine cramped. The boat tipped and steadied. The pack had shifted. He pulled it forward by one strap. The zipper was gritty and loud.

From three places at once, zippers answered.

Tent zipper. Gear shelter zipper. Pack zipper. The old sound of people opening and closing little human weather.

Joel found the headlamp by touch, closed the pack without zipping it, and settled back before the canoe could swing broadside. His hands shook too much to fix the strap around his head. He held the lamp against his chest, cupped the beam with his fingers, and clicked it on.

Weak yellow light leaked between his knuckles.

The map appeared in pieces: blue lake, black line, wet wrinkle, dirty thumb, the written phrase on the torn margin. BLUE BAG UNDER THE FRONT. The pencil letters had softened at the edges. They looked older than they were, as if writing aged faster here.

The line matched enough. Lake into channel. Channel bending north and east before the next water. North Road Bridge beyond. Road beyond. Highlander beyond, unless the road had washed out, unless the battery had died, unless the key was only one more object that promised more than it could give.

Enough.

He clicked the lamp off.

Dark returned thicker for having been cut.

The headlamp click kept ringing after the light was gone. Plastic against nail. A cheap battery contact closing. The kind of sound a man made in a tent without thinking, half inside sleep, opening a little private circle of yellow.

The answer came from the right bank.

Then behind him.

Then, too quietly, from the pack at his feet, though the lamp lay visible and dark between his knees.

He did not touch it again.

Mike said from ahead, very softly, "Just keep moving."

For once the dead voice gave useful advice. Joel hated that and obeyed the water, not the sound.

He stowed the lamp loose between his knees and took up the paddle. Current gathered the canoe by inches. He gave two short strokes to keep the front off the reeds.

Something rose in the channel ahead.

Low. Pale. A broken piece of reflected sky, except it bent once against the current. Not an animal he could name in the time he had. Not a log. Not a man, because a man would have made the water tell a different story. It broke the surface without a splash and slipped under before the canoe reached it.

The lake closed over with barely a ripple.

Joel's arms stopped.

The canoe did not.

It moved toward the place where the shape had gone under, current and last momentum carrying him, reed shadows sliding on both sides. The paddle hung half raised in his hands. If he dug too hard, he might turn broadside. If he stopped, he would drift over it anyway. If he looked for it, the dark water would become all the places it was not.

From the right bank, Will said, "There's a difference."

Joel put the paddle in.

Small stroke. Close to the boat.

Another.

Another.

The hull passed over the place where the pale thing had been. Nothing struck. Nothing touched. Water made its ordinary small talk along the ribs. His breath sawed in and out through his nose because his mouth was closed and would stay closed.

His body wanted proof after the danger passed. It wanted to look back, lean over, shine the weak lamp into the water and make the pale thing enter one of the old categories: log, fish, reflection, animal. Explanation was another appetite. He kept paddling.

Behind him, in Mike's voice: "We're exactly where we ought to be."

Ahead, in Joel's voice: "Wait. Just wait."

To the left, in Alex's: "You coming or writing a poem?"

The voices no longer tried to be plausible. They tried to be many. They made old friendship into a net.

There were other strands the thing did not have, or did not use. Mike's hand on a shoulder after a bad landing, brief enough not to embarrass them both. Will going quiet when he was truly hurt, all correction drained out of him. Alex doing useful work in silence and then ruining the kindness later by making a joke of it. Those things had not been sound, or had not been useful sound. The dead were smaller in the thing's mouth than they had been in life. That did not make them easier to refuse.

Joel looked at the channel.

He looked at the water he could see.

He looked once at the blue bag, dark under the front seat.

Then he chose against the voices again.

He set the front of the canoe into the black cut and committed to the channel, and to whatever crossing waited beyond it.
