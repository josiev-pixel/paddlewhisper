# Chapter 14 - The Old Map

In the morning Mike spread the old map on a wet rock and put stones on the corners.

The rock was not flat enough. Nothing there was. The lake had dropped in the night or seemed to have, leaving a black line on the stones where water had held and withdrawn. The canoes lay higher than the mark, pale and cold under spruce shadow. The tarp sagged over the root shelf. Will's sleeping bag hung like an animal skin no one had finished cleaning. Every object they owned looked borrowed from a worse version of the same trip.

Joel stood with his arms folded against his chest and watched water gather in the creases of the map case. The plastic was clouded from old scratches. Under it the printed lakes and portages held their clean colors, blue and green and brown arranged with confidence. Little broken lines crossed land as if land had agreed to be crossed. A square sat inland from the portage they had missed the day before, small enough to be nothing, exact enough to become hope.

Maintenance cabin, the old legend said.

The print was thin at the edges. A previous fold ran directly through the word cabin, making the letters step over a white break.

Mike had found the map before coffee. He had not slept, or had slept only in dropped pieces. His eyes were dark under his cap. The stubble along his jaw had come in gray and uneven. He held himself with the hard attention of a man who had woken before doubt could get up with him.

"Here," he said.

Will did not bend closer. He stood above the rock with both hands tucked under his armpits, one palm bandaged from a small cut the night before where he had caught himself against granite. The bandage had already loosened at one edge. Damp made every repair temporary.

Joel could see the old route as Mike saw it. Emergency Camp below them at the wrong shore. The missed portage north and west along the lake, reachable if the wind stayed down and the repaired canoe behaved and no sound pulled them apart at the landing. The cabin inland from that portage, connected by a faint black line to something marked as an old fire road. Past that, perhaps a road a truck could use. Perhaps a ditch. Perhaps a memory of a road. Perhaps nothing.

Paper was good at perhaps. It held all possibilities in the same ink.

"We leave the boats cached," Mike said. "Take what we can carry for two days. Repair kit, first aid, filter, stove, one pot, food. Sleeping bags. Tarp. Map. Compass. If the cabin has anything useful, we use it. If there is a road, we follow it. If there isn't, we come back."

"That's a lot of if," Will said.

"Everything is if."

"No." Will leaned down then, because he could not resist the map even when he hated it. "Some things are distances. Some things are contours. Some things are false hope printed in black by a guy in an office thirty years ago."

Mike's throat moved. He did not clear it.

Joel noticed the restraint because the habit had become visible by its absence. A day earlier he might not have known what Mike was stopping. Now the unsounded thing seemed to stand between them with the rest of the sounds they were trying not to give away.

"You want to paddle big water with a cracked boat and voices?" Mike asked.

Will looked toward the lake.

The lake had eased in the night but not forgiven them. Small wind marks ran over it from the middle out, darkening and smoothing by turns. The far shore had no scale. Trees lined up there in black ranks, all of them looking close until Joel imagined crossing and understood again how distance hid itself on water. The voice last night had not needed a body to cross that distance. Sound carried where men could not.

"No," Will said.

Mike waited.

"I don't want to walk into wet brush because of a square," Will said. "Those are different categories of stupid."

"Great. Pick a category."

Will's mouth tightened. He had slept in the least wet bag and looked worse for it, as if warmth had only given his body more room to shake. "The map says there was a cabin."

"Map says there's a cabin."

"You hear the difference?"

"I hear you making every sentence longer."

Joel crouched beside the rock. The old map had been opened and folded so often the route wore a softness like cloth. He touched the plastic above the square, then drew his finger along the faint line toward the fire road. Under the case, the line looked deliberate. On the ground it would be alder, blowdown, wet leaves, rotten corduroy maybe, old tire ruts full of black water. A human mark did not mean human presence. It meant only that at some time, somebody had wanted the same thing they wanted now: a way through.

"If the road is gone?" Joel asked.

Mike looked at him with a flash of relief. A practical question was a handhold.

"Then we turn around before dark."

"Before dark," Will said.

"Yes."

"We have become excellent at arriving places before dark."

Mike folded his lips in, held them there, and let the line pass. That was new too. He did not have energy to punish every challenge. Or he had begun to understand that every argument became inventory for whatever listened.

Joel took the notebook from the plastic bag. Its pages had dried in warped ridges near the spine. He turned past the columns from the night before. Alex. Mike. Will. Camp. Joel. The names made a poor shelter. They had held facts for a few hours and nothing more.

On a clean page he wrote:

CABIN PLAN - DAYLIGHT ONLY.

ALL THREE.

NO SCOUTING.

Mike watched the pencil. "Add return by dusk."

Joel wrote:

TURN BACK IF NO CABIN / NO TRACE BY MIDDAY.

Will made a small sound.

Joel looked up.

"Midday is optimistic," Will said.

"Then say it with the pencil."

Will took the notebook. He held the pencil awkwardly in his bandaged hand, then shifted it to the other and wrote in block letters:

OLD MAP MAY BE WRONG.

He pushed the notebook back.

Mike read it. "That's useful."

"It is."

"It's just pessimism with handwriting."

"No, it's a constraint."

Joel wrote beneath it:

ASSUME WRONG UNTIL SEEN.

That quieted them for a moment. It was the rule they had been making without wanting to admit it applied to more than voices. Assume wrong until seen. A friend's voice. A latch. A boat knock. A square on a map. A line through trees. The world had become a thing that needed witnesses.

Mike looked away first. "Pack."

The word had enough command in it to organize the next hour.

They made coffee because coffee was a normal human act and because hands needed heat. The stove click made all three of them glance down before flame came. Will pointed at it with two fingers, the small ceremony from the night before repeated without speech. Seen. The blue flame flattened in the lake wind, then steadied when Joel set his pack as a shield. They drank from metal cups that tasted faintly of old rice. No one wanted food, so they ate bars and the last of a bag of trail mix by count, three shares laid on the map rock with one bright raisin rolling toward the edge.

Joel almost stopped it with Alex's name in his mouth.

He caught the raisin instead and put it on Will's pile.

Will saw. His face changed and changed back. He ate it without comment.

Packing for a possible exit was harder than packing for a route. A route allowed redundancy. You carried extra socks, extra line, an extra headlamp, a book you thought you might read on a point in evening light. Escape made every object argue for itself. The first aid kit mattered. The repair kit mattered if they came back to boats. The stove mattered if they were out overnight and wet. Food mattered. Sleeping bags mattered, though Will's was still damp at the foot and would add weight without granting much mercy. The tarp mattered. The tent body did not. A spare paddle did not, unless the road failed and the boats waited and the cracked paddle gave up. A fishing rod had no argument left in it.

Will held his rod tube for a long time, then set it under the gear shelter.

"Don't say anything," he said.

Mike lifted both hands.

"I mean it."

"I didn't say anything."

"Your face said a short essay."

"My face is conserving language."

Will stared at him, then gave one dry breath. Not a laugh. A failed start at one.

The small almost-laugh frightened everyone less than the real one would have. Joel felt the old friendship move under the fear like water under late ice. Still there. Still dangerous to trust.

They sorted the food barrel into a smaller dry bag and two side bags. Mike wanted to take more than they could carry and less than they might need. Will wanted the bear line, not because he believed in proper storage anymore but because abandoning ordinary precautions felt like inviting every kind of failure at once. Joel wanted weight low and simple. The argument had no clean winner. They took two days of food, the line, and the barrel lid's smell on everything.

The food barrel latch snapped when Mike closed it for the last time.

All three men looked toward the hanging barrel in the trees.

It hung open.

The sound had come from Mike's hands. They had seen it. Still, Will's shoulders rose to his ears.

"Mine," Mike said.

"Seen," Joel said.

Will nodded too fast.

They cached what they could not carry under spruce boughs and the low fly of the failed gear shelter. Cache was too grand a word for a pile of wet equipment hidden from nothing that wanted it and very little that understood it. Joel packed the tents in one dry bag, the rod tube, the spare cookware, extra clothes too wet to justify their weight, Alex's remaining odds and ends sealed inside the cracked headlamp bag because no one could decide who should carry them. He put Alex's hot sauce bottle there too. It had rolled out from a side pocket when Mike shook out a pack, red plastic bright against the moss.

Will looked at it. "He would haunt us for leaving that."

No one answered. They were already being haunted by less.

Joel slipped the bottle into the bag. The cap made a tiny click against the headlamp case. He saw it. He wished seeing helped with memory.

Down by the shore, they worked on the canoes. The damaged one went first, dragged higher above the waterline with all three of them on the gunwales. The repaired thwart creaked under its own accusation. Mike flinched at the sound, then set his jaw and lifted again.

"Up here," he said.

"That's visible from the water," Will said.

"Good. We need to find it again."

"Also visible to anything with eyes."

"Have you seen eyes?"

Will looked at him. "Is that the standard now?"

Mike did clear his throat then, once, quiet and real.

Joel hated how his body reacted to it. Every muscle around his ribs tightened before thought arrived. Mike was three feet away. His mouth had made the sound. His hand was on the canoe. The sound belonged to him and still did not feel safe, because belonging had become a thing that could be counterfeited.

"We need them not to float away," Mike said. "We need to find them if we come back. We don't need to build a museum exhibit."

"I'm just saying."

"I know. You are always just saying."

The old rhythm rose and failed. Alex should have cut in there. He would have given Will a title, made Mike defend the stupidest possible interpretation of his own words, turned the canoe cache into a bit about survival influencers with discount hatchets. Joel could hear the shape of it without sound. That was grief's cruelty: it made an imitation inside you and called it memory.

They pulled spruce boughs over the hull. The green needles left resin on Joel's fingers. He tied the painter to a black spruce trunk, then retied it higher when he saw the old water mark on the bark. The knot came out ugly. He made himself redo it. Fatigue wanted him to accept almost anything. The woods had already accepted too much from them.

The second canoe they carried farther into brush and turned upside down between two downed logs. It was the better boat, and hiding it felt wrong. Leaving it visible felt worse. The paddles went under it except for the cracked one, which Mike insisted they carry in case a pole or splint became useful. Joel took it because it was his damage and because the orange cord on the shaft made it easy to track by sight.

Mike kept his PFD on.

Will watched him buckle it over his rain jacket. "We are not walking through a lake."

"Pockets."

"You have pockets on your actual body."

"Key's in this one."

Mike touched the zip pocket on the left side of the PFD. The gesture was quick but not quick enough to hide. The Highlander key. The staged exit. North Road Bridge sitting somewhere beyond all this with Mike's old vehicle waiting beside a road that still belonged to the human world.

"Put it in the notebook," Mike said.

Joel looked at him.

"If something happens," Mike said.

For a second no one moved.

That was the first time Mike had allowed the sentence to reach that shape. Not if we get delayed. Not if the cabin is nothing. If something happens. The plainness of it stripped the morning colder.

Joel opened the notebook and wrote:

HIGHLANDER KEY - MIKE PFD LEFT ZIP.

He showed the page to both of them.

Will said, "Maybe one of us should carry it."

Mike's hand stayed over the pocket. "No."

"That is not a plan, it's ownership."

"It's my car."

"It's our exit."

Mike turned on him. "You want to do this now?"

Will's face went hard, then tired. "No."

Joel did not say that Will was right. He did not say Mike was not wrong. The key was a practical object and a symbol and a future problem. Saying that aloud would only make all three versions heavier. He wrote instead:

KEY LOCATION KNOWN.

Mike read it and nodded once, as if the page had ruled in his favor.

They took one more pass through camp. Put out what had never been a fire. Tighten the gear tarp. Hang the remaining food barrel high enough to satisfy ritual. Mark the canoe cache on the map by sight: bent cedar, split granite face, three dead spruce in a row like black fingers. Joel sketched the shape in the notebook because paper could remember without making sound.

The emergency camp looked smaller once packed. Not safer. Just reduced. The root shelf, the bad hammock trees, the stones where the stove had sat, Will's wet footprints pressed dark into the duff. By evening, if rain came, much of it would disappear. By another week the site would become only disturbed moss and a few crushed ferns. The woods did not need malice to erase them. Erasure was one of its ordinary skills.

Will stood at the edge of the shelf, looking uphill. "Where does the trace actually start?"

Mike glanced at the map, then at the slope. "Old landing path. Should cut above the inlet, then bend toward the portage."

"Should."

"Yes, Will. Should. As in, not guaranteed by God."

Will's mouth opened. Joel thought he might make some church joke because old reflexes chose bad moments. Then Will closed it.

Joel was grateful and sorry at once. He and Will had grown up in rooms where men spoke certainty from pulpits and boys learned to say amen before they understood the question. Joel had spent half his adult life removing that tone from his own head. Mike had a different version of it, trail-made and brash instead of holy, but it worked the same way under pressure: say the thing firmly enough and maybe the world would line up beneath it.

The world did not line up. It lay wet and irregular above them.

They climbed.

The first stretch was not a trail but a direction. Spruce branches slapped water against their sleeves. The ground rose in slick shelves of granite broken by moss. Packs caught on dead limbs. Twice Mike pointed with his whole hand and the others followed because none of them had anything better. Joel kept the lake behind him when he could see it, a gray glint through trunks. When he could not, he trusted the slope. Trust was too strong a word. He used the slope.

No one spoke.

Silence made simple movement clumsy. A branch whipped back because Mike forgot to hold it. Will stopped too abruptly and Joel nearly stepped on the backs of his sandals. Joel wanted to say sorry and did not. Will lifted his hand without turning, two fingers bent back. Fine. Or stop. Or I know. They were inventing a language under duress, and like all inventions made in fear, it broke often.

After ten minutes they found the old path.

It appeared all at once and then, under looking, became almost nothing: a flattening between young spruce, grass in the center, alder leaning inward from both sides. Along one edge a rotten cut stump wore moss over the saw face. Another stood beyond it at the same height. Human repetition. Not enough to save them, but enough to be followed.

Mike stopped at the entrance and looked back.

There it was, the thing he wanted: proof. Not rescue. Not yet. But a mark on paper answering a mark on the ground. For a moment his face almost softened.

Will saw it too. He could have taken it from him. The correction was available. A path was not a cabin. A trace was not a road. An old cut stump was not a promise. Joel could see each sentence arranging itself behind Will's eyes.

Will looked down instead and adjusted the strap on his pack.

Mike said, quietly, "Okay."

The word did not belong to certainty. It belonged to relief.

Joel wrote in the notebook while standing:

TRACE REAL.

Then, after a pause:

NOT PROOF OF CABIN.

He showed it to Will first.

Will nodded.

He showed it to Mike.

Mike read it and looked away, but he did not argue.

They entered single file: Mike first, then Will, then Joel. That order was Mike's without being discussed. He wanted the front because the front gave him decisions. Will in the middle because someone needed to watch him and because Joel, behind, could see both living men at once. Joel did not know if Mike had thought of that part. He hoped not. He wanted one good decision from the day that had not come from control.

The trace held for fifty yards. Grass dragged cold across Joel's calves above his sandals. Mud took the prints of all three men and filled them slowly with brown water. Once a raven knocked loose from a tree ahead and beat away through branches, black wings loud in the damp air. All three stopped. All three saw it. The bird went on being a bird.

Mike lifted one hand without turning. Keep moving.

They did.

The woods were different away from water. At the lake every sound had space to reveal itself, even when it lied about distance. Here sound came already handled. Branches broke close but unseen. Drops fell from high needles and struck leaves at ankle height. Their own packs produced small betrayals: buckle tick, nylon brush, the cracked paddle knocking once against Joel's water bottle until he caught it with his hand.

The trace bent left around a blowdown and narrowed. Will put one hand back, palm low. Joel stopped. Mike stopped because Will had stopped, and the three of them stood in a line among alder with their breathing loud and useless.

From the brush ahead came Mike's throat clear.

Not behind Joel. Not from the living man ten yards ahead, turned half-profile in a gap between branches. Ahead of him. Beyond him. The same small preparation of the throat before instruction, before reassurance, before a plan. Familiar as the first click of a stove.

The living Mike did not move.

Joel saw the back of his neck go pale.

Will turned his head by inches until Joel could see one eye behind the smeared lens of his glasses. Question, fear, the old terrible wish to ask if the others had heard.

Joel shook his head once.

No.

The woods waited with them.

Then Mike's voice came from the brush beyond Mike, close enough to seem useful.

"Just keep moving."

The sentence entered the old trace and took up space there. It had Mike's impatience, his route confidence, the clipped end that turned suggestion into order. It was not loud. It did not need to be. It sounded exactly like the thing a competent man would say when the others froze too long.

Mike's real hand lowered slowly to his side.

Joel could see him wanting to answer himself. Anger moved through his shoulders. Shame too, maybe. There was something obscene in having your authority borrowed while you were still alive to hear it.

Will's lips parted.

Joel stepped forward and put two fingers against the back of Will's pack.

Will closed his mouth.

For a while nothing happened. Rainwater ticked from alder leaves. A mosquito worked near Joel's ear and he let it whine because swatting made sound and because some part of him now believed even irritation could be learned. The false voice did not repeat. That restraint made it worse. Repetition would have been a machine showing itself. One use felt chosen, though Joel knew want and choice were words they had no right to spend.

Mike turned at last. His eyes moved from Will to Joel, then down to the notebook in Joel's chest pocket.

Joel took it out. His hands shook enough that the pencil point broke on the first stroke. He turned it and wrote with the splintered wood:

MIKE THROAT CLEAR - AHEAD OF MIKE.

MIKE VOICE - "JUST KEEP MOVING" - NOT MIKE.

He added:

WE SAW MIKE.

Mike read the page. His jaw worked.

Will pointed to the line, then to Mike, then held both hands apart as if measuring a distance. Maybe asking how far. Maybe saying too close. The new language failed him there. He wanted words with edges.

Joel wrote:

NO ANSWER.

Will nodded once, hard.

Mike kept looking at the sentence that denied him. Not Mike. Joel expected him to object, not because it was wrong but because seeing it written made the theft official. Instead Mike touched the PFD pocket where the key sat and turned back toward the brush.

He did not say the thing again. He did not say anything.

He lifted one hand, palm open this time, not command exactly. Signal. Then he stepped forward into the trace because standing still had its own dangers and because the map's square was still ahead and because every choice left something hungry behind it.

Will followed.

Joel looked once over his shoulder. Through the trees he could no longer see the lake or the canoes or the bad camp they had made on the wrong shore. Only spruce, wet alder, the path closing behind them as branches settled back into place.

He put the notebook away and followed the two living bodies he could still see.
