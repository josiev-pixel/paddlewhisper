# Chapter 22 - The Channel

Morning came with frost on the tarp edge and no sleep worth naming.

Joel knew he had gone under once or twice because each time he came back the world had shifted without moving. The fog had thickened. The canoe had become a darker shape. Will had changed from one curled outline under the tarp to another, closer to the food barrel, his bad hand held up against his chest as if sleep had required him to keep protecting it. But none of it had the depth of rest. It was more like being dropped through a hole and jerked back by the rope on his wrist.

The rope was still there.

He lifted his hand slowly. The shortened painter ran from his wrist to the driftwood snag, then to the canoe's front handle. Frost had roughened the line where it crossed the gravel. The canoe sat where they had left it, half gray, half black with dew, its side dull in the light before sunrise.

For a while Joel did nothing but look at it.

The boat had not moved. That was fact. The rope had not been cut. The knot had not slipped. The food barrel stood under the tarp between them, black ring closed. Will was visible. Joel was visible to himself in pieces: hand, sleeve, wet pant leg stiff with cold, the dark crescents under his nails from mud he had stopped noticing.

Sight first. Then thought.

Across the channel, something small moved in the alder and stopped.

Joel did not turn his head fast. That had become one of the new rules too, though they had not written it. Fast movement made the body choose before the eyes had finished. He moved only his eyes, then his chin, and saw nothing but alder stems and the pale thread of mist between them.

Will opened his eyes.

Joel put two fingers to his own eyes and pointed at him.

Will nodded once. Visible.

They ate cold food from the bag. Tortillas gone stiff at the edges. Peanut butter too cold to spread, squeezed straight from the pouch onto whatever could hold it. A handful of trail mix passed from Joel to Will, then back when Will could not get the plastic open cleanly with his swollen hand.

Will hated that. Joel saw it in the set of his mouth.

Will accepted the opened packet anyway.

The old Will would have made a remark about assistive technology, or about how the human hand was an overrated interface, or about Mike buying medical tape that had the adhesive integrity of wet lasagna. Any one of those would have irritated Joel. Any one of them would have made the morning human. Instead Will pinched the packet between two fingers and ate without looking up.

The river steamed where first light touched it. Not a true river here, not enough width or authority for that, but a channel under alder, water moving because somewhere upstream more water insisted. It slid black and slow around the gravel bar, carrying leaves, foam, a sliver of bark turned pale-side up. The fog came off it in low torn strips. Where the sun found the surface, the mist rose and vanished. Where the spruce leaned over, it stayed close to the water.

Joel pulled the notebook from inside his jacket.

The pages had swollen. Corners curled. Pencil marks from the night before had blurred where damp had gotten in, but the words still held their blunt shapes.

WE HAVE SIGHT.

WE HAVE BOAT.

WE HAVE EACH OTHER IF WE DON'T GIVE THAT AWAY.

Will watched him read it. His face was drawn so tight that his eyes looked too large.

He reached for the pencil.

Joel hesitated, then gave it to him.

Will wrote slowly:

IF I AM RIGHT, WRONG CONTEXT EXPOSES IT.

The sentence sat there, technical and miserable. Will's handwriting had always leaned forward as if impatient with the hand that made it. Now it cramped into block letters. The pencil shook at the end of context and tore a small dark fleck from the page.

Joel read the line twice.

He wrote:

SURVIVAL EXPOSES IT ENOUGH.

Will's eyes moved over the words. He did not answer on paper.

That was answer enough.

They packed without speech. The tarp came down stiff and wet. Each fold made small crisp sounds where frost had settled along the edge. Joel hated all of them. Will held the food barrel steady while Joel tightened the strap. Joel held the canoe while Will lowered the first dry bag in with his good hand and knee. Every task took longer because both men worked as if every noise might need a witness.

The worst part was that work still required trust.

Joel had to turn his back while Will lifted the hatchet and stowed it by his knee. Will had to look away while Joel checked the key by pressing two fingers to the inside of his jacket and not opening the zipper. They had to allow one another small unobserved movements. The rules demanded sight, but no human task could survive total sight. You could not pack a boat, tie a knot, take a piss, rewrap a wound, or sleep with another man's eyes fixed on you forever.

Proof had a cost. By morning, both of them were already in debt.

At the water's edge, Will held up one finger and pointed to his hand.

Joel nodded.

They unwrapped the plastic enough to check the wound. The palm had puffed overnight. The cut looked more purple than red now, and the skin around it had the waxy look of something held too long in cold water. Will flexed his fingers. Two moved badly. He did not make a sound, but his jaw went hard and his eyes watered.

Joel wrote on the notebook cover because opening it again with wet fingers seemed foolish.

LESS FRONT PULL.

Will read it and made a face. Then he tapped his chest, pointed to the canoe, and made a paddling motion.

Me. Still paddle.

Joel shrugged once. Yes, but less.

Will seemed almost offended by the concession.

They pushed off.

The channel ahead bent through alder so tight the canoe entered it like a thought no one had room to finish. Branches leaned from both sides and touched above them in places, making a low ribbed tunnel. The water under the branches was black with leaves and tannin. Old beaver sticks lay half-sunk along the banks, stripped white at the ends. Grass mats floated loose in the eddies. Gravel showed through wherever the current thinned.

Joel sat in back, paddle ready across his knees more often than in the water. Will sat in front, shoulders rounded, his good hand doing too much. The food barrel pressed between them. The tarp, wet sleeping bags, stove, filter, first-aid kit, map case, Mike's repair kit, hatchet, spare paddle, and the last useful pieces of the trip all rode low inside the hull.

The canoe moved with the burdened reluctance of something that had been asked to become shelter, road, witness, and coffin all at once.

Joel made the first correction with a small draw. Will saw it and stopped paddling before the front hit the alder. Good. The boat slid by with only a brush of leaves along the side.

They had become quicker with gestures. Two fingers to the eyes. Flat hand. Point. Fist. A palm turned down to slow. A touch to the food barrel to mean load. A point to the rope to mean watch the painter. These were not language exactly, but they were close enough that Joel distrusted them. Anything repeated became a pattern. Anything patterned began to feel offered up.

But the woods could not see a hand sign, he thought.

Then he corrected himself.

They did not know that.

The first obstruction came before the sun cleared the spruce.

A beaver dam crossed the channel at an angle, not large, but enough to stop them. Water slid through the sticks in small glassy tongues. Mud had collected on the downstream side. On the upstream side, the level was a foot higher and flat as a held breath.

Will lifted his paddle, looked back.

Joel pointed left. There was a low place where the dam had sagged. They might shove over without unloading.

Will pointed at his hand.

Joel nodded. He knew.

They slid into the sag. The canoe's front climbed, stuck, tilted sideways. Water touched the outer lip and rocked back. Joel shifted his weight, too much, then corrected. Will braced with his good hand and hissed air through his teeth before he could stop it.

The sound was barely a sound.

It still went out.

Both men froze.

The channel held them. Water worked through the sticks below. A kingfisher rattled once downstream, then stopped as if ashamed of its own voice.

Nothing answered.

Joel tapped the hull once. Stop.

He got out first.

Cold water came over his sandals and into the legs of his pants. Mud took his feet with a soft suction. He kept one hand on the canoe and one on the dam. The sticks were slick with algae and older than they looked. One snapped under his palm with a small pistol crack.

Will closed his eyes.

Joel raised one hand. Wait.

They listened.

The alder dripped. Water worked through the dam. Somewhere ahead, not close, there was the sound of a paddle knocking once against a gunwale.

Will opened his eyes.

Joel shook his head.

No boat was visible. No person. No bright dry bag through the brush. Only the narrow thread of channel beyond the dam and fog lifting in tatters from it.

The knock came again.

Same distance. Same hollow body.

The sound was so ordinary that Joel had to fight the old impulse to place it properly. Another party. A canoe around the bend. Help. Witnesses. Men with a satellite device clipped to a shoulder strap because they were better prepared, or luckier, or simply not them.

He did not believe it.

Not believing did not stop hope from making a small room inside him and lighting it.

Will pointed ahead with the question in his face.

Joel touched two fingers to his eyes and pointed through the alder. See first.

Will nodded, but his mouth had tightened. The same argument lived in both of them now. If they ignored a real human sound, they might paddle past help. If they answered a false one, the proof might kill them.

Joel leaned his weight into the canoe.

The hull rasped over the dam. Too loud. Everything was too loud. Aluminum on wet sticks, packs shifting, the food barrel thudding once against the thwart, Will's breath catching, water pouring off the front as it tipped over. The channel took the sound and carried it ahead.

From beyond the bend, Will's voice called, "You guys hear that?"

Will was in front of Joel, visible from the back: shoulders hunched, wrapped hand held high, good hand clenched on the painter.

The false voice had his exact upward hook. Not panic. Not command. Invitation. The phrase that made other men stop and give him their ears.

Joel's body answered before his mind did. Every muscle paused toward listening.

He hated that. He hated Will for having given the thing a question so useful. He hated himself more for still wanting to answer it.

Will did not turn at first. His real body stood shin-deep in black water, still as a stump. Then, slowly, he looked back at Joel.

Joel put two fingers to his eyes and pointed at him.

Visible.

Will's face was gray. He nodded once.

The voice came again from ahead.

"You guys hear that?"

This time it sounded closer to annoyed. Still Will. Still living. Still wrong.

Will pointed to himself, then ahead.

Joel nodded. Yes. It used living voices now in full daylight. Not just grief. Not just rain and night and the mind after death. Daylight did not clean it. Visibility did not keep sound honest. It only gave the lie something to contradict.

They got the canoe over the dam. Joel climbed in with mud to his knees. Will followed awkwardly, lowering himself with his good hand and almost losing balance when the front shifted. Joel steadied the boat with his paddle. Their eyes met for one sharp second. Anger passed between them because fear had no place to go.

Then they moved.

The channel became work without space.

Gravel shallows. Beaver cuts. Grass mats. Alder stems slapping shoulders. Once the canoe caught on a submerged root and spun half-sideways before Joel corrected it with two hard strokes he should not have made so loudly. Once a drowned log rose under the hull and lifted them both as if a back had arched beneath the boat. Will turned to ask without speaking. Joel pointed down. Real. Here. Then, from the right bank, the same dull bump came out of the roots.

Will's eyes widened.

Joel shook his head and kept them moving.

He could feel the shape of Will's thought forming ahead of him. Boat sounds at boat moments. Voices at decision moments. Mike at movement, Alex at hesitation, Will at questions. Maybe that was understanding. Maybe it was only proximity and repetition. Maybe those two things were close enough to kill them.

At the next shallow they had to get out again.

The water was colder now because shade held this bend. Joel stepped into it and felt the shock climb from ankle to knee to groin. His wet waistband from the first slip had never dried; now cold found that line and widened it. He gripped the back handle and pushed. Will took the painter and pulled with his good hand.

The canoe slid six inches and stopped.

Joel pushed harder. His sandals sank deeper. Gravel shifted under mud. The boat groaned against something hidden.

Will braced and pulled.

His bad hand dropped by reflex to help. The plastic bag scraped the rope. He jerked it back with his mouth open in a silent curse.

From behind them, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will froze.

The painter went tight from his hand to the canoe. The water moved around his legs. He stood with his head turned a little, not enough to have answered, enough to have heard.

Alex said it again, farther back along the channel.

"Hold up."

Same brisk annoyance. Same practical timing. Not the big dead laugh. Not the campfire performer. A working Alex from before the bad world, the one who had called from a portage or a dam because his foot was stuck or a strap had snagged, the one who could make inconvenience into an event and still be genuinely asking for help.

Joel wanted to see him.

The wanting was immediate and physical. It arrived before belief. A body hears a friend ask for delay and prepares to delay. A body hears a dead man use a living errand and prepares to forgive the impossibility because the errand is ordinary.

Joel lifted his hand slowly, palm flat.

Not to Will. To himself.

Wait.

Then he shook his head.

Will's shoulders trembled once. He turned forward and pulled.

The canoe came loose.

They walked it another twenty yards before the water deepened. Joel climbed in first. Will handed him the painter with shaking fingers, then got in and sat low. His face had gone remote. Not calm. Removed from the expression it should have had.

Joel wanted to write something.

Not Alex.

Good.

Stay with me.

All of those were useless or dangerous or both. He put the notebook back in his jacket and paddled.

By late morning the fog had lifted. The sky showed itself in a dull lid of cloud, high enough to flatten the light but not high enough to promise weather. The channel ran north by the compass and the wet map, though in the body it ran nowhere but bend to bend. Every turn seemed to return them to some earlier version of the same alder, the same black water, the same torn mats of grass, the same low lodge half-swallowed by brush.

The map case was still gritty at the seal. Joel opened it only when they could drift in a wider reach and Will could keep them from touching either bank. The paper inside had softened at the folds. Blue water lines, portage dashes, marsh marks, old campsite dots. Human symbols holding themselves together under plastic and damp.

The channel they were on ran into a narrow lake if the map still told the truth. Beyond that, a portage tomorrow. Beyond that, water again. North Road Bridge beyond too many small decisions to count.

Joel touched the place inside his jacket where the Highlander key rested.

Will saw the motion.

He pointed at Joel's chest, then at the map, then made the flat little rectangle of a car with his hands.

Joel nodded.

Car.

The word had no sound between them and still seemed childish. A child's drawing of rescue. Four wheels, doors, heat, a road. Mike's old Highlander with its bad smell and cracked console and the little St. Louis Cardinals keychain he had mocked but never removed. Joel could see it too clearly. The dirty windshield. The map folded in the back seat. The spare sweatshirt Mike had probably left there because he left things in vehicles the way other men left messages.

Will lifted one finger and traced on the damp plastic of the map case, not marking, only moving through the film of water:

IF IT IS THERE.

Joel knew the words without seeing them.

He nodded again.

If it was there. If the bridge was there. If the road was passable. If the Highlander started. If the key still worked. If a human-made thing did not turn out like the cabin, real and useless.

The channel narrowed again before noon.

They came to a place where three threads of water separated around a low island of alder and dead grass. The left looked deeper but disappeared under brush. The middle moved fastest over stones. The right was almost still, choked with floating leaves. The map did not care about the distinction. It showed one line.

Will looked back.

Joel held up a flat hand. Wait.

The old version of this moment had been simple. Mike would have decided loudly. Will would have argued the hydrology or the map scale. Alex would have complained that all three choices looked like a place to ruin shoes. Joel would have picked the least bad water if asked, or followed the boat ahead and adjusted.

Now the decision sat in silence.

Will pointed left, then lifted his hand palm up.

Joel studied it. The left channel had depth but no sightline. Alder leaned over it so low the canoe would have to be ducked through blind. The middle had sound. Water quick over rocks, too shallow maybe, but visible through most of the run. The right looked like a trap for the hull.

Joel pointed middle.

Will shook his head, pointed to the rocks, then to the loaded canoe.

Bad boat.

Joel pointed to his eyes, then middle.

Visible water.

Will looked at him for a long moment. His expression had changed from disagreement into something more private. Resentment, yes, but not only that. Joel knew the look because he had worn a version of it at Mike: the route had narrowed until one man's fear could call itself judgment.

Joel took the notebook.

He wrote:

MIDDLE IS VISIBLE. LEFT MAY BE BETTER BUT BLIND.

Will read it.

He wrote:

THAT IS NOT A RULE. THAT IS A PREFERENCE.

Joel stared at the sentence. It was so Will that for one terrible second he almost heard it aloud. There is a difference. Preference. Rule. Evidence. Assumption. The old machinery of him still turning under frost and grief.

Joel wrote:

YES.

Will's face tightened.

Joel added:

I CHOOSE VISIBLE.

Will did not like it. He nodded anyway.

The middle run scraped them twice and almost pinned them once. Joel had to get out and ease the back around a stone while Will held the painter from the front. Water climbed over Joel's knees. His legs went numb in patches. The canoe made a thin shriek against rock that seemed to travel downstream and wait there for them.

At the end of the run, Mike cleared his throat from the left channel they had not taken.

The sound came from under the alder canopy, exactly where a man might have gone if he had chosen better water and wanted the others to follow. It was Mike's throat-clear before practical certainty. Not grief. Not panic. Management.

Will flinched so hard the painter jerked in his hand.

Mike said, "Just keep moving."

The phrase moved through the brush with tired authority.

Joel felt it hit Will first. Then himself. Because the words were what Joel had been saying without saying. Move anyway. Forward. Morning. Car. Mike's dead voice and Joel's living rule had found the same shape.

Will looked at him.

There it was again. Not accusation exactly. Recognition.

Joel took the notebook with wet fingers and nearly tore the page getting it open.

NOT MIKE.

Will read it.

He took the pencil.

NOT ONLY MIKE.

Joel looked at the line and understood why Will had written it.

The woods had not only stolen Mike's voice. It had found the part of Mike that still lived in Joel's decisions. Keep moving. Do not stop. Do not answer. Survive now, know later. Being right would not make the resemblance harmless.

He wanted to write, I know.

He wrote nothing.

They went on.

Afternoon thinned the day without warming it. The cloud lid brightened and then began to lose light at the edges. They ate while drifting in a wider stretch, each taking food from the same open bag without letting the canoe turn broadside. Will's hand shook worse after the cold water. Joel made him stop twice to flex it. The fingers moved, but the swelling had made the knuckles blunt. When Will closed them around the paddle shaft, his grip looked borrowed.

Once, from the right bank, Will's dry cough sounded in three soft pieces.

Will looked at the bank, then deliberately coughed once into his own shoulder.

The living cough was rougher. Wetter. Smaller.

Joel lifted two fingers to his eyes.

Will nodded. He was visible. His cough was not proof by itself. Only his body made it his.

At the next bend the channel opened suddenly into a pond-shaped widening. Wind touched the surface there. Not much, but enough to make small scales of light where the clouds broke thinly. Dead trees stood from the water near the far end, gray trunks with no bark, each one reflected below itself in a darker line. A beaver lodge rose on the eastern bank. Fresh sticks lay over old sticks. Mud packed the gaps. A slide ran down to the water.

For one minute there was space.

Joel let the canoe drift.

The relief was dangerous. Open water made the body imagine options. A person could paddle hard here. A person could cross. A person could gain time. The trees drew back, and with them the feeling that every sound came from directly beside his ear.

Then something slapped the water near the lodge.

Will jerked.

It could have been a beaver. It probably was a beaver. A real animal's flat tail warning the pond against them. The sound cracked across the widening, hard and wet, and echoed once from the far bank.

Joel watched the water rings spread.

A second slap came from behind them, where no lodge stood.

Then a third from the left, dry and wrong, too much like a hand striking a canoe hull.

Will looked back at Joel, eyes sharp with the hunger to make a pattern of it.

Joel shook his head before Will could reach for the notebook.

Do not make a lesson from it here.

Will's eyes narrowed. He understood the refusal and hated it.

They paddled across the widening. No more slaps came. That made it worse in a way Joel had no use for. Repetition could become evidence. Silence after wrongness could become bait. He kept his eyes on the dead standing timber, then on the outlet, then on Will's shoulders as the channel took them again.

The final beaver obstruction was higher.

It crossed the outlet in a packed brown wall of sticks, mud, grass, and cut saplings. Water spilled over in three places. The drop on the downstream side looked close to two feet. No loaded shove-over would work. They would have to unload enough weight to lift, or line the canoe around the left edge through muck and brush.

Joel pointed to a small mud shelf on the left.

Will nodded.

They landed in pieces. Food barrel first, because it was heavy and central. Then the wet bags. Then the stove bag, map case, repair kit. Joel kept the Highlander key on his body. Will kept the hatchet in sight on the mud shelf, not because it would save them but because losing it would be another failure with a handle.

The mud shelf stank of wet leaves, old wood, and the mineral cold of disturbed bottom. Their sandals sank past the straps. Mosquitoes lifted weakly from the grass despite the chill. Joel's hands slipped on the canoe handle. Aluminum numbed his palms.

They lifted together.

The empty canoe was still awkward because the place gave them nowhere to stand. Will's bad hand failed halfway up. He caught the weight against his forearm and made a sound through his nose.

From the downstream brush, in Joel's own low voice, someone said, "Wait. Just wait."

Joel nearly dropped the boat.

Will saw it. That was the worst part. He saw Joel's hands open a fraction. He saw the body answer itself.

The voice did not repeat. It did not need to. It had used him rarely enough that every use felt less like bait than like trespass.

Joel tightened his grip.

He and Will lowered the canoe carefully onto the dam.

Joel stood with his palms on the gunwale and his head down. Water slid over sticks below him. His breathing had gone too fast. He counted it down without words. Four in. Four out. Not because counting saved anyone, but because it gave the body a task that did not involve listening.

Will touched his sleeve.

Joel looked up.

Will pointed two fingers to his eyes, then at Joel.

Visible.

Joel nodded.

Will took the notebook from Joel's jacket pocket himself, slowly, showing each movement. He wrote with the pencil braced against the canoe:

IT HAS YOU TOO.

Joel read it.

The line should not have hurt. They had already known it. The voice had proved it days ago near the cache. But knowledge changed when another person wrote it down. The page took the damage out of Joel's private body and put it between them. Will had seen the thing reach in and pull out a usable sound.

Joel took the pencil.

ONLY A LITTLE.

Will read it and, despite everything, gave him a look that almost became an old look. Dry. Skeptical. You absolute idiot.

Then the expression passed.

They got the canoe over.

The downstream side was worse. Mud to mid-shin. A tangle of submerged sticks. Water too shallow for the canoe, too deep for clean footing. They reloaded in stages and lined the boat fifty yards before they could climb in. By then Joel's legs had gone from cold to burning to a numbness that made each step uncertain. Will's lips had a pale edge.

Joel wanted to stop.

Stopping had become as suspect as sound.

They continued.

The channel lengthened into a straighter reach by late afternoon. Alder gave way to black spruce and tamarack. The banks rose a little. Gravel appeared in tongues along the inside bends. The water deepened enough that the canoe moved more cleanly. Wind came at them from ahead, small but steady, carrying the smell of open water.

The map said the channel would join a longer lake. Narrow, but a lake. If they made enough distance today, tomorrow might hold the final portage. North Road Bridge beyond that. The Highlander beyond that, if the road and bridge and machine had all kept their human promises.

Will's strokes began to fail before the lake.

At first he only shortened them. Then the paddle dipped late. Twice the blade skimmed the surface without bite. The third time he missed the water completely and stared at the paddle as if it had changed its agreement with him.

Joel tapped once.

Will shook his head.

Joel tapped again, harder.

Will held still.

Joel pointed to a gravel shelf on the left bank, just before the channel opened. It was no campsite, only a shoulder of stone and mud with spruce roots behind it and a strip of level ground perhaps wide enough for two bodies if they did not mind being half in the trees. But it was above the water. It had sightline to the canoe. It was not brush on all sides.

Will looked at the shelf, then at the widening water beyond.

He wanted the lake. Of course he did. Another hour, another mile, another visible payment toward the car.

Joel pointed to Will's hand.

Will lifted it as if surprised to find it still attached. The plastic was cloudy with moisture inside. Blood or water had darkened the gauze at the palm. His fingers would not close all the way.

The look he gave Joel was almost pleading and almost furious.

Joel pointed to the gravel shelf again.

They landed.

At camp, if the gravel shelf could be called camp, they unloaded less than seemed wise and more than seemed safe. Food barrel. Tarp. Sleeping bags. Filter. Notebook. First-aid kit. Hatchet. They left everything else lashed in the canoe in case the night decided for them. Joel pulled the boat high enough that it could not drift on its own, then tied the shortened painter around a spruce root and left the loose end where he could loop it to his wrist again after the tarp was set.

Will sat on the gravel with his paddle across his knees. He did not move for several minutes.

Joel rewrapped the hand.

The wound was worse. Not dramatically. Nothing useful like a clear line between safe and dangerous. Only swelling, heat, stiffness, a grayness around the wet places. Will watched Joel clean it with boiled water gone cold and a sting of antiseptic from the kit. He trembled once but did not pull away.

Joel wrote:

NO MORE FRONT PULLING IF POSSIBLE.

Will took the pencil.

THEN WE ARE SLOWER.

Joel wrote:

WE ARE SLOWER IF YOU LOSE THE HAND.

Will read it.

He wrote:

I HAVE ANOTHER ONE.

Joel looked at him.

Will gave a small shrug with one shoulder. It was the ghost of a joke, so thin it hardly counted as one. Joel let it be.

They filtered water at the edge of the shelf. The pump squeaked. Joel kept the strokes slow, then stopped every few pumps to listen. Once the same squeak came from across the channel and continued after his hands stopped. Will stared at the filter. Joel held it up so both men could see it motionless.

Squeak.

Squeak.

Then the food barrel latch clicked behind them.

Both men looked.

The barrel sat between the tarp roll and the first-aid kit, black ring shut. Visible.

Click.

The sound came from farther behind the spruce roots, where no barrel had ever been.

Will closed his eyes. When he opened them, the anger had come back. It steadied him more than fear did.

They finished filtering.

The light slid out of the channel by degrees. Not sunset yet, but the first withdrawal. The spruce darkened from the trunks outward. The water lost its brown surface and became a narrow piece of sky laid between banks. The lake beyond the opening showed small wind marks, silver and then dull.

Joel did not want to sleep here.

He did not want to continue.

Those two refusals sat in him with equal weight. The body wanted a third thing the world did not contain.

Will took the notebook.

His first line came hard, the pencil digging into the softened paper:

CAN IT LEARN FROM WRITING?

Joel looked at the question and felt a fear different from voices.

He had treated writing as safe because the thing was sound. The page had become shelter, law, apology, argument, command. It had held them since the cabin. It had let grief pass between their hands without entering the air.

But assumptions had killed better men than him.

The entity could not read minds. They believed that. It could mimic sounds it had heard. They believed that too. Writing made only small noises: pencil on softened paper, page scrape, breath, sleeve against damp cover. It removed tone. It made Will's need to explain less dangerous.

None of that proved the page was private.

Joel wrote:

NO PROOF. DON'T TEACH IT.

Will took the pencil back too quickly.

IF WE NEVER TEST, WE NEVER KNOW.

Joel wrote:

KNOWING IS NOT THE SAME AS LEAVING.

The woods beyond the gravel shelf gave Will's dry cough.

Will turned his head toward the sound before he could stop himself.

Joel tapped once on the notebook.

Will turned back.

Ashamed. Angry. Alive.

From the same darkening trees, in Will's voice, very close to the living one, came the question again.

"You guys hear that?"

Will stared at the page.

Joel put two fingers to his eyes and waited until Will looked up.

Visible.

Will nodded.

Joel wrote, pressing so lightly the pencil barely marked:

I HEAR YOU HERE.

Will read it. His face changed. For a moment all the argument went out of him, leaving only exhaustion and the old person Joel had known before jobs, before trips, before the group's jokes had become ammunition. Will touched two fingers to his own eyes, then pointed at Joel.

I see you too.

The channel moved beside them. The false cough did not come again. The false question did not need to. It had done what questions did. It had made two men prove where they were.

Joel closed the notebook.

They still had to make the tarp low. They still had to decide what counted as food safety, what counted as sleep, what counted as watch. They still had to pass through the night with less speech than breathing wanted.

The canoe rested on the gravel, tied and visible. The lake waited beyond the mouth of the channel. The road waited beyond the map or did not.

For now, the only answer they owned was the one they did not give.
