# Chapter 15 - The Trace

The trace disappeared three times before it became a road again.

For the first twenty minutes after the false Mike had told them to keep moving, moving stopped meaning progress.

It meant bodies kept in sight, feet placed where the ground would take them, packs watched for stray sounds, every branch that snapped assigned to a branch before the sound could become a voice. Mike led with the cracked paddle in one hand and the old map under his rain jacket. Alex's unused bear spray rode on the front of his PFD, orange and clean in a way nothing else was clean. Will followed with his shoulders drawn up around his ears. Joel came last and watched both of them as if attention were a line he had to hold without letting it show.

The first loss happened so gently that Joel did not know it had happened until Mike stopped. A minute before, the old path had been grass and mud, two shallow wheel ruts holding brown water, alder leaning in from both sides. Then it was only wet brush. No ruts. No cut stumps. No straightness. The woods took back the human mark without drama, as if the mark had never been more than grass pressed briefly in one direction.

Mike lifted one hand.

Will stopped behind him. Joel stopped behind Will. The order still mattered. Mike first because Mike could read a route and wanted decisions where he could touch them. Will in the middle because he was more likely to turn toward sound. Joel last because he could see both living men, and because by then seeing had become the only mercy left.

No one spoke.

Rain from the night before still lived in the branches. It fell one drop at a time onto hoods, pack lids, the backs of their bare calves above the hiking sandals. Mosquitoes had returned with the mildness after the weather. They worked at wrists and ears and the seams where collars had loosened. Joel let one land on the back of his hand and fill itself. Slapping it would make sound. He watched the insect darken with blood and kept his hand still.

Mike crouched and pushed wet grass aside with the cracked paddle Joel had carried from camp. He used it like a staff, like a probe, like one broken thing could still be made useful if a man refused embarrassment. The orange cord on the shaft had darkened in the wet. It touched a rotted stump hidden under fern, then stopped there while Mike leaned closer.

Mike pointed.

The stump was cut flat, or had once been. Moss had rounded the saw face until it looked almost natural. Beyond it another stump stood at the same height, twenty feet off, and beyond that the ground dipped in a shallow line where water had learned to sit. Human repetition. A decision made by hands and tools, almost erased but not erased. The old road was not gone. It had only withdrawn under growth.

Mike looked back.

Will did not give him the satisfaction of a nod. He adjusted the bandage on his hand instead. The gauze had gone slack and dirty around his palm. He kept flexing the fingers, then remembering the cut and making them still. His face had the waxy look of a man running a fever or anger or both, but he stayed in the line.

Joel took the notebook from his chest pocket and wrote while the paper rested against his thigh:

TRACE LOST / FOUND BY STUMPS.

Under it he added:

ALL THREE VISIBLE.

That line had become a prayer stripped of religion. It asked nothing to intervene. It stated what still held, and because it was written it did not add another voice to the woods.

Mike read it and tapped the stump with the paddle. Once. Soft.

Seen.

Then they went on.

The trace took them uphill at an angle that made no clean sense from the map. It might have followed old grade, avoided low marsh, or bent around a place where the road had always been bad. The map showed intent, not water sitting black in a rut for thirty years, not a poplar dropped across both wheel lines and becoming soil, not spruce seedlings growing where tires had once kept the earth open. It did not show the time they had promised themselves they would still have.

Will wanted to say this. Joel could feel the want in him the way he felt heat from a stove. It came off his shoulders. It worked in his jaw. Twice he raised his good hand as if to stop Mike and begin a diagram in the air. Twice he lowered it.

The third time he failed.

"Old logging roads move," Will said.

Mike did not turn.

Will's voice came out low, almost reasonable. That made it more dangerous. "I mean, not move. Obviously. But the map can be right and wrong at the same time. It can mark a legal line, a winter route, a skid road, a thing somebody drove once and then--"

Mike stopped.

Joel watched the back of his neck. The tendon there tightened.

"Write it," Mike said.

Will blinked. "What?"

Mike turned halfway. "You have a theory, put it in the book."

"It's not a theory. It's just--"

Joel held the notebook out.

Will looked at it as if it had insulted him. Then he took it. His bandaged hand made the pencil awkward, and when he shifted the notebook a dark bead pressed through the gauze near his thumb. He used the other hand, pressing too hard.

MAP CAN BE RIGHT AND STILL NOT HELP.

He pushed the notebook against Joel's chest.

The sentence looked better than it had sounded. Writing removed tone and left a tool small enough to carry.

It was the kind of failure Will could bear better than panic: a failure with a hinge, a category, a cause he could put his thumb on. A map could be right and useless. A road could exist and not save them. If the world would give him a flaw with edges, he could still make himself useful by naming it.

Mike read it. His mouth moved once, no sound. Joel thought he might argue with the page. Instead Mike nodded, one hard dip, and turned back to the brush.

They kept moving.

The second loss of the trace came in blowdown.

A wind years old had taken a strip of spruce and laid the trunks in the same direction, root plates standing shoulder-high and black with soil. The old road ran into the mess and did not come out where it should have. Branches had dried into gray nets. New alder filled the gaps. Their packs caught on everything. Sleeping bags bulged under rain covers. The pot in Joel's side pocket knocked against his water bottle until he stopped and tightened the strap with fingers made clumsy by cold, fatigue, and the obligation to know exactly what every sound belonged to.

In ordinary time they would have complained loudly. Alex would have turned it into a bit about premium wilderness experiences, about how people paid good money to crawl under dead trees and lose shin skin. Will would have explained windthrow until someone threatened him. Mike would have said people were soft now. Joel would have let them make noise and would have been privately glad for it.

Now the work happened in fragments of breath and gesture. A point. A palm. Two fingers on a pack strap. Their old talk had filled gaps like this without anyone noticing. Without it, the gaps widened.

Mike pointed under one trunk. Will shook his head and pointed over. Joel looked at the root plate and saw a dark hollow beneath it where water had run, or where an animal had used passage, or where nothing should put a human head. He touched Will's pack and pointed over.

Over, then.

They climbed.

The bark came away wet under Joel's palms. His sandal slipped on a branch polished by rain and his shin hit hard enough to make him see white at the edges. He opened his mouth around a curse and closed it on air. The pain moved up his leg and became heat. He held still until it settled into a shape he could walk with, one more private fact he did not dare make public.

Ahead, Will made a small dry cough.

Joel saw him do it. Saw his shoulders jerk once. Saw the hand rise halfway toward his mouth and stop too late. Will turned back immediately, eyes wide behind smeared glasses.

Sorry, his face said.

No one wrote it down. There was nothing useful in punishing a cough after the body had already betrayed him.

They pushed another twenty yards through the blowdown.

Then the same cough came from ahead of Mike.

Not loud. Not theatrical. It did not echo. It was Will's cough exactly, the small irritated clearing before correction, before a sentence beginning no or actually or that is not how. The living Will stood six feet in front of Joel with one hand on a dead branch and the other pressed against his own mouth as if he could take the sound back into himself. His shoulders were shaking, but he stayed turned toward Joel.

Mike froze.

Joel froze.

The cough did not repeat. It did not need to. One exact sound, placed once, left little for the mind to argue with and much for the body to obey.

The woods resumed its ordinary noises around the absence: water ticking from needles, the faint rub of pack cloth where Joel's weight shifted, one far raven knocking once and then nothing. Ordinary sound had become a set of suspects. Each one had to show its hands.

Will looked at Joel.

Joel shook his head.

No answer.

He took the notebook out. His hands were wet enough that the page softened under his thumb.

WILL COUGH - AHEAD.

WILL SEEN.

Mike read it. His face stayed closed, but Joel saw the arithmetic behind it. If Will could be in one place and Will's voice in another, then Mike's own voice from the trace ahead of him had not been an exception. It had been instruction in a rule none of them wanted.

Will wrote beneath Joel's lines:

IT CAN USE LIVING.

Then, after a pause:

NOT JUST DEAD.

The page looked colder with that on it. Until then, part of Joel had wanted the wrongness to belong to Alex, to death, to the place where the trip had broken. The line took that comfort away.

Mike stared at the words for too long. Then he reached up and wiped rain from the bill of his cap with two fingers.

"We knew that," he said.

His voice sounded too large in the blowdown.

Will flinched. Joel did too.

Mike's jaw set as he heard himself hearing himself.

"Write," Will whispered.

It was barely a sound, but it was a sound. He knew it. Shame went through his face so quickly it would have been easy to miss in any other life.

Joel took the pencil and wrote:

LESS TALK.

He underlined it once. Not as command. As fact. The pencil groove cut through the damp top fibers of the page.

They found the trace again where the blowdown thinned. Mike found it, really. He angled left when the easier ground went right, cut around a wet hollow, and stopped beside a line of stones sunk under moss. The stones had been laid by hand. Maybe to hold a culvert, maybe to harden mud, maybe by men who had never imagined three strangers decades later reading their labor for permission to keep going.

Mike touched the stones with the paddle tip.

There was no triumph in the gesture now. Only use.

The trail rose. The woods changed by degrees. Black spruce gave way in patches to jack pine and young birch. The ground under the moss became sandier, then wet again. Grass grew in the center of the old grade. In places the ruts were clear enough that Joel could imagine tires: a pickup, a maintenance truck, a ranger's vehicle with the door hinge squealing and a man stepping out to clear brush from a sign. Imagination had become suspect too. It could give the woods shapes it had not earned.

At midday, or what Joel guessed was midday from the pale place above the clouds, they stopped beside a granite knob with enough open surface to sit without soaking through immediately. The old road passed below them in two wet suggestions, almost visible from that angle and therefore almost reassuring.

No one wanted to stop. All three needed to.

Mike checked his watch. The face was scratched and fogged at the edge. His thumb stayed on the rim too long. He checked the compass next, then the map in its cloudy case. He did it with the care of someone handling evidence after being accused. Joel could see him choosing not to say the time.

Will sat on the rock and peeled the dirty bandage from his palm. The cut had opened. Not badly, but enough. Blood made a bright line in the wet crease below his thumb. He looked at it with irritation, as if his hand were making a poor argument.

Joel crouched by him and took out the first-aid kit.

Will let him clean it. That was how tired he was.

Alcohol made Will's fingers jerk.

"Sorry," Joel said, before he could stop himself.

The word hung there.

It was small. Human. Useless. It had crossed the air and become available.

Joel felt Mike look at him.

Will looked at the ground. "Seen," he said, too softly.

Joel nodded. He wrote anyway:

JOEL SAID SORRY - TO WILL HAND.

It felt foolish to inventory kindness as if it were a dropped wrapper. It felt worse not to. The notebook had become a ledger of what they had given away.

Will read the line while Joel wrapped the new gauze. He did not smile. He touched two fingers to the clean edge of the bandage, then to the page.

Seen.

They ate bars because the body would not negotiate forever. Mike divided them by sight on the rock: three damp rectangles, two with chocolate gone gray at the edges, one oat thing that Alex would have refused with a face elaborate enough to make a meal out of it.

Joel saw the empty fourth share before anyone made it.

There was no fourth share. There had not been since Second Camp. Still, his hand paused with the wrapper half open.

Will saw him pause and looked away.

Mike chewed without looking at either of them. His beard held crumbs. He was still wearing the PFD over his rain jacket, orange shoulders dulled by mud, straps dark with wet. On land it made him look both prepared and absurd, like a man who had misunderstood the category of danger. But the pockets mattered. The Highlander key mattered. North Road Bridge mattered somewhere past water, road, time, and whatever had learned to speak with them. The vest made Mike ridiculous; the key made him necessary.

Mike's hand went to the left zip pocket.

Joel saw the gesture before Mike knew he had made it. So did Will.

Will raised his eyebrows.

Mike caught him and lowered his hand. For one second the old answer formed in his face. Some blunt defense. Some managing sentence.

He said it anyway.

"Truck doesn't help if we lose the key."

It was a reasonable sentence. It was even necessary. Spoken aloud, it became material.

The three of them listened to the woods after he said it. Even practical sentences had an afterlife now.

Nothing answered.

Joel wrote:

MIKE: TRUCK DOESN'T HELP IF WE LOSE THE KEY.

Then, because the practical mattered more than the fear:

KEY STILL MIKE PFD LEFT ZIP.

Will read it and nodded.

Mike read it too. "Good."

His voice was low. Tired. Almost grateful. Then he seemed to hear the softness in himself and closed down again.

Will was still looking at the pocket. Not accusing. Worse than accusing. Waiting.

Mike looked past him into the brush. "I know what I said about the beacon."

The sentence landed harder than the key line. It did not excuse anything. It only opened a place in the day where the absent object stood: the small box they had not carried because the route was known, because the vehicles were staged, because men who had come home before expected to come home again. Mike had not forbidden it. That would have been cleaner. He had made it sound unnecessary until unnecessary became the group decision.

Will's good hand twitched toward the notebook.

Mike shook his head once. "Don't."

Joel wrote anyway, smaller:

MIKE: BEACON.

He did not write apology. Mike had not offered one, exactly. He had put the missing object on the table between them and let it stand there without a use. It was the closest Mike had come to saying he was afraid of his own judgment.

They rested five minutes more. Maybe seven. Time had become distance, light, water, breath. Joel's watch still worked, but he had stopped trusting numbers to mean what they had meant before Alex died. A mile on a map was not a mile in alder. Noon did not promise afternoon. A cabin on paper did not promise shelter.

Will took the notebook and wrote:

DISTANCE DOESN'T MEAN TIME.

Then, under it:

TIME DOESN'T MEAN SAFE.

He stared at what he had written and gave Joel the pencil without comment.

Mike folded the map and stood.

The third loss came after the old road dipped into cedar.

It was darker there though the day had not darkened. Cedar branches held the wet close and low. The ground turned soft enough to take the whole shape of their sandals and then drink the prints back in. Each step made a quiet sucking sound. Their packs brushed both sides of the corridor. The trace, if it was still a trace, narrowed to discomfort and refusal. The air smelled of cedar rot and cold water.

Mike slowed. Then stopped.

Will almost walked into him.

Ahead, the grade split around a mound of deadfall. Left looked lower and wetter. Right looked higher and wrong. Straight was impossible. The map did not acknowledge choice at that scale. It offered a line. The ground offered a place to be mistaken.

Mike took out the compass.

Will leaned to look and stopped himself halfway. His restraint looked painful.

Joel watched Mike align the compass with the map case against his forearm. Mike was good at it. Even exhausted, even afraid, his hands knew the work. He sighted past two spruce trunks, checked the slope, checked the light, checked something in the ground Joel could not see. Competence again. The terrible comfort of it. The fact that it had not saved them did not make Joel want it less. Alex's spray tapped once against the zipper of Mike's PFD as he leaned, a small hard sound Joel saw before he could mistrust it.

Will could not stand it.

"Contour says higher," he said.

Mike closed his eyes.

Will pressed on, softer but still speaking. "I'm not being an asshole. The old road's probably staying out of that wet. If there was a culvert, it's gone. If the line bends, it bends around drainage. That's all I'm--"

"Will."

"No, I'm trying to help."

"Then help quietly."

"Quietly doesn't change the contour interval."

Mike turned.

There was anger in him, but the larger thing was shame. Joel saw it and did not want to. Mike heard every correction now as proof that he had brought them here wrong. The map, the route, the missing beacon, the cabin square, Alex under stones back at Second Camp: it had begun to gather around him. Will was not wrong, and Will was trying to help. That was part of the cruelty.

Joel put the notebook between them.

Will looked at it, then at Mike. His mouth made a hard line. He wrote:

HIGHER PROBABLY BETTER.

Mike read it.

"Probably," he said.

Will snatched the notebook back and added:

NOT CERTAIN.

Mike gave one breath through his nose. Not quite a laugh. "That's new for you."

Will's face flickered. Under other weather that might have become something. A jab returned. A little pressure released. Here it collapsed almost at once.

From the left, through the wet cedar, Mike's voice said, "It opens up after this."

All three men went still.

The living Mike stood in front of Joel, visible in profile, map case in one hand, compass in the other. His mouth was closed. His eyes had gone flat with shock.

The false voice had come from the lower, wetter option, the one Mike had not yet chosen and the one Will had almost argued them away from.

It had Mike's clipped certainty, the old impatience with hesitation, the practical shove toward motion. It did not sound malicious. That was what made Joel's legs want to obey it. It sounded, worst of all, useful. It sounded like relief arriving from the wrong ground.

Will's good hand rose. Not to his ears this time. To his mouth.

Joel stepped close enough to put two fingers against Will's pack.

Will closed his eyes.

The woods waited.

Then, from behind them, Alex laughed.

Bright, exact, wrong.

It came from the trace they had already walked, from the direction of the canoes and Emergency Camp and the body they had left farther back than that. Not the little laugh the thing had used at night, but the hard bark Joel remembered from the motel parking lot, from the truck, from Alex making himself the center of any room too quiet to resist him. It had no grief in it. The grief belonged to the men who heard it.

Will bent forward as if someone had hit him in the stomach, but he did not turn.

Mike's hands tightened around map and compass.

Joel looked behind them because not looking felt like superstition. There was only the cedar corridor and their own disturbed passage through it. Leaves still moved where packs had brushed them. Water fell from one branch to another. Nothing with Alex's face stood there. Nothing with any face.

He wrote:

MIKE VOICE LEFT / LOWER ROUTE.

ALEX LAUGH BEHIND.

ALL THREE SEEN.

NO ANSWER.

SPRAY TAP SEEN - MIKE PFD.

Will read it with a concentration almost violent. Mike read it too.

Mike pointed right. Higher.

Will nodded.

They took the higher side.

For twenty minutes the old trace was nothing but inference. Mike led them along faint grade, then lost it, then found a line of old blazes so grown over they were only scars under bark. One cedar had a rusted nail in it, head swollen with orange. Human evidence, small and mean. Joel touched it as he passed, then regretted touching anything. Rust flaked onto his fingertip. He wiped it on his pants and kept going.

The higher way did open, eventually.

No one said so. Saying it would have made the false voice right in a way none of them could bear.

The cedar thinned into mixed spruce and birch. The ground firmed. A faint roadbed reappeared, broad enough now that they could walk not quite single file. Light entered from the west in a colorless wash. The afternoon had advanced without permission. Shadows were longer. Or Joel was imagining shadows because the idea of dusk had entered him and begun to work.

Mike did not look back for credit. That made it harder to refuse him credit. He had chosen the higher ground. The false voice had chosen lower. Skill still mattered. The same confidence that had narrowed the world badly could still find a road inside brush.

They crossed a shallow ditch where a culvert had collapsed. Black water moved under broken metal. The culvert pipe was rusted through in two places, its edges thin as torn paper. Mike stopped there and looked at it with a strange expression.

"Road," he said.

The word came out before he caught it.

It was not triumph. It was need: a man asking the world to acknowledge one thing he had read correctly.

Will opened his mouth.

Joel could see the correction waiting there. This is not a road. This was a road. There is a difference. The phrases had weight. They wanted out.

Will swallowed them.

He wrote instead:

ROAD WAS REAL.

Then he handed the notebook to Joel.

Joel added:

WAS DOES NOT HELP YET.

Mike read both lines and said nothing.

They stepped over the culvert one at a time. On the far side, Joel's left sandal sank to the ankle in black muck. Cold water went under the strap and between his toes. He pulled free with a wet pop too close to the old sounds from beaver mud and portage muck and Alex swearing theatrically about losing footwear. The memory rose whole and stupid.

From the birch to Joel's right, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel stopped.

The line was aimed at him. He knew it before he understood how he knew. It had Alex's teasing lift, the affectionate cruelty of being caught observing instead of moving, the exact little push he used when Joel lingered over light on water or the arrangement of camp as if attention itself were a vanity.

For an instant Joel was not on the old fire road. He was back on Day 1, with a canoe to unload and Alex turning impatience into theater, alive enough to be irritating, alive enough that Joel could resent him without consequence. That was the cruelty of the line. It returned Alex not as a ghost but as an inconvenience, the living man made whole for half a second by annoyance.

The voice came again, closer by a few yards, or seeming closer because grief moved distance.

"You coming or writing a poem?"

Will turned toward Joel, not toward the sound.

That saved him. Or helped save him. It was the first time Joel saw Will choose the living witness over the voice, and the choice cost him. It showed in the hard set of his mouth.

Mike was ahead and had not turned all the way around. He watched Joel from the corner of his eye, giving him the dignity of not making too much of it.

Joel's throat worked. It wanted Alex's name. It wanted an apology for every private irritation, every time Joel had wished the man would be quieter, every small judgment now made grotesque by absence. It wanted to make grief into language because language was what men had in place of touch when they did not know how to touch. The woods had learned their jokes. Joel would not teach it their remorse if he could help it.

He put the pencil on the page instead.

ALEX: POEM LINE - RIGHT.

TARGET JOEL.

He stopped. The pencil point pressed a dark spot into the paper.

Then he wrote:

DO NOT GIVE IT GRIEF.

That one he did not show them at first. It seemed too private and too general, a rule and a confession.

Will reached for the notebook gently, which was so unlike him that Joel let it go. He read the line. His face changed. He passed it to Mike.

Mike read it and looked back into the birch. His face did not soften, but something in his grip on the map did.

The false Alex did not speak again.

"Move," Mike said.

The word was too useful to punish. They moved.

After that, hope came into the day in small practical increments, which made it harder to distrust.

The roadbed widened by inches. Grass grew waist-high in the middle, wet seed heads dragging across their thighs. The old ruts became visible again, shallow but continuous. Here and there the brush on both sides looked cut back by some more recent force than the original road: an animal trail, storm damage, maybe men five years ago or ten. The trees no longer pressed so closely against their faces. Air moved.

Joel did not trust the relief, but his body took it anyway. His shoulders lowered. His stride lengthened. Will lifted his head. Even Mike's pace changed, not faster exactly, but cleaner. The world had begun to resemble the marks on paper, and the human mind was weak before resemblance.

They passed a rusted can half sunk in moss. Then another. A strip of black rubber curled beside a stump. Old flagging tape hung from a branch in pale pink tatters, so faded it was almost the color of skin.

Will pointed to the tape.

Mike nodded.

No one said recent. No one said not recent. The strip moved once in a draft Joel could not feel, then hung still.

The grade bent around a stand of spruce. Joel smelled old ash before he saw anything. Not smoke. Nothing warm. Just the mineral ghost of burned wood under damp rot, the way a fire ring smelled after rain.

Mike stopped so suddenly Will had to put a hand out against his pack.

Ahead, through trunks, a hard dark line cut across the vertical woods.

Roof.

For one minute the whole day changed.

Joel knew better. A roof was not help. A square was not rescue. A cabin was not a radio, a road was not a truck, and a sound was not a man. He knew all of it with the dry exactness of the notebook in his pocket. Still, the sight entered him below knowledge. His body saw a made thing in the wet and believed, briefly, in the species that had made it.

Will moved faster.

Mike lifted a hand, warning, but he moved faster too.

The trees opened onto a small clearing. Grass grew high and yellow-green around rusted cans, a collapsed sawhorse, and a pile of cut rounds gone soft with fungus. The cabin sat at the far side, square and deliberate and failing. Its roof sagged toward the back. One shutter still hung beside a dark window. Another window was boarded from inside with mismatched planks. The door stood open three inches, not welcoming, just not latched. No smoke. No antenna. No solar panel. No fresh axe chips at the woodpile, no recent boot line through the grass that Joel could see. Beyond the cabin, an opening in the trees suggested where the old road had once continued and did not promise that it still did.

Mike stopped at the edge of the clearing and looked back at them.

Something like vindication crossed his face before exhaustion pulled it down. He had not imagined the square. He had not led them to nothing. That mattered, even if it did not matter enough. Joel hated the part of himself that was relieved on Mike's behalf.

Will was too tired to deny him.

Joel took out the notebook.

CABIN REAL.

He paused, then added:

NOT HELP UNTIL SEEN INSIDE.

Mike read it. His mouth twisted.

"You are a joy," he said.

His voice was hoarse enough that the joke almost disappeared.

Will read the line and gave Joel a look that might have been gratitude or simple agreement. His glasses were fogged at the edges. Mud had dried in a streak along his cheek where he had wiped at a mosquito bite with a dirty wrist.

They stood listening.

The clearing held too much quiet. Not true silence. The grass ticked against itself. Water dripped from the roof at the back corner into something metal. A fly worried at the open door and made a small hard sound against wood. After the close brush, the open space felt staged. It gave sound room to arrive and three men nowhere to hide.

Mike pointed to himself, then the cabin, then to Joel and Will: stay together.

Joel shook his head and pointed all three fingers toward the door.

All three.

Mike stared at him.

Joel held the stare.

At last Mike nodded.

They crossed the clearing in a loose line, no more than arm's length apart. Grass soaked Joel's thighs. One rusted can crushed under Mike's sandal with a faint metallic complaint and all three stopped until they identified it. Seen. Can. Mike lifted his foot. The can had flattened into the mud, its orange label long since weathered to blank.

At the door, the smell strengthened: mouse droppings, wet wood, old ash, mildew, and something mineral under the rot, as if the cabin had been slowly becoming soil from the inside.

"Careful," Joel said.

Mike almost smiled. "Now we're careful?"

Nobody answered.

He pushed the door with the cracked paddle, not his hand.

The hinges gave a long groan.

The sound moved through the clearing and came back from the trees too late, thinner at the edges.

No one stepped in until the echo or answer or imitation had died.

Mike went first because he still went first. Will followed with his bandaged hand held close against his chest. Joel entered last and stopped just inside, keeping the door, the two men, and as much of the room as possible in sight.

Inside, light came through roof gaps in thin white pieces. Dust and damp had made a skin over everything. There was a table built from rough boards, two broken chairs, a rusted stove pipe tilted away from a small black stove, an empty shelf, a coil of wire too corroded to trust, a mouse nest in the corner made of paper and insulation. A black pot sat under one leak with water in it and no sign of use except gravity. On the empty shelf, a clean rectangle in the dust showed where something had once sat and been removed years ago or yesterday or by a hand no longer useful to imagine. Names had been cut into one wall by people who had believed, at least while carving, that a mark could hold them in a place.

RAY 1998.

STUMP CREW 04.

KELLY.

Three initials inside a heart, the heart cut poorly and much deeper than the letters.

Will laughed once.

Not humor. Exhaustion.

"Okay," he said. "Cabin."

The word was too large and too small.

Mike stood in the middle of the room with the map in his hand. For a moment Joel saw what he wanted: a radio on the shelf, a ranger's log, a road chart, a can of fuel, anything with current use clinging to it. Instead there was the old square made actual around them and already failing. The roof existed. The walls existed. The world had met the map only far enough to make the map's failure personal.

Outside, from the far side of the clearing, Mike's voice said, "Map says there's a cabin."
