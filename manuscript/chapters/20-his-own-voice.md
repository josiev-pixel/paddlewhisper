# Chapter 20 - His Own Voice

After Joel heard himself, the trip reached backward.

Before that, some private part of him had kept a boundary. The thing belonged to Alex's death, to Mike's mistake, to Will's noise, to the brush around Second Camp. It began where the world had broken badly enough to deserve it. A man could bear almost anything if he could make it start after his own innocence.

His voice proved otherwise.

It had been near from the beginning, or near enough. At the landing. At the first water. At the places they had felt almost safe because nothing had happened yet. It had been present while they made ordinary mistakes and called them ordinary. It had heard a small caution said by a quiet man at a gravel put-in, and it had kept it, or the woods had kept it, or whatever moved through the woods had no useful difference between hearing and keeping. The week did not begin with Alex stepping past the light. It reached back and put a hand on the first boat, the first strap, the first harmless correction.

Will kept looking at him as if Joel might explain what a man was supposed to do with the sound of himself.

Joel had no explanation. He had the notebook, the pencil, the Highlander key zipped against his chest, and mud drying in a hard crust along the backs of his hands.

They stayed crouched at the bad fork for too long. The lower path shone with water through alder. The higher trace vanished and returned in pieces. Behind them, uphill, his own voice had gone quiet. That did not help. Silence after a voice was no longer absence. It was only the place a voice might return from.

Will took the pencil carefully, the way a man might take a blade from a child. His injured hand made his grip awkward. He wanted to comfort; Joel could see it in the way he leaned toward the page before he had words. Then he wrote:

NOT YOU.

Joel looked at the words.

Will underlined them once, then added:

ONLY SOUND.

Joel felt something in him rise hard enough that it almost became speech. No. Don't. Stop making it smaller. Stop trying to help me by lying in a quieter medium.

His mouth opened.

Will saw it and went still.

Joel shut his mouth so sharply his teeth clicked. The sound seemed enormous. A small white bite of enamel in the wet air. Both men listened after it, because even a tooth click had become information. It had come from him. That was not the same as being safe.

He took the notebook back and wrote:

IT IS USEFUL SOUND.

Then, because that was not enough:

THAT IS WORSE.

Will read it and did not argue. The mercy of that cost him. Joel could see it in the muscles at his jaw, in the way his eyes wanted to move past the page and make a theory out of everything. He wanted to say that sound was not self, that repetition was not presence, that a copy did not own the thing copied. He was probably right. It did not help.

Joel pointed to the lower path, then held up one hand. Wait by sight. He crouched and studied the mud.

The rain had taken most of yesterday. Their tracks had come down this way or not. Mike had led. Will had followed, talking then because they still allowed talking, because the cabin had still been a possible answer, because Alex had been dead but Mike alive, which had seemed at the time like a different kind of world. Joel looked for Mike's boot tread and found only oblong pressures filled with brown water. He looked for the diagonal scratch of Will's sandals and found roots, leaf rot, and a black feather stuck to mud by its own wet barbs.

The feather made him remember the raven at the put-in, its call breaking off over the trees while Alex snapped a strap and said something ugly about wilderness ambiance. He hated how quickly memory began to revise itself. The old landing, the first portage, the joke about poems, the food barrel latch, the hollow knock they had blamed on rope: all of it rearranged itself under the pressure of his voice from the trees. The past did not have to change to become unusable.

Nothing in the past had changed. Everything had.

Will touched Joel's sleeve and pointed.

On the left edge of the lower path, a cedar branch had been cut clean through at knee height. The cut was pale under rain, not fresh-white, not old. Hatchet work. Mike's work, probably. Yesterday, on the way in, he had been making the old trace more real by cutting what resisted it.

Joel nodded. Lower.

They moved toward the water.

The ground softened within twenty yards. Mud took their footprints and folded them into itself. Alder crowded the path and slapped water onto their sleeves. Joel went first, then stopped, turned, and confirmed Will by sight. Will had stopped rolling his eyes at the checks. He endured them now with a concentration that looked almost angry. Joel understood. To be watched every fifteen steps was to be reminded every fifteen steps that the living body had to keep proving itself.

Once, from behind them, his own voice said, "Wait. Just wait."

It was placed well. Not too close. Not theatrical. The exact distance a man might stand behind another on a bad portage, asking him not to shift a canoe too soon.

Joel did not turn.

Will did. Not toward the voice, but toward Joel.

That was the rule doing its work. It should have comforted him. Instead it made him feel briefly, stupidly, jealous of the rule. Will trusted the page faster than he trusted Joel's face now. The page had become a third body between them, thinner than a man and colder, but harder to imitate.

Joel lifted two fingers to his eyes. Will nodded.

They kept going.

The water smell strengthened. It was not open lake, not wind and distance. It was narrow water, tannin and mud, old leaves soaking under alder roots. The kind of channel that took a canoe because it had to, not because it welcomed one. Through the trees came the dull shine of it, broken by black stems. No wind reached them there. Sound did not travel far so much as stay trapped.

Joel had imagined the canoe cache as an object. Two boats, gear under spruce, the route returning to weight and balance. He had wanted the cache with a child's stupid faith: if they reached it, one kind of terror would end. A canoe was not a voice. A pack did not ask to be believed. Rope held or failed in the hand.

But objects had their own treacheries. They could be moved. They could be made to say where a person had been without proving who had touched them. A knot could be evidence or bait or only a knot. The world of things was safer than speech only because it resisted being borrowed. It did not refuse.

The cache was not where it should have been.

For three seconds Joel thought they had missed it. Then he saw the split granite face, the three dead spruce in a row, the bent cedar Mike had marked on the map. He saw the low roof of boughs they had made over the gear. He saw the dark line where the better canoe's hull should have been hidden under spruce.

The line was wrong.

He stopped so abruptly Will bumped his pack.

Will's good hand came up, caught Joel's strap, and then released. He looked over Joel's shoulder.

The damaged canoe was still under spruce boughs near the high-water line, the aluminum patch on its thwart dull through the green. The boughs over it had been moved. Not torn off. Not scattered. Moved aside and laid in a neat fan on the mud, their cut ends aligned as if someone had understood that cut ends belonged together but not why anyone would hide a boat.

The better canoe had been dragged six or seven feet from where they had left it. It sat half out of brush, angled toward the water, its keel resting in a fresh smear of mud. The painter rope ran from its front handle to an alder stem and looped twice around the trunk. The knot at the end was neither a bowline nor a trucker's hitch nor any of Will's tidy canoe knots. It was a hard, wet clot of rope, ugly and effective.

Joel could hear Will breathing through his nose.

The food barrel was where it had been, hung badly and too low from a spruce limb because none of them had cared about animal standards when the cabin had still existed as hope. Its blue side showed through brush. The black lid ring appeared closed.

One dry bag had been pulled partly from under the gear tarp and left in the mud. It was the long one with the tent bodies and some clothing, the one they had decided was too heavy to carry inland and not important enough to guard. Mud covered one end. Water had pooled around the roll-top. The map case lay near the food barrel with grit stuck along the seal, its clear plastic filmed brown at the edges. Joel could see the folded map inside, softened at one corner, its printed blue water made darker by damp.

The rod tube lay beside it.

Will stepped forward.

Joel caught his sleeve.

Will's face came around fast. For a moment Joel thought he would wrench free. Not because of the canoe. Because of the rods. They were a piece of Will from before: his hands working line through guides, his voice explaining why a lake with no visible structure might still hold fish along the downwind shore, his body made quiet by attention rather than fear. Fishing gave his talking a place to go. It made waiting active.

Joel had mocked him for it often enough. Not cruelly, or not only cruelly. Back at First Camp, with Alex still complaining about the food and Mike making the stove sound like a performance review, Will had stood ankle-deep at the granite edge and cast three times into dark water as if the lake had asked a question in a language he could almost read. Joel had thought then that Will looked most like himself when no one was answering him.

Joel shook his head and pointed to the ground between them.

Stop. Look first.

Will swallowed. He nodded once, too hard.

They stood at the edge of the cache and inspected it like men approaching an animal that might only be sleeping.

No tracks made sense. Their own prints from yesterday had been softened by rain and overrun by new disturbances: long smears in mud where the canoe had dragged; two round punched places that might have been the ends of branches; shallow half-moons from Will's sandals or Joel's or Mike's, all blurred into each other. Near the alder stem, the mud held a line of compressed grass as if someone had knelt. Or as if the rope had been pulled low. Or as if a body had crouched there without leaving a body-shaped truth. Joel did not write any of it down. A written fact still needed a fact under it.

Joel pointed to the better canoe. Then to Will. Then made a slow circling motion with one finger: inspect.

Will shook his head and pointed at Joel.

Joel understood. He was in back. He would steer. He needed to decide if the hull was trustworthy.

They approached together.

The rope knot tightened when Joel put weight on it. It had been tied by force, not skill. The painter had bitten into the alder bark. Two loops crossed each other, then a series of jammed half-turns had been pulled down until the wet rope hardened. A human hand could have done it in panic. Mike could have done it with one glove on and no patience. Alex could have made worse and called it fine. Will would never have left such a thing unless he wanted to make a point about other people's standards.

That was the worst part. It was not impossible enough. It sat inside the edge of things a frightened person might do badly.

Will crouched beside it, touched one finger to a crossing loop, and stopped. His mouth shaped, impossible to stop, Can it--

Joel put two fingers hard against Will's shoulder.

Will closed his mouth.

The question hung between them anyway.

Can it tie knots? Can it understand what tied means?

Joel took the notebook out.

DOESN'T MATTER.

He added:

CUT IT.

Then, because Will was still looking at the knot as if knowledge might come out of it, Joel wrote one more line.

NO TESTS.

Will stared at the page a second longer than necessary. Then he took the hatchet from his belt.

Cutting instead of untying felt wasteful and correct. They had begun the trip as men who preserved rope, packed spare cord, debated knots, saved lengths that might be useful later. Now Joel held the painter taut against the alder while Will chopped carefully through the outer loop. The first strike thudded into wet fiber. The second opened white strands. The third severed the line and left the knot clamped to the tree like a fist.

Will looked at the cut end, then at the knot left behind. His face had the bright, sick look of a man refusing to ask a good question.

The loose rope fell into mud.

From the water, behind the reeds, came a hollow knock.

Both men froze.

The loaded canoe was not loaded yet. The damaged canoe sat under boughs. The better canoe lay at their feet. No hull moved.

Another knock came from farther out, beyond the first bend of the channel where fog had collected in a low seam above the water.

It was the old sound. Hull on stone. Paddle against gunwale. Rope tapping aluminum. Ordinary enough to have a dozen causes until all the causes were in sight and still.

Will wrote with the pencil braced against his thigh:

BOAT SOUND / NO BOAT.

Joel nodded.

Then the food barrel latch clicked.

The barrel hung fifteen feet away, visible through spruce, black ring closed, blue body swaying not at all.

Click.

Will turned his head only far enough to see it. His discipline had become painful to watch.

Click.

The sound came now from behind the damaged canoe.

Joel wrote:

BARREL VISIBLE.

Will tapped the line once. Yes. I know. The tap left a crescent of mud on the paper.

They worked.

Work was the only answer that did not add material. Joel checked the better hull first. No crack he could see. No puncture. No new gouge deep enough to matter. He ran his hands along the outside while Will held the boat tilted. Cold water spilled from a shallow pocket near the seat and ran over Joel's knuckles. The caned front seat had one broken strand. The back seat was sound. The thwarts held. The shortened painter could be retied later, badly but by them.

He made himself check slowly. The urge was to hurry because every second at the cache felt like standing in a doorway with his back exposed. Hurry would miss the split seam, the loose seat bolt, the hairline crack that became a sinking boat a half mile from shore. Fear wanted speed. Water wanted accuracy.

The damaged canoe was worse. Mike's repair on the cracked thwart still held when Joel pressed it, but the aluminum splint had shifted and one wrap of cord had loosened. Rainwater had collected along the keel line. A small gray leaf had plastered itself over a dent in the aluminum, and for one stupid second Joel did not want to touch it. It would float. It might carry gear. It might fail under portage strain or current. Two men with two canoes made less sense than two men in one boat, but one boat meant all weight, all risk, all hope in a single hull.

Joel set his palm on the better canoe.

Will looked at him.

Joel held up one finger.

One boat.

Will looked at the damaged canoe, the gear, the fog, the channel. His face did not protest. His body did. He shifted once as if the old argument had entered his knees. Two boats gave redundancy. Two boats let one man survive if the other went wrong. Two boats also meant more loading, more rope, more distance between them, more turns of the head, more sounds made by things they could not see at the same time.

Will took the notebook.

ONE BOAT = IF LOSE IT WE DIE.

Joel wrote:

TWO BOATS = WE SEPARATE.

Will read it. His mouth went tight.

Joel added:

STAY VISIBLE.

Will nodded.

They made a pile of what would go. Food barrel, because hunger was not optional. Filter. Stove and one pot. Map case, though grit had gotten into the seal and the paper inside felt damp at the edges. First aid. Tarp. Sleeping bags, one still damp enough to weigh like an animal. Mike's repair kit. The hatchet. Two paddles and the cracked repaired paddle as spare. The small blue dry bag with the emergency paddle stayed lashed under the front seat, more fixture than cargo. The notebook stayed inside Joel's jacket when he did not need it, buttoned under the rain shell. The Highlander key stayed in his inner pocket, zipped behind fabric, checked once by touch and then not touched again.

Every decision reduced four men to two. It was not sentimental math. It was volume and weight. One pot. One tarp. Two bags. One boat. The extra things had once meant comfort, redundancy, preference, personality. Now extra meant time at the shore.

They left the mud-soaked tent bag. Too heavy. Too wet. Too much time. They left extra clothes that were no longer extra in any meaningful sense. They left the extra cookware, a small sack of trash, the bent tent stakes, the roll of cord that had sat in water and knotted itself into uselessness. They left Alex's cracked headlamp and hot sauce bottle in the small bag where his remaining things had stayed, because neither man could decide to carry them and neither could throw them away. The abandoned pile looked at once excessive and poor, the remains of four men reduced to what two men could not lift.

Will picked up the rod tube last.

Joel looked away at first, giving him privacy he knew was false. Privacy required a world where a man could step ten feet off and still remain himself. Still, Joel turned to the canoe and tightened a strap that did not need tightening.

Behind him, plastic scraped mud.

Will had opened the tube and taken out the two rods. One had a fine crack near the second guide. The other looked all right. He held them awkwardly with his bandaged hand, their tips trembling in the damp air. The reels had grit in them. Line clung in wet loops.

For one moment, with the rods in his hand, Will looked almost normal. Not better. Not safe. But placed in a shape Joel recognized. A man at a lake, making small decisions about weight and lure and angle. A man who would say, quietly and with too much precision, that fishing was not about catching fish, not exactly. It was about keeping an instrument in the water long enough for the world to answer without using words.

Will looked at the channel.

The channel gave them nothing but fog and the second hollow knock, softer now, from where no canoe moved.

Will closed the rod tube.

He did not ask.

He laid the rods under the damaged canoe they were abandoning and covered them with the same boughs that had been moved aside. His hand worked slowly because of the bandage. He put one spruce bough, then another, then corrected the first because a tip still showed. The care did more damage than a sob would have. Will could leave them, but he could not leave them exposed.

When he finished, he stayed crouched.

Joel waited.

Will took the notebook and wrote:

TELL ME THIS IS NOT STUPID.

The sentence sat there, too human and too dangerous even in pencil.

Joel wanted to write, It isn't. He wanted to write, You can buy new rods. He wanted to write some ordinary lie about objects and survival and later. But later had become a superstition, and the rods were not the point.

He wrote:

IT COSTS.

Will read it. His face changed once and closed.

He wrote:

YES.

He tore that small corner of page away, folded it once, and put it into the rod tube before he slid the tube under the canoe. Joel did not stop him. A note that said only yes could not help the rods. It could mark that someone had known what was being left. It could say, without a voice, that abandonment was not the same as indifference.

They loaded the canoe low. Food barrel near the center. Heavy bag against it. Sleeping bags compressed badly under the thwart. Map case flat and reachable. Hatchet tucked beside Will but not under anything. Spare paddle lashed where Joel could see it. Every buckle they closed sounded too loud. Every strap pull was a small confession. The thing had all of these sounds already. That did not make giving them again feel harmless.

From the alder behind the cache, Mike cleared his throat.

Will did not look.

Joel did, because the sound went through him before the rule caught it. Only a quick glance. Brush. Wet black stems. No body. The glance cost nothing and everything. It proved again that his body still belonged to the old world first.

"We're fine," Mike said.

Joel's hand closed on the canoe gunwale.

The words had once been a gift even when false. Men used false gifts constantly. We're fine. Almost there. Weather's moving off. This will hold. Now the phrase had no weight behind it. It was an empty shape wearing Mike's mouth.

Will touched Joel's sleeve. Two fingers. Gentle.

Joel looked back at him.

Will pointed at the notebook.

Joel opened it, but Will took the pencil.

NOT MIKE.

Then, below that:

NOT JOEL.

Joel stared at the second line.

Will underlined nothing. He did not try to comfort him again. He had learned that distinction at least: the page could mark fact without pretending fact was repair. It could stand beside damage and not make a speech over it.

Joel touched the second line with one muddy finger. NOT JOEL. The page did not absolve him. It only refused the voice behind him a body.

Joel tapped both lines.

They carried the canoe to water.

The launch took too long. Everything silent took too long. Joel held the back while Will steadied the front with one good hand and one bandaged hand wrapped in plastic. The mud sucked at Joel's sandals. Cold water entered over the straps and took the heat from his feet at once. The canoe rocked under the weight. The food barrel shifted. Joel stopped it with his knee and almost said wait.

The word rose in his throat with the exact shape the woods already had.

He swallowed until his throat hurt.

Behind them, from the cache, his own voice said, "Wait. Just wait."

This time it sounded nearer. Not better. Nearer.

Will's eyes went to Joel's face. Not to the voice. The rule again. The rule, working.

Joel shook his head once.

Together they pushed the canoe free.

A rope tapped somewhere in the fog. Then another tap, nearer the far bank. The shortened painter lay visible across the front pack, wet and muddy and theirs. The sound went on anyway, a patient little knock against nothing they could see.

Will climbed in first, awkward and careful, placing his knees and then sitting low in the front. Joel held the canoe while it tried to turn broadside. He looked once at the cache: damaged canoe under spruce, rod tube hidden, wet gear abandoned, the strange knot still clenched around the alder stem, the cut rope hanging dark and wet from it. A place of human decisions already losing their shape.

The food barrel latch clicked from the trees.

Joel stepped in.

The canoe dipped, steadied, and took them.

For a moment they drifted without paddling. The channel ahead was narrow and dark. Fog took the far bank in pieces and gave it back as alder, then shadow, then nothing. Behind them the cache disappeared faster than it should have, not by magic, only by the ordinary fact of bends and brush and water accepting whatever men pushed onto it. The abandoned canoe was gone before Joel stopped listening for the latch.

Joel lifted his paddle.

Will turned enough for Joel to see his face.

No words.

Joel set the blade in the water, and the canoe moved.
