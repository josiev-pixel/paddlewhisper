# Chapter 6 - The Rough Carry

The alder gap did open, but not into relief.

It let them out into a run of water broken by stone. The current gathered itself in narrow places and slipped fast over gravel, then loosened into brown pools where foam collected against grass. The boats scraped twice before anyone could pretend the line was clean. Mike raised a hand from the back of his canoe and pointed them left, then right, then left again, reading the channel one word at a time.

The beaver work had left all of them slow. Mud dried on their calves and cracked when they shifted. Joel could feel grit under the straps of his sandals. In the front of his canoe, Alex sat with his paddle across his knees for half a breath too long, watching the tongue of current narrow between two stones.

"Left of that rock," Joel said.

"I see the rock."

"Good. Now miss it."

Alex dug hard on the right. Too hard. The canoe answered at once, nose swinging more than Joel wanted. He corrected from the back, blade deep, and the hull slid over gravel with a sound like a long zipper pulled under water.

"That was controlled," Alex said.

"By me."

"Group project."

Ahead, Will looked back from Mike's canoe. "If this is what opens up, I have notes."

"No one is accepting notes," Mike said.

The current carried the first canoe around a low grass point. For a second only Will's red PFD showed between alder stems, moving without the rest of him. Then Mike's paddle flashed and the whole canoe came into view again, ordinary and exactly where it should be.

Joel did not know why he noticed that. A man saw a boat vanish behind brush and come back. That was what bends did. The day was still only a day.

Still, the channel had begun to shift sounds around. A hull scrape came from ahead after the hull that made it had cleared the gravel. Mike's paddle knocked once against a submerged stem and the note came back duller from the bank. Will said something too low to catch, and the alder returned only the shape of it. Nothing was wrong. Water carried sound. Brush broke it apart. The ordinary world had enough tricks.

Beyond the run, the water widened.

The lake did not look large on the map. Nothing about it had looked large when they were drinking coffee at Will's kitchen table three weeks earlier, Mike tracing the route with one thick finger while Will corrected scale and Alex asked whether there would be fish he could morally refuse to eat. On paper, this lake had been one more pale shape between black lines. In front of them it took the wind cleanly from the west and turned it into long dark strips.

Mike let his canoe drift just short of the opening. Will had the map case braced across his knees, one hand on it as if paper could blow away even through plastic.

Mike cleared his throat.

It was a small sound, almost nothing, but Joel had learned to hear the decision inside it. Mike made that sound before he merged into traffic, before he told a waitress the bill was wrong, before he moved a canoe against other people's judgment. The throat cleared. Then the world received the plan.

"We cross close to shore," Mike said. "No hero lines."

"I was saving my hero line for dinner," Alex said.

No one gave him much. The wind had already reached into the channel and found the front of Joel's canoe.

Will looked down at the map. "Camp is after the portage on the north side."

"Correct."

"How bad is the portage?"

"It exists."

"That is not a measurement."

"It's a portage."

"Also not a measurement."

Mike gave him a flat look over one shoulder. "Do you want me to say it's charming?"

"I want you to say if it's a half mile of polite dirt or a mile of murder roots."

"Those are not standardized units," Alex said.

"Thank you, Alex."

"I'm pro-science."

Mike pointed with his paddle. "We go now. We don't sit in the wind talking about vocabulary."

That was reasonable. It was also Mike. Those two facts often arrived together in a way that made arguing feel indulgent. Joel set his paddle low and said, "Ready?"

Alex rolled his shoulders. His sleeves were still damp from the beaver work, and there was a mud line dried along one forearm like a cuff. "Seattle prepared me emotionally for this."

"Seattle prepared you to complain in layers."

"Exactly. Emotional layering."

"Paddle."

"Yes, Dad."

Joel did not answer that, partly because the wind took the canoe then and partly because fatherhood, even as a joke, belonged to a room he had not entered yet. He and Mara had talked about children in the careful half-specific way people used when they wanted a future but did not want to set a trap for it. Maybe. Someday. If work held. If the house was not too small. If wanting did not turn too quickly into duty. She had said this at the kitchen counter in Madison, one hand on the mail, the other on a tape measure because they were supposed to be deciding whether a crib would ever fit in the small back room. Joel had said there was time. He had been saying that about many things.

Here there was no room for the future to be that grammatical. There was only the next stroke, the next gust, Alex in front of him dipping too hard on one side, the canoe wanting to turn its face to the wind.

"Left a little," Joel said.

"My left or boat left?"

"The boat's left is your left."

"That's comforting in a limited way."

"Just a little."

Alex adjusted. He was better when there was something to fight. In easy water he let the paddle wander and turned his head toward every remark he wanted to make. In wind he became more useful, almost solemn. He leaned into the strokes. His shoulders worked under the PFD. Spray came over the front and darkened his sleeves again, making the morning's mud soften and run.

Ahead, Mike and Will angled along the lee side of the shore where the waves had less distance to build. Even there the lake slapped at the hulls. The spruce on the western shore bent all one way. Clouds had thickened in that direction, not storm clouds yet, just low gray freight moving over the trees.

"How much farther?" Alex asked.

"Across," Joel said.

"Great. Precise."

"Do you want a number?"

"Would it help?"

"No."

"Then I want a better lie."

Joel smiled despite himself. "Fifteen minutes."

"See? That feels like care."

It took longer than fifteen. It took the kind of time that made the body stop counting and begin bargaining in small pieces. Get to the leaning pine. Get to the rock with the white streak. Get past the opening where the wind came harder. Joel corrected and corrected again. Alex's paddle rose too high when he tired, scattering water back into Joel's lap.

The waves were not large enough to frighten anyone with sense. That made them worse in a smaller, meaner way. They were only large enough to keep correcting the boat, only cold enough to sting when spray came over the side, only steady enough to remind Joel that the lake did not have to become dangerous all at once. It could spend them by ounces.

"Lower," Joel said.

"I know."

"Then lower knowingly."

"You have a very managerial tone for a passenger."

"I'm in the back."

"Emotionally a passenger."

Mike called something from ahead. The wind tore the first half away.

"What?" Will called back, though Mike was three feet behind him.

"Stay in. Don't cut across."

"I wasn't cutting across."

"I know what your boat looks like when you're thinking about it."

Will said something that looked from a distance like a correction but did not carry. His hand moved in the air, two fingers and a little chop. Then he bent back to paddling.

A few strokes later, after Mike's canoe had gone behind a standing dead pine on the point, the same command seemed to come thinly off the shore.

Stay in.

Not a voice, Joel told himself. Not even words, really. Wind in spruce. Mike's last call snagged and thrown back by the bank. The lake was shaped for tricks: rock face, water, trees bent in one direction, all of it taking a simple sound and shaving the source from it. Joel looked ahead until he had Mike's shoulders in the back of the canoe, Will's cap low over the map case. Sight settled the matter, or gave him permission to keep paddling.

"What?" Alex said.

"Nothing."

"You keep hearing nothing."

"Paddle lower."

"That is a separate issue."

For a while the two canoes moved with the same ugly rhythm. Dip. Pull. Correct. Drift. Dip again. The wind flattened the small talk before it could become talk, and that helped. Joel could hear his own breath and Alex's breath, not in sync, not far from it. Once Alex missed a stroke and said, quietly enough that only Joel heard, "Come on," not to the lake or to Joel but to himself.

Joel liked him most in those unadvertised seconds. Then Alex looked over his shoulder and said, "You enjoying my suffering back there?"

"Academically."

"That feels like a yes."

The lake had no interest in their argument. It let them move, resisted them evenly, accepted the blades and gave back distance in stubborn increments. Joel had always liked that about water when he trusted it: the absence of persuasion. It took shape from wind, rock, depth, and pressure. It did not become kinder because someone had planned well.

Close to shore, the water turned the color of tea over stone. The landing appeared and disappeared behind the lift of the bow: alder, dark notch, low rock slick with wave wash. Mike raised his paddle once and pointed, though everyone could see there was nowhere else to go.

The far shore came closer. Alder overhung a dark notch where the portage landing should be. The waves were smaller there but messier, coming off the rock and crossing themselves. Mike brought his canoe in first and badly. The front scraped sideways on slick stone. Will stepped out too soon, planted one sandal, and sat down hard in two inches of water with a slap that made Alex bark out a laugh before he could stop himself.

"Grace," Mike said.

"Physics," Will said, sitting there with both hands braced behind him.

Alex lifted his paddle like a microphone. "Ladies and gentlemen, the aquatic dignity of Will."

"Don't," Will said.

Alex did it anyway, because of course he did. He put one hand to his chest and announced to the trees, "A man. A map. A complete loss to surface conditions."

"Alex," Joel said.

"What? This is how legends happen."

Will pushed himself up, wet from hip to calf on one side. He checked the map case before he checked himself, saw it had stayed sealed, and then looked angry that this was the sort of man he was. "If you make that a story, I will put a leech in your food."

"My food has standards."

"Your food is peanut butter noodles."

"Exactly. Standards."

Joel steered toward the same sloping rock, already irritated, already preparing to tell Alex to get out cleanly and hold the boat instead of turning the landing into a stage. The irritation was ready before Alex had earned it. That was the private unfairness Joel kept discovering in himself. Alex had made himself easy to judge, and Joel had taken the convenience.

"Front out," Joel said.

Alex put the paddle down, caught the painter line, and stepped into the water without flourish. His first foot slipped, but he recovered with a hard little hop and planted himself against the boat. "Hold up."

Joel held.

Alex bent, got both hands on the line, and took the canoe's weight before the next wave could turn it sideways. He did not look back to see if anyone had noticed. Joel stepped out, water cold over his ankles, and together they dragged the canoe high enough that it stopped knocking.

Will came back for his pack, trying to act dry enough to preserve authority. One of the shoulder straps had twisted and cinched wrong, the buckle buried under the load.

"Here," Alex said.

"I have it."

"You have it badly."

"That's different from needing help."

"Everything is different from everything if you talk long enough. Turn around."

Will stood there a second, suspicious on principle. Then he turned. Alex loosened the strap, fed it flat, tugged it through the buckle, and thumped the pack once with the heel of his hand. The plastic clicked cleanly into place.

"There. Try not to drown in the forest."

Will rolled his shoulders. The pack sat better. "That was almost useful."

"I contain multitudes."

"You contain crackers."

"Also multitudes of crackers."

Joel looked away before Alex could catch him looking. He had wanted the clean pleasure of annoyance and had been handed evidence against it. That was Alex's worst habit, maybe: not the performing, not the needling, but the way some small honest usefulness would appear inside it and make judgment feel cheap. A man could be exhausting and still good. The hard part was having to keep both facts.

Will did not thank him again, but when he lifted the pack he lifted it as if trusting the adjustment. That was the kind of gratitude their group allowed: an argument dropped before it could become one, a correction swallowed, a hand not slapped away the second time. Alex missed it because he was wringing water from one sleeve and making a face at the lake. Joel did not miss it.

The portage began behind the alder as a low track through wet grass. For twenty yards it seemed almost reasonable. Then the ground rose into spruce and rock, and the trail changed its mind.

They unloaded in heat trapped under trees. The wind that had bullied them across the lake did not enter the portage except as a high sound in the spruce tops. Down on the trail the air was close and damp. Mosquitoes found the sweat under collars and behind ears. Packs came out. Rods were lashed awkwardly. Paddles were collected and redistributed. Mike stood over the gear and assigned loads with the rough confidence of a man moving familiar pieces.

The landing gave them no place to be graceful. One canoe rocked half in water while Joel held it. The other lay at a bad angle on wet rock, back end still knocking when a wave found it. Bags had to be passed hand to hand, not set down, because everything below the first line of grass was mud or slick stone. Will kept saying, "Careful, careful," which helped no one except Will.

Mike checked his watch and then the sky. He did not say what the watch told him.

"Double carry," Will said.

"We can single if we don't baby it," Mike said.

"We have two canoes, four packs, food barrel, loose crap, rods, paddles, and your antique map collection."

"We can single."

"We can injure someone single."

"The trail's not that long."

Will stared at him. "Based on?"

"Based on me looking at the map."

"That's not a trail report."

"It is literally a map."

"Maps are documents of optimism."

"You're a document of something."

Alex lifted the food barrel an inch, set it down, and made a face. "This barrel has been eating at night."

"You're carrying paddles," Mike said.

"I feel seen."

Joel looked at the load and then at the trail. The canoes had to go. The packs had to go. The only question was how many times the same strip of mud and root would receive them. He had no desire to single carry badly for the pride of it. He also had no desire to spend an hour ferrying loads while daylight thinned. The day had already begun to lean away from them.

"We do the canoes and packs first," Joel said. "Come back for barrel and loose stuff if it sucks."

Mike glanced at him. Joel had entered the route argument, which was not his usual place.

"It won't suck," Mike said.

"Then the second trip will be scenic."

Alex pointed at Joel. "Madison with the wisdom of compromise."

"Don't reward him," Will said. "He'll form a committee."

Mike looked from the trail to the gear. His jaw worked once. Then he nodded. "Fine. Canoes and main packs. Quick."

This was how they accepted compromise. They pretended it had been the plan.

Mike took his canoe first. He bent under it, found the yoke, and lifted in one practiced motion that made the hull rise over him like a dull green roof. Will shouldered his pack and gathered rods. Joel took the second canoe. The weight settled across his shoulders, familiar and still unpleasant. A canoe was light until it was not. The first fifty steps always lied. Then the yoke found the same tender place on both shoulders and began teaching.

Under the hull the world became private and loud. Drops that had clung to the gunwales ran down and fell near Joel's ears. A trapped mosquito whined against the aluminum, furious at the sky it could see and could not reach. His own breath came back at him.

Alex took a pack and the bundle of paddles. "Look at me," he said. "A support class."

"Less talking," Mike called from under his canoe.

"More supporting."

"Walk."

The trail climbed.

At first Joel could see only the pale mud between his sandals, the black roots shining through it, the swinging tips of Alex's paddles ahead. Then the canoe shifted and lowered his world to a thin green strip at his feet. The underside of the hull held the sound close: his breath, the knock of a thwart against a branch, the creak of pack straps from the men ahead, mosquitoes whining in the trapped air beneath the canoe. The forest became fragments. Root. Stone. Mud. Alex's heel slipping. Will's rod tube tapping his pack. Mike's voice somewhere farther ahead.

"Step left. Rock's loose."

The warning came back down the line. Will repeated it. Alex said, "Left rock bad," which was not the same warning but did enough.

Joel stepped left.

The trail did not improve. It went up because some older route had decided that up was better than swamp, then crossed exposed granite where many feet had polished the lichen slick. Blowdown forced them into the brush twice. The first tree could be stepped over with care. The second lay at an angle across the trail, too high to step over cleanly under a canoe and too low to duck under without scraping the hull.

Mike, ahead, handled it with profanity and a sideways crouch. Will talked himself through it in short technical pieces.

"Angle. Don't put weight on that root. That's not stable. No, no, this is stupid."

"You need help?" Mike said.

"I need civil engineering."

Alex waited on the far side and looked back at Joel. "You good?"

"Not elegantly."

"Brand consistency."

Joel lowered one end of the canoe enough to see. The hull scraped bark. He backed up, shifted right, tried again. The yoke bit into one shoulder. His sandal slid in mud and caught. For a second the canoe wanted to twist him off the trail. Alex stepped in and got one hand on the front thwart.

"Hold up."

"I am."

"No, hold up with intention."

"Say something useful."

"Tilt right."

Joel tilted right. Alex lifted the nose just enough. The canoe cleared the blowdown and came through with a hollow clack against a branch that made something in the brush go still.

Not silent. Still.

Joel stood on the far side, breathing hard. The canoe pressed his shoulders. A fly landed on his cheek and walked there.

"You're welcome," Alex said, but softly, as if the stillness had reached him too.

Joel shifted the yoke. "Thank you."

Alex blinked. "Wow. Full sentence."

"Don't waste it."

From behind him came Will's cough.

Small. Dry. Almost a laugh but not a laugh. The sound Will made when a correction had jammed itself in his throat and wanted out.

Joel stopped.

He knew immediately what it was. He also knew where Will was: ahead of him, beyond Alex and the blown-down tree, bent under his pack beside Mike. Joel had seen him there seconds earlier.

The cough had come from behind.

The body did not believe in interpretation. It believed in direction. Joel turned as much as the canoe allowed and saw the trail dropping away behind them through spruce trunks and alder, empty except for wet roots and the place where their sandals had torn the mud. No person. No red PFD. No bright pack. The lake was hidden below the rise, though wind came up from it in pieces.

"What?" Alex said.

He was twenty feet ahead now, one hand still on the paddles, looking not frightened but inconvenienced by Joel's pause.

"Nothing."

"That's a bad answer."

"Bug."

"A bug coughed?"

Joel looked at him.

Alex lifted both hands as much as the paddles allowed. "I'm just saying, suspicious bugs out here."

Ahead, Will coughed.

Real. Visible. He had stopped beside Mike and bent his head, one fist near his mouth. The same sound, or close enough that the difference had no place to live. Then Will called, "You guys coming?"

Mike said, "Keep moving."

Alex looked at Joel. "See? Answered."

Joel started walking.

There were explanations, and because there were explanations Joel used them. Sound bent under a canoe. Sound bounced off rock. Will might have coughed ahead and the hull might have held it and thrown it behind him. A bird might make a dry click almost like a cough. Another group might be somewhere on the route, though they had seen no boats and heard no voices. Fear made duplicates. It took one fact and printed another beside it.

He told himself all this while the trail climbed.

The second half of the portage was worse because it looked better. The grade eased, but the tread vanished into wet moss and black mud. Where the trail crossed low places, old sticks had been thrown down by previous travelers, making a narrow corduroy that rolled underfoot. Pack straps creaked. Someone's buckle clicked over and over, regular as a clock. The canoe shifted with each step and made Joel's neck ache. Sweat ran down his spine and cooled when the wind found gaps between the trees.

At a small rise they stopped.

Not because Mike called a rest. Because everyone reached the same private limit at almost the same time and pretended the clearing had requested them. The trees opened enough to show water through black trunks on the other side, another lake or a long widening of the channel, dark under the west wind. The trail continued down through spruce toward it.

Joel lowered the canoe carefully, first to his thighs, then to the ground. The release of weight felt less like relief than proof of damage. His shoulders pulsed where the yoke had been. He stretched his fingers. They had gone numb around nothing.

Will dropped his pack and immediately checked his rod tips.

"If you say one thing about fishing," Mike said, "I will break that rod and make you carry both pieces."

"I wasn't going to say anything."

"You were looking at the water."

"Looking is not fishing."

"For you it is foreplay."

Alex made a wounded sound. "Gentlemen, I'm trying to recover spiritually."

"Recover quieter," Mike said.

Mike had the older map out before he drank water. Not the newer waterproof printout with clean labels and confidence, but the older folded sheet he liked because it had more contour lines, more little marks, more of the suggestion that somebody had once known the interior intimately enough to draw it. It was soft at the folds despite the map case. He put one thumb against their route and another farther inland.

Joel watched his thumb more than the map. The nail was flattened and pale from years of tools, boats, work Mike complained about and then did well. It pinned the paper as if the paper wanted to get away.

Will leaned over. "We're here?"

"Here." Mike tapped.

"Or here."

"No. Here."

"Those are not as different as your tone implies."

"They are if you know how to read a map."

"Jesus, listen to this guy," Alex said, but tiredly, with less theater in it than usual.

Joel drank from his bottle. The water tasted faintly of plastic and iodine from some old trip, though the bottle had been washed many times since. He watched Mike's thumb move to a tiny square inland beyond the next camp, connected to the route by a thin broken line that might have been trail, old road, boundary, or wish.

Before Mike spoke, he touched the left pocket of his PFD, a quick check as unconscious as a man patting his wallet before leaving a restaurant. The Highlander key was in there, sealed away with all the ordinary rescue of pavement and ignition. Then his hand returned to the map, and the future became paper again.

"Old maintenance cabin," Alex said. "Is that a cabin cabin or a map lie?"

Mike did not look up. "Map says there's a cabin."

The phrase came out with the flat sturdiness Mike gave to things he wanted settled. Map says. Ink as witness. Paper doing more work than paper should.

Will gave his dry half laugh, which became a cough at the end. "Map says there was a cabin when somebody cared enough to print this."

"Still a cabin."

"Could be a square of moss."

"Could be useful if weather comes in."

"Useful how? Does it have a phone? A road? A concierge?"

"It may be on an old maintenance trace."

"May be is doing a lot of unpaid labor."

Mike folded the map back just enough to keep Will from breathing on it. "If the wind stacks up tomorrow, or somebody twists an ankle, we know there is a feature inland."

"A feature," Will said.

"Yes."

"That's different from a plan."

"It is not the plan. It is information."

Joel looked from the printed square to the forest around them. Human marks did not feel permanent here. They felt like requests. A cabin was a promise people made to themselves about shelter, labor, authority, return. Then the roof went soft. The trail vanished. A tree fell through a wall. Moss took the steps. The map kept the square because paper aged more slowly than wood.

He almost said that. He had the sentence formed enough to know it would sound like him at his worst, watchful and useless and a little pleased with its own shape. Alex would call it a poem. Will would correct the historical assumptions. Mike would say a cabin was a cabin until proven otherwise. So Joel drank water instead.

Alex crouched beside the packs, elbows on his knees. For once he did not fill the pause. His face had gone slack with fatigue, and the absence of performance made him look older, not old exactly but closer to the age they all were when no one was reflecting them back. He noticed Joel looking and immediately raised his eyebrows.

"You coming or writing a poem?"

"Drinking water."

"Poem about water."

"Very short poem."

"Let's hear it."

"No."

"Needs work."

Mike capped his bottle. "Five minutes."

"That was not five minutes," Will said.

"It will have been."

They laughed a little, because the wording was stupid and because stopping too long would let their bodies understand what had been done to them. Joel checked his phone out of habit and found nothing. No service, no surprise. The battery was still high because airplane mode had made the device into a clock and camera and dead little mirror. He saw his own face darkly in the glass: sweat, stubble, one mosquito smear near his jaw. Behind his reflection, spruce needles and sky. He put the phone away before the blankness could become another fact.

The second trip back for the remaining gear was not scenic.

They left the canoes and two packs at the rise, marked by nothing because there was no one else to take them, and went back down for the food barrel, the smaller bags, the paddles Alex had grown tired of carrying, and the loose indignities of travel. Without the canoe Joel could see too much. The trail looked worse in full view. Every root seemed placed to prove intention was not required for malice. Mud held the shape of their first passage, heel marks filling with black water. At the blowdown, bark showed fresh scratches from the hulls.

Coming down empty-handed should have felt easier. It did not. Their bodies had already spent the easy part. Knees wobbled on the descent. Wet sandal straps rubbed the same raw places. The heat held low between trunks, close enough that each breath seemed used before it entered the lungs. Above them the wind kept moving in a freer country, visible only as spruce tops leaning and righting themselves.

Halfway down, something cracked off to the right.

All four men stopped. Not dramatically. Just the same small halt, bodies arriving at caution before conversation.

The crack was followed by a brushy movement low in the spruce and alder, quick enough to be an animal and brief enough to be nothing.

Will whispered, "You guys hear that?"

"Yes," Mike said.

"That's the second one today."

"Second what?"

"Animal in brush."

"We are in a forest."

"I'm aware of the setting."

Alex looked toward the sound, no smile on his face. The joking Alaska part of him had gone quiet. The actual Alaska, or whatever part of him had learned to listen before naming, stood in its place.

"Probably not large," he said.

"Probably," Mike said.

"I don't love that word."

"You don't have to love it. You have to pick up the barrel."

The brush did not move again. Joel watched the place until leaves became only leaves. There were many reasons for brush to move. Hare. Grouse. Squirrel. Wind arriving late at ground level. A branch settling after their passage. The mind wanted an actor. The woods supplied motion and withheld the rest.

"Could have been us," Joel said.

Will looked at him.

"From before," Joel said. "Branch settling after the canoes."

"Sure," Will said, too quickly.

Mike picked up the food barrel. "Good. Solved."

No one believed solved. They believed in moving because standing there made belief unnecessary.

They retrieved the gear.

On the climb back, the food barrel took turns punishing each of them. Mike carried it first and made no complaint, which made the load look lighter than it was. Will took it next and immediately began describing improved harness designs no one had asked for. Alex carried it after that and accused the group of packing "emotional ballast." Joel carried it the last stretch, one hand on the barrel strap, the other on a paddle bundle, the weight knocking against his thigh until a bruise began to announce itself.

The barrel latch clicked once against the buckle when he shifted it higher, the same clean plastic sound Alex had made fixing Will's strap.

He knew what it was. He was holding it. Still, the small hard sound made him look down, as if an object in his own hand had spoken from elsewhere.

At the rise, they loaded again. Nobody said lunch. Lunch had happened in the boats, in pieces, hours ago. Nobody said second camp as if naming it would make it closer. The afternoon had moved from warm to heavy. The light under the trees had flattened, and the brightness over the water beyond the trail had taken on the dull pewter look that came before evening.

The descent to the next water was steep in short sections, then muddy where springs crossed the trail. Mike went first with a pack. Will followed with rods and one loose bag held away from his body like it contained evidence. Alex took the paddles and the smaller dry bag. Joel lifted the canoe again.

His shoulders objected before the yoke settled. He made a sound through his nose and stood.

"You good?" Alex asked.

"Yes."

"That was a married yes."

"I'm not married yet."

"Practicing."

Joel adjusted his grip. "Walk."

Alex walked.

The trail narrowed between black spruce. Roots came across it in hard ribs. Once Joel's sandal slid and he had to take three quick steps under the canoe to keep from going down. The hull clipped a sapling and sprang it back. Somewhere ahead Mike called, "Rock on the right."

Will repeated, "Rock on the right."

Alex said, "Rock on the right, but spiritually."

The phrase came down the line and thinned out. Joel stepped left to avoid the rock, then right to avoid a mud hole. He could hear water ahead now: not lake chop, not the wind-crossed slap from the larger water, but a narrow sound, channel water moving through stones or over a lip. Second Camp was supposed to be near a constricted channel or marsh edge. Sparse sites, Mike had said. Good enough sites, Mike had also said, which meant sites that required agreement from people too tired to object.

The sound of water ahead should have helped. Water meant the end of the portage. It meant the weight would come off, even briefly. But after the wrong cough, direction had a small flaw in it. Ahead was still ahead. Behind was still behind. He knew this. Knowing it did not stop him from checking both.

The trail leveled. Light showed between trunks. Joel smelled mud before he saw water.

Then, behind him, from uptrail, came a soft click.

Not a branch. Not quite.

A buckle, maybe. A pack strap settling. One hard plastic tooth touching another.

Joel turned his head and the canoe turned with him, awkward and slow. The trail behind was empty. It rose between spruce trunks, already dark in the spaces where the afternoon could not get in. He stood still, listening. His breath filled the hull. His heartbeat moved in his ears. There was only wind high in the trees and the small water ahead.

"Joel?" Alex called from below.

He faced forward again. "Coming."

"Or writing a poem?"

The old joke irritated him less this time. Maybe because Alex sounded tired. Maybe because the question, repeated at a distance on a narrow trail, made the space behind Joel feel briefly inhabited by all the ordinary things that could have made that click: the remaining pack, a loose strap, a branch springing back, their own passage settling after them. The woods kept speaking in half-syllables and refusing the rest.

"Neither," Joel said. "I'm suffering plainly."

Alex grinned up at him from the lower trail. "That's the brand."

He waited there until Joel reached him, one hand on the sapling that marked the last bad step. He did not offer help because offering would make Joel refuse. He only held the sapling out of the way so the canoe could pass.

"You're becoming considerate," Joel said.

"Take it back."

"Withdrawn."

"Thank you."

They reached the water late.

The put-in was a bad one, mud and angled grass with a black channel sliding past. The lake beyond was not much of a lake, more a widening where water gathered itself before squeezing between alder again. Across it, past a ragged spit of spruce, a small break in the trees suggested the campsite. Or suggested a place where a campsite might be forced by need.

Mike stood with one hand on his hip, looking at the sky as if the sky had failed to meet posted expectations. Will set the rods down with exaggerated care. Alex dropped the paddles and sat on a wet log without checking it first. No one mocked him. That was how tired they were.

"We still crossing?" Will asked.

Mike looked at the distance, the wind, the darkening water. He cleared his throat, but this time no plan came out right away.

That pause did more than any admission would have done.

"It's right there," Mike said at last.

"Right there in daylight," Will said.

"We have daylight."

"We have the concept of daylight."

Alex leaned back on his hands. "I vote for any plan that ends with me horizontal before I become a labor issue."

Mike ignored him and looked at Joel. Joel disliked being pulled into the authority of a decision Mike still wanted to own.

"We can make that," Joel said. "If that's the camp."

"It's the camp," Mike said.

Will picked up the map case, then let it hang at his side. He was too tired to open it.

Alex looked across the water at the break in the trees. He had mud on one cheek now, a gray streak where he had wiped at a mosquito and made himself look younger and worse. "That's a campsite?"

"It's a site," Will said.

"Feels like a legal distinction."

"It is absolutely a legal distinction."

Mike bent for a pack. "Boats."

They loaded without ceremony. The crossing was short and ugly. Wind came down the channel in gusts and pushed them sideways. The canoes rode low with damp gear and tired men. Alex paddled hard in bursts, then stopped too long, then corrected when Joel told him to. Mike and Will led across, their boat dark against the dull water. The ragged spit of spruce slid past on their left. A raven called once from somewhere inland, harsh and unimpressed.

Halfway across, a hollow knock came from behind them.

Joel looked back. Their wake loosened in the wind. The far shore they had left was already a dark line, alder and spruce and mud. No boat. No paddle. Nothing moving except water against grass.

The sound had the body of a paddle touching a gunwale. Not hard. Not a signal. The kind of accidental knock that had followed them since the put-in, ordinary enough to disappear if a person had not become tired enough to count ordinary things. Joel found their paddles by sight: Alex's blade in front, his own blade dripping behind, Mike's and Will's rising together ahead.

"What?" Alex said, without turning.

"Paddle," Joel said.

Alex paddled.

The knock did not repeat. That helped less than it should have.

They came in at the far side one at a time. Mike and Will first, then Joel and Alex, noses of the canoes sliding into mud under overhanging alder. The landing smelled of wet leaves, old ash, and marsh. Mosquitoes lifted from the grass as if the ground had exhaled them. Back from the water, through spruce trunks and brush, Joel could see a small opening and the gray stones of a fire ring.

Second Camp.

No one said thank God. They were not that kind of men, or not where the others could hear. They pulled the boats higher and set packs in a line above the mud. The day had taken longer than Mike had promised and longer than Joel had privately allowed. It had not broken anything yet. It had only spent them down and placed them here, at a camp with poor sightlines and close brush and water talking quietly behind it.

The fire ring had been used recently enough to hold blackened foil, but not recently enough to mean company. Two cut log ends lay half-rotten at the edge of the opening. Beyond them the brush came close, alder and young spruce knitted tight enough that anything ten yards out would be sound before it was shape. The channel behind them kept moving, narrow and dark, taking the late light in broken pieces.

Alex stepped into the opening and turned once, assessing it with theatrical injury and real fatigue.

"Rustic," he said.

Nobody answered. Even Alex let the word die where it was.

Mike stood at the edge of the opening, breathing through his nose.

For once, he did not immediately tell them it was fine.

Then the wind moved in the spruce tops, and the mosquitoes found their faces, and work became the only available answer.
