# Chapter 21 - Bad Boat

The bad boat was not bad enough to abandon and not good enough to trust.

That was the shape of everything now.

Joel sat in back. Will sat in front with his injured hand wrapped in a plastic bag and tape, paddling awkwardly on one side. The canoe rode low but clean. Fog lifted from the channel in slow pieces. Alder brushed the gunwales. Water made almost no sound unless they touched bottom.

They used taps. One tap on the hull meant stop. Two meant look. A hand flat meant wait. Pointing was direction, warning, accusation, prayer.

The first beaver obstruction took fifteen minutes because they would not speak. Joel got out in cold water and steadied the stern. Will pulled from the front, grimacing when his bad hand slipped. The canoe rasped over sticks. The sound went out through the fog.

From downstream, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will froze with both hands on the painter rope.

Joel shook his head.

Alex said it again, farther away.

Will pulled. Joel pushed. They got over the dam and climbed back in with water running off their sleeves.

Later, Mike's voice called from behind, "Just keep moving."

They did.

Joel began to understand that refusal was not dramatic. It was physical and repetitive. Hear the voice. Feel the body answer. Stop the body. Paddle. Hear another voice. Stop again. Paddle. Survival became a set of small withheld motions.

At a narrow bend Will tapped twice. Joel looked.

On the right bank, in mud below alder roots, was a print. Not clear. Not enough. Broad at one end, long at another. Bear if you needed bear. Human if you needed worse. Water had filled it and made the edges soft.

Will pointed to it, then to his eyes, then lifted both hands in a question.

Joel shook his head. No time. No answer.

Will's jaw worked.

The channel widened near evening into a slow reach between black spruce. Fog thinned. The sky beyond it had the yellow-gray color of weather not finished with them. They needed a camp and did not want one. Camps were places that taught the thing.

They found a gravel bar barely high enough for bodies. No tents. No fire. Tarp low against a driftwood snag. Food beside them. Canoe pulled up and tied to Joel's wrist with the shortened painter, because losing the boat in sleep would be the simplest death left.

Will took the notebook.

If it only repeats, it cannot lie about new facts, he wrote.

Joel read the sentence twice.

Will wrote under it:

We can test.

Joel wrote:

No.

Will wrote:

There is a difference between answering and testing.

Joel underlined NO until the page tore.

Across the channel, Will's own voice said, "There is a difference."

Will shut his eyes.
