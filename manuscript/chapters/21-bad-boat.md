# Chapter 21 - Bad Boat

The bad boat was not bad enough to abandon and not good enough to trust.

That was the shape of everything now.

Joel sat in back with the Highlander key zipped inside his jacket and the shortened painter rope coiled where he could see it. Will sat in front, shoulders hunched, his injured hand wrapped in a plastic bag and taped at the wrist. The bag made a soft white shine whenever he lifted the paddle. It was the loudest thing about him.

The canoe rode low.

Food barrel just ahead of center. Tarp and damp sleeping bags wedged under the middle thwart. Map case flat in the bottom with grit still caught in the seal. Stove, pot, filter, first-aid kit. Mike's repair kit. Hatchet by Will's right knee. Spare paddle lashed on Joel's side where his hand could find it without searching. They had loaded the boat the way men loaded a sentence they were afraid to say, each object made to carry more than it should.

The canoe floated clean enough at first. That was worse than an obvious failure. A cracked hull would have decided for them. A split seam, a broken seat, a hole taking water from the first push. Instead the boat accepted the weight and settled into the tannin-dark channel as if consent meant safety.

Will looked back once.

Joel held up one hand, palm down. Easy.

Will nodded and faced forward.

They had decided on taps because taps were almost not speech. One tap on the hull meant stop. Two meant look. A hand flat meant wait. A pointed finger meant direction or danger or I mean that thing and not the other thing. A fist meant hold. A hand to the chest meant me. Two fingers to the eyes meant confirm by sight. They had written these into the notebook at the cache, though both men already understood that writing a rule did not make the body obey it.

The old canoe language was gone. Draw. Back. Left. Right. Hold up. Watch that rock. Wait. They had used those words for years, casually and without gratitude. Now every missing word made the boat worse.

Joel dipped his paddle and corrected the back end. Will pulled once on the right. The front nosed toward a black stitch of open water between alder stems. Fog lay low over the channel. It did not cover the water completely. It exposed pieces, then took them away.

Behind them, at the cache, the food barrel latch clicked.

Joel kept paddling.

Will's shoulders lifted, then settled. He did not turn. The blue barrel sat in the canoe in front of Joel, black ring closed, its side pressed against a wet dry bag. Visible. Accounted for. Heavy as fact.

Click.

Joel watched the barrel instead of the brush. It did not move.

Click.

Will tapped the gunwale once with two fingers. Not a signal. A reflex. He stopped himself and put his hand back on the shaft.

The channel bent left. Alder brushed both sides. Their branches stroked the gunwales with small dry fingernails, though the leaves were wet and shining. The canoe went through slowly because speed made sound and sound made decisions. Joel could feel every scrape in his knees.

He had trusted boats before this trip in the same unnoticed way he trusted floors. They were objects that held you above what would otherwise take you. Even when they rocked, even when they shipped water, even when Alex did some showy incompetent thing in front and laughed at his own balance, the boat had remained a boat. A human bargain with water. Shape, weight, skill, and buoyancy.

Now it was only one thin hull between two men and the cold fact beneath them. One boat. One route. One key in Joel's pocket. The arithmetic was simple and therefore indecent.

The first knock came from ahead.

Not loud. A hollow tap of wood or aluminum, placed somewhere beyond the fogged bend.

Will stopped paddling.

Joel let the canoe glide. He lifted his blade and held it over the water until drops fell from the tip. Drip. Drip. Drip. Each drop made a small ring and then no evidence.

The knock came again, farther right.

There could have been another canoe beyond the bend. There could have been a submerged log lifting with current, a branch tapping stone, a loose paddle in a boat they had not seen. There could have been a man with help, a ranger, another party, a fisherman who would look at them and understand none of it but still have a radio, a motor, a human explanation.

Joel knew how much hope sounded like a reason.

He touched two fingers to his eyes, then pointed at Will.

Will looked back and nodded. Visible.

Joel pointed ahead, then held up his flat hand. Wait by sight.

They drifted until the current gave out against a mat of drowned grass. The bend opened. No canoe. No person. No bright hull pulled into reeds. Only a fallen spruce leaning from the far bank, its root mass black and wet, and a crescent of foam caught against sticks.

The knock came once more from behind them.

Will's mouth tightened.

Joel shook his head.

They moved on.

Silence made them bad at everything. In ordinary travel, one word from the back corrected a bad angle before it became a problem. One curse made a submerged rock visible to the man in front. A laugh turned error into nothing. Without that, the canoe became a stubborn object. It found every shallow, every hidden branch, every place the current pressed sideways against alder roots.

Will's injured hand cost them time. He could paddle with it, but he could not grab quickly. When the canoe slid toward a snag, his good hand crossed awkwardly over the bad and the plastic bag squeaked against the shaft. He looked down each time as if betrayed by his own body. Joel knew that look. He had seen it on Will as a boy in church basements when a model rocket failed or a chess game turned because he had missed something obvious. Will did not hate failure most. He hated the moment when the world proved he had been using the wrong rule.

At the next shallow, the canoe ground to a stop.

Joel tapped once.

Will held still.

The boat had settled on gravel hidden under six inches of tea-colored water. The current pressed from the side, not enough to move them, enough to turn them if Joel gave it slack. He shifted his weight. The boat creaked.

Both men froze.

The sound was ordinary. A loaded canoe flexing under two tired men. But ordinary sounds no longer stayed in their assigned places. They went out into the woods and returned wearing someone else's need.

Joel set his paddle across the gunwales and leaned over the side. Water entered cold over his knuckles. The gravel under them was pale and small. No crack that he could see. No widening seam. The boat was hung, not broken.

He tapped the hull once, then pointed to himself and over the side.

Will shook his head sharply. His eyes went to the bank, then the fog, then back to Joel.

Joel understood the argument without paper. If Joel got out, they were slower. If Joel got out, there was a moment when the boat had one man in it and one man in the water. If Joel got out, cold took more from him. If they stayed, the current could turn the hull broadside and pin it harder. There was no choice that did not introduce a new way to fail.

Joel pointed to the shallow, then made a pushing motion. He pointed to Will, then the front rope.

Will's jaw moved once around a word he did not let out. He nodded.

Joel slid over the side into water that reached just above his knees.

The cold bit cleanly. It took the heat from his calves, then his thighs, then made a narrow cold line upward through his body. His hiking sandals sank into gravel and mud. He held the back handle with both hands and lifted enough to lighten the hull.

Will got out at the front more slowly. His bandaged hand stayed high. With the other hand he took the painter and pulled. The canoe rasped over gravel. The sound carried forward through the fog, a long rough admission.

From downstream, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will stopped with the painter tight across his body.

Joel shook his head once. Hard.

Alex said it again, farther away. Same brisk annoyance as a man whose rope had caught on a beaver dam. Same useful command. Same dead mouth.

Will's eyes shone in the gray light. Not tears exactly. Cold, exhaustion, the body's stupid water. He turned forward and pulled.

The boat came free.

They walked it another ten yards before the channel deepened. Joel climbed in first because if the back swung loose they would lose the boat's direction. His wet legs dragged mud over the bottom. Will got in with a quiet broken motion, planting his good hand, folding the bad one close, settling low.

Neither looked back.

For a while they made progress.

The channel narrowed, widened, and narrowed again. Beaver work had changed it since any map had cared. Old sticks silvered under water. Chewed points rose from the mud like sharpened stakes. On one bank a lodge humped under alder, its upper sticks black with rain. The place smelled of wet bark and old leaves and animal labor. The beavers had made a world suited to themselves. Men entered it by dragging aluminum across the evidence.

Twice they slid over submerged dams. Once Joel got out again and felt mud take his sandal to the ankle. Once Will nearly lost the front rope when his bad hand closed late and the painter burned across the plastic. His face went white. He did not cry out.

Joel stopped the boat with his thigh against the gunwale. The cold water pressed fabric to skin. He pointed to Will's hand.

Will lifted it. The plastic bag had torn at one taped edge. Water had gotten in. The bandage beneath showed a spreading gray.

Joel made for shore.

They found a strip of mud under spruce roots, not a landing but a place the canoe could be held. Joel kept one hand on the gunwale while Will knelt and unwrapped the bag. The wound across his palm looked angry, swollen at the edges, the skin too pale around it. Not infected yet, maybe. Or only not visibly enough for the word to help.

Will stared at it with the same offended attention he gave broken gear.

Joel took the notebook from inside his jacket. The cover was soft from damp. He wrote:

REWRAP. NO ARGUMENT.

Will read it and gave him a look that almost had humor in it.

He wrote beneath:

I WAS SAVING MY BIG SPEECH.

Joel surprised himself by almost smiling. The expression stopped before it reached his mouth. Humor had become another form of sound, one more thing the world might learn if they gave it air.

He took the first-aid kit from the top pack. They worked without speaking. Dry gauze, as dry as anything remained. Tape. A new plastic bag from the food scraps. More tape at the wrist. Will flexed his fingers and winced.

Across the channel, hidden in spruce, Mike cleared his throat.

The sound did what it always did. It arranged the air into expectation. Decision coming. Complaint coming. Some blunt pronouncement meant to reduce the number of possible worlds.

Will stared at Joel.

Mike said, "Just keep moving."

Not loud. Not commanding in a theatrical way. Almost tired. A better imitation than some because Mike had often sounded tired when he meant to sound certain.

Joel wrote:

NOT MIKE.

Will took the pencil.

IT KNOWS WHEN WE STOP.

Joel looked at the line.

He wanted to write: It knows sound, not time. He wanted to write: It can hear us stop. He wanted to write: We do not know what it knows. All of those were ideas shaped like small doors, and Will's mind would go through each one and open another.

Joel wrote:

MAYBE. MOVE ANYWAY.

Will read it. His eyes came up, sharp and resentful.

That was the first clear look of the afternoon: not fear, not grief, but distrust. Will was beginning to believe that Joel's restraint had become its own authority, another Mike phrase with less volume. Do not answer. Do not test. Do not say names. Keep moving, but in Joel's handwriting. The thought struck Joel with enough force that he almost wrote an apology.

He did not.

Apology was not the same as repair. It might not even be safer in pencil.

They pushed off again.

The fog thinned by degrees. The sky above it had a yellow-gray cast, late afternoon already draining from the day. Light came flat through spruce and made every wet thing show its edge. Joel checked the map when the channel widened enough to drift. The paper inside the case had dampened at the folds. He could still read the blue line, the faint bend, the small marsh sign, the next widening that might be a lake or might be another lie told at map scale. North Road Bridge was still beyond too many inches of paper. The Highlander key in his pocket pressed against his ribs when he leaned forward.

A key was an absurd object in wilderness. A little cut piece of metal and plastic waiting to become important only if a man reached a machine on a road. Before that, it was weight without use. After that, it might be everything. Joel kept touching it anyway, once each hour or each fear. The habit was dangerous because habits had sound. Zipper. Fabric. Fingernail against plastic. He forced his hand away.

The canoe bumped something under the surface.

A dull knock rose through the hull and into Joel's knees.

Will turned, eyes wide.

Joel pointed down: here. Real.

Then another bump came from the left bank.

Not echo. Not the same sound carrying. A separate hollow knock from alder roots six feet away, where no hull was moving.

Will's face changed. Joel saw the thought arrive. If it could make boat sounds from the bank, it could make damage where there was none. Or it could cover real damage with false evidence. Sound had become bad data.

Joel tapped once. Stop.

They held still in the current. The canoe turned a few degrees. Joel corrected with the smallest possible stroke. He leaned over and ran his fingers along the inside seam by his seat. Water in the bottom, but not rising. A few cups from wet gear and feet. Nothing pouring. Nothing breathing through the hull.

Will pointed ahead. His face said: continue?

Joel nodded.

Will pointed at the boat, then the water, then made a flat falling motion with his hand.

If it fails.

Joel nodded again. Yes.

Will took the notebook and braced it on the food barrel. Writing in a canoe was awkward and slow. The pencil tore at the damp page.

IF BOAT FAILS WE LOSE EVERYTHING.

Joel read the line while the canoe drifted. He could hear water sliding along the hull, each small touch precise and unhelpful.

He wrote:

BOAT FAILS HERE OR BACK. FORWARD HAS CAR.

Will stared at the word CAR.

It looked childish on the page. Not Highlander. Not take-out. Not North Road Bridge. Just CAR, the object reduced to its promise. Doors. Engine. Heat. A road. A machine that could carry a body without asking him to paddle.

Will wrote:

IF ROAD STILL THERE.

Joel remembered Mike's map spread on the hood before the trip, the North Road Bridge drawn with the same printed confidence as lakes and portages and the cabin that had given them nothing. Human marks aged badly. Roads became brush. Cabins became mouse nests. Bridges washed out, or did not, with no interest in men's plans.

He wrote:

IF NOT, STILL FORWARD.

Will did not answer.

The next bend gave them the print.

Will saw it first. Two taps on the hull.

Joel looked right.

Below a wall of alder roots, in a small tongue of exposed mud, a depression held brown water. Broad at one end, long at another. Not clear enough to name and too clear to ignore. The edges had softened in the rain. One side showed something like a heel. The other splayed into torn mud. Bear if you needed bear. Human if you needed worse. A kneeling place if you needed a third category and had no word for it.

Will pointed to the mark. Then to his eyes. Then lifted both hands slightly from his paddle, palms up.

Joel shook his head.

Will pointed again, harder. Evidence.

Joel pointed at the channel ahead, then at the sinking light.

Will's jaw worked. The old Will would have said there is a difference between not knowing and refusing to know. The old Joel would have told him to save it for camp and meant later, genuinely later, a time when speech was not a gift to the trees.

No time. No answer.

The canoe drifted past the print.

Joel looked at it until the bend took it. He wanted it to become one thing before it vanished. It did not. The mud held its shape without confessing.

Late afternoon made the water look stiller than it was. The channel opened into a slow reach between black spruce, no wider than a narrow road, with a gravel bar shouldering out from the left bank. The bar rose only a foot above the water. A driftwood snag lay across it, silver and smooth where old floods had rolled it. Behind it, spruce grew too tight for tents. Beyond the spruce, nothing Joel could see promised higher ground.

They needed a camp and did not want one.

Camps were where human routine collected. Tarp lines. Food. Zippers. Fire. Bodies turning in sleep. Names nearly spoken. Every camp taught the thing a room and then asked men to lie down inside it.

Joel pointed to the bar.

Will looked at it, then downstream, then back at Joel.

No one liked the decision. That made it likely.

They landed with care that took too long. Joel stepped into ankle-deep water and held the canoe while Will climbed out stiffly. Gravel shifted under both of them with small clicking sounds. They unloaded only what could not stay in the boat overnight or what they needed within arm's reach. Food barrel. Tarp. Sleeping bags. Filter. Notebook. Hatchet. The rest stayed lashed. If they had to move fast, the boat had to be more than a pile of choices.

Joel pulled the canoe up until its front rested on gravel and its back still touched water. Then he pulled it higher, hated the scrape, and pulled again. He tied the shortened painter around the driftwood snag, then around his own wrist with a loose loop he could free if he had to. He sat for a moment studying the knot.

Will watched him.

Joel wrote:

IF BOAT MOVES, I WAKE.

Will took the pencil.

IF YOU SLEEP.

Joel read it. The line had no accusation in handwriting, which meant it had all of them. He had not slept properly since before Mike died. Will had not either. Their faces had become unfamiliar from fatigue, stripped to bone and wet beard and eyes that looked older than their bodies.

Joel wrote:

IF EITHER SLEEPS, GOOD.

Will gave a silent breath that might once have been a laugh.

They made the tarp low against the driftwood and two spruce roots, more cover from dew than shelter. No fire. Smoke would not matter much, but flame made a center, and centers drew old habits. Food stayed beside them instead of hung. Animal risk had not disappeared, but climbing into brush in near dark to hang a barrel felt like obeying a worse animal. Joel set the hatchet where both could see it and neither could reach for it accidentally.

They filtered water at the edge. The pump made a soft, regular squeak.

After ten strokes Joel stopped.

The same squeak continued across the channel.

Will's head came up.

The filter in Joel's hands was still.

Squeak.

Squeak.

Then water dripping from a lifted paddle. Slow drops. Exact drops.

Their paddles lay on the gravel beside the canoe.

Will closed his eyes for one second. When he opened them, his face had hardened into something almost calm.

They finished filtering. Joel hated each pump. The sound went out and did not belong to him once it left.

Dinner was cold food eaten with fingers. Tortillas damp at the edges. Peanut butter from a pouch. A handful of trail mix with grit in it. Will sorted raisins out without seeming to know he was doing it, then stopped and shoved the whole handful into his mouth. Alex would have said something about that. Joel knew the shape of the joke without hearing words. He looked at the water until the impulse passed.

The light lowered. Fog returned from the channel in strips. The canoe sat where they had left it, ugly and necessary, its aluminum side dull, its shortened rope running from the handle to the snag to Joel's wrist. Bad boat. Good boat. Only boat.

Will took the notebook.

He wrote slowly because his hand hurt.

IF IT ONLY REPEATS, IT CAN'T LIE ABOUT NEW FACTS.

Joel read the sentence twice.

Will wrote under it:

WE CAN TEST.

The bar seemed to narrow around them.

Joel took the pencil.

NO.

Will did not look at him. He kept looking at the page, as if the page were the less frightening face.

He wrote:

TESTING IS NOT ANSWERING.

Joel wrote:

YES IT IS.

Will's mouth opened, closed. He pressed the pencil so hard the point broke. He stared at the broken graphite with an anger so tired it had no heat.

Joel found the spare pencil stub in the notebook pocket and handed it to him.

Will wrote:

WE NEED TO KNOW WHAT IT UNDERSTANDS.

Joel almost wrote, It does not understand. But that was faith disguised as caution. They knew less than they needed. They had survived by acting on assumptions and calling them rules because rules were easier to obey than guesses.

He wrote:

KNOWING COSTS SOUND.

Will read it and shook his head once.

He wrote:

WRITING COSTS NOTHING.

Joel looked at the dark line of the opposite bank. The spruce there stood close enough together that dusk gathered between trunks before it gathered on the water. The channel made a narrow mirror of the sky, then broke it around stones and drowned sticks.

Writing removed tone. That was why it had saved them. It made grief blunt and orders plain. It kept Will from making a sentence into a door the thing could walk through. But writing had begun to collect its own false comfort. It felt safe because it was silent. It felt private because the page stayed between their hands. Both feelings might be wrong.

Joel wrote:

WE DON'T KNOW THAT.

Will read it.

His face changed, not toward fear exactly. Toward insult. The last room had lost its lock.

He wrote:

THEN WE HAVE NOTHING.

Joel wanted to deny it. The want rose with the old physical urgency of speech. He could see Will at sixteen, sitting in a church basement on a folding chair, explaining to a youth pastor why the lesson's metaphor did not work if anyone cared about actual shepherding. He could see Will at twenty-six, drunk in Joel's first apartment, correcting Mike about a canoe neither of them yet owned. He could see Will two mornings ago, line in hand, trying to believe fish still existed as fish and not as sound waiting to be used.

Joel wrote:

WE HAVE SIGHT.

Then:

WE HAVE BOAT.

Then, after a pause:

WE HAVE EACH OTHER IF WE DON'T GIVE THAT AWAY.

Will looked at the last line for a long time.

Across the channel, in Will's own voice, someone said, "There is a difference."

Will shut his eyes.

The phrase had been his once, ordinary as a cough, said on the first real day when distinctions still helped men feel clever. There is a difference. Between echo and voice. Between a map and ground. Between testing and answering. Between surviving with someone and merely remaining alive beside him.

The imitation did not know which difference it meant.

That did not make it harmless.

Joel put two fingers to his eyes and waited until Will opened his.

Visible.

Will nodded. His eyes were wet now, unmistakably, and furious about it.

Joel wrote:

IT HEARD THAT BEFORE.

Will took the pencil.

IT USED IT CORRECTLY.

Joel looked at the line and felt the cold of the water return inside his clothes.

He wrote:

IT USED IT NEAR YOU.

Will read it. His hand stayed on the page.

The difference mattered. Maybe. It was not understanding, necessarily. It was placement. Association. Will plus correction. Will plus argument. Will plus a phrase that had always opened another sentence. The thing did not need meaning if it could learn which wound a sound belonged to.

From behind the tarp, a rope tapped aluminum.

Joel looked at the canoe.

The painter ran visibly from its front handle to the snag to his wrist. The hull did not move.

Tap.

Will turned his head only enough to confirm the boat by sight.

Tap.

The sound came again from behind the tarp, though the canoe sat in front of them.

Joel wrote:

NOT BOAT.

Will touched the rope where it crossed Joel's wrist. Not affection. Confirmation. The line was real. The boat was there. Joel was there. Will's fingers were cold through the plastic.

They lay down under the tarp before full dark because darkness would come whether they prepared for it or not. Joel kept his wrist outside the sleeping bag so the painter's loop could tug him awake if the canoe shifted. The gravel under his hip was sharp. The tarp breathed damp air against his face. Will lay close enough that Joel could feel each controlled movement, each withheld cough, each time his body almost turned toward a sound and stopped.

Across the channel, Mike cleared his throat once.

Neither man moved.

Farther downstream, Alex laughed under his breath.

Neither man moved.

Then, from somewhere in the fog where no boat touched shore, came the hollow knock of a paddle against a gunwale. Once. Then silence long enough to become another kind of knock.

Joel kept his eyes open on the canoe's dim shape. Bad boat. Only boat. The thing that might fail them and the thing they had to trust until it did.

The rope around his wrist held.

For that night, before sleep or whatever passed for it, that was the whole world.
