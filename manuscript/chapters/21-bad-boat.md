# Chapter 21 - Bad Boat

The bad boat was not bad enough to abandon and not good enough to trust.

That was the shape of everything now.

Joel sat in back with the Highlander key zipped inside his jacket and the shortened painter coiled where he could see it. Will sat in front, shoulders high, his injured hand wrapped in plastic and taped at the wrist. The bag had gone cloudy from his sweat. It flashed white whenever he lifted the paddle.

The canoe rode low.

Food barrel just ahead of center. Tarp and damp sleeping bags wedged under the middle thwart. Map case flat against the ribs, grit still caught in the seal. Stove, pot, filter, first-aid kit, Mike's repair kit. Hatchet by Will's right knee. Spare paddle lashed on Joel's side where his hand could find it without searching. Every object had been chosen against another object. Every thing they carried made the boat worse. Every thing left behind made the future smaller.

The hull accepted the weight and settled into the tannin-dark channel.

That was worse than an obvious failure. A split seam would have decided for them. A cracked seat, a hole drinking water from the first push, a thwart giving under Will's hand. Instead the canoe floated, heavy and reluctant, as if staying above water meant consent.

Will looked back once.

Joel held one hand palm down.

Easy.

Will nodded and faced forward.

They had made a system at the cache because systems were what men made when the world stopped answering properly. One tap on the hull meant stop. Two meant look. A flat hand meant wait. A pointed finger meant direction or danger or that thing, not the other. A fist meant hold. Two fingers to the eyes meant confirm by sight. Hand to chest meant me. They had written the signs into the notebook, though both men knew writing a rule did not make the body obey it.

The old canoe language was gone. Draw. Back. Left. Right. Hold up. Watch that rock. Wait.

They had used those words for years without gratitude. Now each missing word made the boat harder to keep alive.

Joel dipped his paddle and corrected the back end. Will pulled once on the right. The front nosed toward a black stitch of open water between alder stems. Fog lay low over the channel, not hiding everything, only making pieces of it. Bank, then nothing. Spruce root, then water. A pale stick floating where a rock should have been.

Behind them, at the cache, the food barrel latch clicked.

Joel kept paddling.

Will's shoulders lifted, then settled. He did not turn. The blue barrel sat in the canoe in front of Joel, black ring closed, side pressed against a wet dry bag. Visible. Accounted for. Heavy as fact.

Click.

Joel watched the barrel instead of the brush. It did not move.

Click.

Will tapped the gunwale once with two fingers. Not the signal. Reflex. He caught himself and put his hand back on the shaft.

The channel bent left. Alder brushed both sides. Branches stroked the gunwales with small dry sounds, though the leaves were wet and shining. The canoe moved slowly because speed made noise and noise made choices. Joel felt every scrape in his knees.

He had trusted boats before this trip the way he trusted floors. They were objects that held a body above what would otherwise take it. Even when they rocked, even when they shipped water, even when Alex did some showy incompetent thing in front and laughed at his own balance, the boat had remained a boat. A human bargain with water. Shape, weight, skill, buoyancy.

Now it was one thin hull between two men and the cold fact beneath them. One boat. One route. One key in Joel's pocket. The arithmetic was simple and indecent.

The first knock came from ahead.

Not loud. A hollow tap of wood or aluminum, placed somewhere beyond the fogged bend.

Will stopped paddling.

Joel let the canoe glide. He lifted his blade and held it over the water until drops fell from the tip. Drip. Drip. Drip. Each drop made a small ring, then no evidence.

The knock came again, farther right.

There could have been another canoe beyond the bend. There could have been a submerged branch tapping stone, a loose paddle in a boat they had not seen, a fisherman who would look at them and understand none of it but still have a radio. There could have been help. Hope had always known how to sound practical.

Joel touched two fingers to his eyes and pointed at Will.

Will looked back. Visible.

Joel pointed ahead, then held up his flat hand. Wait by sight.

They drifted until the current gave out against a mat of drowned grass. The bend opened. No canoe. No person. No bright hull pulled into reeds. Only a fallen spruce leaning from the far bank, root mass black and wet, and a crescent of foam caught against sticks.

The knock came once more from behind them.

Will's mouth tightened.

Joel shook his head.

They moved on.

Silence made them bad at everything. In ordinary travel one word from the back corrected a bad angle before it became a problem. One curse made a submerged rock visible to the man in front. A laugh turned error into nothing. Without that, the canoe became stubborn and literal. It found every shallow, every hidden branch, every place where current pressed sideways against alder roots.

Will's hand cost them time. He could paddle with it, but he could not grab quickly. When the canoe slid toward a snag, his good hand crossed awkwardly over the bad, and the plastic bag squeaked against the shaft. Each time he looked down as if betrayed by a tool.

Joel knew that look. He had seen it in church basements when a model rocket failed to launch, in Will's first apartment when a chess game turned because he had missed an obvious fork, on lake trips when a knot held badly after he had told everyone else to learn the difference. Will did not hate failure most. He hated the moment when the world proved he had been using the wrong rule.

At the next shallow, the canoe stopped.

Joel tapped once.

Will held still.

They had settled on gravel hidden under six inches of tea-colored water. Current pressed from the side, not enough to free them, enough to turn them if Joel gave it slack. He shifted his weight. The boat creaked.

Both men froze.

A loaded canoe flexing under tired men. Ordinary. But ordinary sounds no longer stayed where they were made. They went into the brush and came back wearing purpose.

Joel set his paddle across the gunwales and leaned over the side. Cold water covered his knuckles. The gravel under them was pale and small. No crack that he could see. No seam opening. Hung, not broken.

He tapped the hull once, pointed to himself, then over the side.

Will shook his head sharply. His eyes went to the bank, the fog, Joel's face.

Joel understood. If Joel got out, they were slower. If Joel got out, one man was in the boat and one in the water. If Joel got out, cold took more from him. If they stayed, the current could turn the hull broadside and pin it harder. There was no choice that did not open another way to fail.

Joel pointed to the shallow and made a pushing motion. He pointed to Will, then to the front rope.

Will's jaw moved once around a word he did not let out. He nodded.

Joel slid over the side into water just above his knees.

The cold bit cleanly. It took his calves first, then his thighs, then sent a narrow line upward through his body. His sandals sank into gravel and mud. He held the back handle with both hands and lifted enough to lighten the hull.

Will got out at the front more slowly, bad hand held high. With the other hand he took the painter and pulled. The canoe rasped over gravel. The sound ran forward through the fog, long and rough.

From downstream, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will stopped with the painter tight across his body.

Joel shook his head once. Hard.

Alex said it again, farther away. Same brisk annoyance. Same practical timing. A man whose rope had caught on a beaver dam. A man asking not for belief, only delay.

Will's eyes shone in the gray light. Cold, exhaustion, the body's water. He turned forward and pulled.

The boat came free.

They walked it another ten yards before the channel deepened. Joel climbed in first, because if the back swung loose they would lose the boat's direction. His wet legs dragged mud over the bottom. Will got in with a broken care, planting his good hand, folding the bad one close, settling low.

Neither man looked back.

For a while they made progress.

The channel narrowed, widened, and narrowed again. Beaver work had changed it since any map had cared. Old sticks silvered under water. Chewed points rose from mud like sharpened stakes. A lodge humped under alder, its upper sticks black with rain. The place smelled of wet bark and old leaves and animal labor. The beavers had made a world suited to themselves. Men entered it by dragging aluminum across the evidence.

Twice they slid over submerged dams. Once Joel got out and felt mud take his sandal to the ankle. Once Will nearly lost the front rope when his bad hand closed late and the painter burned across the plastic. His face went white. He did not cry out.

Joel stopped the boat with his thigh against the gunwale. He pointed to Will's hand.

Will lifted it. The plastic had torn at one taped edge. Water had gotten in. The bandage beneath showed a spreading gray.

Joel made for shore.

They found a strip of mud under spruce roots, not a landing, only a place to hold the canoe while the water worried at the back. Joel kept one hand on the gunwale while Will knelt and unwrapped the bag. The wound across his palm looked swollen at the edges, the skin around it too pale. Not infected yet, maybe. Or only not visibly enough for the word to help.

Will studied it with the offended attention he gave broken gear.

Joel took the notebook from inside his jacket. The cover had gone soft from damp. He wrote:

REWRAP. NO ARGUMENT.

Will read it and gave him a look that almost had humor in it.

He wrote beneath:

I WAS SAVING MY BIG SPEECH.

Joel almost smiled. The expression stopped before it reached his mouth. Humor had become another sound they could not afford to let out.

He took the first-aid kit from the top pack. They worked without speaking. Dry gauze, or what passed for dry. Tape. A new plastic bag from the food scraps. More tape at the wrist. Will flexed his fingers and winced.

Across the channel, hidden in spruce, Mike cleared his throat.

The sound arranged the air into expectation. Decision coming. Complaint coming. Some blunt pronouncement meant to reduce the number of possible worlds.

Will looked at Joel.

Mike said, "Just keep moving."

Not loud. Not theatrical. Almost tired. Better because Mike had often sounded tired when he meant to sound certain.

Joel wrote:

NOT MIKE.

Will took the pencil.

IT KNOWS WHEN WE STOP.

Joel looked at the line.

He wanted to write that it knew sound, not time. He wanted to write that it could hear them stop. He wanted to write that they did not know what it knew. All of those were doors, and Will's mind would go through each one and open another.

Joel wrote:

MAYBE. MOVE ANYWAY.

Will read it. His eyes came up, sharp and resentful.

The look held. Not fear, not grief. Distrust. Will was beginning to see Joel's restraint as another authority, another man telling him what was useful, what counted, what could be risked. Do not answer. Do not test. Do not say names. Keep moving, but in Joel's handwriting.

Joel felt the resemblance and hated it.

He did not apologize.

An apology might repair nothing and still give the woods the shape of repair.

They pushed off.

The fog thinned by degrees. The sky above it had a yellow-gray cast, late afternoon already waiting inside the day. Light came flat through spruce and made every wet thing show its edge. Joel checked the map when the channel widened enough to drift. The paper inside the case had dampened at the folds. He could still read the blue line, the faint bend, the small marsh sign, the next widening that might be a lake or might be another lie told at map scale. North Road Bridge was still beyond too many inches of paper.

The Highlander key pressed against his ribs when he leaned forward.

A key was absurd in wilderness. A small cut piece of metal and plastic waiting to become important only if a man reached a machine on a road. Before that, it was useless weight. After that, it might be everything. Joel kept wanting to touch it. Zipper. Fabric. Fingernail against plastic. Even checking a fact had sound. He forced his hand away.

The canoe bumped something under the surface.

A dull knock rose through the hull and into Joel's knees.

Will turned, eyes wide.

Joel pointed down. Here. Real.

Then another bump came from the left bank.

Not echo. Not the same sound carrying. A separate hollow knock from alder roots six feet away, where no hull moved.

Will's face changed. The thought had arrived: if it could make boat sounds from the bank, it could make damage where there was none. Or cover real damage with false evidence. Sound had become bad data.

Joel tapped once.

They held still in the current. The canoe turned a few degrees. Joel corrected with the smallest stroke he could manage. He leaned over and ran his fingers along the inside seam by his seat. Water in the bottom, but not rising. A few cups from wet feet and gear. Nothing pouring. Nothing breathing through the hull.

Will pointed ahead. Continue?

Joel nodded.

Will pointed at the boat, then the water, then made a flat falling motion with his hand.

If it fails.

Joel nodded again. Yes.

Will took the notebook and braced it on the food barrel. Writing in a canoe was awkward and slow. The pencil tore at the damp page.

IF BOAT FAILS WE LOSE EVERYTHING.

Joel read the line while the canoe drifted. He could hear water sliding along the hull, each small touch exact and useless.

He wrote:

BOAT FAILS HERE OR BACK. FORWARD HAS CAR.

Will stared at the word CAR.

It looked childish on the page. Not Highlander. Not take-out. Not North Road Bridge. Just CAR, reduced to doors, engine, heat, road.

Will wrote:

IF ROAD STILL THERE.

Joel remembered Mike's map spread on the hood before the trip, the bridge printed with the same confidence as lakes and portages and the cabin that had given them nothing. Human marks aged badly. Roads became brush. Cabins became mouse nests. Bridges washed out or did not, with no interest in men's plans.

He wrote:

IF NOT, STILL FORWARD.

Will did not answer.

The next bend gave them the print.

Will saw it first. Two taps on the hull.

Joel looked right.

Below a wall of alder roots, in a small tongue of exposed mud, a depression held brown water. Broad at one end, long at another. Too soft at the edges to name and too clear to ignore. One side showed something like a heel. The other splayed into torn mud. Bear if you needed bear. Human if you needed worse. A kneeling place if you needed a third category and had no word for it.

Will pointed to the mark. Then to his eyes. Then lifted both hands slightly from his paddle.

Joel shook his head.

Will pointed again, harder.

Evidence.

Joel pointed at the channel ahead, then at the sinking light.

The old Will would have said there is a difference between not knowing and refusing to know. The old Joel would have told him to save it for camp and meant later, genuinely later, a time when speech was only speech.

No time. No answer.

The canoe drifted past the print.

Joel looked until the bend took it. He wanted it to become one thing before it vanished. It did not. The mud held its shape without confessing.

Late afternoon made the water look stiller than it was. The channel opened into a slow reach between black spruce, no wider than a narrow road, with a gravel bar shouldering out from the left bank. The bar rose only a foot above the water. A driftwood snag lay across it, silver and smooth where old floods had rolled it. Behind it, spruce grew too tight for tents. Beyond the spruce, nothing Joel could see promised higher ground.

They needed a camp and did not want one.

Camps were where human routine collected. Tarp lines. Food. Zippers. Fire. Bodies turning in sleep. Names nearly spoken. Every camp taught the thing a room and then asked men to lie down inside it.

Joel pointed to the bar.

Will looked at it, then downstream, then back.

No one liked the decision. That made it likely.

They landed with care that took too long. Joel stepped into ankle-deep water and held the canoe while Will climbed out stiffly. Gravel shifted under both of them with small clicking sounds. They unloaded only what could not stay in the boat overnight or what they needed within arm's reach. Food barrel. Tarp. Sleeping bags. Filter. Notebook. Hatchet. The rest stayed lashed. If they had to move fast, the boat had to be more than a pile of choices.

Joel pulled the canoe up until the front rested on gravel and the back still touched water. Then higher. He hated the scrape and pulled anyway. He tied the shortened painter around the driftwood snag, then around his own wrist with a loose loop he could free if he had to.

Will watched him.

Joel wrote:

IF BOAT MOVES, I WAKE.

Will took the pencil.

IF YOU SLEEP.

The line had no accusation in handwriting, which meant it could hold all of them. Neither of them had slept properly since before Mike died. Their faces had become unfamiliar from fatigue, stripped to bone and wet beard and eyes older than their bodies.

Joel wrote:

IF EITHER SLEEPS, GOOD.

Will gave a silent breath that might once have been a laugh.

They made the tarp low against the driftwood and two spruce roots, more cover from dew than shelter. No fire. Smoke would not matter much, but flame made a center, and centers drew old habits. Food stayed beside them instead of hung. Animal risk had not disappeared, but climbing into brush near dark to hang the barrel felt like obeying a worse animal. Joel set the hatchet where both could see it and neither could reach for it by accident.

They filtered water at the edge. The pump made a soft, regular squeak.

After ten strokes Joel stopped.

The same squeak continued across the channel.

Will's head came up.

The filter in Joel's hands was still.

Squeak.

Squeak.

Then water dripping from a lifted paddle. Slow drops. Exact drops.

Their paddles lay on the gravel beside the canoe.

Will closed his eyes for one second. When he opened them, his face had hardened into something almost calm.

They finished filtering. Joel hated each pump. The sound left his hands and no longer belonged to him.

Dinner was cold food eaten with fingers. Tortillas damp at the edges. Peanut butter from a pouch. Trail mix with grit in it. Will sorted raisins out without seeming to know he was doing it, then stopped and shoved the whole handful into his mouth.

Alex would have said something about that. Joel knew the shape of the joke without hearing words. He looked at the water until the impulse passed.

The light lowered. Fog returned from the channel in strips. The canoe sat where they had left it, ugly and necessary, its aluminum side dull, its shortened rope running from the handle to the snag to Joel's wrist.

Bad boat. Good boat. Only boat.

Will took the notebook.

He wrote slowly because his hand hurt.

IF IT ONLY REPEATS, IT CAN'T LIE ABOUT NEW FACTS.

Joel read the sentence twice.

Will wrote under it:

WE CAN TEST.

The bar seemed to narrow around them.

Joel took the pencil.

NO.

Will did not look at him. He kept looking at the page, as if the page were the less frightening face.

He wrote:

TESTING IS NOT ANSWERING.

Joel wrote:

YES IT IS.

Will's mouth opened and closed. He pressed the pencil so hard the point broke. He stared at the broken graphite with exhausted anger.

Joel found the spare pencil stub in the notebook pocket and handed it to him.

Will wrote:

WE NEED TO KNOW WHAT IT UNDERSTANDS.

Joel almost wrote, It does not understand. But that was faith disguised as caution. They knew less than they needed. They had survived by acting on assumptions and calling them rules because rules were easier to obey than guesses.

He wrote:

KNOWING COSTS SOUND.

Will read it and shook his head once.

He wrote:

WRITING COSTS NOTHING.

Joel looked at the dark line of the opposite bank. The spruce there stood close enough together that dusk gathered between trunks before it gathered on the water. The channel made a narrow mirror of the sky, then broke it around stones and drowned sticks.

Writing removed tone. That was why it had saved them. It made grief blunt and orders plain. It let Will argue without feeding the trees his voice. But the page had begun to feel like shelter, and Joel no longer trusted anything that felt too much like shelter.

He wrote:

WE DON'T KNOW THAT.

Will read it.

His face changed, not toward fear exactly. Toward insult. The last room had lost its lock.

He wrote:

THEN WE HAVE NOTHING.

Joel wanted to deny it. The want came with the old physical urgency of speech. He could see Will at sixteen in a church basement, explaining to a youth pastor why the shepherd metaphor did not work if anyone cared about actual sheep. He could see him at twenty-six, drunk in Joel's first apartment, correcting Mike about a canoe neither of them yet owned. He could see him two mornings ago, line in hand, trying to believe fish still existed as fish and not as sound waiting to be used.

Joel wrote:

WE HAVE SIGHT.

Then:

WE HAVE BOAT.

Then, after a pause:

WE HAVE EACH OTHER IF WE DON'T GIVE THAT AWAY.

Will looked at the last line for a long time.

Across the channel, in Will's own voice, someone said, "There is a difference."

Will shut his eyes.

The phrase had been his once, ordinary as a cough, said when distinctions still helped men feel clever. There is a difference. Between echo and voice. Between a map and ground. Between testing and answering. Between surviving with someone and merely remaining alive beside him.

The imitation did not know which difference it meant.

That did not make it harmless.

Joel put two fingers to his eyes and waited until Will opened his.

Visible.

Will nodded. His eyes were wet now, unmistakably, and furious about it.

Joel wrote:

IT HEARD THAT BEFORE.

Will took the pencil.

IT USED IT CORRECTLY.

Joel looked at the line and felt the cold of the channel return inside his clothes.

He wrote:

IT USED IT NEAR YOU.

Will read it. His hand stayed on the page.

The difference mattered. Maybe. It was not understanding, necessarily. It was placement. Association. Will plus correction. Will plus argument. Will plus a phrase that had always opened another sentence. The thing did not need meaning if it could learn which wound a sound belonged to.

From behind the tarp, a rope tapped aluminum.

Joel looked at the canoe.

The painter ran visibly from its front handle to the snag to his wrist. The hull did not move.

Tap.

Will turned only enough to confirm the boat by sight.

Tap.

The sound came again from behind the tarp, though the canoe sat in front of them.

Joel wrote:

NOT BOAT.

Will touched the rope where it crossed Joel's wrist. Not affection. Confirmation. The line was real. The boat was there. Joel was there. Will's fingers were cold through the plastic.

They lay down under the tarp before full dark because darkness would come whether they prepared for it or not. Joel kept his wrist outside the sleeping bag so the painter's loop could tug him awake if the canoe shifted. Gravel pressed into his hip. The tarp breathed damp air against his face. Will lay close enough that Joel could feel each controlled movement, each withheld cough, each time his body almost turned toward a sound and stopped.

Across the channel, Mike cleared his throat once.

Neither man moved.

Farther downstream, Alex laughed under his breath.

Neither man moved.

Then, from somewhere in the fog where no boat touched shore, came the hollow knock of a paddle against a gunwale. Once. Then silence long enough to become another kind of knock.

Joel kept his eyes open on the canoe's dim shape. Bad boat. Only boat. The thing that might fail them and the thing they had to trust until it did.

The rope around his wrist held.

For that night, before sleep or whatever passed for it, that was the whole world.
