# Chapter 11 - The Second Night After

By the second night after, they had begun to know what to listen for and still did not know what it was.

No one called it the second night after. There was no useful after yet. Alex was not behind them in any way the word could hold. He was ten yards through alder and wet brush, under a gray groundsheet and stones, close enough that a man could reach him in less than a minute and far enough that no one living could make himself go.

Rain closed the camp. It came down without force, a cold steady rain that made everything near and indistinct. It put a roof over the smallest sounds and flattened the rest. The tarp became an instrument. Each drop struck, joined another, became one continuous hand on nylon. Water gathered along the low edge and released in ropes. The spruce above them held its own store of rain and spent it later, heavier, as if another weather had been waiting in the branches.

The men sat close without comfort.

Mike kept his own bear spray across his knees, the nearly spent canister smaller in the dark, its orange cap wet and dull, its weight wrong for what they wanted from it. Alex's unused spray lay beside Mike's pack with the safety still in, too clean and practical to look at for long. Will had the map case open though there was no light to read by. He kept one hand on the plastic as if the route might move under him if he let go. Their sleeping bags stayed rolled inside damp sacks under the packs. The tent bodies and flies lay in wet bundles where they had dropped them, unused and already sour. Joel's hammock hung black between two cedars, useless except as another shape to check. No one had gone into a tent. No one had taken off wet socks. Joel had his notebook in the dry pocket of his jacket and had not taken it out yet.

They had agreed on watches. Then they had all stayed awake.

The agreement itself had been brief and useless. Mike had said first watch, because saying first watch sounded like command. Will had said no, no, no, we need actual rotations, and then had been unable to produce one that mattered. Joel had proposed two-hour blocks, not because two hours was sensible but because any number was better than the three of them staring into rain until morning. They named the blocks and immediately broke them. Mike would not close his eyes. Will closed his twice and opened them both times before a minute passed, ashamed of his own body for wanting anything as ordinary as sleep. Joel sat near the tarp edge, hood up, one knee braced against a pack, watching the food barrel hang in the bad spruce beyond the gray line of rain.

The barrel was still where they had put it before dark. Closed, latched, strapped, hauled into the same inadequate tree because moving farther from camp would mean moving nearer the alder gap, and none of them had said that aloud. The rope ran black against the trunk. The barrel turned a little when the wind found it, not enough to swing, only enough to remind Joel that it was there.

Beyond it, somewhere behind brush and weather and the orange strip Mike had tied that morning, Alex lay under the groundsheet.

No one had checked him before full dark.

Will had asked once. Not directly. He had said, "The wind could get under plastic," while staring at the gap, and Mike had said, "It won't," too quickly. Joel had watched Will's mouth move around the next sentence and fail. That was how the old friendship worked under strain: the worst thing approached, became visible, and then they all looked at the practical object nearest to hand. Knot. Stove. Map. Rope. An object could be handled. It did not require an answer.

Now there were no objects left to fix. There were only objects to guard. Barrel. Boats. Paddles. Packs. Map case. Bear spray. The covered place beyond the alder was not an object. They treated it like one because the living had run out of better duties.

"If it is a bear," Will said, too softly, "it may still be nearby."

Mike's head shifted. "It is nearby because we're still here."

"That's not a reason."

"It's the only reason that matters."

"No. No, it's not."

Joel said, "Save it."

Both men looked at him. He rarely used that tone. He did not like how easily it came, how quickly fatigue found a harder voice in him and put it on without asking. The rain filled the space after it.

Will looked back at the map. "I'm not trying to be an ass."

"You don't always have to try," Mike said.

Will gave him a thin look. "Good. Nice. Productive."

"Then stop."

"Stop what? Thinking about the large animal that may or may not be a large animal? Because I have made several attempts."

Mike drew a breath, held it, let it out through his nose. Joel saw his throat move. He did not clear it. The withheld sound was visible and more intimate than the habit itself.

"We treat it like a bear until there is a better thing to do," Mike said.

"There isn't a better thing to do," Will said. "That is not the same as it being a bear."

"Will."

"There is a difference."

The phrase entered the tarp space and sat there with them. Joel thought of the first camp, Will saying it over some minor correction, a lure knot or a map distinction or one of the thousand ways he made the world bearable by cutting it into categories. There was a difference. There should have been. Between bear and not bear. Between echo and answer. Between friend and the sound of a friend.

Joel said, "Not tonight."

Will looked at him.

"The difference doesn't help tonight," Joel said.

Will's face tightened. He wanted to argue. It was not only habit now. It was survival to him, or the nearest thing he had. If he could say it correctly enough, separate cause from coincidence and animal from voice, the world might become answerable again.

Joel had known him as a boy in church basements where adults used language the same way. Prayer requests. Testimonies. Concerns. A job loss became a season. A sick child became a burden. A death became a homegoing if the room was frightened enough of death. Joel had hated that before he knew he hated it, the soft hand it laid over anything sharp. Will had not hated it, or had not needed to name the hatred. He had been bored and restless and overbright, whispering wrong jokes during youth group and coughing into his sleeve when he tried not to laugh. Joel could see him at twelve, fluorescent-lit and too thin, making Joel look down at his Bible so the adults would not see his face.

They had learned early that some rooms punished the direct sentence. They had learned to speak around the thing and let tone do the work. The woods did not punish the direct sentence. The woods kept it.

A drop struck Joel's wrist and ran under his cuff. He pulled his hand back and tucked it inside his sleeve.

"What are you thinking?" Will said.

"Nothing useful."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the one I've got."

Mike shifted the bear spray on his knees. "We need less talking."

Will laughed once. No humor in it. "You first."

Mike turned his face toward him. In the dark under the tarp his eyes had no color. "I mean it."

"So do I."

Joel lifted a hand. Not high. Just enough. Both of them saw it and both of them stopped, which surprised him. He kept his hand up another second, then let it fall. He listened until the rain separated again into layers: tarp above, spruce beyond, water dripping from a paddle blade somewhere near the boats, the lake shouldering itself against the channel mouth.

Then the food barrel latch clicked.

It was a small sound. Ordinary. One of the first trip sounds. Plastic, metal, pressure released. The kind of sound a body could identify before thought arrived. Joel's skin tightened before his mind placed it.

The barrel hung outside the tarp, pale in the rain, ten yards away and too high for any man to touch without the rope.

No one moved.

The click had not come from the barrel. Or the barrel had made a sound without moving. Or something below it had made the barrel's exact sound. Joel watched the hanging shape. The strap remained tight. The lid did not lift. The rain ran down its curved side. Nothing stood beneath it.

Mike's hand closed over the bear spray.

"Don't," Joel said.

"I didn't."

"I know."

"Then don't tell me don't."

Will whispered, "It was the latch."

"I heard it," Mike said.

"The latch is up there."

"I can see that."

"Then how--"

"I can see that."

The second time was quieter and worse. Mike was not arguing the fact. He was refusing what the fact required of him.

Joel leaned forward until his rain jacket pulled tight across his shoulders. He kept his eyes on the barrel and tried to make his body understand the new order: a thing could be seen shut and heard open. A man could stand beside him and cough from camp. Cause and proof were no longer the same thing.

Something moved at the edge of the tarp.

Not outside. On the tarp itself. A bead of water traveled along the seam above Mike's shoulder, gathered, fattened, and dropped onto his sleeve. Mike flinched, then hated that he had.

Will covered his mouth with both hands. It might have been to stop a laugh. It might have been to stop speech. His fingers were pale where he pressed them into his face.

"Barrel can make noises," Mike said.

Joel looked at him.

"Wind twists it," Mike said. "Strap settles. Plastic flexes. Temperature drops."

"Sure," Joel said.

Mike heard the flatness. "What?"

"I said sure."

"You mean bullshit."

"I mean possible."

"Possible is not agreement."

"No."

Will lowered his hands. "There is a difference."

Mike's head snapped toward him.

Will's eyes widened a little, as if he had heard the phrase only after saying it. He pressed his mouth closed.

They sat in the rain, three living men under a piece of nylon, each trying not to give the night an obvious shape to take.

The tent zipper came from the dark behind them.

It was not loud. It did not need to be. A small tooth-by-tooth rasp, rising, then stopping. A pause. Then the downward rasp, slower. Joel knew the sound from every camp he had ever slept in, knew the slight catch where wet fabric pulled and the slider dragged over grit. He also knew where the tents were. The larger tent body lay rolled under the tarp at his left, fly folded badly on top. Alex's smaller tent sat half-packed in its open stuff sack by the packs, door panel folded against itself, zipper teeth visible and still. Joel's hammock and bug net hung dark between cedars, straps slackened by rain but visible. No tent stood in the dark behind them.

The zipper moved again.

Up. Down.

Same pace. Same catch in the teeth.

Will said, "The tents are packed."

"I know," Joel said.

"No," Will said. "No, I mean right here."

"I know."

Mike stood too fast and hit his shoulder on the tarp line. Water shook down over all of them. He raised the bear spray toward the sound, though the sound had already ended.

"Show yourself," he said.

"Don't," Joel said.

Mike's jaw worked. "It's an animal."

"Then it doesn't understand English."

Mike froze with his mouth half open.

That, more than anything, made Joel afraid. Not the zipper. Not the latch. Mike had wanted to answer. The night had offered him the one motion his life had practiced: challenge it, name it, push back. A bad deal, a wrong map, a man arguing from the front of a canoe, a route going ugly. Stand up. Make noise. Take command. Here command was only another sound.

Mike sat back down slowly. Rain dripped from the brim of his hood onto his hands.

"I'm not going to sit here while something walks around camp," he said.

"You don't know it's walking," Joel said.

"Oh, that's comforting."

"You don't."

"So what is it doing?"

Joel kept his eyes on the tent bundles. "Making sounds."

"Christ."

Will made a soft, broken sound. "That's the first true thing anyone's said in two hours."

Mike turned on him. "Do not start."

"I am deeply past starting."

Joel put one hand on Will's sleeve. Not gripping. Just contact. Will looked down at it, then at Joel.

"What?" he said.

Joel shook his head once.

Will swallowed the next sentence. It cost him. Joel could see it in the muscles around his mouth, in the way his body needed to push language out and had nowhere to send it. He looked suddenly young and old at the same time, the boy from church and the unemployed man under a wilderness tarp.

Out in the rain, Alex laughed once.

Will bent forward as if struck in the stomach. Mike did not move. Joel's hand tightened on Will's sleeve without his meaning to.

The laugh came from beyond the barrel, near the alder but not exactly at it. Alex's bark of laughter, the hard bright start, the little scrape at the end when he had been drinking coffee or trying to get everyone to turn toward him. It did not sound far away. It did not sound close. Rain made a room of the camp and put the voice in every corner.

"Stop," Will said.

Joel's grip tightened.

The laugh came again, farther off this time, or shaped to sound farther off. Thinner. Less body in it.

"Stop," Will said, louder.

"Will."

"Stop it."

Joel grabbed his sleeve with both hands. Will tried to pull free. For one bad second Joel thought he would go, not because he believed Alex was alive but because grief does not need belief to move the body. Then Will stopped fighting. His breath came in small hard pulls, almost coughs, and he held them back until his shoulders shook.

Mike said, "Maybe somebody's out there."

Nobody believed him fully, but they all needed the sentence. A poacher. Another group. A ranger. Some human cruelty so improbable it became attractive. Men could be punished. Men could be found. Men could explain why they had a dead friend's laugh in their mouths.

"Then why Alex?" Will said.

Mike did not answer.

The rain thickened. It drummed hard enough to make talk private and impossible at once. Joel heard the lake beyond the channel as a low shove, felt it more than heard it. The water had kept them here. That was an ordinary fact. Wind and rain and one bad boat. No monster was needed for that. The wilderness could kill without intention. The awful part was that something intimate had entered all that indifference and learned to use its acoustics.

Mike lifted the bear spray, looked at it, and set it back across his knees.

"If we hear it again," he said, "we don't answer."

Will laughed into his hands. "Now you arrive."

"I said don't answer."

"I heard you."

"Then act like it."

Will's head came up. "What do you think I'm doing?"

"Talking."

"So are you."

"Because you keep--"

Joel said, "Enough."

It came out quieter than before. It worked better. Mike looked away. Will looked at the wet ground between his sandals. Joel could see his toes, pale and wrinkled in the straps, dirt caught under one nail. Everyone had been wet too long.

"We need rules for the dark," Joel said.

Mike's mouth tightened. "Rules."

"Don't answer voices. Don't leave camp. Don't split up. There's your rules."

"Visual confirmation," Joel said.

Will looked at him.

"If one of us hears one of us," Joel said, "we look first. We confirm by sight. We don't move toward sound unless we can see the person making it."

Mike stared into the rain. "This is insane."

"Yes."

Will gave a short, surprised breath that almost became a laugh and did not.

Joel took the notebook from his jacket pocket. The cover had softened at the corners despite the plastic bag. He opened it under his coat, shielding the page with his body, and clicked his pen once. The sound was small but all three of them noticed it. Even the pen had become a presence in camp.

"Sorry," he said.

"Don't apologize to the pen," Will said.

For a second Joel almost smiled.

He drew four columns because columns were better than prayer. Columns did not comfort. They made a place for facts to stand without touching.

Alex.

Mike.

Will.

Camp.

The pen shook once before he steadied it. His hand was cold and clumsy; the page took the ink badly where damp had reached the paper. Under Alex he wrote laugh. mothering. hold up. He paused before the last one. Not a return. Not yet. Material. Joel crossed nothing out. Instead he made a small mark beside it.

Under Will he wrote cough. no no no. He hesitated, then added difference.

Under Mike he wrote we're fine. opens up. throat clear. The words looked foolish on the page, too ordinary to justify fear. They looked like notes from a bad meeting, evidence for an argument no one would believe.

Under Camp he wrote latch. zipper. paddle knock. rope tap. stove click.

Mike leaned over. "What is that?"

"What it might have," Joel said.

"You're making a ghost spreadsheet?"

"I'm making a list."

"Same thing, Madison."

The old nickname landed almost normally. Joel had been Madison since he moved there, as if relocation were a defect and the city itself had replaced whatever name they used before. Alex had made it louder. Will used it when he wanted to make Joel provincial in a different direction. Mike used it when Joel was being, in Mike's view, too careful, too cleanly reasonable.

For half a second Joel almost laughed. The almost hurt.

"Put that down," Will said.

Joel looked at him.

"Madison," Will said. "It heard that. Put it down."

Mike stared at him. "Jesus, Will."

"No, I'm just saying."

"Stop just saying."

Will flinched, then pressed his lips together.

Joel wrote Madison under Joel, in a fifth column he had not meant to make. He did not like seeing his own column there. He wrote wait beneath it, then stopped. Whatever was outside had heard him too. It had heard all of them. Maybe from the landing. Maybe from the first knock at First Camp. Maybe only from Second Camp. The timeline mattered. Tonight it only widened the dark.

"What about if it makes a sound that isn't a voice?" Mike said.

Joel capped the pen halfway, then uncapped it. "Same rule."

"How do you visually confirm a paddle knock?"

"You don't move toward it."

"What if it's the canoe?"

"Then it will still be the canoe in daylight."

Mike looked at him, angry because the answer was good enough to obey and not good enough to relieve him.

Will said, "What if it wants us not to move? What if this is the whole point? It pins us. It makes camp unsafe. It waits."

"Then we wait better than it does," Mike said.

Will blinked.

Mike seemed almost embarrassed by the sentence. "For tonight," he said.

That was the closest thing to courage anyone had said since the rain began.

Joel wrote: night rule - no sound without sight.

The words were too neat. He made himself underline sight once.

Something knocked from the shoreline.

Hollow wood on stone. One clean knock.

No one laughed. No one breathed loudly enough for the sound to hide inside it.

The knock came from near the canoes, or near the water beyond them, or from a place the rain had invented between. Joel could see the two canoe hulls pulled high and turned partly on their sides, dark and shining. Paddles were stacked beneath one boat, blades inward, exactly where he had left them. Painter ropes lay tight. No hull moved.

Another knock.

Not louder. Not closer. The same. The kind of repeat a person might make if the first had not been answered.

Will's hand went to the map case. His fingers pressed the plastic until it crackled.

Mike whispered, "Bear won't do that."

Will turned his head slowly toward him.

Mike did not look back. He had given up the word by accident and knew it.

The knock came a third time, but wrong. Softer at the start, with a scrape after it, like a paddle shaft sliding on granite. Joel saw in memory the first portage, a paddle knocked from under a thwart, Alex cursing with too much theater, Will saying that is why you don't put them there, Mike telling him to load faster and lecture less. The sound in the rain had no memory. It only had the shape.

"Gear too," Joel said.

He had not meant to say it aloud.

Both men looked at him.

The rain made the sentence small enough that the camp might not keep it. That was what he told himself. A lie, probably. Or not a lie. He did not know how close a sound had to be to matter. He did not know whether rain protected them or delivered them in pieces.

"What does that mean?" Will said.

Joel looked at the list. "It doesn't need just voices."

Will's face changed. Until that second, even after the cough, even after Alex's laugh, the danger had arranged itself around speech. Do not answer. Do not call. Do not give it phrases. But camp itself had been talking since the first day. Buckles. Zippers. Latches. Ropes. Paddles. Stove. Nylon. Every practical action had made a vocabulary.

The shoreline knocked once more.

Mike lifted the bear spray and lowered it again.

"We should bring the paddles in," he said.

"No," Joel said.

"They're right there."

"No."

"If something takes a paddle--"

"Then we deal with that when we can see."

Mike stared at the black shapes under the canoe. "We may need them fast."

"We're not getting on the water tonight."

"You don't know what happens tonight."

"I know we don't chase a paddle knock in the dark."

Will whispered, "I hate that he's right."

Mike shut his eyes. His face folded inward for a second. He was exhausted. They all were, but Mike wore exhaustion as a private insult. He opened his eyes and kept his body still.

The knock did not come again.

They sat until the rain changed pitch. The hard drumming softened into a finer hiss. Water ran from the tarp edges steadily. Somewhere to Joel's left a pack strap ticked against a buckle. He reached over and stopped it with his thumb. The absence after it stopped was immediate and large.

"Good catch," Will whispered.

Joel shook his head.

Will nodded once. No praise. No more words.

Time lost its edges. Joel checked his watch under his sleeve and saw that only twenty-three minutes had passed since he last looked. Mike sat with his chin tucked, eyes open. Will shifted, winced, shifted again. His knees must have hurt. Joel's own lower back had become a hot band beneath cold clothing. His feet were numb at the toes and raw where the sandal straps rubbed. He wanted to take the sandals off, dry his feet, change socks, do any of the small maintenance that kept a body from becoming stupid. He did none of it. Bare feet in the dark felt like surrender.

"You can sleep," Joel said, barely audible.

Will shook his head.

"Close your eyes for ten."

"That's how every terrible camping story begins."

"No talking."

Will made a face but closed his eyes.

For almost a minute he stayed that way. Then, from beyond the tarp and to Joel's right, Will said, "You guys hear that?"

Will's eyes opened.

He had not spoken.

The copied voice was quiet and exact enough in its parts to be wrong as a whole. It had Will's dry edge, the little lift at the end, the old invitation to make everyone else listen with him. But it carried no breath before it, no body after. It was a sentence removed from a man and set in the rain.

Mike's face went slack. Only for a second. Then anger covered it.

"No," Will whispered.

Joel put one finger to his lips.

Will's eyes filled, not with tears at first but with rage. His own phrase, his own small anxious hook, had been placed outside him and used without permission. Joel understood the rage better than the fear.

The voice came again. "You guys hear that?"

Same phrase. Same direction. Too even this time. It did not correct for the fact that no one had answered. It had only the sound of invitation.

Mike turned his head toward Joel slowly, asking without asking.

Joel pointed two fingers at his own eyes, then toward Will sitting under the tarp. Visual confirmation. Will was here. Therefore Will was not there. The conclusion was crude and lifesaving.

Will mouthed something.

Joel shook his head.

Will's mouth closed.

The copied voice did not speak again.

After a while, Mike leaned close enough that Joel could smell stale coffee and rain on his breath. "What did you write?"

Joel showed him the page.

Mike took it in with a strained, impatient glance. The columns. The small fifth column with Joel's name. The underlined rule.

"Add that," Mike whispered.

Joel wrote under Will: you guys hear that.

The pen moved too loudly. He stopped between words and listened. Nothing answered the scratch of ink on damp paper. Maybe that meant nothing. Maybe it meant the page had no voice unless one of them gave it one. Writing took tone away. It made Will's panic look like shapes, Mike's certainty like shapes, Alex's laugh like a word too small to contain him. The thought steadied Joel more than it should have. A limit, however small, was a kind of shore.

He wrote more carefully: writing safe? Then put a question mark beside it. He did not know. He only knew the page had made no sound except the pen, and the pen had not yet become bait.

Will watched him write. His eyes moved over the page but he did not speak. Joel saw the question in him, bright and hungry. Can it learn from this? Does it understand? Is it only recording? The questions crowded his face. Joel put the notebook back in the plastic bag before Will could borrow it and turn questions into air.

Mike saw the motion and nodded once.

It was the first time all night the three of them had agreed on anything without saying it.

The rain eased near midnight, or what Joel thought was midnight. The woods grew louder when the rain softened. Water dropped from needles. The lake resumed its individual slaps against the channel bank. Somewhere far off a loon called once and then did not call again. The sound was so ordinary Joel almost hated it. A real animal in a real night, carrying on with its life in the same dark that held Alex's voice.

Will's head dipped.

Joel watched him. The dip became a nod, the nod became a jerk, and Will woke with a sharp inhale that he managed to cut off before it became a cough. He looked ashamed.

Joel touched two fingers to his own chest, then pointed at Will, then curled his hand under his cheek: I watch, you sleep.

Will shook his head.

Joel pointed harder.

Will looked at Mike. Mike, after a moment, jerked his chin once in agreement. That decided it because Mike still had enough authority to make surrender feel like compliance.

Will lay down on his side under the tarp, not in the tent, not in the hammock, just on the thin pad they had dragged near the packs. He did not take off his rain jacket. He put the map case against his chest like a child with a book. His glasses stayed on. Within minutes his breathing grew uneven, not sleep exactly but a border state his body took without permission.

Mike remained upright.

"You too," Joel whispered.

"No."

"Ten minutes."

"I said no."

Joel did not push. Mike needed to refuse something. If sleep was the thing, fine.

They listened together.

Without Will talking, Mike seemed less angry and more frighteningly alone. The rain had carved the force out of him. He sat with the spent canister, a man built for decisions in a place where every decision fed the wrong mouth. Joel could see him wanting daylight with a physical hunger. Daylight would not solve it, but it would return cause to shape. It would let them count objects. Canoes. Paddles. Packs. Barrel. Map case. Bear spray. Body.

"Tomorrow," Mike whispered, "we move."

Joel nodded.

"I don't care if it's ugly."

Joel did not nod to that.

Mike saw the distinction and almost smiled. Not with humor. Recognition, maybe. "Fine. We assess and move."

Joel nodded once.

Mike looked toward the hanging food barrel. "That thing makes the latch again, I am not going out there."

"Good."

"Don't good me."

Joel looked at him. Mike looked back. For a second they were simply two wet men being petty in the dark, and the pettiness was human enough to hurt. Agreement would have been too intimate. Irritation still belonged to them.

Then Mike cleared his throat.

He stopped halfway through the sound.

Joel saw his face change.

The unfinished throat clear hung in the tarp space, small and living. Mike's own habit had caught him by surprise. He put one hand over his mouth, as if he could take it back. No answer came from the woods. No copied throat clear, no route instruction, no we're fine in Mike's voice from beyond the barrel. The absence did not relieve them. It held.

Mike lowered his hand.

"I hate this," he whispered.

Joel nodded.

There was nothing useful to say to that, and because there was nothing useful, he said nothing.

Will slept for maybe twelve minutes. Then his body, treacherous with exhaustion, coughed.

Not loud. A dry catch in the throat, the familiar small bark. He woke as it left him and clapped both hands over his mouth, eyes wide behind rain-spotted lenses.

All three men listened.

The answer came from near the food barrel.

The same cough. Will's cough. Wet night around it. A little embarrassed finish, as if the body that made it wanted to apologize.

Will began to shake.

Mike raised the bear spray and kept it aimed at the dark below the barrel. Joel put his hand on Will's shoulder, hard enough to hurt if hurt would keep him here.

The cough did not repeat.

Joel leaned close to Will's ear. He did not speak. He pressed two fingers against Will's shoulder, then pointed at Will, then at the ground: you are here. Will stared at him. Joel did it again.

You are here.

Will nodded. Once. Then again.

The gesture steadied Joel too. That was the terrible simplicity. The man you can see is the man. The sound is only sound until sight gives it a body.

Something brushed the outside of the tarp.

All three froze.

The brush came again, low along the edge behind Mike. Fabric dented inward an inch and released. Mike turned with the bear spray. Joel's mind supplied branch, wind, water weight, a rope end shaken loose. The tarp edge moved again, harder this time, then stopped.

No voice followed.

No laugh. No latch. No zipper. No knock.

That was worse in its own way. The silence after a touch was nearly a shape.

Mike crawled one knee forward, slow, keeping under the tarp. Joel caught his sleeve.

Mike looked back.

Joel shook his head.

Mike pointed at the tarp edge with two fingers, then at his own eyes.

Visual confirmation.

It was almost funny that Mike had learned the rule well enough to use it against him.

Joel hesitated. The tarp edge was three feet away. Not ten yards. Not the alder. Three feet. They could look without leaving shelter. They could also give the night exactly what it wanted: a body leaning out, a face at the edge, a man responding to a sound because not knowing had become intolerable.

Will reached for the notebook.

Joel let him.

Will opened to a blank lower corner and wrote with slow block letters, the pen nearly tearing the damp page.

WAIT.

Then, after a moment, he added:

DAYLIGHT.

He pushed the notebook toward Mike.

Mike read it. His face moved through anger, insult, calculation, and finally exhaustion. He sat back.

The tarp edge did not move again.

Joel took the notebook and wrote below Will's words:

NOT SOUND. SIGHT.

Will read it and nodded.

Mike read it and looked away.

It was not a theory. Theories wanted more words. It was not even a full rule. It had no exception for kindness, panic, or a dead friend needing rain pulled off his face. It was a hard little refusal written before any of them could soften it.

The night went on.

It was not dramatic after that. That was part of its cruelty. They did not spend the whole night in a sequence of voices and signs. Much of it was only wet discomfort and waiting. The body made its demands without respect for terror. Joel had to piss and did not. Will's leg cramped and he worked it out silently, teeth bared. Mike's hands grew stiff around the bear spray until Joel pointed at them and Mike flexed his fingers with visible irritation. Rainwater found a path down Joel's neck. The tarp sagged, and at one point all three men had to push upward with their palms to dump a cold pool off the far edge. The water hit the ground outside with a heavy slap. Everyone listened afterward to see if the slap would be returned.

It was not.

Once, near the hour before dawn, Alex said, very softly, "Quit mothering me."

Not from the alder. Not from the body site. From the camp center, or the shore, or the rain's false room. The phrase came with less of him in it than the laugh had. The shape of Alex without Alex's heat. The old joke emptied of the face that made it survivable.

Will did not move. Mike did not lift the bear spray. Joel closed his eyes for one second and saw Alex at the motel, Alex at the first muddy pullout, Alex holding up the hot sauce like a sacrament, Alex alive enough to be irritating. Joel had resented that life when it filled the air. Now the absence used the same air and asked for an answer.

No one gave it one.

The phrase did not repeat.

Gray began slowly, not as light but as a thinning of black. The trees appeared first as darker decisions inside dark. Then the hanging food barrel took shape, wet and pale, latch closed. The canoes emerged from the shore gloom, still there. The paddles remained stacked under the nearer hull. The tent bundles. Joel's hammock straps. Packs. Stove. Map case. Mike's hand around the bear spray. Will's face, unshaven and slack with the beginning of shock beyond fear.

The world looked almost accountable again.

No one said morning. No one said fine.

No one said Alex.

Joel opened the notebook one last time before they began the work of leaving. The page had buckled from damp. The columns were ugly, crowded, childish in their certainty and still the best thing they had.

Alex: laugh. mothering. hold up.

Will: cough. you guys hear that. no no no. difference.

Mike: we're fine. opens up. throat clear.

Camp: latch. zipper. paddle knock. rope tap. tarp touch.

Joel: Madison. wait.

At the bottom, in Will's block letters:

WAIT.

DAYLIGHT.

And beneath it, Joel's own:

NOT SOUND. SIGHT.

Mike leaned over, read the last line, and said nothing. Will read it too. His eyes stayed on the words for a long time.

Joel understood only this: something had learned the sound of their camp. It had learned the parts of them that carried without bodies. It had learned latch and zipper and paddle, cough and laugh and the little phrases men used because silence felt too large. It had not used the page. Not yet. Maybe not ever. Or it had learned and had not spent that learning yet.

That was not hope.

It was only a fact with a hard edge, and by morning a hard edge was enough to hold.
