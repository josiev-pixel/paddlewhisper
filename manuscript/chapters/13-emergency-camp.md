# Chapter 13 - Emergency Camp

Emergency Camp had no fire ring and no mercy.

It was not a camp until they had made enough mistakes around it to call it one. Before that it was only a steep poor shore, a wet shelf of root and stone, two canoes dragged up out of reach of the chop, and three men trying not to look toward the places where sound gathered.

The afternoon thinned without becoming warmer. Wind came over the lake in hard panels and broke against the spruce, shaking water loose from the branches long after the rain had ended. Every gust made the tarp snap unless Joel kept the line in his hand. When he let go, the cloth slapped hard enough to make all three of them look. The line had been too short for a proper pitch, then too long after he retied it, then the wrong angle once Mike moved the gear under it. Nothing wanted to fit.

They set the tents where roots rose highest from the mud. One tent went crooked between a jack pine and a spruce with half its stakes buried sideways under stones. The other would not pitch at all because the ground fell away under one corner, so they made it into a gear shelter, fly pulled low and patched where the fabric had abraded against a pack buckle during the landing. The silver repair tape shone in the dim light, too bright for the rest of the camp.

"This is ridiculous," Will said.

He said it flatly, without heat. That made it worse.

"It's shelter," Mike said.

"It is fabric losing an argument with gravity."

"Then argue quieter."

Will looked at him, and for a second Joel thought the old exchange would build itself out of habit. Will would explain load angles or water runoff. Mike would tell him to stop narrating weather. Joel would say something small enough to pass as help. Alex would make it a performance, would step back from the sagging fly and judge it like bad theater, would call them pioneers in the dumbest possible voice.

The space where Alex would have stood remained open.

Joel tightened the tarp line again. The hook at the dead cedar ticked once against bark. He saw it, wrapped the loose tail around the line, and stopped the sound.

Sight.

That was the word under everything now. Not faith. Not reason. Not even trust. Sight was smaller and meaner and more useful. Sight was the barrel visible when the latch clicked. Sight was Mike behind them when Mike's voice had come from ahead. Sight was Will in the front of the canoe while Will's own question moved out of the trees. Sight was what the world had been reduced to after sound became a bad witness.

They had written that in the notebook and still kept forgetting, because men had lived by answering one another for forty years and rules made in a morning did not cut deep enough by evening.

The wet sleeping bag hung from a line between two spruce trunks, lifted and dropped by the wind. It was Will's. The footbox had taken water when the dry bag failed, and the dark wet strip along one side did not shrink no matter how many times they wrung it. Not soaked enough to abandon. Wet enough to become a problem after midnight. Will stood under it with both hands in his armpits, watching it as if attention might dry fabric.

"Synthetic," he said again.

No one answered. They had used the good news already.

Mike knelt beside his canoe under the low tarp, redoing the repair on the cracked thwart. The first lashing from the shoreline had held, but he did not trust it. Trust had become one more thing they did not have enough of. He had taken the repair sleeve from the kit and cut two thin strips of aluminum with the multi-tool, working the metal back and forth until it gave. Now he set the strips along the split wood, braced them with a tent stake, and wrapped cord through the seat frame with furious care.

It was the best thing about him and the worst. Problems became objects. Objects could be held, splinted, forced back toward function. If the damage had an edge, a screw, a wet seam, a cracked place, Mike could give himself to it completely and become almost gentle.

His fingers shook from cold. He pretended they did not.

Will watched too closely.

"It's a field repair," Mike said without looking up.

"I didn't say anything."

"You were breathing critically."

"There is a difference between skepticism and breathing."

Joel almost smiled. The almost hurt.

He wrote the line down later because the thing outside might have heard it. That was what affection did now. A living man's small joke became inventory. Joel hated the quickness with which his mind made Will useful to the notebook while Will was still alive, cold, standing ten feet away with wet glasses and hands that could not stop finding tasks.

The repair held when Mike leaned on it. It creaked, but the creak came from the canoe in front of them, visible, accounted for. Mike leaned again, harder than necessary.

"Don't prove a point by breaking it," Joel said.

Mike glanced up. "That's what field repairs are for."

"For breaking?"

"For finding out before you're in the middle of the lake."

Will's mouth opened. Joel could see the correction rise, technical and helpless. Then Will shut it again. The effort showed in his jaw.

Mike saw it too. "Say it or don't."

"I was not going to say anything."

"You were absolutely going to say something."

"Fine. I was going to say testing to failure is not the same as checking."

"Congratulations. You said it."

Will looked toward the trees as if ashamed of the sound of his own voice.

That was new, and because it was new, Joel noticed it more sharply than the argument. Will had always filled air the way some men fed a stove. He put words into space and warmed himself by them. Now each sentence left him looking after it, frightened of where it might go.

The wind pushed through the tarp and brought lake smell with it: cold tannin water, bruised cedar, mud opened by their sandals. Joel's socks were damp inside his camp shoes. His wet shorts had dried only at the edges and remained cold at the waistband. When he bent for the pot, the muscles under his ribs fluttered once and kept fluttering. Hunger came over him in shallow waves and broke before it became appetite.

They cooked because they had to warm something. It took too long. The stove sputtered in the wind, blue flame flattening under the pot, then flaring when Mike moved his pack as a shield. The click of the igniter made all three of them freeze before flame took. Joel saw the stove. Mike saw the stove. Will pointed at it with two fingers anyway, a small bitter ceremony.

"Sight," Will said.

"Yeah," Joel said.

"We are going to become insane in a very organized way."

"We're ahead of schedule."

Will gave one breath that might have become a laugh if it had more strength. He held it in. The held-in thing seemed to remain between them.

They ate rice from the pot because bowls were packed somewhere stupid. Mike wanted to keep food simple, no smells, no waste, no mess. Will wanted more salt and did not ask. Joel scraped the bottom with a spoon and divided the browned, stuck layer into three equal pieces. He almost made a fourth. His hand moved before thought did.

No one mentioned it.

Alex had complained about food constantly and eaten constantly. He had treated every bland meal as a personal betrayal and then gone back for more, making noises over tortillas and cheese like a man forced to survive a hostage situation. Joel had wanted to tell him to shut up at least twice a day. Now the quiet around the food was so complete it felt staged.

Mike sealed the pot in the kitchen bag and reached for the barrel. The latch snapped under his thumb.

Will flinched.

Mike lifted the barrel toward him. "Me."

"I know."

"I'm right here."

"I said I know."

Mike set the barrel down too hard. "Don't make me responsible for every noise."

Will's face went white in a way that had nothing to do with cold. "I am trying to keep track of reality."

"So am I."

"No, you're trying to pin it down and yell at it until it becomes a route problem."

Mike stood. The tarp was too low, and his shoulder struck it, shaking cold water onto all three of them.

Joel put a hand up. Not between them, exactly. Just up.

The gesture worked because everyone wanted it to. Mike turned away first. Will sat back on his heels and pushed both hands through his hair. The rainwater from the tarp had spotted his glasses again. He took them off and wiped them on a shirt already too damp to help.

From somewhere behind the camp, in the spruce above the root shelf, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will turned before Joel could stop him.

It was not dramatic. That was the horror of it. No shout. No scream. Just Will's body obeying an old arrangement: a friend calls, you turn. His shoulders twisted toward the dark. His head lifted. For less than a second he looked not afraid but ready, almost relieved, as if the world had made a mistake and was about to correct it by sending Alex down through the trees, wet and loud and too pleased with himself.

Mike grabbed Will's shoulder.

"No."

Will stopped so sharply his glasses fell from his hand and landed in the duff.

The voice came again, softer. "Hold up."

It stood behind them in the trees with Alex's impatience in it. The little lift at the end, the half-command, half-request. The exact sound of him at a beaver dam, at a landing, catching a painter rope, making everybody wait because he had found a better way or a worse joke.

Will whispered, "Alex."

Mike's fingers tightened on his shoulder. "No. It isn't."

Those words changed the camp more than the voice had.

They had all known it in pieces. Joel had written denials in the notebook. Will had said repetition and almost said more. Mike had conceded uncertainty, maybe, with the map in front of him and the water behind him. But Mike had not given up the world out loud. He had kept bear as long as he could. He had kept weather, shock, echo, grief, mishearing. Any hard word with a normal body behind it.

Now he stood with one hand on Will's shoulder and said that their dead friend's voice was not their dead friend.

Will bent slowly and picked up his glasses. He did not put them on right away.

Nothing moved in the spruce.

Joel took one step sideways until he could see both men and the black line of trees beyond them. He wanted to write. The need came so strongly it embarrassed him. His hand went to the notebook in his jacket as if paper were a weapon. It was not a weapon. It was a place to put what sound had ruined.

Mike let go of Will.

"You okay?" he said.

Will looked at the ground. "That's a stupid question."

"I know."

"Then why ask it?"

"Because I don't have a better one."

Will put on his glasses. One lens was smeared dark. "No."

Mike nodded once. "Okay."

That was all the comfort they managed.

Joel opened the notebook under the tarp with his body blocking what little wind came through. The pages had swollen at the edges. Pencil still worked better than pen. He turned past the existing columns, then back again because the columns mattered. Alex. Mike. Will. Camp. Joel had added his own name last night and wished he had not, as if writing it had made an opening.

Under Alex he wrote:

HOLD UP - BEHIND CAMP - NOT ALEX

He hesitated and added:

TWO TIMES

Will crouched beside him. "It used the same phrase at the shore."

Joel nodded and wrote:

SHORE BEFORE CAMP

"Same voice?"

Joel did not answer. He looked at Will.

Will closed his eyes. "Right. Sorry."

Mike came over and knelt on the other side. "Put no movement."

Joel looked up.

"No brush. No steps." Mike's face was hard with the effort of being accurate. "If we're writing evidence, write evidence."

Joel wrote:

NO VISIBLE MOVEMENT

Will leaned closer. "That's not evidence of absence. That's only absence of observed movement."

Mike stared at him.

Will held up both hands. "I'm not being cute."

"No one thought you were being cute."

"I'm saying we should be precise."

"Write a peer-reviewed article after we get out."

Will's face tightened. "Precision is not the enemy here."

"Talking might be."

The sentence landed harder than Mike seemed to intend. It was too close to true and too blunt to survive as an ordinary insult.

Will looked away first.

Joel wrote:

LESS TALK.

Then, because the phrase looked like command and because commands had become suspect, he crossed it out and wrote beneath:

TALK ONLY WHEN NEEDED.

The page did not make that easier. It only made it visible.

For a while the emergency camp held itself around them. Wind. Tarp. The lake below speaking in its low side-on way against the stones. Will's sleeping bag lifting and dropping. The repaired thwart creaking once as the canoe settled under its own bad fix. Each sound required a glance. Each glance used energy. Fear had become clerical.

Will took the pencil from Joel and wrote in the margin:

IT REPEATS.

Then he stopped, pencil tip pressed hard enough to darken the paper.

"Maybe not only repeats," he said.

Joel looked at him.

Will swallowed. The words wanted out of him. Joel could see them crowding behind his teeth. Will was a man built out of distinctions, and this distinction had weight.

"Maybe it can recombine," he said, barely above breath. "Not invent. Not like us. But if it has enough sound. Little pieces. Mouth-shapes. Tone. It might be able to assemble something close to new. Like speech synthesis, except not, because that implies a model and training data and I know that sounds insane, but listen. If it can take Alex's voice and put a phrase where Alex never stood, maybe the phrase is fixed and the use isn't. It could be building from pieces."

"Will," Joel said.

"No, that's important."

"I know."

"Then let me finish."

"Don't answer it," Joel said. "Don't give it more."

Will's mouth stayed open.

The warning had not been sharp. Joel wished it had been. Sharpness would have given Will something to argue with. Instead it came out tired and almost kind, and Will received it like a hand against his chest.

Mike said, "He's right."

Will turned on him. "You don't even know what I'm saying."

"I know you're feeding it."

"That's not how this works."

"Then don't explain how it works where it can hear you."

"That's exactly the kind of sloppy thinking that gets people killed."

"People are already killed."

Silence took the sentence at once. Mike looked as if he would take it back if the woods had not already heard it.

Will stood and walked two steps out from under the tarp, then stopped because two steps was as far as the rule allowed without making everyone follow. His shoulders shook once. Not sobbing, or not quite. Cold, rage, grief, all of it using the same muscles.

Joel wanted to say Alex's name. Not to answer the thing. Not to call him. Only to put the name where it belonged, with them, in the camp, among the living who had known him. But the name felt like food left out.

So he wrote it instead at the top of the column, already there, already insufficient.

Alex.

The pencil mark did not sound like him.

Dusk took the trees by degrees. First the separate needles went away. Then the branches became large black hands. Then the spaces between trunks filled until the woods were one thing standing above camp. The lake kept a little light longer, dull strips between the lower trunks. The emergency camp shrank as the light left it, tarp and tents and men drawn tighter against the dark.

They brought the paddles fully under the tarp. Joel's cracked paddle had been taped and marked with orange cord, a warning to the hand. Will checked the tape twice and then stopped because checking became another kind of fear. Mike tied both painter ropes off again down by the canoes and climbed back up wet to the knees.

"Boats are high," he said.

Will nodded.

"Ropes tied."

Joel nodded.

"Barrel's as good as it's getting."

The barrel hung shoulder-high between two trees, a humiliating compromise. No clean branch. No good distance from camp. No one willing to go deeper into the spruce at night to perform proper food storage for an animal they no longer believed was the main problem. Mike had tied it anyway. Not tying it would have meant surrendering one of the last ordinary rules.

Will glanced toward it. "If a bear comes, it gets a nice convenient snack."

"If a bear comes, we can almost call that refreshing," Mike said.

No one laughed. The line deserved a laugh from some earlier version of them.

The tent zipper sounded from the gear shelter.

All three men turned.

The shelter sat five yards away, half pitched low, its patched fly silver at one corner. The zipper pull hung at the door where Will had left it. It hung still.

Then the zipper moved again.

Not the whole length. Not a theatrical opening. Just three careful teeth separating, the small dry rasp of fabric and metal. The door gap widened by an inch, maybe less.

They looked.

Looking was allowed.

Will made a small sound through his nose.

Joel raised one hand, palm down. Stay.

Mike's face had gone empty in the way it did when he was very angry. He took one step toward the shelter.

Joel caught his sleeve.

Mike did not turn. His arm hardened.

Joel shook his head.

The zipper did not move. The shelter showed no shape inside but bags, a coil of rope, the corner of a blue dry bag, the pale round lid of a pot. All visible. All ordinary. All arranged badly enough that a man could turn one wrong step into another problem.

"Wind," Mike said, without conviction.

Will whispered, "No."

Mike looked at him.

Will pointed. His hand shook. "Pull tab is tucked under the flap. Wind can't get that angle."

"Don't," Joel said.

Will's eyes flashed. "I'm identifying a mechanical constraint."

"Quietly."

Will closed his mouth.

Mike stepped back under the tarp. That was harder for him than going forward would have been. Joel could see the effort in his neck, the humiliation of retreat from a three-inch zipper sound. He did it anyway.

The gear shelter remained half open. No one fixed it.

After full dark, the paddle knock began below them.

One hollow tap from the shore.

Joel's hand went at once to the paddles at his feet. The cracked one. The spare. Will's. Mike's. He touched each shaft as if counting heads. All there.

Tap.

The sound came again from lower down, near the canoes or beyond them. Wood on wood, maybe. Paddle against gunwale. Hull settling on stone. The lake putting a small thing in motion. There were enough possible causes to build a reasonable world from, and none of them had the shape of the sound in the dark.

Mike reached for the bear spray and stopped with his hand beside it.

"Boats are high," Joel said.

Mike looked at him.

"Ropes tied. Paddles here. Sight."

Tap.

Will leaned forward, elbows on knees, hands over his ears without fully covering them. "It's using camp."

Joel nodded.

"Not us. Camp."

"It heard camp too."

"Jesus."

The word was out before Will could stop it. He took his hands down and looked at the dark as if he owed it nothing else.

Mike said, "Maybe it wants us to check the boats."

"Maybe it wants us to answer," Will said.

Joel looked at the dark below the trees. Want was a dangerous word. It put a person inside the sound. It gave the dark a center, a plan, a face it had not earned. Everything out here could kill without wanting. Cold did not want. Wind did not want. Water did not want. A bear could want only in the simple body sense of hunger or fear, and even that was not malice. Want made the thing too human.

He took the pencil and wrote:

DO NOT ASSUME WANT.

Will read it and gave a short, bitter nod. "Fine. Function."

Joel wrote:

FUNCTION: MAKE US MOVE / ANSWER / SEPARATE?

The question mark mattered. He pressed it into the paper.

Mike crouched closer, rain jacket whispering. "Add old cabin."

Will looked at him sharply. "What?"

"Tomorrow."

"No."

"We can't sit here."

"And we can't just chase a square on an antique map because the lake scared you."

Mike's throat worked. He did not clear it. Joel noticed that. The swallowed habit, the authority denied its own sound.

"It isn't because I'm scared of the lake," Mike said.

Will gave him a look so exhausted it had almost no anger left. "Mike."

"The canoe is damaged. The paddle is damaged. Your bag is wet. Weather's not stable. We're off the route and we missed the portage landing. The filter's slow. The barrel's hanging like a gift basket. If we keep hugging shore looking for the portage, we spend half a day in the wind beside water where voices carry. If we cross again wrong, we lose a boat or one of us goes in. The old map puts a maintenance cabin inland from the next portage. Maybe there's a trace. Maybe there's a road."

"Maybe there's a fairy goddamn godmother."

"Map says there's a cabin."

The phrase came out smaller than when he had said it before. Not declaration. Not certainty. A man offering up the last solid object he had.

Joel felt Will beside him gather for the correction. Map says there was a cabin. Map says someone once paid a printer. Map says a lot of things. Joel could hear all of it, and because he could hear it he knew the woods might too.

"Tomorrow," Joel said.

Both men looked at him.

He wished he had a better reason. He wished he believed in the cabin. What he believed was narrower: they could not move at night; they could not stay for another full day without becoming colder and more stupid; the water had nearly killed them in daylight; the woods were no safer, but at least a trace would put solid ground under their feet. The cabin did not have to be real. It only had to be the next testable human mark.

"We look in daylight," Joel said. "All three. No scouting alone. If it is nothing, we come back."

"Come back to what?" Will asked.

Joel did not answer.

The paddle knock stopped.

That made the silence after it feel selected.

Mike rubbed both hands over his face. "We need sleep."

Will looked at the hanging bag. "That's optimistic."

"Rest, then."

"Also optimistic."

"Horizontal misery," Joel said.

Will stared at him for a second, then gave a laugh so small it barely had air in it. The laugh frightened him as soon as it left. He looked toward the trees.

No answer came.

They arranged the night by sight and exhaustion. Nobody called it a watch schedule because schedules belonged to people who expected the future to obey an order. Mike took the first sitting position under the tarp with the bear spray beside him but not in his hand. Joel would stay in the hammock at the bad angle and try to rest without closing himself away from the others. Will would take the one pitched tent, because his sleeping bag needed body heat and because arguing about fairness would only produce more sound.

Will objected anyway.

"You take it," he told Joel.

"No."

"Your hammock is garbage."

"Your bag is wet."

"That's not a moral category."

"It's a heat category."

Will looked too tired to appreciate his own kind of language coming back at him. He ducked into the tent and stopped before closing the zipper. The pull was between his fingers. All three of them watched it.

"This is me," he said.

"Seen," Joel said.

Will drew the zipper slowly, then stopped halfway and left it there. "I don't want to make the whole sound."

No one told him that half a door was stupid. Fear had its own engineering now, and every design was bad.

Joel climbed into the hammock. The hang was wrong, shoulder higher than hip, fabric pinching behind his knees. Cold came through the underquilt where it had been compressed against his wet clothes. Above him the tarp sagged inches from his face and shivered with each gust. He could see Mike's boots, the orange can of spray, the notebook in its plastic bag beside the stove. He could see the half-open gear shelter. He could see Will's tent wall, pale in the dark under the tarp edge, moving softly when Will shifted inside.

He could not see Alex.

That remained the basic fact. Not from camp. Not under the tarp. Not on the shore. Not behind the spruce. The dead did not get a vote because the dark could make his voice.

Joel took the notebook from the bag and wrote by headlamp under his jacket so the light stayed small.

Alex:
HOLD UP - SHORE / CAMP - NOT ALEX

Mike:
IT OPENS UP AFTER THIS - WATER - NOT MIKE

Will:
YOU GUYS HEAR THAT - FAR SHORE - NOT WILL

Camp:
LATCH - DAYLIGHT - BARREL VISIBLE
ZIPPER - GEAR SHELTER - NOT WIND?
PADDLE KNOCK - PADDLES UNDER TARP

He paused over the last column.

Joel:

The blank after his name seemed louder than the lake. He had given it less, maybe. Not nothing. No one living among others gave nothing. Wait. Just wait. Eyes front. Paddle. Small jokes. His voice had moved over the same water, under the same trees. If sound could be kept, then parts of him were already out there without permission.

He did not write that.

On a clean page he wrote in block letters:

DO NOT ANSWER.

He looked at the sentence. It was too simple and not simple enough. A dead friend could ask. A living friend could call from out of sight. A boat could knock. A zipper could open. A bear could actually come. The rule was not a rule for survival so much as a rule for choosing which danger to prefer.

He added beneath it:

UNLESS SEEN.

Then he crossed that out.

Too late for that. The living men could be seen and still have their voices stolen elsewhere. The rule was not answer because of sound. Answer only to the body in front of you, to gesture, to what eyes could hold.

He wrote:

CONFIRM WITH SIGHT. THEN MOVE.

Mike leaned over from his sitting place and read it upside down. His face in the headlamp was all planes and grime, eyes sunk deep.

"That's the plan?" he said.

"For tonight."

Mike looked toward Will's tent, then the trees, then the lake. "Great plan."

Joel waited.

Mike rubbed at the bridge of his nose. "I don't have a better one."

From inside the tent, Will said, "That's going in the minutes."

His voice was muffled by nylon, ordinary and exhausted and alive. Joel saw the shape of him through the tent wall, one knee raised, one elbow moving as he fought the wet sleeping bag around himself. Sight gave the voice back to him for one second.

Then, beyond the camp, low among the spruce, Alex laughed once.

Not loud. Not the full bark. A small breath of it, as if he had heard Will and could not help himself.

Will went still inside the tent.

Mike's hand closed around the bear spray.

Joel put the notebook page where both men could see it, black letters in the narrow light.

DO NOT ANSWER.

No one did.

The laugh did not repeat. The lake worked below them. The tarp ticked once, and Joel found the line with his eyes. The food barrel hung where they had left it, too low and visible between two trunks. The gear shelter's zipper remained half open. The canoes down by the shore were pale shapes turned away from the dark water. Nothing moved that could be trusted. Nothing spoke that had to be believed.

They held their silence until it became less like courage and more like another kind of labor.

By the time Mike's head began to lower against his chest and Will's shivering settled into uneven breaths, Joel had understood the cabin for what it was. Not rescue. Not safety. A square on an old map, a human mark in a place that was steadily taking human things apart: voices, routes, jokes, certainty, the clean usefulness of saying a name.

In the morning they would put stones on the map corners and pretend paper still knew how to tell the world what to be.
