# Chapter 3 - First Water

The first lake was narrow enough that it never let them forget shore.

Spruce leaned over the right side, black at the waterline and green where the sun struck the higher limbs. To the left, granite shelves came down in pale steps, some dry and white, some dark with old water marks. Small cedars had found cracks in the rock and grown there crookedly, as if the place had allowed them but had not made room. Fog lifted in torn strips ahead of the canoes and came apart when the sun reached it. Each cleared patch showed another bend, another short reach of black-green water, another piece of the route that looked obvious only after they were inside it.

Joel paddled from the back and let Alex set whatever rhythm he could manage in front.

Alex was strong. That helped. He was also uneven, which did not. He would paddle hard for thirty strokes, shoulders and back doing more than skill required, then look off toward a bird or snag or some imagined thing in the trees and let the blade rest on the gunwale until the canoe began to yaw. Joel corrected without saying much. A quiet draw. A little push on the left. A stroke held longer than it needed. The boat came back.

That was the arrangement outside the canoe too. Alex made weather in front of him, and Joel trimmed from behind. He had been doing some version of it for years: letting louder men fill the air, then managing consequence. Mara had called it useful once, standing in the half-painted second bedroom with a tape gun hanging from her wrist, and evasive the next week when he let her answer the venue email because she was better at sounding certain. Both times she had been right enough to irritate him. He did not hate Alex for needing an audience. That would have been cleaner. He liked him in pieces and resented him in waves.

After ten minutes Alex glanced over his shoulder.

"You're doing something back there."

"I am."

"Feels judgmental."

"That's steering."

"Same vibe."

"Paddle on the left."

Alex switched sides with a small flourish and dipped the blade too far forward, splashing his own knee.

"Was that wrong?"

"It moved us."

"Spoken like a man protecting morale."

Ahead of them, Mike and Will moved cleaner. Mike was in back, compact and economical, doing less than he seemed to. Will sat up front with a rod laid along the side of the canoe and kept turning his head toward shadows near shore. He paddled well when he remembered paddling was the point. Every patch of downed wood, every dark pocket under alder, every ring on the surface took part of his attention.

Mike's voice came over the water. "Will."

"I see it."

"You see what?"

"Nothing. That's the problem."

"Paddle."

"I am paddling."

"You're browsing."

Alex smiled. "Fishing is just aquatic browsing."

Will did not turn. "That's not terrible."

"Don't encourage him," Mike said.

The sound carried strangely on the lake. Not wrong. Just widened. Mike's words lay flat over the water. Will's answer came back thinner from the granite, scraped down by distance. Joel knew enough not to make anything of it. Lakes did that. Fog and rock and cold morning air could take a sound apart and hand it back changed.

He had heard his own paddle drip behind him before. He had heard ducks sound like men coughing. The woods were not quiet. They made quiet men look busier than they were.

They kept to the right side because Mike had said the first portage would be easier to find from that shore. The lake ran long and crooked, more like a widened river than open water. Twice they passed narrow cuts that looked like routes until they drew close and saw deadfall and shallow weeds. A beaver lodge rose on the left bank, sticks piled high and silvered at the tips. The water around it held a scum of chewed bark. A row of peeled sticks floated near the entrance like something sorted by small hands.

Alex pointed with his paddle.

"That occupied?"

Will said, "Probably."

"Do beavers attack?"

Mike said, "If you annoy them."

Alex looked back at Joel. "Why is that always the answer?"

"It's the honest answer to most animal questions."

Alex dipped his blade again. "I grew up around animals that could kill you. Beavers feel insulting."

"Beavers can ruin your day," Will said.

"Lots of things can ruin my day. Plain oatmeal. Small talk. Men named Todd."

Mike looked over his shoulder. "Paddle."

Alex paddled.

The lake narrowed further. The water changed from black-green to brown where a creek entered through grass and alder. Mud smell came through the cold, rich and sour. A kingfisher rattled from a dead branch and crossed in front of them, blue-gray and quick, sounding offended by their presence.

Joel watched the bird go. For one clean second he wanted nothing from the trip except this: water under the boat, a bird's harsh little engine of a voice, the old stupid relief of being required by a task.

"You coming or writing a poem?" Alex asked.

Joel dipped his paddle. "Waiting for you to remember yours."

"See, that's the wounded quiet-guy thing I'm talking about."

"Paddle."

Alex laughed and paddled. The canoe straightened.

For the first hour Joel felt the old pleasure return in the muscles before it reached the mind. The pull of the blade. The little shift of weight in the hips. The corrected angle. The canoe sliding as if effort were being translated into something cleaner than effort. In Madison, his days had become too square. Rooms, screens, calendar blocks, errands fitted between calls, wedding decisions that seemed small until there were fifty of them. Mara had been kind about his usefulness and less kind about his silences. She wanted a date, a guest list, a house that did not keep accumulating boxes in the second bedroom, and some evenings he had loved her most when she stood in the mess with a marker in her teeth, naming everything before it could drift. Here the work was dumb and whole. Push water. Move north. Avoid rock. Keep gear dry. Watch weather. Eat when possible.

That was why he came. Or part of why. The rest was harder to name and easier to ignore. The trip let the old group exist in the tense it understood. Present work. Present weather. Present jokes. A man could become useful without saying what ordinary life had asked him to decide.

Mike raised his paddle ahead and pointed left.

Will said something Joel could not hear. Mike pointed again, sharper this time. Then he cleared his throat, small and official even across the water.

"Portage is after the bend," Mike called. "Stay right until the dead pine. It opens up after this first tight stretch."

Will turned partway. "Define opens up."

"Bigger water."

"That's a description, not a definition."

"It's water. It does not need a glossary."

Alex turned. "What's the verdict, back there?"

"Portage soon."

"Like portage portage?"

"No. Decorative kind."

"I'm asking because backpacking made me emotionally prepared for carrying things, but not boats."

"You'll be fine."

"I love when people lie gently."

They followed Mike and Will toward a low opening in the shore that did not look like a portage until they were almost on it. A strip of mud, two rocks placed by human hands, alder pressed back enough to suggest a path. There was an old blaze on a spruce trunk, nearly healed over. The mark had not disappeared, but the tree had been working on it for years. The landing smelled of muck and wet leaves.

Mike got out first in calf-deep water and held the canoe. Will stepped into the shallows, slipped once in his hiking sandals, recovered, and swore with unnecessary precision.

"Rock," he said.

Mike looked down. "Yes."

"Under silt."

"Where rocks live."

Joel brought their canoe in beside them. Alex reached for a branch and missed it. The canoe swung sideways.

"Left," Joel said.

"My left or boat left?"

"Your actual left."

"Good. I only brought the one."

Joel planted his paddle and held them steady. Alex got out with one foot in water and one on rock, paused in a shape no man could hold long, then committed to the water with a splash.

"Dry feet lasted ninety-three minutes," he said.

Will looked over. "That's optimistic."

"Emotionally dry."

The first landing took their voices and lowered them. Alder crowded the shore, and the lake behind them was already partly hidden by leaves. Mud took their footprints and filled them with shining brown water. A few dragonflies moved low over the surface, bright-bodied and mechanical.

They unloaded enough for the portage. Mike did not want to double back more than necessary. Will did not want to overload the boats on the trail and crack something. This produced ten minutes of argument beside the water while mosquitoes found them in the alder shade.

"We single portage if we're not stupid about it," Mike said.

"We double portage if we're not stupid about it," Will said.

Alex slapped at his wrist. "Those sound suspiciously similar."

Joel lifted one pack and weighed it by feel. "How long is it?"

"Quarter mile," Mike said.

Will made a small dry cough into his shoulder.

Mike turned. "What?"

"Old report said closer to half."

"Old report included whining."

"You can't correct distance with attitude."

"Watch me."

Joel looked at the path. It rose almost immediately from the landing, muddy at first, then root-bound under spruce. He could see ten yards before it bent and disappeared. Quarter mile, half mile. Both were short if the footing was good. Both were long under a canoe.

"Let's not make the first portage heroic," he said.

Mike gave him a look that asked whether he was taking Will's side. Joel ignored it and pulled his pack from the canoe.

They decided on a modified double portage, which meant everyone got to act as if compromise had occurred. First trip: heavy packs, rods, paddles, map case, the food barrel carried between Mike and Alex on a strap because neither trusted the other with it alone. Second trip: canoes and whatever had been left to punish them for optimism.

The food barrel latch snapped when Mike checked it, one clean plastic click under the alder.

"Still closed?" Will asked.

"It was closed before," Mike said.

"That is often when things stop being closed."

"Stop helping."

Alex slipped one end of the barrel strap over his shoulder. "I want the record to show I am carrying group food and therefore group morale."

Mike took the other end. "It's mostly tortillas and oats."

"Exactly. Fragile morale."

The trail was worse than it had looked and better than it could have been. Mud at the start, then roots, then a rise over granite where the moss had been scraped by other sandals and boots and never quite recovered. Spruce branches brushed their shoulders. A few birches leaned pale and thin among the darker trees. The air inside the portage held less wind and more insect.

It was a narrow enough trail that the order of men became a kind of statement. Mike in front because he had claimed the route. Alex close behind because the food barrel forced him there. Will then Joel, old church boys in middle age, one talking too much and one answering less than he meant. Behind them the lake went on being itself without witnesses.

Will walked ahead of Joel with two rods in one hand and a pack that looked too high on his shoulders.

"You good?" Joel asked.

"I'm calibrated."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the answer before the answer."

Up ahead, Mike said, "Step left here."

Alex said, "Your left or my left?"

"Trail left."

"That's not a thing."

"It is if you understand trails."

"I understand mountains. Trails there have dignity."

"These have water."

"Water has no dignity."

The food barrel knocked against a tree with a hollow plastic thud.

Mike said, "Watch it."

Alex said, "The tree attacked me."

"Tree had right of way."

Joel walked with his head slightly down, watching footing. Mud took the tread of his hiking sandals and gave back a sucking sound. His heel scraped once on stone, a dry little slip under the wet noise. The pack straps bit into the muscle above his collarbones. Sweat started under his fleece despite the cool morning. His body had not yet accepted the trip. It was still negotiating.

Halfway through the portage, the trail crossed an open patch of rock. Sun came down clean there. Joel stopped and shifted the pack to ease one shoulder. From the rock he could see a narrow piece of the lake behind them through black trunks, bright where the fog had burned off. Their canoes were hidden by alder at the landing. The water looked empty in the way water looked empty when a man was too far from it to know.

Something knocked.

Once.

Wood on hollow wood. Or a paddle against a gunwale. A small sound, ordinary and exact enough to make his body answer before his mind did.

Joel turned his head.

There was no wind strong enough to move anything heavy. The men ahead were still ahead, Mike's voice low and irritated, Alex answering with too much brightness. Will was just in front of Joel, close enough that Joel could see sweat darkening the back of his shirt and the two rod tips trembling beside his shoulder.

Will stopped ahead of him. "What?"

Joel listened. Mike and Alex kept moving ahead, still arguing about whether rice was a side dish or a failure of imagination.

"You hear that?"

Will held still. His face changed into the attentive blank he used when listening for a small mechanical fault or a fish taking line. The woods gave them insects, breath, the soft movement of water too far behind them to see.

"Hear what?" Will asked.

Joel looked back through the trees. The lake flashed between spruce trunks. No canoe showed. No person. No movement except water moving around what he could not see.

"Nothing," Joel said.

Will waited a second longer. "Branch maybe."

"Probably."

"You sure?"

Joel smiled faintly. "That's your job."

"My job is to introduce uncertainty, not resolve it."

"Then you're doing great."

They moved on.

He expected the sound to come again. It did not. That should have settled it. One sound was not a pattern. In the woods, a single knock had more possible bodies than a man could count.

At the far end, the portage dropped to a second landing, better than the first. Flat rock sloped into a narrower piece of water that ran north between alder and low marsh. The lake they had crossed was behind them now. Ahead, the route tightened into something that might be called a creek if no one wanted to sound grand.

Mike and Alex set the food barrel down. Alex bent with his hands on his knees.

"Quarter mile, my ass."

Will came in behind Joel and looked at Mike.

Mike said, "It was a quarter mile emotionally."

"That's not how distance works."

Alex pointed at Will without looking up. "There it is."

They drank water and went back for the boats.

The return trip felt shorter because their bodies knew the path and nothing heavy sat on their backs. The sound of their own steps changed after they had walked a trail once. First pass, every root was a question. Second pass, the body had made a rough map. Step here. Duck there. Mud on the left. Rock on the right. Branch at eye level if you stopped paying attention.

At the first landing, the two canoes lay where they had left them, half-hidden in alder and shadow. Water lapped softly against one hull. A painter rope tapped once against the front of it, light and quick.

Joel looked at it longer than necessary.

There was the answer. Rope. Water. Boat shifting. A small hard tap, light enough to travel farther than it deserved. Refusing it would have felt theatrical.

Mike took the first canoe with Will. They lifted it, rolled it up, and set the center yoke on Will's shoulders. Will adjusted under the weight and took a few testing steps.

"You want me to take it?" Mike asked.

"No."

"You sure?"

"Yes."

"You're walking crooked."

"I have a canoe on my head."

Mike reached up anyway and moved one loose strap so it would not swing into Will's face.

"Better?" he asked.

Will stood still under the boat, surprised into honesty. "Yeah."

"Try not to die before lunch."

"That's the warmth I came for."

Alex watched them go. "That looks bad."

"It is," Joel said.

"Do we all pretend it's spiritual?"

"Mostly."

Joel and Alex carried the second canoe. Alex wanted to try the yoke and lasted thirty yards before admitting that backpacking had not prepared him for carrying a boat upside down while blind to the sky and personally hated by every branch on the route. Joel took over without comment.

Alex did not make a production of surrendering it, which Joel appreciated despite himself. He simply stopped, braced the canoe with both hands, and said, "This is stupid in a way I respect." Then he let Joel step under the yoke as if they had planned the exchange. That was the other thing about Alex. He could demand the room and still know when to hand over weight.

Under the canoe, the world became a strip of ground and feet. Joel saw mud, roots, Alex's sandals ahead when Alex carried the paddles, Mike's heels beyond him when the trail straightened. The canoe amplified some sounds and removed others. His breath came back at him. Brush scratched the hull inches from his ears. Once, a branch dragged along the canoe's side with a dry fingernail rasp.

He thought of the knock again and was annoyed with himself.

There were four of them. Two canoes. Paddles. Ropes. Trees. Water. Any wilderness trip produced a hundred hollow sounds before lunch. The mind kept the ones that arrived while it was facing the wrong way.

At the open rock, Alex stopped ahead and looked back.

"You alive?"

Joel shifted under the yoke. "Keep moving."

"That's not a yes."

"It's canoe for yes."

Alex waited until the trail widened by a foot, then reached up and lifted the front of the canoe over a leaning branch before it could scrape Joel's face.

"See?" Alex said. "I provide value."

"Put that on your resume."

"I will. Under wilderness leadership."

"Under branch awareness."

"Specialized field."

At the far landing they set the boats down and loaded again. This took longer than unloading because the order now mattered. Will's fishing rods had to be accessible without becoming spears. Alex's food could not be buried under the first-aid kit because he claimed low blood sugar made him philosophical. Rain gear near the top. Map case where Mike could reach it from the back. Joel's day bag behind his seat. Spare paddle along the left side.

Mike crouched by his canoe and pressed the Highlander key pouch inside the left side of his PFD as if the key might have become wet through suspicion alone. Joel saw the gesture and then looked away. The key belonged to another day, and another shore. Thinking of it here made the present route feel thinner, as if the whole week were a line stretched between two locked vehicles.

They pushed off into the narrow water just before noon.

The route after the portage was not much wider than two canoes. Alder leaned over both sides, and in places the branches met overhead. The water moved slowly enough that they still had to paddle. It was darker here, stained by leaves and old beaver work. The bottom showed in the shallows as a wavering brown skin over mud and stone.

The channel changed the scale of the day. On the lake, each man's voice had room to flatten and travel. Here every sound had edges. A paddle drip ticked near the ear. A branch scraped a pack and seemed to happen inside the boat. Their small jokes lost distance and became breath, close and human, under the alder roof.

Will had his rod in his lap within five minutes.

Mike said, "No."

"I'm not fishing."

"You are holding a fishing rod."

"I'm preparing to fish."

"Prepare to paddle."

Will looked back over his shoulder. "There is a difference."

"Not to the boat."

Alex whispered, loudly enough for everyone, "There's trouble in the marriage."

Joel corrected their angle around a half-submerged log. Alex's strokes had grown lazy after the portage, but he admitted it without shame.

"My arms have filed a complaint."

"Denied," Joel said.

"You're a harsh captain."

"I'm not a captain."

"Back guy."

"Still no."

"Rear admiral."

"Absolutely not."

The channel bent right, then left, then right again. Mike called back directions in ordinary language. "Stay left." "Come right." "Shallow middle." Will argued with some of them and obeyed most. Joel followed their line when it made sense and chose his own when it did not. He liked being in back because the back let him see the whole boat's mistake before it arrived, and because correction looked almost like patience from there.

Once, the channel pinched so tight that both canoes had to wait while Mike and Will scraped through a slot between a drowned log and a mat of grass. The hull made a low rasp against hidden gravel. Mike leaned right, then corrected.

"Slow," he called back.

Alex looked over his shoulder. "Slow like cautious or slow like we're about to learn something?"

"Just slow."

"I hate the minimalist school of instruction."

Joel let the current take them a foot, then stopped the canoe with a brace. The branch tips ticked against Alex's sleeve. The sound was small and close. Behind them, somewhere back in the alder, water dropped from a lifted branch in a neat series of taps, counting down to nothing.

Alex turned once and saw Joel looking past him.

"You always this serious on water?"

"Yes."

"Good to know."

"You always this chatty?"

"Also yes."

"Good to know."

Alex smiled and faced forward. For a few minutes he paddled better.

They stopped for lunch on a low gravel bar where the channel widened under a stand of jack pine. The place was not a campsite, just a place to get out. Enough stone to stand on, enough sun to push back the bugs, enough current that the water sounded clean over the shallows.

Will had a line in the water before Mike had opened the food barrel.

"I thought you were hungry," Mike said.

"I am spiritually hungry for fish."

"You catch one now, we're not cooking it."

"I didn't ask you to be involved."

"You're in my canoe."

"That's a logistical arrangement, not a moral framework."

Mike opened the food barrel. The latch snapped up, then the second one. Two small sounds, useful and exact. Alex sat on a rock and opened his private bag of bland food. He pulled out tortillas, peanut butter, and a packet of tuna he regarded with uncertainty.

"Branching out?" Joel asked.

"This is advanced work."

"That tuna has lemon pepper."

"I know. I'm not afraid."

Will cast once into the seam of current beside a downed branch. The spinner flashed and vanished. He worked it back with small motions of his wrist, face emptied of all the restless talk he used everywhere else. Fishing made him quiet in a way nothing else did. Or not quiet exactly. Directed.

Mike noticed Joel noticing.

"Only time he shuts up," Mike said.

Will did not look back. "I can hear you."

"I know."

"I choose not to respond because I am spiritually large."

"You're five-nine."

"Five-ten with purpose."

The reel ticked under Will's thumb. Line whispered through the guides. The lure hit the current with a small bright plink and vanished as if the water had closed over a coin.

Alex took a bite of tuna in a tortilla and made a face.

Mike said, "Too spicy?"

"Too narrative."

Joel laughed. He could not help it. Alex looked offended and pleased.

It irritated Joel to enjoy him that easily. Alex worked for reaction the way Will worked for precision and Mike worked for control. Sometimes the work showed. Sometimes, like now, it arrived as warmth in the middle of miles, mud, and Mike's watch.

The sun held while they ate. The day had warmed enough that steam rose faintly from wet cuffs and sandal straps. Bugs worked the shaded edges but left the gravel mostly alone. A breeze moved down the channel and brought the smell of mud, pine pitch, and old water. Joel sat with his elbows on his knees and watched the current fold around a stone.

For a while nobody needed anything from anyone.

Then Mike stood. "Ten minutes."

Alex groaned. "We just sat down."

"We have miles."

"We always have miles. Miles are your love language."

Will reeled in. "He's right, unfortunately."

"Thank you," Mike said.

"I said unfortunately."

They packed lunch, refilled bottles through the filter, checked the boats, and pushed on. The food barrel latch clicked shut again. Joel noticed because the latch had become part of the day, as ordinary as paddle drip or Will's reel.

The afternoon became more physical. The channel twisted and shoaled. Twice they had to get out and walk the canoes through ankle-deep gravel. Once Alex stepped into mud up to mid-calf and came out missing a sandal for two dramatic seconds before the suction gave up with an obscene sound.

"I have been personally attacked," he said.

Will turned in the front of Mike's canoe. "By geology."

"Geology has had it in for me since Alaska."

Mike said, "Stop standing where it's black."

"The entire bottom is black."

"Then be smarter than the bottom."

Joel held their canoe steady while Alex rinsed his sandal. The water was cold enough to make Joel's fingers ache when he reached down to help clear mud from the straps. Alex looked at him, then at Mike and Will ahead, as if checking whether gratitude would be witnessed and therefore ruined.

"You don't have to."

"I know."

"Very Midwestern of you."

"I'm engaged. We covered this."

Alex laughed, but quieter than usual.

For a moment the performance fell away from him. He was just a man with one bare foot on gravel, one muddy sandal in hand, calf hair slicked flat by black water. He looked younger without the noise, and more tired. Then he tightened the strap, stamped once to settle his foot, and put the face back on.

"Gentlemen," Alex said, louder, "my foot has returned from war."

"Good," Mike said. "Tell it to paddle."

By three, the channel began to widen again. The alder fell back. Ahead, through a low opening, Joel saw broader water and a strip of pale rock beyond it. Mike's canoe drifted while he checked the map case. Will used the pause to cast toward a submerged log.

Mike said, "Put the rod away."

"One cast."

"You said that six casts ago."

"Time is a construct."

"So is swimming back for your rod if I throw it."

Will reeled in and set the rod down with exaggerated tenderness.

Mike looked at Joel. "First camp should be another hour. Maybe less."

"Granite point?"

"If nobody's there."

Alex turned around. "And if somebody's there?"

"Then we keep going."

"How far?"

Mike did not answer right away.

Will did it for him. "Far enough to make us complain."

"That could be anywhere," Alex said.

Joel looked toward the widening water. The wind touched it lightly. Not enough to worry about. Enough to show direction. The sun had shifted west and softened. The day was still good, but it was no longer unused.

They paddled out of the channel and into the next lake.

It opened slowly, not all at once. First a widening, then a view past a point, then water running north in a long dark reach with granite rising on the left and low spruce on the right. Far ahead, too far to matter yet, a bird lifted from shore and crossed low over the lake. The canoes separated a little in the larger water. Mike and Will moved toward the left-hand point, and Joel let their boat run in the small wake.

Alex rested his paddle across the canoe.

"Okay," he said. "This part does not suck."

Joel let the boat glide. "Careful. Mike might hear."

"He already knows. That's what makes him insufferable."

Ahead, Mike lifted his paddle and pointed toward the left shore where the granite point pushed into the lake. Will called something back, but the wind took it. Mike pointed again.

Joel angled the canoe to follow.

Behind them, from somewhere near the channel mouth, came a single hollow knock.

Not loud. Not dramatic. Just wood against wood, or paddle against canoe, or a branch shifting in water. The same family of sound as before, if a man wanted to make families out of sounds.

Joel turned.

The channel behind them lay empty. Alder. Brown water. A few floating leaves turning in the slow current. The place they had just come through held still in the afternoon light. The sound had not echoed or wandered. That was what bothered him. It had arrived with a clean edge, aimed at the back of his neck.

Alex looked back too. "What was that?"

Mike and Will were ahead, their canoe moving toward the point. Will had his paddle across his lap, not in the water. Mike's blade flashed once on the right. Their boat gave him nothing he could use.

"Probably a branch," Joel said.

Alex waited, listening. For once he did not improve the silence.

The sound did not come again.

"Sure," Alex said.

He turned forward and took up his paddle.

Joel stayed looking back for one second more. The channel was already closing into distance. Sound carried over water. Boats made noises after you left them. Branches knocked. Ropes tapped. The world had joints and hinges everywhere if you listened long enough, and most of them did not mean anything.

He dipped his paddle and brought the canoe back on line.

They went on toward the first camp with enough daylight to feel successful. Behind them, the channel made no more sound. That helped. Joel let it help while it still could.
