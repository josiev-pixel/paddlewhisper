# Chapter 9 - Bear Work

Morning did not make Alex easier to see. It only made the details insist on themselves.

For a while there was no sunrise, only the dark thinning into usable gray. Fog sat low in the channel and did not move like weather. It lay over the black water in a torn sheet, caught in the alder and the spruce roots, and the far bank came and went inside it. The campsite smelled of wet ash, bear spray, blood, mud, crushed alder, and the sour human fear that had come out of them in the night and dried cold on their shirts.

Nobody had slept. They had leaned under the tarp, then straightened, then jerked out of half dreams when water dripped from the seam or a spruce needle ticked against nylon. The cold had got into the joints of their hands. Their headlamp straps had left marks in their hair. At some point Will had begun shaking without admitting he was cold. At some point Mike had stopped saying anything at all.

When there was enough light to make the world accountable, Mike stood.

"We have to look," he said.

Will did not move. He was still sitting with his back against the turned canoe, knees up, the map case clamped between his hands. He had held it most of the night. The plastic was bent where his thumbs had worried the edge.

"No," he said.

Mike looked toward the alder. His jaw worked once. "Yes."

"You can say that in your little trip-leader voice all you want."

"It isn't a vote."

"Everything is a vote after somebody dies."

Joel stood before Mike could answer. His knees hurt. The night had put ten years into them. He picked up the hatchet from where it lay beside the packs, then looked at it in his hand. It was a camp tool with a nicked orange handle and black pitch dried below the head. It had split kindling two nights ago while Alex complained that nobody appreciated the wood arts. In Joel's grip it became both more serious and more useless.

Will watched him. "You too?"

"We have to know what we can know," Joel said.

The sentence sounded like something from an office, a meeting with a whiteboard and bad coffee, a way to reduce damage to categories. He hated it as soon as he said it. But the other choices were worse. Stand here. Sit here. Listen. Let the brush keep Alex.

Mike took the bear spray. There was less in it now. Joel could tell by the way Mike weighed it without meaning to, the little drop of his wrist. Mike saw Joel see it and looked away.

"Stay close," Mike said.

"Really?" Will said.

Mike's head turned a fraction.

Will's mouth stayed open, but no more came out. He got up slowly, as if every part of him had to be persuaded separately. He still held the map case.

"Leave that," Mike said.

Will looked down at it as if surprised to find it there. "No."

"It won't help."

"Neither will your can of spicy air, apparently."

Mike took one step toward him. Joel said, "Enough."

The word was small, but it landed because none of them had strength for the old shapes yet. Mike stopped. Will tucked the map case under one arm and followed.

They went through the same gap in the alder they had made at night. In daylight, the distance was obscene. Ten yards. Less than the length of two canoes. A man could throw a mug from the fire ring and hit the place where Alex had died. Joel had spent the night making the distance larger because terror needed space to justify itself. Morning took that away.

The alder held the marks of them and of whatever else had moved there. Branches were snapped at shoulder height and knee height. Leaves hung torn and silver-backed. Mud was smeared on stems. The black ground underfoot had been cut by sandals, knees, roots, something broader, something dragged. Bear spray had left orange dust on low leaves. It looked artificial in the gray, like paint from a child's toy.

Joel tried not to step in blood and found that there was no clean path.

Mike stopped first. Will stopped behind Joel with a hard intake of breath.

Alex lay where they had left him.

That fact had its own violence. Joel had known it would be true, but some childish part of him had expected morning to have corrected the scene, to have moved Alex into a form that could be approached. Morning had no such mercy. The body was still half turned toward the water, one arm bent under him, one hand open in the mud as if he had been interrupted while reaching. His shirt was torn and dark. His hiking sandal was gone from one foot. His other sandal was still strapped on, absurdly intact, heel packed with black muck.

Joel kept his eyes on the hand.

It was less terrible than the rest and therefore worse. Mud had dried in the creases of the palm. One fingernail was broken. The fingers were slightly curled. It was recognizably Alex's hand, not a fact from a report, not a casualty, not a body part in the abstract. It was the hand that had snapped a mosquito dead against his calf and then held it up for inspection. The hand that had stirred noodles like a man conducting. The hand that had reached back without being asked to steady Joel's boat over a beaver dam.

"No, no," Will said.

Not the panic from last night. Smaller. A correction addressed to the world.

Mike cleared his throat. The sound was raw from pepper spray. "We need to check the site."

Will stared at him. "The site."

"Will."

"No, say it again. I want to hear how professional you can make it."

"If it was a bear, we need to know if it's still close."

"If?"

Mike's face tightened. "Because you're going to make a federal case out of the word."

"I am looking at our friend dead in the mud and trying to be accurate."

"Accuracy won't move us."

"Inaccuracy got us here."

The sentence hit harder than Will seemed to expect. Mike's eyes changed. For a moment the whole camp, the route, the two vehicles, the old map, the missing emergency device, all of it stood between them with Alex lying at its center.

Joel stepped between the next words before they could happen.

"Tracks first," he said.

Mike looked at him as if he had forgotten Joel was there.

"Tracks," Joel said again. "Then his spray. Then we cover him. Then we decide."

"We decided," Mike said.

"No, we didn't."

Will's head turned toward Joel.

Joel did not look at him. If he did, the relief in Will's face might ask for more than Joel could give.

They began with the ground because the ground at least pretended to be evidence.

The mud made no simple testimony. It took some marks too well and refused others. Where the water had seeped up through root holes, impressions slumped almost as soon as they formed. Where alder roots crossed under the surface, footprints split and slid. Their own night movements were everywhere: Mike's deeper heel, Will's narrow sandal tread, Joel's own scraped sideways step where he had coughed blind through the spray. Alex's tracks came from camp, paused near the edge, turned slightly as if he had shifted his weight, then disappeared into churn.

Beyond that was damage.

Something had crushed a low tunnel through the alder from the channel side. Or from the brush behind the channel. Or from the place where the channel widened and doubled back. Joel could not hold the map of it in his head. He crouched, one hand on a wet root, and studied a broad mark in the mud near Alex's shoulder.

It could have been a paw.

That was the worst of it. It could have been. Wide. Deep at one edge. A dragged smear where claws or roots or a slipping heel had cut through the top layer. He knew too little about bear tracks to argue with the possibility. He knew too much about pattern-making to trust his desire against it.

"There," Mike said.

He pointed with the bear spray, not touching anything.

Will leaned in. "Maybe."

"It's a print."

"Maybe."

"Don't maybe this."

"I'm not maybeing it." Will's voice shook with cold or anger. "I'm saying maybe because that's the correct word when you don't know."

"It is a bear print."

"How many toes?"

Mike stared at him.

"No, answer. How many toes do you see? Because I see mud and panic."

"Will."

"There should be a track line. Something that heavy doesn't do one magic print and levitate out."

"It's all churned up."

"By us. By him. By something. Fine. But look." Will crouched lower, careful not to touch the mud. His hand hovered over a place near the waterline where the ground should have held anything large leaving. "This is nothing."

"Water washed it."

"In six hours?"

"Current."

"Current goes that way."

"Jesus Christ."

"No, this is exactly the conversation."

Mike stepped back, then turned his lightless headlamp toward the brush as if habit could still make a beam. His throat worked. "A bear came into camp. Alex stepped out. It hit him. It left through the channel. That is what happened."

"You left out the part where he said 'What.'"

No one moved.

Fog pulled through the alder behind Alex, thinned, and caught again.

Joel felt the word from last night reopen in him. What. Alex's voice pitched irritated and curious, speaking to something just beyond his light. Not afraid yet. Not performing. Answering.

Mike said, "He heard brush."

"He answered somebody," Will said.

"No."

"You don't get to no that."

"He answered a sound."

"A sound that sounded like somebody."

Mike's face went hard and blank. "You don't know that."

"Neither do you."

Joel looked at the mud. "We heard him say it. That's all."

Will laughed once, a dry little break. "Great. Court-admissible Joel, everybody."

"I mean it," Joel said.

"I know you mean it. That's why it's annoying."

Mike lowered the bear spray. "The more we stand here talking, the less daylight we have."

Daylight. The word had seemed generous from under the tarp. Here it was already being spent. The fog blurred the channel, but above the trees Joel could see the low ceiling of cloud moving faster than he liked. Wind had not reached the ground yet. It worked in the spruce tops first, a high rubbing sound. Later it would find open water.

They found Alex's bear spray six feet from his body, half under leaves, safety still in place.

Mike stared at it for too long.

"He didn't get it off," Will said.

"I can see that."

"I'm saying--"

"I can see it."

Joel picked it up before either of them could use it in the argument. The canister was cold and wet. Mud filled the groove around the safety clip. Alex had carried it clipped to the front of his PFD because Mike had told him twice to stop putting it in the side pocket of his pack.

Quit mothering me, Alex had said the first time, at Will's house, grinning while he changed nothing.

Then, on the first lake, when Mike pointed at the canister with his paddle and told him again, Alex had clipped it where it belonged with great ceremony and said, Look at me growing as a person.

Joel slid the canister into his own jacket pocket. The pocket sagged.

"Don't take his stuff," Will said.

"It's spray," Joel said.

"I know what it is."

"Then don't make me say why."

Will looked away.

There were other objects. Alex's missing sandal lay upside down in the water weeds, where the current worried at the strap. One of his gloves had been mashed into mud. A torn corner of rain jacket hung on a broken alder stem. The cracked headlamp, which Mike had taken the night before, was back at camp in the cookpot where Joel had put it because he could not stand holding it and could not put it with anyone's personal gear. That small inventory became an accusation. Things could be recovered. A man could not.

They did what they could for Alex.

No one called it burial. The ground was root and water and cold stone under a skin of mud. They had no shovel. They had no right tool and no safe time and no strength for a task that would not protect him from anything. Mike brought the spare groundsheet from Alex's tent. Will objected because it was Alex's, then stopped because every object here was Alex's or no one's now.

They unfolded the groundsheet beside him. It snapped softly in the damp air.

"Careful," Mike said.

Will's eyes flashed. "I was planning to be casual."

"I'm saying--"

"Don't."

Joel knelt in the mud. The wet came through the knee of his pants immediately. He took the corner of the sheet. Mike took another. Will stood useless for two breaths, then crouched and took the third, his hand shaking so badly the plastic trembled.

Moving Alex even a little was impossible and necessary.

Joel closed his mind around the work. Corner here. Lift enough to pull cloth. Don't look at the torn place. Don't let the sheet catch under the arm. Don't put weight on the open hand. Mike said something under his breath that might have been a curse or a prayer. Will made a sound and bit it off. The groundsheet slid under part of Alex and bunched under another part and would not do what they needed. They had to try twice.

At last they covered him as well as they could.

The sheet made a poor shape over the body, too low in one place, too high in another. Rainwater already beaded on it. They gathered stones from the channel edge to hold the corners. The stones were cold and slick. Joel chose each one as if choosing correctly mattered. A flat one for the shoulder corner. A heavier round one near the feet. A granite chunk with lichen on it for the side where the wind might get under.

Will set his stone down, then moved it an inch, then moved it back.

"This is insane," he said.

Mike wiped mud from his hand onto his pants. "It's what we can do."

"That sentence should be illegal."

"You have a better one?"

Will looked at the groundsheet. "No."

The admission emptied him. He stepped back and put both hands on the top of his head, fingers locked in his hair. His map case hung from the crook of his elbow. Joel remembered Will at twelve in the church basement, doing the same thing over a lost youth-group scavenger hunt clue, furious that the world would not yield to close reading. Joel had not thought of that room in years: folding chairs, watered-down lemonade, the smell of dust and floor wax, someone's mother telling them to use indoor voices. Will had wanted the clue to be fair. Joel had already suspected that fairness was something adults said when they had run out of patience.

Now Will stood in the alder beside a covered body and still wanted the clue to be fair.

Joel turned away from him before the memory could make a speech out of itself.

Mike took the map case from Will without asking and opened it on a root-wide patch of ground. The plastic crackled. He pointed to their approximate position with a muddy finger.

"We go back," Will said at once.

Mike did not look up. "We don't know that yet."

"We know it."

"Back means the rough portage, the beaver mess, the wind lake from yesterday, all of it against us with three men and two boats."

"Forward means unknown."

"Forward means shorter to the Highlander."

"On paper."

"On the map."

"Those are the same only when the map isn't lying."

Mike's eyes lifted. "The map is not lying."

"Fine. The map is politely omitting."

Joel could see both routes as bodily pain. Back meant passing the beaver dams again, the rough portage, the narrow channel where each ordinary sound was now contaminated by what had followed them into camp. Forward meant wind they had not tested, camps they had not seen, the old cabin mark that might be nothing, Mike's confidence moving ahead of evidence. Both directions required leaving Alex under a groundsheet in the alder.

"We can't carry him," Mike said.

Will's jaw set. "You don't know that."

"I do."

"He's not gear."

"That's why I'm saying it out loud before someone breaks himself trying to prove he loved him enough."

Will stepped toward him. Mike did not step back.

Joel got between them too late to prevent the words but early enough to prevent hands.

"If we try to carry him," Joel said, "we don't get out today. Maybe not tomorrow. We need both boats moving. We need three people able to portage. We need--"

"Stop," Will said.

Joel stopped.

Will's face had gone rigid. "Don't turn him into logistics."

Joel looked at the groundsheet. The stones. The dark water licking at alder roots. "I don't know how else to get us out."

That was the first fully honest thing anyone had said that morning.

No one answered it.

They marked the site. Mike tied a strip torn from Alex's orange stuff sack to an alder above the body, high enough to see from camp and low enough not to look like a flag. Will hated that too. Joel hated that they needed it. He made a note in Will's little waterproof notebook because his own was packed too deep: Second Camp. Body ten yards east/channel side of camp. Covered with gray groundsheet. Orange marker on alder. Date. Approximate time.

His handwriting looked calm. That was another kind of lie.

Will watched the pen move. "Write bear."

Mike looked at him.

"Go ahead," Will said. "If we're committing."

Joel kept the pen above the paper. "Observed possible large animal sign."

Will laughed without humor. "You are the worst kind of useful."

"I know."

"Put no clear tracks."

Mike said, "Don't."

Joel wrote: no clear track line.

Mike breathed through his nose. The sound was almost a snort, almost the beginning of an argument, but it did not become one.

From the brush on the far side of the channel came a low laugh.

Not loud. Not theatrical. It did not roll through the fog like a ghost story. It was a single bark of amusement, cut short in the old familiar way, the sound Alex made when a joke pleased him before he decided whether to make it bigger. A hard little laugh with his chest in it.

Will stopped breathing.

Mike's face emptied.

Joel's hand closed around the pen until the plastic bent.

The fog held still over the water. A cedar waxwing clicked somewhere upstream, small and real. The channel moved under the alder with its ordinary black patience.

"Bird," Mike said.

No one asked which bird.

The laugh did not repeat.

Joel waited for his mind to reject it. Grief, exhaustion, memory, a raven, a fox, water hitting a hollow root, Will making some involuntary sound and Joel assigning it the wrong owner. There were explanations if he reached for them. They lay around the moment like tools laid out on a tarp.

He did not pick one up.

Will said, very softly, "That was him."

"No," Mike said.

"Mike."

"No."

"You heard it."

"I heard an animal."

"You did not."

Mike turned so fast the map case slid off the root. "Then what? Say it. Say the thing you want to say."

Will opened his mouth.

Joel grabbed his sleeve. Not hard. Enough.

Will looked at the hand on him. His eyes were red, the skin below them gray. He understood, or nearly understood, before Joel did. Whatever came next would be heard. Whatever word they gave the morning would enter the woods and belong partly to the woods.

Will shut his mouth.

They stood that way for several seconds: Mike with the bear spray half raised toward fog, Will caught between speech and silence, Joel holding a pen and another man's sleeve beside a covered body.

Then, from closer, from the alder behind the groundsheet or from the channel bend or from nowhere Joel could put on a map, Alex's voice said, "Quit mothering me."

It was soft. Almost fond. Almost annoyed.

The phrase came out with the exact little drop on mothering, the way Alex had said it when warnings bored him but attention pleased him. It was not a scream from beyond death. It was not even a call. It was conversational, as if he were just out of sight adjusting a strap, as if he expected somebody to roll their eyes and let him continue.

Will made a sound like his body had been struck.

Mike sprayed into the alder.

The hiss tore the morning open. Orange fog burst against wet leaves and came back in pieces. Joel pulled Will backward by the sleeve. The spray drifted low and chemical, cutting through mud and blood and spruce. Mike swept the canister left to right, too fast, wasting what little remained, his arm rigid.

"Stop," Joel said.

Mike did not.

"Mike, stop."

The canister coughed and weakened. Mike lowered it. His mouth was open. His eyes streamed, though the spray had gone mostly away from him this time.

Nothing moved in the alder.

No body shifted under the groundsheet. No shape crossed the channel. No bear crashed away. No Alex stood up laughing at the cruelty of it. The woods gave them wet leaves, fog, black water, the cedar waxwing's last small tick before it flew.

Will bent and vomited into the mud. There was little in him. Coffee acid, water, a string of bile. He stayed folded over afterward, one hand braced on a root, the other still holding the map case against his ribs as if it were an organ.

Mike wiped his eyes with the back of his wrist. "We leave now."

His voice was hoarse and flat.

Will spat. "You don't get to say bear anymore."

"I didn't."

"You were about to."

"I said we leave."

"Because what, the bear learned comedy?"

Mike took two steps toward him and stopped with the spray canister hanging useless at his side. His control had not returned. Only the outline of it. "Because staying beside him is worse."

The words were simple enough to stand.

Joel looked at the groundsheet. Rain or fog had gathered on the plastic and begun to run in narrow lines toward the mud. One bead hung at the edge, swelled, fell onto a leaf. The leaf dipped and came back up.

He wanted to say Alex's name. Not to the brush, not as answer, not as lure. Just once, properly, to the covered shape on the ground. He did not. The wanting itself frightened him. It felt like stepping toward a voice.

They went back to camp without turning their backs until the alder forced them to.

The camp in daylight looked worse than it had in darkness because daylight made its inadequacy visible. The tarp sagged with condensation. The fire ring was a black scrape full of wet ash. Alex's tent stood half open, one guyline slack, the fly torn along the lower edge where some branch or body had caught it in the confusion. The turned canoe lay between the fire ring and the channel like a child's idea of a wall. Paddles were lined along a log where Will had placed them during the night, a neat row of useless tools for a crossing no one wanted.

Mike went straight to the food barrel.

"Don't," Will said.

Mike stopped with his hand on the rope. "We need to know."

"We know enough."

"No. We know one thing we can't use."

He lowered the barrel carefully. The rope rasped over the branch. The sound made Joel's shoulders tighten. The barrel descended in little jerks, pale and round, until Mike caught it against his chest and set it on the ground.

The latch was not open, exactly. One tongue had not seated fully into its keeper. The red strap crossed the lid, but the metal tab sat proud by half an inch, enough to make a small clean shadow.

Joel knew Mike had checked it in the night. He had watched him. Mike had lifted the latch, pressed it down, tied the rope, yanked the line once. The barrel had knocked against the trunk, and all three men had listened to the knock come back from the channel side. But he also knew hands failed under pressure. He knew a latch could look closed when it was not. He knew memory edited itself to reduce blame or increase it.

Mike crouched and touched the tab.

"Don't contaminate the evidence," Will said.

Mike gave him a look.

"Sorry," Will said. "That was the wrong genre."

Mike pressed the latch. It clicked home.

All three men looked toward the alder.

Nothing answered.

The relief was not relief. It was only one expected bad thing declining to happen.

Mike opened the barrel and checked the contents. Oatmeal, coffee, noodles, peanut butter, rice packets, Will's spices in doubled bags, Alex's plain rice meals with his name written on duct tape in thick marker because he had claimed otherwise the communists would steal them. Everything was there. Nothing torn. No claw marks in the plastic. No food dragged through camp.

"Food didn't bring it," Will said.

Mike closed the barrel. "Food could have attracted it without being eaten."

"You hear yourself?"

"Yes."

"Do you?"

"Enough."

Joel expected Will to answer. He did not. He was looking at Alex's labeled rice meals through the translucent side of the barrel. His face had lost the argument and found something worse.

Packing was another form of lying. It required belief in later. Later they would need dry socks. Later they would need the stove. Later a map would matter. Later a pot should be nested correctly and fuel capped and straps tightened. The body wanted only to sit down, or run, or go back into the alder and remove the groundsheet and undo what had happened by force of wanting.

They packed anyway.

They did it badly.

Joel poured too much water into the coffee grounds because his hand shook when he meant to stop. Brown liquid climbed the filter cone and spilled over the rim onto the dirt. He stood watching it run past a spruce root until he heard Alex in memory, not from the brush but from yesterday evening by the stove: This is less coffee than a wet opinion. Joel had rolled his eyes then. Now the line arrived whole and useless, and he gripped the mug until heat hurt his fingers.

Will took down Alex's tent.

No one had asked him to. Maybe that was why he did it. He unzipped the door, then stopped with one hand on the pull. The zipper made a small dry line through the morning.

Mike turned sharply.

"It's me," Will said.

The phrase hung there. It should have been ordinary. It had become impossible to say without meaning too much.

Will ducked into the tent and began handing things out: sleeping bag in its stuff sack, a shirt, socks, a paperback swollen slightly from damp, a spare bland-food pouch from the tent pocket, a little bag of electrolyte tablets, a headnet, spare batteries. Each object came out with an afterlife already attached. Joel put them into Alex's pack because no other category existed.

At the bottom of the tent, Will found a small plastic bottle of hot sauce wrapped in a sock. He held it up and made a sound that was almost a laugh.

"He lied," Will said.

Mike looked over.

"Bland food purity was a lie," Will said.

No one answered.

Will screwed the bottle tighter and put it in the pack with unnecessary care.

Joel took the cracked headlamp from the cookpot. Dried blood darkened one side of the strap. Mud had got into the hinge. He could not decide whether to pack it with Alex's gear or keep it out as evidence or throw it into the channel because looking at it made the morning tilt. He wrapped it in a spare bandanna and put it in the top pocket of Alex's pack.

"We take his pack?" Will said.

"Some of it," Mike said.

"What does that mean?"

"It means we can't carry everything."

"I can carry his pack."

"No."

"You don't know what I can carry."

"I know what you can carry over one portage while crying and not sleeping. I know what you can carry over six."

Will's face twisted. "Go to hell."

"Later."

It was the kind of Mike line that would once have worked. Dark, quick, irritatingly timed. No one laughed. Mike seemed to realize this half a second after he said it. His mouth tightened, but apology would have been a different language and none of them had that language ready.

They divided Alex into necessities and evidence and things too personal to abandon.

Necessities: bear spray, fuel, batteries, first-aid odds and ends, one fleece layer, food. Evidence: headlamp, the safety-on spray, a note about where the body was, the orange marker, photographs if they could take them. Things too personal to abandon: wallet sealed in a dry bag, phone with no service, a small notebook with only mileage estimates and grocery lists in it, the hot sauce because Will put it in his own pocket and dared anyone to speak.

Mike took photographs of the site from the camp edge and from inside the alder. He used his phone, then held it up afterward and checked service again though he knew. No bars. No emergency call. The blankness of the screen had become another voice saying the same thing.

"Battery," Joel said.

Mike lowered the phone. "Yeah."

He turned it off.

Will watched him. "We could climb."

"What?"

"Tree. Ridge. Anything. Signal sometimes--"

"There is no ridge."

"You don't know."

"Look around."

"Trees are not a topographic argument."

"We are not climbing wet spruce for one imaginary bar."

"People get emergency calls out with no displayed service."

"People also fall out of trees."

Joel said, "Wind's coming up."

That redirected them because it was outside and therefore harder to accuse.

The spruce tops were moving now. The fog on the channel had begun to tear into strips. Beyond the narrow water, through the screen of alder, the next lake showed only as a pale hard brightness. The surface was not visible from camp, but they could hear it beginning to work: small waves touching rock, then not touching, then touching again. A steady increase. A clock with no face.

Mike went to the map and spread it on the turned canoe. He put stones on two corners. Will came despite himself. Joel stood back, close enough to see but not close enough to become responsible for the route.

"Back to First Camp," Will said.

"Forward to the larger lake, then the marked site if we can make it," Mike said.

"If we can make it is not a plan."

"It is when every plan starts with if."

"The rough portage is behind us. We know it."

"And it took the four of us half the afternoon fresh."

"We were not fresh."

"We were fresher than this."

"Forward gets us to the cabin mark eventually," Will said, jabbing the map with one finger.

Mike looked at him sharply.

"What? That's your thing. Map says there's a cabin."

"Not today."

"Then why forward?"

"Because the take-out is forward."

"And the body is behind us," Will said.

There it was. The real geography.

Mike folded the edge of the map, then flattened it. His hands were steadier with paper than they had been with the spray. "We leave a note at camp. We mark the site. We travel. We get to the Highlander or to service or to people. We bring them back."

"Bring them back to what?"

Mike did not answer.

"No, say that one too," Will said. "Since we're saying practical things."

Joel looked toward the alder. The orange strip was just visible from camp, a small wrong color in the brush.

"We need to move while we still can," Joel said.

Will turned on him. "You keep saying we."

"Because it's still true."

"Is it?"

The question came without heat. That made it harder.

Joel had no answer that would not sound like a poster in a ranger station. Stay together. Keep moving. Don't panic. All the good advice in the world became thin when spoken beside a dead friend.

Will looked down first.

They finished packing. Mike loaded the canoes as if load order could repair reality. He put the heaviest packs low, centered the food barrel, tied the loose painter with too much force. Will repacked the map case twice. Joel checked the stove cap, then checked it again because he could not remember doing it. They left Alex's tent fly folded under a rock with a note in a freezer bag: ALEX B. BODY MARKED CHANNEL SIDE. GEAR PARTIAL. BEAR ATTACK SUSPECTED. NO SERVICE. GOING FOR HELP.

Joel wrote bear attack suspected because leaving the word out felt like cowardice and putting it in felt like fraud. Suspected was the hinge he could live with for the next hour.

Before they pushed off, Will walked to the edge of the alder gap.

Mike said, "Don't."

Will stopped.

The word had worked because it was not an order this time. It was a plea with bark on it.

Will stood with his back to them. "I'm not going in."

No one moved.

"I just--" He cleared his throat, and the small rough sound went into the trees. Joel flinched before he could stop himself. Will heard the flinch. His shoulders rose, then dropped. "I just need a second."

Mike opened his mouth and closed it.

Joel looked at the channel, at the fog taking itself apart, at the water brightening under wind. He understood that leaving would not be a clean action. It would be repeated all day. Every paddle stroke would leave Alex again. Every portage would leave him. Every practical decision that kept the living alive would commit the betrayal one more time.

Will did not say Alex's name. That was what Joel noticed. He stood at the edge of the brush and gave the silence nothing it could use.

Then, from somewhere near the covered body, very soft, came the click of a food barrel latch.

The actual food barrel sat in Mike's canoe, twenty feet behind them, strapped under the middle bar.

Mike turned. Will turned. Joel did not, for one second. He looked at the barrel in the canoe first. Red strap. Metal latch seated. Rope tied. Still.

The click did not repeat.

Will whispered, "No."

Joel said, "Don't answer it."

The words left him before he could weigh them. They were too loud in the morning and still not loud at all.

Mike lifted the mostly spent spray toward the alder, then lowered it. Something in his face had gone older and smaller.

"Boats," he said.

They got the canoes into the water. The hulls scraped mud and then floated free with a soft, living shift. Three men, two boats. The imbalance showed immediately. Mike would take one canoe alone with the food barrel and heavier gear. Joel and Will would take the other. No one said that Alex's absence had a seating arrangement.

Joel stepped into the back of the canoe. Will got in front with the map case between his knees. His paddle lay across his lap for a moment before he picked it up. Mike shoved off first, too hard, then corrected with two quick strokes.

For a few seconds they drifted in the narrow channel beside Second Camp.

The tarp sagged. The fire ring was black. Alex's tent site had become a flat wet patch under spruce. The orange strip in the alder moved once in the wind and then stilled. The groundsheet was hidden from the waterline, which was good and not good enough.

Joel put his paddle in.

The blade entered without splash. The water took the shape of it, closed around it, and let it go.

Behind them, from camp or brush or the channel's crooked mouth, Alex gave one low bark of laughter.

Will's shoulders seized.

Mike said, without turning, "Keep paddling."

No one said bear.
