# Chapter 9 - Bear Work

Morning did not make Alex easier to see. It only made the world stop helping them not see him.

There was no sunrise. The dark thinned into gray by small degrees, giving back one shape at a time: the turned canoe by the fire ring, the wet black circle of ash, the food barrel hanging too low in the back of camp. Then the alder gap, which had been only a wound in the dark and now was brush again: stems, leaves, mud, a corridor wide enough for a man to step through and not come back.

Fog sat on the channel. It did not move like weather yet. It lay low over the water, torn on alder branches, thickening where the channel bent out of sight. The camp smelled of wet ash, pepper spray, mud, blood, crushed leaves, and three men who had not slept. They had spent the night under the tarp with layers pulled over damp clothes, jerking awake whenever water fell from the seam or spruce needles ticked on nylon. Headlamp straps had cut lines into their foreheads. Their hands were stiff. Their eyes looked smaller in their faces.

Will still sat with his back against the canoe they had dragged sideways as a useless wall. He held the map case in both hands. He had held it for most of the night, maybe because it had edges, maybe because it was the only thing in camp still claiming that the world could be flattened, named, and followed.

Mike stood when there was enough light to see his own fingers.

"We have to look," he said.

Will did not move. "No."

Mike looked toward the alder. His jaw worked once. The skin under his eyes was raw from spray and no sleep. "Yes."

"You can say it in that voice all you want."

"It isn't a vote."

"It is if you want me to walk in there."

Joel stood before Mike could answer.

He did not feel brave. He felt the old pressure of a task no one wanted and no one could make disappear.

His knees hurt. The cold had stiffened them, and the night had put a private age into every joint. He picked up the hatchet from beside the packs. The orange handle was nicked and sticky with old pitch. Two nights ago Alex had held it badly while Mike told him to choke up, and Alex had said, "Quit mothering me," without changing his grip. It had been a camp tool then. A small corrective object. In Joel's hand now it became more serious and no more useful.

Will watched him. "You too?"

"We have to look at what we can," Joel said.

The sentence made Alex into work. Joel hated it. He hated that work was the only mercy left that did not turn at once into suicide or theater. Sit here. Listen. Wait for wind. Go back into the alder. Leave without looking. Every choice failed Alex. This one at least had an order.

Mike took the bear spray. There was less in it now. Joel could tell by the way Mike weighed it without meaning to, the small drop in his wrist before he straightened his hand. Mike saw Joel see it and looked away.

"Stay close," Mike said.

Will gave a dry laugh. "Good. Great. We have a plan."

Mike's head turned a fraction.

Will's mouth stayed open, but whatever he had meant to put through it did not come. He got up slowly, the map case still under one arm. He looked as if standing had become a kind of consent.

"Leave that," Mike said.

Will looked down at the plastic sleeve as if surprised to find it there. "No."

"It won't help."

"Neither did that can, apparently."

Mike took one step toward him.

Joel said, "Enough."

The word was not strong. It worked because none of them had strength for a better argument. Mike stopped. Will tucked the map case against his ribs and followed.

They went through the same gap in the alder they had made in the night. In daylight the distance was obscene. Ten yards. Less than the length of two canoes. A man could have thrown a mug from the fire ring and hit the place where Alex had died. All night Joel had made the distance larger in his head because terror needed space to justify itself. Morning took that away.

The alder kept the record badly. Branches were snapped at shoulder height and knee height. Leaves hung torn and silver-backed. Mud had dried in thumb-thick smears on stems. The black ground underfoot had been cut by sandals, knees, roots, something broader, something dragged. Bear spray had left orange dust on low leaves. In the gray light the dust looked artificial and almost clean, and the cleanness made the rest of the place worse. It was the only part of the scene that admitted a human hand.

Joel tried not to step in blood and found that there was no clean path.

Mike stopped first. Will stopped behind Joel with a hard intake of breath.

Alex lay where they had left him.

That fact had its own violence. Joel had known it would be true, but the night had given disbelief room to work. Morning closed the room. The body was still half turned toward the water, one arm bent under him, one hand open in the mud as if he had been interrupted while reaching. His shirt was torn and dark. One hiking sandal was gone. The other was still strapped on, absurdly intact, its heel packed with black muck.

Joel kept his eyes on the hand.

It was less terrible than the rest and therefore worse. Mud had dried in the creases of the palm. One fingernail was broken. The fingers were slightly curled. It was recognizably Alex's hand, not evidence, not a casualty, not the neutral thing a report would later try to make. The hand that had stirred peanut-butter noodles with solemn incompetence. The hand that had reached back without being asked to steady Joel's canoe over a beaver dam. The hand that had always seemed to be starting a bit before Alex's mouth caught up.

"No, no," Will said.

Not the panic from the night. Smaller. A correction addressed to the world.

Mike cleared his throat. The sound was raw from pepper spray. "We need to check the site."

Will stared at him. "The site."

"Will."

"No. Say it again. I want to hear how official you can make it."

"If it was a bear, we need to know if it's still close."

"If?"

Mike's face tightened. "Because you're going to make a federal case out of the word."

"I am looking at our friend dead in the mud and trying to be accurate."

"Accuracy won't move us."

"Inaccuracy got us here."

The sentence hit harder than Will seemed to expect. Mike's eyes changed. For a moment the whole route stood between them with Alex at its center: the old map, the staged Highlander, the missing beacon none of them had been embarrassed enough to insist on, Mike's certainty, Joel's willingness to let certainty do work for him. The trip had been built from small agreements that had felt like competence until they had to hold a death.

Joel stepped between the next words before they could happen.

"Tracks first," he said.

Mike looked at him as if he had forgotten Joel was there.

"Tracks," Joel said again. "Then his spray. Then we cover him. Then we decide."

"We decided," Mike said.

"No," Joel said. "We didn't."

Will turned toward him.

Joel did not look back. If he did, Will's relief might ask for more than Joel could give.

They began with the ground because the ground at least pretended to be evidence.

The mud made no simple testimony. It accepted some marks too well and refused others. Where water seeped through root holes, impressions slumped almost as soon as they formed. Where alder roots crossed under the surface, footprints split and slid. Their own night movements were everywhere: Mike's deeper heel, Will's narrow sandal tread, Joel's scraped sideways step where he had coughed blind through the spray. Alex's tracks came from camp, paused near the edge, turned slightly, then disappeared into churn.

Joel made himself slow down. He put one hand on a root and felt cold water squeeze up between his fingers. He tried to read direction from bent grass, weight from depth, animal from accident. The ground gave him pieces and refused the line between them.

He had never wanted to be the kind of man who studied the mud around a friend's body. He had wanted facts in other forms: a map folded on a tailgate, a forecast, a mileage note in Will's handwriting, a text from Mara asking for a photograph of the lake because she knew he would forget unless asked. Facts that stayed useful because nobody had died beside them. Here every fact charged admission. To know where Alex had stepped was to picture him stepping. To decide whether a mark was a paw was to stand longer where something might still be close.

Beyond Alex's shoulder was a broad mark in the mud.

It could have been a paw.

That was the worst of it. It could have been. Wide. Deep at one edge. A dragged smear where claws or roots or a slipping heel had cut through the top layer. Joel knew too little about bear tracks to argue it away. He knew enough about fear to distrust how badly he wanted one answer, and enough about Mike to distrust how badly Mike wanted the answer that could be acted on.

"There," Mike said.

He pointed with the bear spray, not touching anything.

Will leaned in. "Maybe."

"It's a print."

"Maybe."

"Don't maybe this."

"I'm saying maybe because that's the correct word when you don't know."

"It is a bear print."

"How many toes?"

Mike stared at him.

"No, answer. How many do you see? Because I see mud and panic."

"Will."

"There should be a track line. Something that heavy doesn't leave one print and vanish."

"It's all churned up."

"By us. By him. By something. Fine. But look." Will crouched lower, careful not to touch the mud. His hand hovered near the waterline where the ground should have held a large animal leaving. "This is nothing."

"Water washed it."

"In six hours?"

"Current."

"Current goes that way."

Mike turned his headlamp, still off, toward the brush as if habit could make a beam. His throat worked. "A bear came in. Alex stepped out. It hit him. It left through the channel."

"You left out the part where he said 'What.'"

No one moved.

Fog pulled through the alder behind Alex, thinned, caught again.

Joel felt the word from the night open in him. What. Alex's voice, irritated and curious, speaking to something just beyond his light. Not afraid yet. Not performing. Answering before any of them knew answer could be a category of danger.

Mike said, "He heard brush."

"He answered somebody."

"No."

"You don't get to no that."

"He answered a sound."

"A sound that sounded like somebody."

Mike's face went blank in the way that meant he was holding several uglier things behind it. "You don't know that."

"Neither do you."

Joel looked at the mud. "We heard him say it. That's what we have."

Will gave one dry little break of laughter. "Great. Very useful."

"I mean it."

"I know you mean it. That's why it's annoying."

Mike lowered the bear spray. "The more we stand here talking, the less daylight we have."

Daylight. The word had sounded generous under the tarp. Here it was already being spent. Above the trees the cloud deck moved faster than the fog below it. Wind worked in the spruce tops first, high and dry, then came down in pieces. Later it would find open water and make the map smaller than weather.

They found Alex's bear spray six feet from his body, half under leaves, safety still in place.

Mike stared at it for too long.

"He didn't get it off," Will said.

"I can see that."

"I'm saying--"

"I can see it."

Joel picked it up before either of them could turn it into another accusation. The canister was cold and wet. Mud filled the groove around the safety clip. Alex had carried it clipped to the front of his PFD because Mike had told him twice to stop putting it in the side pocket of his pack.

Quit mothering me, Alex had said the first time, in Will's driveway, grinning while changing nothing.

On the first lake, when Mike pointed at the canister with his paddle and told him again, Alex had clipped it where it belonged with great ceremony and said, Look at me growing as a person.

Joel slid the canister into his jacket pocket. The pocket sagged.

"Don't take his stuff," Will said.

"It's spray."

"I know what it is."

"Then don't make me say why."

Will looked away.

There were other objects. Alex's missing sandal lay upside down in the water weeds, where the current worried the strap. One glove had been mashed into mud. A torn corner of rain jacket hung from a broken alder stem. The cracked headlamp was back at camp in the cookpot where Joel had put it because he could not hold it and could not put it with anyone's personal gear. The inventory became an accusation. Things could be carried. A man could not.

Mike took out his phone before they covered anything.

For a second no one spoke. The phone belonged to calls, weather, photos of fish, proof that a person had stood smiling on a rock before weather and distance made the smile worth keeping. Mike held it in both hands, the way a person might hold something fragile or loaded.

"We need pictures," he said.

Will's eyes went hard. "Of him?"

"Of the place."

"Say what you mean."

Mike looked down at the screen. "Of enough."

Joel wanted to tell him no. He wanted to tell him yes. He wanted some future person with a badge or radio to have more than three shaking men and one word none of them trusted. Mike photographed the alder gap from camp-facing angles, the possible print without stepping closer, the place where Alex's spray had lain before Joel moved it, the orange pepper dust on leaves, the torn jacket caught on the stem. When he lifted the phone toward Alex himself, Will made a small flat sound.

Mike lowered it.

"Enough," Joel said.

Mike nodded once and put the phone away without checking the picture.

They did what they could for Alex.

No one called it burial. The ground was root and water and cold stone under a skin of mud. They had no shovel, no safe time, no tool equal to the task. Mike brought the gray groundsheet from Alex's tent. Will objected because it was Alex's, then stopped because every object was Alex's or no one's now. Ownership had become another thing the morning did not know how to honor.

They unfolded the sheet beside him. It snapped softly in the damp air.

"Careful," Mike said.

Will's eyes flashed. "I was planning to be casual."

"I'm saying--"

"Don't."

Joel knelt in the mud. The wet came through his pants immediately. He took one corner of the sheet. Mike took another. Will stood useless for two breaths, then crouched and took the third, his hand shaking so badly the plastic trembled.

Moving Alex even a little was impossible and necessary.

Joel closed his mind around the work. Corner here. Lift enough to pull cloth. Do not look at the torn place. Do not let the sheet catch under the arm. Do not put weight on the open hand. Mike said something under his breath that might have been a curse or a prayer. Will made a sound and bit it off. The sheet slid under part of Alex and bunched under another part and would not do what they needed. They had to try twice.

At last they covered him as well as they could.

The groundsheet made a poor shape over the body, low in one place, too high in another. Rainwater or fog already beaded on it. They gathered stones from the channel edge to hold the corners. The stones were cold and slick. Joel chose each one as if choosing correctly mattered. A flat one near the shoulder. A heavier round one near the feet. A granite chunk with lichen on the side where the wind might get under.

Will set his stone down, moved it an inch, then moved it back.

"This is insane," he said.

Mike wiped mud from his hand onto his pants. "It's what we can do."

"Don't make it sound clean."

"You have a better one?"

Will looked at the gray shape. "No."

The admission emptied him. He stepped back and put both hands on the top of his head, fingers locked in his hair. The map case hung from the crook of his elbow. Joel remembered Will at twelve in the church basement, doing the same thing over a lost youth-group scavenger hunt clue, furious that the world would not yield to close reading. Folding chairs. Watered-down lemonade. Dust and floor wax. Someone's mother telling them to use indoor voices. Will had wanted the clue to be fair. Joel had already suspected fair was a word adults used when they were tired.

Now Will stood in the alder beside a covered body and wanted the clue to be fair.

Joel turned away before the memory could make a speech out of itself.

Mike took the map case from Will without asking and opened it on a root-wide patch of ground. The plastic crackled. He pointed to their approximate position with a muddy finger.

"We go back," Will said at once.

Mike did not look up. "We don't know that yet."

"We know it."

"Back means the rough portage, the beaver mess, the wind lake from yesterday, all of it against us with three men and two boats."

"Forward means unknown."

"Forward means shorter to the Highlander."

"On paper."

"On the map."

"Those are the same only when the map isn't leaving something out."

Mike's eyes lifted. "The map is not lying."

"I didn't say lying. I said leaving things out. There's a difference."

Joel could feel both routes in his body. Back meant passing the beaver dams again, the rough portage, the narrow channel where every branch crack now had a shape behind it. Forward meant water they had not tested, wind they could already hear being built, camps they had not seen, and Mike's confidence moving ahead of evidence. Both directions required leaving Alex under a gray sheet in the alder.

"We can't carry him," Mike said.

Will's jaw set. "You don't know that."

"I do."

"He's not gear."

"That's why I'm saying it before somebody breaks himself trying to prove something."

Will stepped toward him. Mike did not step back.

Joel got between them too late to prevent the words but early enough to prevent hands.

"If we try to carry him," Joel said, "we don't get out today. Maybe not tomorrow. We need both boats moving. We need three people able to portage. We need--"

"Stop," Will said.

Joel stopped.

Will's face had gone rigid. "Don't turn him into logistics."

Joel looked at the groundsheet. The stones. The dark water licking at alder roots. "I don't know how else to get us out."

No one answered.

They marked the site. Mike tore a strip from Alex's orange stuff sack and tied it to an alder above the body, high enough to see from camp and low enough not to look like a flag. The color hurt the eye.

Joel wrote the first note in Will's little waterproof notebook because his own was packed too deep. The page took the pen cleanly.

Second Camp. Body ten yards east/channel side of camp. Covered with gray groundsheet. Orange marker on alder. Date. Approximate time.

His handwriting looked calm. The letters did not know where they were. They belonged to grocery lists, route notes, a name on a dry bag. They did not belong beside Alex.

He added the objects because objects were all he could make himself trust: Alex's spray safetied. Body minimally moved to cover. Food barrel intact at camp. No service. Three survivors.

Survivors looked wrong on the page. It made Alex into what the category excluded.

Will watched the pen move. "Write bear."

Mike looked at him.

"Go ahead," Will said. "If we're committing."

Joel held the pen above the paper. "Observed possible large animal sign."

Will laughed without humor. "You would write a clean sentence next to this."

"I know."

"Put no clear tracks."

Mike said, "Don't."

Joel wrote: no clear track line.

Mike breathed through his nose. The sound was almost a snort, almost the beginning of another argument, but it did not become one.

From the brush on the far side of the channel came a low laugh.

Not loud. Not theatrical. It did not roll through the fog. It was one bark of amusement, cut short in the old familiar way, the sound Alex made when a joke pleased him before he decided whether to make it bigger.

Will stopped breathing.

Mike's face emptied.

Joel's hand closed around the pen until the plastic bent.

Fog held over the water. The channel moved under the alder with its ordinary black patience. Somewhere upstream, a small bird clicked once, bright and real.

"Bird," Mike said.

No one asked which bird.

The laugh did not repeat.

Joel waited for his mind to reject it. Grief, exhaustion, memory, a raven, a fox, water hitting a hollow root, Will making some involuntary sound and Joel assigning it the wrong owner. Explanations were available. They were ordinary. They were also too late. The sound had already reached the part of him that knew Alex before it reached the part that needed proof.

He did not pick one up.

Will said, very softly, "That was him."

"No," Mike said.

"Mike."

"No."

"You heard it."

"I heard an animal."

"You did not."

Mike turned so fast the map case slid off the root. "Then what? Say it."

Will opened his mouth.

Joel caught his sleeve.

Not hard. Enough.

Will looked at the hand on him. His eyes were red, the skin beneath them gray. He understood, or nearly understood, before Joel did. Whatever came next would be heard. Whatever word they gave the morning would not stay theirs.

Will shut his mouth.

They stood that way for several seconds: Mike with the bear spray half raised toward fog, Will caught between speech and silence, Joel holding a pen and another man's sleeve beside a covered body.

Then, from closer, from the alder behind the groundsheet or the channel bend or nowhere Joel could place on a map, something in Alex's voice said, "Quit mothering me."

It was soft. Almost fond. Almost annoyed.

The phrase came with the exact little drop on mothering, the way Alex had said it when warnings bored him but attention pleased him. It was not a scream from beyond death. It sounded conversational, as if he were just out of sight adjusting a strap, waiting for one of them to roll his eyes.

That was the cruelty of it. The old request for reaction was still inside the words. Alex had spent the trip tugging at the group until someone looked up, sighed, laughed, corrected him. The sound kept that shape after Alex could not.

Will made a sound like his body had been struck.

Mike sprayed into the alder.

The hiss tore the morning open. Orange fog burst against wet leaves and came back in pieces. Joel pulled Will backward by the sleeve. The spray drifted low and chemical, cutting through mud and blood and spruce. Mike swept the canister left to right, too fast, spending what little remained, his arm rigid.

"Stop," Joel said.

Mike did not.

"Mike, stop."

The canister coughed and weakened. Mike lowered it. His mouth was open. His eyes streamed, though the spray had gone mostly away from him.

Nothing moved in the alder.

No body shifted under the groundsheet. No shape crossed the channel. No bear crashed away. The woods gave them wet leaves, fog, black water, and the bird's last small tick before it flew.

Will bent and vomited into the mud. There was little in him. Coffee acid, water, a string of bile. He stayed folded over afterward, one hand braced on a root, the other still holding the map case against his ribs as if it were an organ.

Mike wiped his eyes with the back of his wrist. "We leave now."

His voice was hoarse and flat.

Will spat. "Don't say bear."

"I didn't."

"You wanted to."

"I said we leave."

"Because what? That was supposed to be a bear?"

Mike took two steps toward him and stopped with the spray canister hanging useless at his side. His control had not returned. Only the outline of it. "Because staying beside him is worse."

Joel looked at the groundsheet. Fog had gathered on the plastic and begun to run in narrow lines toward the mud. One bead hung at the edge, swelled, and fell onto a leaf. The leaf dipped and came back up. The shape under the sheet did not change, and that steadiness was another kind of horror.

He wanted to say Alex's name. Not to the brush, not as answer, not as lure. Just once, properly, to the covered shape on the ground. He did not. The wanting itself frightened him. It felt like stepping toward a voice.

They went back to camp without turning their backs until the alder forced them to.

The camp in daylight looked worse than it had in darkness because daylight made its inadequacy visible. The tarp sagged with condensation. The fire ring was a black scrape full of wet ash. Alex's tent stood half open, one guyline slack, the fly torn low where some branch or body had caught it in the confusion. The turned canoe lay between the fire ring and the channel like a child's idea of a wall. Paddles were lined along a log where Will had placed them during the night, a neat row of tools that had protected nothing.

Mike went straight to the food barrel.

"Don't," Will said.

Mike stopped with his hand on the rope. "We need to know."

"We know enough."

"No. We know one thing we can't use."

He lowered the barrel carefully. The rope rasped over the branch. The sound made Joel's shoulders tighten. The barrel descended in little jerks, pale and round, until Mike caught it against his chest and set it on the ground.

The latch was not open, exactly. One tongue had not seated fully into its keeper. The red strap crossed the lid, but the metal tab sat proud by half an inch, making a small clean shadow.

Joel knew Mike had checked it in the night. He had watched him lift the latch, press it down, tie the rope, yank the line once. The barrel had knocked against the trunk, and all three of them had listened to the knock come back from the channel side. But hands failed under pressure. Latches looked closed when they were not. Memory edited itself to reduce blame or increase it.

Mike crouched and touched the tab.

"Don't touch it," Will said.

Mike gave him a look.

"Sorry," Will said. "Reflex."

Mike pressed the latch. It clicked home.

All three men looked toward the alder.

Nothing answered.

For once, the world withheld the answer they feared.

Mike opened the barrel. Oatmeal. Coffee. Noodles. Peanut butter. Rice packets. Will's spices in doubled bags. Alex's plain rice meals with his name written on duct tape in thick marker because he had claimed otherwise they would all get ideas. Everything was there. Nothing torn. No claw marks in the plastic. No food dragged through camp.

"Food didn't bring it," Will said.

Mike closed the barrel. "Food could have attracted it without being eaten."

"You hear yourself?"

"Yes."

"Do you?"

"Enough."

Joel expected Will to answer. He did not. He was looking at Alex's labeled meals through the translucent side of the barrel. His face had lost the argument and found something worse.

Packing required belief in later. Later they would need dry socks. Later they would need the stove. Later a map might matter. Later a pot should be nested correctly and fuel capped and straps tightened. The body wanted to sit down, run, or go back into the alder and remove the sheet.

They packed anyway.

They did it badly.

Joel poured too much water into the coffee grounds because his hand shook when he meant to stop. Brown liquid climbed the filter cone and spilled over the rim onto the dirt. He stood watching it run past a spruce root until he heard Alex in memory, not from brush but from yesterday evening by the stove: This is less coffee than a wet opinion. Joel had rolled his eyes then. Now the line arrived whole and useless, and he held the mug until heat hurt his fingers.

Will took down Alex's tent.

No one had asked him to. Maybe that was why he did it. He unzipped the door, then stopped with one hand on the pull. The zipper made a small dry line through the morning.

Mike turned sharply.

"It's me," Will said.

The phrase hung there. It should have been ordinary. It had become a claim that needed proof.

Will ducked into the tent and began handing things out: sleeping bag in its stuff sack, shirt, socks, paperback swollen slightly from damp, spare bland-food pouch from the mesh pocket, headnet, spare batteries, little bag of electrolyte tablets. Each object came out changed. Joel put them into Alex's pack because no other category existed.

At the bottom of the tent, Will found a small plastic bottle of hot sauce wrapped in a sock. He held it up and made a sound that was almost a laugh.

"He lied," Will said.

Mike looked over.

"Bland-food purity was a lie."

No one answered.

Will screwed the bottle tighter and put it in his own pocket with both hands.

Joel took the cracked headlamp from the cookpot. Dried blood darkened one side of the strap. Mud had gotten into the hinge. He could not decide whether to pack it with Alex's gear, keep it out as evidence, or throw it into the channel because looking at it made the morning tilt. He wrapped it in a spare bandanna and put it in the top pocket of Alex's pack.

"We take his pack?" Will said.

"Some of it," Mike said.

"What does that mean?"

"It means we can't carry everything."

"I can carry his pack."

"No."

"You don't know what I can carry."

"I know what you can carry over one portage while crying and not sleeping. I know what you can carry over six."

Will's face twisted. "Go to hell."

"Later."

It was the kind of Mike line that would once have worked. Dark, quick, badly timed. No one laughed. Mike seemed to realize this half a second after he said it. His mouth tightened, but apology was a language none of them had ready.

They divided Alex's gear into necessities, evidence, and things too personal to abandon. Even the categories trespassed.

They took the spray, fuel, batteries, first-aid odds and ends, one fleece layer, food. They kept the headlamp, wallet, phone, the note about the site, the photographs if Mike's phone survived long enough to matter, the fact of the safety clip still on the spray. They kept Alex's small notebook with mileage estimates and grocery lists. They kept the hot sauce because Will had already claimed it. The phone went uselessly into a dry bag, as if service might wake inside it if kept dry enough.

Mike photographed the camp edge once more from where the fire ring could be seen, then the low food hang, then the note under the fly. The phone made a small official sound each time. Click. Click. Click. Each one turned a piece of the morning into something flat enough to show later. None of them looked at the screen.

Then Mike held the phone up and checked service though he knew. No bars. No emergency call. The blank screen had become another way for the world to refuse them.

"Battery," Joel said.

Mike lowered the phone. "Yeah."

He turned it off.

Will watched him. "We could climb."

"What?"

"Tree. Ridge. Something. Signal sometimes--"

"There is no ridge."

"You don't know."

"Look around."

"Trees are not a topographic argument."

"We are not climbing wet spruce for one imaginary bar."

"People get emergency calls out with no displayed service."

"People also fall out of trees."

Joel said, "Wind's coming up."

That redirected them because it was outside and harder to accuse.

The spruce tops were moving now. The fog on the channel had begun to tear into strips. Beyond the narrow water, through the screen of alder, the next lake showed only as a pale hard brightness. They could not see the surface from camp, but they could hear it beginning to work: small waves touching rock, then not touching, then touching again. A steady increase. A clock with no face.

Mike spread the map on the turned canoe. He put stones on two corners. Will came despite himself. Joel stood close enough to see and far enough not to own the route.

"Back to First Camp," Will said.

"Forward to the larger lake, then the next marked camp if we can make it," Mike said.

"If we can make it is not a plan."

"It is when every plan starts with if."

"The rough portage is behind us. We know it."

"And it took four of us half the afternoon fresh."

"We were not fresh."

"We were fresher than this."

"Forward gets us to the cabin mark eventually," Will said, jabbing the map.

Mike looked at him sharply.

"What?" Will said. "That's your thing. Map says there's a cabin."

"Not today."

"Then why forward?"

"Because the take-out is forward."

"And the body is behind us," Will said.

There it was. The real geography.

Mike folded one edge of the map, then flattened it. His hands were steadier with paper than they had been with the spray. "We leave a note at camp. We mark the site. We travel. We get to the Highlander or service or people. We bring them back."

"Bring them back to what?"

Mike did not answer.

"No, say that one too," Will said. "Since we're saying practical things."

Joel looked toward the alder. The orange strip was visible from camp, a small wrong color in the brush.

"We need to move while we still can," Joel said.

Will turned on him. "You keep saying we."

"Because it's still true."

"Is it?"

The question came without heat. That made it harder.

Joel had no answer that would not sound like a poster in a ranger station. Stay together. Keep moving. Don't panic. All the good advice in the world became thin when spoken beside a dead friend.

Will looked down first.

They wrote the camp note on a torn page and sealed it in a freezer bag with a strip of orange tape on the outside. Joel wrote because Mike's hands shook when he tried and because Will refused the pen after seeing the first line.

ALEX B. BODY MARKED CHANNEL SIDE OF SECOND CAMP. COVERED WITH GRAY GROUNDSHEET. ORANGE MARKER ON ALDER. BEAR ATTACK SUSPECTED. NO SERVICE. GOING FOR HELP.

Joel wrote bear attack suspected because leaving the word out felt like cowardice and putting it in felt like fraud. Suspected was the hinge he could live with for the next hour.

He tucked the note under Alex's tent fly folded beneath a rock near the fire ring. It looked too small to do what they needed from it. It looked like a thing a person wrote to make himself less guilty.

Will read it once after it was sealed. His lips moved around the words without giving them sound. When he reached BEAR ATTACK SUSPECTED, his mouth flattened and stayed that way. He did not ask Joel to change it.

They finished packing.

Mike loaded the canoes as if load order could repair reality. He put the heaviest packs low, centered the food barrel, tied the loose painter with too much force. Once, without comment, he pressed the small yellow pouch clipped inside the left side of his PFD, feeling for the Highlander key as if the truck at North Road Bridge were a place he could touch. Will repacked the map case twice. Joel checked the stove cap, then checked it again because he could not remember doing it. They kept Alex's wallet, phone, cracked headlamp, unused spray, hot sauce, some food, and one fleece layer. They left the rest under a rock-weighted tent fly with the note and the marker visible from camp.

The division made no moral sense. It only made weight.

Before they pushed off, Will walked to the edge of the alder gap.

Mike said, "Don't."

Will stopped.

The word worked because it was not an order this time. It was a plea with bark on it.

Will stood with his back to them. "I'm not going in."

No one moved.

"I just--" He cleared his throat, and the small rough sound went into the trees. Joel flinched before he could stop himself. Will heard the flinch. His shoulders rose, then dropped. "I just need a second."

Mike opened his mouth and closed it.

Joel looked at the channel, at the fog taking itself apart, at water brightening under wind. Leaving would not be one action. It would repeat all day. Every paddle stroke would leave Alex again. Every portage would leave him. Every practical decision that kept the living alive would commit the betrayal one more time.

Will did not say Alex's name. That was what Joel noticed. He stood at the edge of the brush and gave the silence nothing it could use.

Then, from somewhere near the covered body, very soft, came the click of a food-barrel latch.

The actual food barrel sat in Mike's canoe, twenty feet behind them, strapped under the middle bar.

Mike turned. Will turned. Joel did not, for one second. He looked at the barrel in the canoe first.

Red strap. Metal latch seated. Rope tied. Still.

The click did not repeat.

Will whispered, "No."

Joel said, "Don't answer it."

The words left him before he could weigh them. They were too loud in the morning and still not loud at all.

They were also a rule, though he did not know that yet. A bad first draft of one. Do not answer what has no body. Do not let a sound turn you before your eyes have finished their work.

Mike lifted the mostly spent spray toward the alder, then lowered it. Something in his face had gone older and smaller.

"Boats," he said.

They got the canoes into the water. The hulls scraped mud and then floated free with a soft, living shift. Three men, two boats. The imbalance showed immediately. Mike would take one canoe alone with the food barrel and heavier gear. Joel and Will would take the other. No one said that Alex's absence had a seating arrangement.

Joel stepped into the back of the canoe. Will got in front with the map case between his knees. His paddle lay across his lap for a moment before he picked it up. Mike shoved off first, too hard, then corrected with two quick strokes.

For a few seconds they drifted in the narrow channel beside Second Camp.

The tarp sagged. The fire ring was black. Alex's tent site had become a flat wet patch under spruce. The orange strip in the alder moved once in the wind and stilled. The groundsheet was hidden from the waterline, which was good and not good enough.

Joel put his paddle in.

The blade entered without splash. The water took the shape of it, closed around it, and let it go.

Behind them, from camp or brush or the channel's crooked mouth, Alex gave one low bark of laughter.

Will's shoulders seized.

Mike said, without turning, "Keep paddling."

No one said bear.
