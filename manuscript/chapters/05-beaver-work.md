# Chapter 5 - Beaver Work

Joel woke to the sound of Will's zipper.

Not the tent zipper at first. Smaller. A bag, probably. Then the tent door itself, opened by someone who believed slowness made less sound. It did not. The teeth came apart one by one in the gray morning, dry and careful and loud.

Joel opened his eyes.

The hammock held him in its shallow curve. Above him, the tarp was beaded with dew. Beyond its edge the sky had gone pale over the lake, not sunrise exactly but the idea of it. Fog lay low on the water, a gray skin that made the opposite shore appear and disappear by inches. The air was cold enough that he had pulled his beanie over his ears sometime in the night without remembering doing it. His nose was cold. His feet were warm inside the quilt until he moved them and found the damp in his sandal straps beside the hammock.

Will came out of the tent bent over and half-zipped into a fleece. He stood there for a moment, looking at the lake like a man who had expected it to do something overnight and was checking the result.

"You alive?" Joel said.

Will flinched and looked toward the hammock. "Jesus."

"Good morning."

"You look dead in that thing."

"That's the appeal."

Will rubbed his face. His hair stood up on one side. "I thought about sleeping in mine."

"You thought bravely."

"I made a weather-based decision."

"You slept in the tent."

"I made a tent-based decision."

The fire ring was gray and cold. The tent had not moved except for one side panel sagging where Alex had probably pressed into it. The canoes lay above the shoreline, dark with dew, paddles tucked underneath where they had left them. Joel looked there first without meaning to. The night's knock had not become important. It had only waited for daylight to be dismissed properly.

A loose painter rope touched a hull once in the faint breeze.

Tap.

Then nothing.

Joel pushed the bug net aside and leaned out far enough to see the landing clearly. No paddle had shifted. No hull had slid. No branch had lodged under the stone lip. The shoreline gave him the same objects in the same positions, which was all daylight could do.

He stayed there with one cold hand on the hammock edge while the rope touched the hull again, softer this time, almost private.

A rope tapping a hull was a rope tapping a hull. He had been here long enough, in this kind of morning, to know how many objects could speak once when air moved through camp and then give no testimony.

Will had already seen the rods.

"They're wet," he said.

"Everything's wet."

"Not everything is a precision instrument."

"Your rod is a stick with jewelry."

Will looked offended but too tired to answer well. "That's not even worth correcting."

Mike's voice came from the tent. "If you two are talking about fishing before coffee, I'm leaving you here."

Alex groaned from inside. "Is the wilderness always this administrative?"

Joel swung his feet out of the hammock and sat up. Cold came through his base layer and made him move faster. He pulled on his fleece, then his wet hiking sandals, grimacing as the straps took hold of his feet. He had camp shoes buried somewhere but did not want to open the pack yet. The first step on granite was sharp and cold.

Morning camp took shape without ceremony. Water first. Stove next. Breakfast by negotiation.

Will wanted pancakes because he had remembered them into existence the night before. Mike wanted bars and coffee because he wanted miles. Alex said he supported pancakes in principle but not if the batter had texture. Joel found instant oats near the top of the food bag and held them up.

"This is the compromise."

"That's not compromise," Will said. "That's surrender with hot water."

"Brown sugar."

"Apology sugar."

Mike was already filling the pot. "Oats."

Alex sat up in the tent with his hair crushed flat on one side. "Do oats have seasoning?"

"They have whatever you put in them," Joel said.

"So no."

"Peanut butter."

Alex considered that. "That's a condiment with ambition."

"Oats," Mike said again, because repetition was how he ended meetings.

They boiled water and made coffee with Folgers packets. The packets always seemed like a bad idea at home and a good idea in camp. Tear, pour, stir, drink something hot enough to correct the body. Joel ate oats from his mug and watched fog pull away from the lake in low pieces, like smoke from a fire already out.

The far shore came in and out behind it. Pine, then nothing. Granite, then blank gray. Sound arrived cleanly through the damp: Will's spoon against his mug, Mike folding the map case open, Alex coughing once into his sleeve, the stove valve ticking as it cooled. Each noise came with an object attached.

At home, morning meant machines doing small repeatable work: coffee grinder, furnace, phone alarm, the garage door juddering open under Mara's hand. Here the work was older and less negotiable. Fire, water, food, route. His body liked the reduction before his mind had time to distrust it. A man could be useful by boiling water. A task could end because the pot was empty or the bag was packed.

Mike checked the map while standing. He did not like sitting before the route was decided, even if the route had been decided months ago.

"We want to be moving by eight," he said.

Will looked at the sky. "That means nine."

"It means eight."

"Map agrees with eight?" Alex said.

Mike tapped the plastic case with one finger. "Map doesn't care."

Will leaned close enough to see the blue line under the cloudy plastic. "Map also doesn't show beavers."

"It shows water," Mike said.

"That's my point."

"Your point is always that maps have betrayed you personally."

Will's dry cough came once, more irritation than illness. "I'm just saying a line can be right and still not tell you what kind of day you're about to have."

Joel understood the irritation because it was not only about the map. Will wanted the missing difficulty named before it arrived. He wanted a system honest enough to include its own failures in the legend. Mud. Pullovers. Morale loss. Time eaten by things with teeth. A man could lose a job to a larger system and still come to the woods expecting paper to account for water.

Alex, still inside the tent, said, "It means someone will be looking for his headlamp at eight-ten."

"I know where my headlamp is," Mike said.

Nobody said anything.

Mike looked up. "I do."

Joel sipped coffee. "Then we're ahead of schedule spiritually."

They broke camp in cold stages. Sleeping bags stuffed. Pads rolled. Tent shaken, fly wet enough to be annoying but not soaked. Joel took down his hammock last because he liked how quickly his whole bedroom became a fist-sized bundle of fabric and straps. Will packed his unused hammock with the expression of a man putting away a tool he had not deserved.

The food barrel latch snapped closed. The sound was ordinary enough that no one looked up. Mike tested the latch with two fingers, then set his palm flat on the lid as if sealing a small argument. The stove cooled, clicked once as he handled it, and went into its bag. Paddles came out from under the canoes. Wet blades, grit on the shafts. Will wiped his rod guides with a cloth and pretended no one noticed.

"Fishing while we move?" Alex asked.

"In spirit," Will said.

"No," Mike said.

"I said in spirit."

"I heard the part you didn't say."

"You often do, and usually badly."

They loaded the boats. The morning light grew cleaner but not warmer. Mosquitoes were thin near shore, slow enough that they seemed confused by their own hunger. The lake held the canoes without wave. Mike pressed his thumb against the small yellow pouch clipped inside the left side of his PFD, where the Highlander key lived, then checked the front strap on the food barrel as if both objects belonged to the same future. Road and dinner. Ignition and calories.

Whatever had knocked in the night had left no sign. No shifted hull. No loose paddle. No branch caught on the rocks. Joel checked the landing while Mike tightened straps and Will repacked his rod case. Checking was easier than wondering. His body settled only after his eyes had done the work.

By a little after eight-thirty they pushed off.

The first part of the morning was easy water. The lake stretched north in a long reach, then narrowed around a low point where black spruce crowded the shore. Mike and Will led. Joel and Alex followed. Alex paddled better after breakfast than he had near the end of the day before. Maybe sleep helped. Maybe the cold made complaint less efficient than work.

They passed the point and entered a channel not much wider than the one from yesterday, but lower and flatter. Alder came down to the water on both sides. Grass leaned out over mud. The water lost the clean dark of lake depth and turned brown and slow. It smelled of wet bark and old leaves, the sour richness of things busy rotting into something useful. Every bend seemed to have been made for an animal's body rather than a canoe: low, close, shoulder-height in the brush, water deep enough to float them until it was not.

"Here we go," Mike called back.

"Here we go good or here we go bad?" Alex said.

"Beaver work."

Will turned his head. "Plural?"

"Could be."

"Great."

Alex looked back at Joel. "Define beaver work."

Joel said, "Sometimes you get out and drag."

"That's work for us."

"Correct."

"Misleading name."

"They did their part."

Alex looked at the low banks sliding past, the cut stems bright as bone near the waterline. "I grew up around animals that could kill you. Beavers feel insulting."

"Beavers can ruin your day," Will said.

"Lots of things can ruin my day. Plain oatmeal. Small talk. Men named Todd."

Mike looked over his shoulder. "Paddle."

Alex paddled.

That was what made Joel's irritation unreliable. Alex made every instruction into a small public trial, but he did the work. He complained, turned discomfort into theater, and put his blade in the water with the rest of them. The performance was weather. Under it, something kept taking weight.

The channel kept narrowing. The alders leaned until their upper branches crossed over the water and made a low, jointed roof. The paddles clipped drowned stems. Once, Joel's blade struck something under the surface that gave back a hollow wooden note, and Alex looked at him.

"Rock," Joel said.

"Rocks don't sound like doors."

"Submerged branch, then."

"Better."

Ahead, Will said something to Mike that Joel could not catch. Mike answered with one hand lifted off the paddle, palm down, the universal sign for enough. Will turned forward again. He had his rod across his lap in a way that pretended not to be ready.

The first dam came twenty minutes later. It crossed the channel in a low brown ridge of sticks and mud, water shining over the top in thin sheets. On one side a narrow chute had been opened by previous travelers and half-closed again by the animals. Boot prints had softened on the muddy lip. A cut alder end showed pale where someone had tried to improve the gap with a saw or hatchet and had not improved it enough. The dispute looked current.

"This on the map?" Alex asked.

Will gave the kind of laugh that had no pleasure in it. "No. The map people neglected to consult the beavers."

"It's normal," Mike said.

"Normal is not the same as fast."

"Nobody promised fast."

Joel looked at the dam, at the water seeping through it in several patient places. The map had promised passage by leaving this out.

Mike guided his canoe toward it, stepped out in knee-deep water, and held the boat while Will got out and pulled from the front.

"Looks simple," Alex said.

"Don't say that," Joel said.

"Why?"

"Because the river hears confidence."

Alex smiled. "That sounds like something from your Wisconsin cult."

"I'm from St. Louis."

"Madison changed you."

They came in on the right. Joel stepped out first. Cold water went up over his sandal straps and around his calves. The mud under the surface accepted one foot, then the other, and did not want to give them back. It was not the clean mud of a trail puddle. It had leaf rot in it, trapped gas, broken sticks that pressed without warning against the soft place below the ankle. Alex got out and immediately sank deeper.

"Oh, come on."

"Don't fight it straight up," Joel said. "Rock your foot."

"I am negotiating with it."

"You're losing."

The canoe slid halfway onto the dam, then stopped. Joel pushed from the back. Alex pulled from the front. The hull rasped over wet sticks. Something under it cracked. Water ran around their legs with more force than it had seemed to have from the boat, all that brown quiet narrowing itself over the dam and becoming current again. Behind them, Mike's boat bumped the dam once, a hollow aluminum note that traveled along the low banks and came back thinner.

"Hold up," Alex said.

Joel stopped.

Alex bent and freed the painter rope from a branch woven into the dam. He held it up with theatrical patience. "See? Useful."

"I didn't say otherwise."

"You were thinking it."

"I was thinking many things."

Mike, already on the far side, called, "You coming?"

Alex looked back at him. "We're preserving infrastructure."

"You're standing in beaver trash."

Will said, "Technically impressive beaver trash."

"That's your review of everything in nature," Mike said.

They got the boat over. It dropped into the water beyond with a hollow slap and a little roll that sent water against Joel's thighs. Alex made a sound between a laugh and a curse.

"Refreshing," he said.

Joel steadied the canoe and let the mud take back the footprints they had made. It closed slowly, dark water filling the shape of their legs. The dam stayed where it was.

The second dam was worse.

The channel bent left and disappeared behind alder. The dam sat just beyond the bend, higher, newer, still wet with the look of recent labor. Water backed up before it in a shallow pond full of drowned grass. A lodge rose on the left, sticks piled against a bank where fresh cuts showed pale. The chew marks were clean and angled, each sapling taken down by a mouth that knew its work.

There was nothing mysterious about it. That almost made it worse. No malice, no warning, no intention toward them at all. Just another creature improving its house, and in doing so making their route harder. The wilderness did not need to hate them. It only needed to be busy with something else.

Will said, "Somebody's active."

Mike said, "Beavers usually are."

"I mean recent."

"Are we filing a complaint?"

Alex lifted his paddle. "I support labor."

The pull-over took longer. They unloaded a few bags because the boats were catching on the crest. Food barrel out. Day bags out. Rods handed forward like fragile diplomatic gifts. The barrel was heavier than it had been at breakfast, or seemed heavier because now it had to be lifted from an unstable boat while mud held Joel by the calves. Mike took it first, set it on a mat of sticks above the water, and looked offended when it leaned.

"Don't let it roll," he said.

"I had planned to release it into the wild," Will said.

"It's the food."

"I understand the food barrel concept."

Alex took one rod and held it away from himself.

"This thing makes me feel irresponsible."

"It should," Will said.

Joel pushed and slipped. One foot went sideways between two submerged branches. For a second the mud held him and the current leaned against his knees. He caught the gunwale with one hand and steadied.

"You good?" Will called.

"Yeah."

"Don't break Madison," Alex said.

"Madison bends," Joel said.

Mike was on top of the dam, pulling his canoe by the front line. "Lift, don't drag."

"Buy lighter boats," Joel said.

Mike looked over his shoulder. "That line's less funny when you're wrong."

"It wasn't meant to be funny."

"It never is."

They got the boats over one by one. By the end, everyone was wet to the knee or higher. Alex had mud up one thigh. Will had a leech on his ankle, which he noticed only after he had already described leech behavior in too much detail.

"No, no, no," he said, more annoyed than scared. "Absolutely not."

Mike looked down. "It's tiny."

"It's attached."

"That's how they work."

Will flicked at it with a fingernail and missed. "That's not how I want it to work."

Alex crouched beside him. "Allow me. I grew up with real parasites."

"You grew up in Alaska, not a medieval swamp."

Alex pinched the leech off and held it up for half a second before flinging it into the grass. "Nature defeated."

Will stared at his ankle. "I hate that I needed you."

"You'll heal."

Alex stood and handed Will's rod back carefully, grip first, without making a production of that part. The gesture was almost formal, the kind of care he hid because care without a bit was too naked for him. Then he noticed Joel noticing and widened his eyes.

"Don't make it weird," he said.

"I wasn't."

"You were warming up."

They moved again.

The morning warmed. The chill lifted from their clothes but left them damp. The sun came through breaks in the alder and lit the water in copper patches. Every few bends the channel opened into a marshy pool where lily pads held yellowing edges and dragonflies stitched the air low over the surface. A beaver slapped somewhere ahead once, flat and hard, and the sound crossed the channel like a board dropped on water. A few seconds later a lower knock came from behind them, more wood than water. Joel looked back. Only the bend, the grass, the disturbed brown line of their passage. He could assign it if he wanted: hull cooling, branch settling, the dam behind them changing under its own weight. Will looked too, then faced forward before Mike could tell him to. The route tightened again and made them work.

Mike did not say they were behind. He did not have to. He checked the map case more often and put it away more quickly each time, as if the blue line might change if anyone looked at it too long. When Will asked once whether the next lake was the long one or the before-the-long-one, Mike said, "After this," and kept paddling.

The route was changing them by small degrees. It was too early for drama, and the day was still bright enough to make seriousness feel theatrical, but the first-day jokes had less gloss on them. Mike's instructions were shorter. Will still talked, but more of it came between breaths. Alex performed, forgot to perform, then remembered when silence lasted too long.

The water gave them a clear stretch, then a snag. A little current, then mud. A turn that looked open until the boat entered and the alder took both sides of the canoe. A blue line could be true and still leave out the truth. It did not say how many times a man would step into brown water and feel his foot disappear.

At a shallow gravel bar, Mike stopped to check the map. Will cast once while standing in ankle-deep water, because his body had been waiting for the chance before his mind approved it.

Mike said, "Really?"

"One cast."

"You said that yesterday."

"And I have grown as a person."

The lure landed near a sunken branch with a small plip. Something moved in the shadow. Will's whole body changed.

"Fish?" Alex said.

"Maybe."

"Useful fish or emotionally symbolic fish?"

Will reeled slowly. "All fish are both."

The lure came back clean.

Mike gave him five seconds of silence. "Done?"

Will clipped the lure to a guide. "Done."

"Miracles happen," Mike said.

They ate lunch out of the boats because the ground was mostly mud and grass. Tuna packets, Triscuits, trail mix, jerky already diminished by theft and democracy. Alex examined a salmon packet as if it might argue with him.

"Just eat the crackers," Mike said.

"I'm branching."

"You branch like a dead tree."

Joel handed him a strip of jerky. "Here. Safe food."

Alex took it. "Your usefulness continues."

Will said, "Don't praise him too much. He'll dehydrate a whole cow."

"I've considered it," Joel said.

"Of course you have."

The channel after lunch became less forgiving. Beaver cuts narrowed the turns. Drowned branches reached up from underwater, invisible until a hull scraped them. Mike called back directions in clipped pieces. Left. Stay right. Shallow. Watch the snag. Joel relayed what mattered to Alex when Alex was looking at the wrong thing.

The work made a kind of language: blade drip, hull scrape, breath, boot pulled from mud, branch released and whipping back. A man could listen to it and know where the others were without seeing them. Mike's canoe ahead by the harder knocks. Will's rod tip ticking once against aluminum. Alex's paddle coming out too high and scattering water beside Joel's knee. Before anything was wrong, this was trust. Joel only knew where the other canoe was by sound alone.

A branch cracked in the alder to their left.

Not loud. A clean snap, followed by brush moving low and fast, not through the tops but under them. It had a body in it, or suggested one well enough that Joel's own body answered first: shoulders tightening, paddle stopping half out of the water, breath held because breath was suddenly another sound.

Alex stopped paddling mid-stroke.

The canoe turned slightly.

"Hold up," he said.

Joel put his paddle in to correct. Ahead, Mike's boat kept moving three more strokes before Will turned.

"You guys hear that?"

Mike looked toward the alder. "Animal."

It sounded like an answer. It was only a category.

"What kind of animal?" Alex asked.

"The kind in bushes."

"That narrows it."

Will held his paddle across his knees and listened. The brush moved once more, farther back. Leaves shook. A thin stem kept trembling after the rest went still, a visible piece of an invisible animal. Then nothing.

No splash followed. No grunt. No second branch. The channel kept moving under them with small brown sounds, and the alder bank offered concealment without the courtesy of an animal. The canoes drifted a few feet while they listened, enough that Joel had to correct again or let the current set them sideways into grass.

"Beaver?" Joel said.

"Could be," Mike said.

"Could be bear," Alex said, but lightly.

Mike turned his head just enough. "Don't start."

"I'm not starting. I'm identifying risk factors."

"That's Will's job," Joel said.

Will, still listening, said, "Bear would probably sound heavier."

"Probably," Mike said. "Everybody paddle."

The word everybody did its work. They moved.

Joel looked once into the alder as they passed. He saw leaves, mud, the dark arch of roots where the bank had undercut, a few fresh chips on a cut sapling. No animal. No shape. Sight gave him facts, but not enough of them.

No reason to remember it except the way Alex had stopped before anyone else did, shoulders tightening under the straps of his PFD, paddle held over water so no drop fell.

A person could grow up in Alaska and still become alert to brush. Maybe especially then. Maybe larger country gave you practice hearing the small thing before the large thing chose to show itself.

The channel opened after that, though not into easy water. It widened into a marshy run where the current slowed and the wind found them from the side. The sky had changed while they worked the beaver dams. It was still clear overhead, but a smear of cloud sat low in the west. The light had gone flatter. Far ahead, a wider lake showed between low banks.

Mike checked the map without stopping, one hand on the paddle, the case open against his knees.

"Bigger water after this," he said.

"How big?" Alex asked.

"Big enough."

Will made a sound.

Mike said, "What?"

"Nothing."

"Say it."

"I'm just saying, 'big enough' is not a measurement."

"It's not the problem lake."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the lake before the problem lake."

Alex turned around. "Do the lakes know which one is the problem lake?"

Joel said, "Mike tells them."

Mike ignored that. "We get across this, short ugly bit, then camp."

"Second camp?" Alex said.

"No. Lunch two-point-five."

"Don't name things."

"I'll name whatever I survive."

They paddled into the wider water.

The wind was mild but steady, enough to worry the surface and press Alex's lazy strokes into consequence. Joel had to correct more often. The canoe showed him every small failure in the front. Mike and Will angled left toward a low shore where the map showed the next narrow exit. Will kept looking back toward the marsh.

Joel saw it and did not ask.

The branch crack was gone. The brush had stopped moving. Nothing followed them but small waves and water dripping from lifted paddles. A drip could sound like a step if a man wanted it to. Joel did not want it to.

Still, for a while no one joked.

That did not last. It never did.

Alex finally said, "For the record, if a beaver kills me, lie."

Will turned in the front of Mike's canoe. "About which part?"

"All of it."

"Say bear?"

"Say moose. Say I fought a moose for the honor of the group."

Mike said, "I'm saying you fell in mud and got unionized."

Alex laughed, and because the water was wider now the laugh carried out from him and came back from nowhere in particular. It skipped once against the low shore and thinned into the marsh behind them.

Joel paddled and watched the lake ahead. He could feel the day going long. Not late yet, but moving that way. First Camp was behind them. Second Camp was somewhere past work they had not done.

Mike had rounded the route down. Maybe not by much. Maybe by the exact amount competent men rounded discomfort down when selling it to themselves first and others second. Joel knew the habit from gentler rooms: a house repair, a budget, a wedding date that could wait one more week because nobody had said otherwise out loud. The scale changed. The habit did not.

They reached the far side later than Mike wanted and earlier than worry required. The exit was a gap in alder, half-hidden by grass. Water moved through it in a narrow brown pull, just enough current to show the way.

Will lifted his rod and looked at the widening water behind them.

Mike said, "No."

"I didn't say anything."

"You thought it loudly."

"There could be pike."

"There could be time."

Alex looked toward the alder gap. "Does it open up after this?"

Mike gave him a hard look.

Alex smiled. "Asking for morale."

Mike cleared his throat. The sound was small, almost lost under the wind and water.

"After the next stretch," he said. "Just keep moving."

Will glanced at Joel. Not alarmed. Just registering the phrase the way he registered all of Mike's route talk.

Joel dipped his paddle and followed the first canoe into the gap.

The alder closed around them. The light narrowed. Water slid under the boats with a dark, steady pull, and from somewhere ahead came the faint sound of it dropping over stones. Not danger, not in any form a man could name from the back of a canoe. Only current. Only rock. The route kept going after men had described it too easily.
