# Chapter 29 - North Road Bridge

The Highlander opened with a tired chirp.

Joel nearly dropped the key.

The sound was too domestic, too obedient. A machine answering a signal. He stood beside the driver's door with water running from his clothes onto road gravel and waited for the answer to become something else.

Nothing spoke.

The dome light came on when he opened the door. It showed the old interior exactly as Mike had left it: gas receipts in the console, a fleece hat on the passenger seat, a paper coffee cup collapsed in the cup holder, road dust filmed across the dashboard.

The smell hit him hard. Upholstery, stale coffee, cold plastic, Mike's particular mix of old smoke and gum and damp gear. The smell was not a voice, but it brought Mike closer than the voices had.

Joel stood outside the open door and held the frame.

The Highlander had been waiting here through everything. Rain had dried on its windshield. Needles had gathered in the wiper well. A spider had drawn one line from the side mirror to the door seam. The ordinary patience of it felt indecent. Mike had parked it on the first morning, locked it, made some remark about no one stealing an old Toyota at the edge of nowhere, and the vehicle had done exactly what it was asked to do while the men who had asked it went into the route and did not come back.

Joel got in.

The driver's seat was too far back. Mike's seat. Mike's angle on the world. Joel pulled the lever and dragged himself forward until his shaking foot could press the brake. The key would not go into the ignition on the first try. He turned it against the column, missed the slot, stopped, and looked at his hand as if it belonged to someone else.

From outside came water under the bridge.

Only water. Moving where it had always moved.

He put the key in.

The engine turned once, failed, and left the car ticking.

Joel held still.

Mike would have said something about Japanese loyalty or American decline or the false economy of replacing a battery before it failed. Will would have corrected the year, the voltage drop, the actual odds of starting after a week. Alex would have accused both of them of flirting with the alternator. The old responses rose in Joel without sound, complete and useless.

He turned the key again.

The engine caught.

Heat did not come at once. The fan coughed cold air through vents that smelled of dust and mouse-nest. The dashboard glowed green. A warning light stayed on. Another went out. The seat belt chime began its small, disciplined complaint.

Joel shut the door too hard.

The sound filled the vehicle. Not a camp sound. Not a woods sound. Door seal, latch, car body. Human enclosure. It should have comforted him. Instead it proved only that sound did not need trees to become too large.

He locked the doors.

He did not know why. The road was empty. The bridge was empty. The spruce beyond the ditch stood as spruce stood in any headlight beam, still until wind moved them. But his hand found the lock button and pressed it, and the four locks went down in sequence with soft mechanical knocks.

One. Two. Three. Four.

He flinched from each of them.

The phone had no service.

He held it above the steering wheel, then near the windshield, then toward the driver's window as if a few inches could matter after seven days of wrong distances. The screen lit his wet fingers and the cracked dirt in the lines of his palm. No bars. Emergency calls only, then not even that. He had expected the road to mean network, network to mean voice, voice to mean rescue. The map had taught him nothing if not the stupidity of that kind of conversion.

Joel put the phone in the passenger seat.

The Highlander's headlights showed the road as two pale tunnels between black spruce. Gravel, puddle, washboard, a road marker leaning away from winter, alder crowding the ditch. Behind him the bridge held the dark gap where he had landed. Below it the canoe lay half out of the water, the small pack on the passenger floor now, the notebook inside it, the blue bag still down by the hull because he had not had enough hands or mind to gather every fact.

He almost went back for it.

The thought put his hand on the door handle.

Blue bag under the front.

He did not know whether the phrase had come from memory or fear. It had no sound. It had no source but him. That should have made it safe.

It did not.

He took his hand from the handle, shifted into drive, and turned the Highlander away from the bridge. The canoe, the blue bag, the emergency paddle, the scrape of hull on black gravel: he left them in the headlights and then in the mirror and then in the dark behind him.

Roads still did what roads did.

Slowly at first. Then badly faster. The tires found ruts, climbed out, dropped into them again. The steering wheel shuddered against his palms. Gear rattled somewhere behind him. Every rattle sorted itself into the old ledger: food latch, buckle, paddle knock, zipper. The rear cargo cover tapped against its stop. A loose ice scraper clicked on plastic. A bottle rolled under the passenger seat and knocked twice against metal.

Joel kept both hands on the wheel and did not answer any of it.

The heater began to work. Warmth reached him as pain. His feet, which had been only weight and pressure, returned in burning pieces. His fingers stung around the steering wheel. His wet clothes steamed against the seat. The windshield fogged, cleared, fogged again when he breathed too hard. He used the wipers though there was no rain. The rubber dragged across dry glass and made a sound so close to a paddle blade scraping stone that he turned them off with more force than the lever needed.

The road climbed.

He passed a cut where spruce gave way to a low run of rock. Dawn was beginning behind cloud, not light exactly but a loosening of black into gray. The world outside the headlights appeared by degrees and without mercy. Gravel shoulders. Fireweed gone to seed. A culvert mouth with torn grass around it. The tracks of some truck that had come through days ago or yesterday or an hour before and belonged to a life in which men drove roads, checked timber, ate sandwiches, complained about gas prices, went home.

Joel's phone chimed.

He braked too hard.

The Highlander fishtailed on loose gravel, corrected, stopped at an angle across the road. The chime had been small. A connection notice, a system sound, maybe a message trying and failing. He stared at the phone on the passenger seat. One bar flickered and vanished. Then one came back.

He did not touch it for several breaths.

The phone was a machine. It carried voices. It could pull any living person out of the air and put them in the car with him, reduced to sound. His fiancee's name sat somewhere behind the locked screen. So did his mother. So did Will's mother, because Will had once texted him her number after a delayed flight and a favor that no one remembered now. The device contained people as possibilities. That had been ordinary. It was not ordinary now.

He dialed 911.

The call failed.

He tried again.

This time a woman answered.

"Nine-one-one, what is the address of your emergency?"

Joel closed his eyes.

Her voice was flat with training, cleanly transmitted, edged with static. A stranger's living voice, nowhere near him and very close. It asked for language. It asked for the world to be reduced into pieces that could be moved.

"North Road," Joel said.

His own voice came out wrong. Rough, small, unused.

"North Road where, sir?"

He looked through the windshield. The road marker leaned at the edge of the headlights, green paint scratched pale. He read it once and could not keep the numbers in his head. He opened the door and cold came in. Gravel under one foot. Engine idle. Heater ticking. He leaned out and read the marker again.

"Mile eleven," he said. "Maybe eleven-point-two. North Road Bridge road. I came from the bridge."

"What is the callback number you're calling from?"

He knew his number. He had known it for years. It still took three tries to give it in the right order.

"Are you injured?"

He looked at his hands on the wheel. The cuts had opened again. Mud lived under every nail. His knuckles were swollen. His pants were wet to the thigh and streaked dark with old blood that might not be his.

"Yes."

"Is anyone with you?"

The question was worse than the bridge.

Joel looked at the passenger seat. Mike's fleece hat. His small pack. The phone bright on the vinyl. No one.

"No."

"What happened?"

He could hear keys clicking on her end. A room somewhere. A headset. A person entering fields into a system that would send other people toward him.

Fields. Boxes. Nature of incident.

"Canoe accident," Joel said. "Animal attack. Three men dead or missing."

The words did not belong together. Accident softened attack. Attack made missing a lie. Dead sounded too complete for men whose voices had kept crossing water after their bodies were gone. But the operator took them. The system had places for them.

"How many men total?"

"Four."

"Including you?"

"Yes."

"Are you safe where you are?"

Joel looked at the spruce beyond the road. He looked at the rearview mirror, where the road fell away toward the unseen bridge. He looked at the locked doors and the lit dashboard and his own face in the dark glass, hollow-eyed and unfamiliar.

"I don't know."

There was a short pause. Not fear. Adjustment.

"Are you in the vehicle now?"

"Yes."

"Can you keep the doors locked and stay on the line?"

"Yes."

"I'm sending law enforcement and medical," she said. "If the vehicle can run and you are not in immediate danger, keep the doors locked and stay where you are."

Stay where you are.

The phrase had no malice. It was a procedure, not a lure. It still reached the same place in him as every voice from the trees that had wanted his body held in one place long enough for something else to arrive.

He put the Highlander in park, set the parking brake because Mike would have, and stayed.

The road emptied around the idling car. He watched the mirror. He watched the trees. The operator asked questions in a careful order. Names. Ages. Vehicle description. Last known locations. Weapons. Bear spray. Weather. Food. Water. Whether he had slept. Whether the animal was still present.

"I don't know," he said, and "yes," and "no," and "I don't know."

The woman never asked about voices.

For that he was grateful until he hated her for it.

The call dropped once. The phone went quiet in his hand, and the quiet was so sudden he almost threw it across the passenger seat. Then it rang, the same number coming back to him from the county dispatch center, and he made himself answer because the screen was visible, because the voice had a source, because the living world was allowed to call twice.

The first deputy arrived with lights but no siren.

Red and blue moved through spruce before the truck itself appeared, silent at first behind the Highlander's closed windows. Then tires on gravel, a door opening, a man's boots. Joel had the irrational conviction that if he rolled down the window, the voice outside would be wrong.

The deputy approached slowly with one hand visible and the other near his belt. Not afraid of Joel exactly. Ready for him to be an animal problem, a weapon problem, a shock problem. He was young enough that his beard looked deliberate and old enough to have learned not to rush men who looked as Joel looked.

"Joel?"

The sound of his name struck him like cold water.

He nodded.

"I'm Deputy Harris. I'm going to open the door, all right? Medics are behind me."

All right. A question that was also a plan.

Joel unlocked the door himself.

The deputy took one look at him and stopped using the voice for normal traffic stops.

"Okay," he said. "Okay. Let's get you out slow."

The word repeated. Not predatory. Human. A man making a small bridge with language because that was what people did. Joel wanted to tell him repetition was not comfort. He did not.

Cold air entered. Joel stepped down and nearly fell. Harris caught his elbow. Joel jerked away so hard the deputy let go at once and raised both hands.

"No problem," Harris said. "You're good."

Joel was not good. The phrase passed over him and failed to attach.

An ambulance came next, then a conservation officer in a green truck, then another deputy. Men and one woman moved around him with radios, gloves, flashlights, clipboards, questions. Harris asked where the canoe was, and Joel pointed back down the road. He could not say under the bridge. He pointed until Harris understood and sent the second deputy to look.

A thermal blanket went over Joel's shoulders. It made a dry plastic crackle like a cheap tarp. He tried to pull it off and a paramedic named Elise told him she needed him to keep it on.

"It's just a blanket," she said.

Just.

The word belonged to every failed explanation.

He kept the blanket on.

They sat him on the rear bumper of the ambulance. Someone put a blood pressure cuff on his arm. Someone shone light in his eyes. Someone asked if he had struck his head. Someone asked again how many in the party. The conservation officer unfolded a map on the hood of Harris's truck and weighted the corners with a radio, a flashlight, a coffee mug, and a box of nitrile gloves.

A map on a hood.

Joel looked away.

"Can you show us where you put in?" the officer asked.

He was older than Harris, square-faced, with gray in his mustache and black dirt in the creases of his hands. His name tag said Reardon. He had the look of a man who knew water and paperwork and did not confuse them.

Joel made himself stand.

The map was not their map. Different scale, clean paper, official markings. Lakes named with names he had never learned because Mike and Will had spoken mostly in shapes and sequence: first lake, rough carry, the big one, the bad channel, final portage. The official map had certainty printed all over it. Roads in black, water in blue, contour lines thin and confident. It looked like a document from a world that had not gone in.

Joel put a shaking finger on South Access.

"Here."

Reardon watched his hand, not his face.

"First night?"

Joel traced water and portage in the air above the paper. His finger would not stay steady enough to touch every line. He marked the granite point. Then Second Camp. The place where Alex had stepped out to the edge.

"Alex died here," he said.

The men around the hood became quieter.

"Animal?" Reardon asked.

Joel looked at him.

The officer did not add bear. He left the space open.

"We thought bear," Joel said.

"At the time?"

"Yes."

"And now?"

Joel felt the road under his boots. Gravel, not mud. He pressed one heel down until a stone hurt through the sandal.

"I don't know."

Reardon nodded once, as if not knowing could be entered somewhere if handled carefully enough.

They moved north on the map. Weather delay. Bad crossing. Emergency camp. Old cabin. Mike. Return. Will. The route became a confession made with a dirty fingernail. Joel gave them enough to find bodies and not enough to explain why there were rules in a notebook against answering sound. He did not take the notebook out of his pack. He could feel it there against the ambulance bumper when he sat down again, damp and swollen and more accurate than anything he said aloud.

"You left notes?" Reardon asked.

"At the first body," Joel said. "At Alex."

"What did they say?"

"Names. Date. Bear attack. Direction of travel."

"Good," Reardon said.

Joel almost laughed. The sound caught behind his teeth and stayed there.

Search teams assembled while morning became a colorless noon. A helicopter was requested and then delayed by ceiling. Ground teams staged at both access roads because nobody liked trusting one route in and one route out. Deputies drank coffee from paper cups. Radios spoke in clipped phrases. Units, last known, shoreline, grid, possible remains. Each message came from a body Joel could see or from a speaker clipped to a shoulder. Source, then sound. Source, then sound. He watched for it with the devotion of prayer.

No one noticed the rule they were obeying for him.

The second deputy came back from the bridge with mud to his knees and said there was a canoe below, half pulled out. Blue dry bag in it. Small paddle. No one else. He said it gently, as if the canoe might count as a kind of witness.

Joel nodded once. The bag had stayed where he left it. That should have eased something. It only proved that objects could be faithful and still fail.

At some point they put him in the ambulance because his temperature was low and his blood pressure was doing something they did not like. The doors closed. The interior smelled of antiseptic, plastic, rubber, old fear cleaned badly. Cabinets latched. Straps hung from the cot. A monitor beeped before anyone attached him to it.

"Sorry," Elise said when he flinched. "That's ours."

Ours.

The word steadied him more than it should have. A sound with an owner. A noise claimed by someone living, someone visible.

"Can you tell me what you heard before the first attack?" Harris asked from the ambulance doorway.

Joel looked at him too sharply.

The deputy's face changed. He was trying to be gentle and had stepped on something he could not see.

"Before Alex was injured," Harris said. "Anything that might help searchers. Brush. Animal noise. Him calling out."

Joel closed his hands around the thermal blanket. The plastic whispered under his fingers.

He could have told the truth.

Hold up. What. Alex answering something at the edge. Food barrel latch where no barrel was. Laughter after the body was covered. Will's cough from the wrong place. Mike's throat clearing without Mike. His own voice on water. The planted phrase returned perfectly and emptily from shore to shore. He could have filled the ambulance with it until every person there looked at him with pity first and then caution, until the true parts of the story spoiled the useful parts.

"Brush," Joel said. "Alex. Then impact."

Harris wrote it down.

"Did he call out?"

Joel watched the pen move. Black ink on a small pad, making a record that would be carried farther than his voice.

"Yes."

"What did he say?"

Joel thought of Alex's mouth forming old jokes, complaints, bids for audience. He thought of the thing using those shapes long after the man was gone. To give the words now felt like handing them across another threshold.

"I don't know," he said.

By late afternoon the ceiling had lifted enough for the helicopter to work low, and a team reached Second Camp because Joel could mark it.

No one told him the details. Reardon came into the hospital room two towns south with his hat in his hands, and Joel knew from the careful way he closed the door.

"We located your friend Alex," he said.

Located. The word did not ask the body to be a body.

Joel nodded.

"The note helped."

Good, Joel thought again, and hated the use of it.

They had moved him to the hospital after the ambulance crew won the argument he had not had strength to continue. He had been undressed by strangers and dressed in paper and cotton. Warm fluids entered his arm. A nurse cleaned grit from his palms with gauze. Someone photographed bruises. Someone bagged his clothes. He asked for the small pack until they brought it in and set it on the chair where he could see it.

The room had a window that did not open. A television mounted high in the corner. A sink. A sharps container. A clock whose second hand moved without sound. The hallway outside carried ordinary distress: carts, soft shoes, a child crying and stopping, a man coughing, a nurse laughing once at something said at the desk. Human sounds, sourced and unsourced as people passed out of sight.

Joel kept the door half open so he could see the hallway.

Then he asked for it closed because the hallway had too many voices.

Then he asked for it open again.

The nurse did not comment.

Harris came back before dark with another officer whose name Joel immediately lost. They asked for a formal statement. Not all of it now, they said. Enough now. More when he was medically cleared. They used words like preliminary and timeline and incident. The words were clean tools. They did what they could.

"Do you want to call someone first?" Harris asked.

Joel thought of his fiancee's voice.

Not the idea of her. The sound itself. Sleep-thick if he called late, sharp with fear if she saw the number, trying to stay calm because he was alive enough to call and because three other men were not. He thought of a phone taking that beloved sound and making it arrive from nowhere, with no face attached, no body to confirm it. He thought of having to say her name into a device and trust what answered.

"Not yet," he said.

Harris looked as if he wanted to object, then did not.

That was one mercy.

The official story began forming while the search teams moved through the route.

Bear. Panic. Separation. Exposure. Possible drowning. Survivor shock. Incomplete recollection under trauma. Decisions made in weather. Men leaving camp when they should not have. A route more remote than expected. Gear lost. Communication absent. It was not malicious. It was not even stupid. It was what people did with facts too large and strange to carry whole. They cut them down until the pieces fit into reports, then passed the reports from hand to hand.

Joel helped them.

He said weather trapped them.

He said they made bad decisions.

He said sound carried strangely over water.

No one disagreed.

No one asked whether he had written anything else.

The notebook stayed in the small pack on the chair. Its pages were swollen, edges buckled, pencil lines softened until some words looked rubbed into the paper rather than written on it. It was evidence in the way a scar was evidence. It proved something had happened. It did not prove what.

After dark, Reardon returned.

He had mud on his cuffs now. Real mud from the route. It made him harder to look at and easier to trust.

"Air crew saw evidence near the old cabin," he said. "Ground team is working in. We believe we found Mike."

Believe. Found. Evidence. Each word holding something back because the room did not need the whole weight of it.

Joel looked at the sheet over his knees.

"And Will?"

Reardon took a breath through his nose. "Pack and gear near the final portage. Visual only so far. Search is ongoing."

Will had hated imprecision unless it was his own. Search is ongoing would have made him sit up, ask what grid, what conditions, what exact confidence interval lay behind ongoing. Joel heard the correction in memory and did not let it reach his mouth.

"Okay," he said.

Reardon did not pretend it was okay.

Later, when the room had thinned to machines and one chair and the small pack within sight, Joel gave the statement they asked for.

It was true in parts and useless as a whole.

He started with the motel because starting with Alex's death made the officer's pen hesitate. He named the men. He named the vehicles. He named the launch, the first camp, the weather, the second camp, the attack. He said bear because that was the first word they had used and because the woods had left enough violence for the word to stand near it without shame. He said they tried to leave. He said wind stopped them. He said they looked for an old cabin. He said Mike was separated. He said Will was separated. He said he reached the take-out alone.

He did not say the dead men kept speaking.

He did not say that silence had become a skill.

He did not say that every question in the room still felt like bait.

When he finished, the officer read parts of it back to him. Joel listened to his week translated into a voice not his own. It sounded plausible. It sounded thin. It sounded like something that could be filed.

"Is that accurate?" the officer asked.

Joel looked at the small pack on the chair, the notebook inside it swollen with water and pencil, full of rules no one in the room would know how to read.

"Yes," he said.

Then, because yes was not enough and because no was unusable, he added, "As much as it can be."
