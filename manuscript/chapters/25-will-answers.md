# Chapter 25 - Will Answers

Joel reached him before the trees took him, but not before Will spoke again.

"Say something new," Will called into the brush. His voice cracked on new. "Come on. If you understand, say something new."

The woods answered with Mike: "We're fine."

"No," Will said. "That's old."

Joel grabbed his pack. Will twisted free with strength Joel would not have credited to him an hour earlier.

From the left, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will laughed once, wild and broken. "Old."

From ahead, Will's own voice said, "There is a difference."

"Old," Will said, and stepped off the trail.

Joel went after him. Branches whipped his face. The ground dropped, not steeply but enough. Will moved toward a small opening where stones showed through moss. Beyond it the forest was dim and wet and crowded.

"Will," Joel said.

The name was out before he could stop it.

Will froze. So did Joel.

For a second nothing happened.

Then Joel's voice came from behind them. "Will."

Will turned the wrong way.

Something moved in the opening. Not a bear. Not not a bear. A pale angle between trunks, a wet dark shoulder, limbs or branches crossing where no branch should be. Joel's mind refused the whole and kept the fragments as punishment.

Will said, very softly, "Oh."

That was his last living word.

The motion was fast and low. Will went down without a scream. Joel saw his hand strike stone, saw the bandage flash white, saw his body pulled sideways into alder that thrashed once and then stilled.

Joel had the hatchet in his hand. He did not throw it. He did not charge. He backed away, making no sound at all, because the part of him that loved Will was not stronger than the part that understood the rule.

From the brush came Will's voice, panicked now, perfect and empty.

"No, no, no."

Joel backed to the trail.

"No, no, no."

He lifted the canoe because leaving it meant losing the route. His legs shook so badly the hull knocked against a spruce trunk.

"No, no, no."

He walked.

At the place where the trail bent, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel did not answer.

He put one foot down, then the next, and carried the canoe toward the last lake alone.
