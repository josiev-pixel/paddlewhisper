# Chapter 25 - Will Answers

Joel reached him before the trees took him, but not before Will gave the woods one more thing.

"Say something new," Will called.

His voice cracked on new. It was not quite a shout. The portage would not accept a whole human sound. It broke everything into alder, wet needles, the black spaces under young spruce. The words carried just far enough to become available.

"Come on," Will said. "If you understand, say something new."

Joel had one hand fisted in the back of Will's vest and the other pressed against the notebook inside his own jacket. For a second he knew his remaining life by pressure. The notebook damp at the corners. The Highlander key zipped in the inner pocket, hard under his pulse. The hatchet at his belt. The smaller pack biting across his shoulders. The dropped canoe behind him, green hull canted in brush where he had let it slide off wrong.

The car was not here. The road was not here. The key was only a small piece of metal against his chest. Still his mind kept trying to make a chain of it.

Key. Car. Bridge. Road.

From ahead, in Mike's voice, came, "We're fine."

Will let out one breath that might once have become a laugh.

"Old," he said.

Joel jerked on the vest.

Will twisted out of his grip with a strength Joel would not have credited to him an hour earlier. The heavy pack swung on Will's shoulders. The proper paddles lashed along one side knocked against the food barrel inside, a low wooden clack. Mud sucked at his sandal when he planted his foot. The trail here was no longer a path so much as a decision other people had made before the brush recovered from it.

The same clack answered from deeper in the portage, farther ahead than either man had gone.

Will looked toward it.

Joel pointed back to the trail. Back to the canoe. Back to the strip of mud and cut brush that meant route, however little route promised now.

Will shook his head without looking at him.

From the left, Alex said, "Hold up."

Will turned his face toward the voice.

"Old."

It was worse than belief. Belief could be fought. Joel could write ALEX DEAD until the pencil tore through the page. He could point toward Second Camp, where Alex lay under gray fabric and stones, miles of bad water and failed judgment behind them. But Will knew. He was not following Alex. He was following the break in the imitation: the old phrase set down in the wrong place, the friend's voice with the friend missing from it.

Joel pulled the notebook out and opened it against his forearm.

He wrote:

THIS IS HOW IT MOVES YOU.

Will read it, then jabbed one finger toward the trees. He pointed at the line. He made a small cutting motion in the air.

Not moves. Shows.

Joel wrote over his own words, hard enough to smear them:

MOVE NOW.

From ahead, in Will's own voice, came, "There is a difference."

The sentence had his dryness, his exact little lift on difference, the mild irritation of a man correcting a distinction no one else cared about. But it did not belong to the moment. It answered nothing. It divided nothing. It was a hinge with no door.

Will's face changed.

Not wonder. Not fear. Something leaner. The look of a man hearing a gear skip. For months before the trip, after the LED company had made him unnecessary in a language of restructuring and budget, Will had been living on small demonstrations of usefulness: a reel cleaned, a strap fixed, a bad solder joint found under a magnifier. A flaw proved by his hand was a kind of acquittal. Something had failed, and he could point to where.

"Old," he said again, softer.

Joel clamped a hand over his mouth.

Will's eyes came to him, close and furious. Not only furious. Hurt. The look was old enough to cross the years between them without help. Boys in church basements that smelled of carpet glue and powdered lemonade. Adults telling them when to speak and when to bow their heads. Will mouthing a joke at the wrong time, Joel looking down at his shoes so no one would see him laugh. Later, when Joel had stopped believing and Will had stopped caring enough to argue belief with him, the habit had remained. Will talked. Joel withheld. Each had trusted the other to know the difference between silence and abandonment.

Now Joel's palm was over Will's mouth.

Will pulled Joel's hand away with his good hand. His injured hand hung open and swollen at his side, the bandage gray at the palm and red where it crossed the knuckles.

"Listen to it," Will whispered.

Joel shook his head.

"No. Listen. It can't answer the question."

The whisper was almost nothing, but it was air. It was material. Joel felt it leave Will's mouth and enter the wet brush with the ticks of water, the scrape of packs, the small noises of a world that had never agreed to be quiet.

From behind them, near the landing they could no longer see, Mike cleared his throat.

Joel turned despite himself.

Nothing. No orange PFD. No body at the bend. Only alder leaves, a strip of standing water in an old boot print, the dropped canoe half-hidden where its green hull leaned against spruce. The trail behind them looked longer than it had been.

Mike's voice said, "Just keep moving."

It still sounded practical enough to obey.

Will smiled. Small and awful.

"You see?" he whispered. "It contradicts itself."

Joel wrote one-handed:

NO. IT REPEATS USEFUL THINGS.

Will looked at the line and made a small choking sound. Pain, fatigue, triumph, grief, all caught at once. He was not hearing rescue. He was hearing an error he could survive if he named it accurately enough.

"That's what I mean," he said.

From ahead, in Joel's voice, came, "Wait. Just wait."

Both men froze.

The sound of Joel's own voice did not feel like hearing another person. It felt like finding a handprint on the inside of his chest. Low, flat, nearly embarrassed by its own urgency. The phrase he had given the trip without ceremony. A practical brake. Now it stood ahead of them in the wet portage, wearing his mouth.

Will turned toward him at once. Not toward the sound. Toward living Joel.

For a half second the rule worked. Sight beat sound. Joel was there, breathing, pencil in hand, jacket zipped over the key. The false Joel had come from ahead, and the real Joel stood beside him.

Will's face softened.

Then the false Joel said again, deeper in the portage, "Wait. Just wait."

This time the timing was wrong. Too clean after the first. No impatience in it. No body behind the restraint. Will heard the wrongness and leaned toward it as if toward light through a door.

Joel grabbed his sleeve.

Will said, "It has your phrase. Not why you say it."

Joel shook his head. He could not write fast enough. He could not make the page do the work of his hand, and his hand had already failed.

He wrote:

IT DOES NOT NEED WHY.

Will touched the line.

"We do," he said.

The sentence was so quiet Joel almost wished the woods would miss it.

From the right, Alex's hard laugh broke once. It had all of Alex's surface in it, the bark, the demand that the group turn toward him, the big physical delight at becoming the room's center. It had none of the warmth that used to follow. None of the breath gathering for the next dumb thing. None of Alex waiting to see whether the joke landed.

Then Alex said, "Quit mothering me."

Farther right, as if another piece had been selected and set down, came, "I'm taking a piss. Nobody panic."

Will closed his eyes.

Joel saw the voice enter him. Not as belief. As memory. Alex in the old light at First Camp, making a face over bland food. Alex on the beaver dam, strong and annoyed and funny. Alex at Second Camp, half outside the firelight, wanting everyone to notice his exit. Every dead use of him was crude and exact. The woods did not have to understand grief. It only had to strike the shape grief left.

Will opened his eyes and said to the trees, "That's not new."

Joel slapped him.

Not hard enough to hurt the way he meant it to. Hard enough that Will stopped.

The sound of skin on skin moved out into the brush.

Both men listened.

From somewhere ahead, the same slap returned. Dry. Palm-flat. Impossible.

Will inhaled.

Joel seized the front of his vest and put his face close enough that the words should have belonged only to breath.

"Stop," Joel said.

The word came out before judgment reached it.

Will's eyes widened. Not because Joel had spoken, but because of what the word gave away. Stop. A command. A need. A sound with Joel's full weight behind it.

From the trees to their left, Joel said, "Stop."

The false voice had no breath on Will's cheek. It could place the word but not the warmth. It could make Joel into air and leave the body out.

Will turned his head slowly toward the left.

Joel put both hands on him and drove him back toward the trail. The shove surprised them both. Will stumbled, the pack striking a spruce trunk with a hollow thud. The paddles clattered. The food barrel shifted inside the lash. The portage answered with all of those sounds at once, from ahead and behind and then from the right, a brief inventory of their remaining life repeated by something that had no use for any of it except as bait.

Will shoved Joel off with his forearm.

The shove was weak and badly aimed, but Joel was tired enough that it worked. He stepped back onto a slick root. His heel slid. The notebook fell from his hand and opened in the mud with a wet slap that sounded too much like a body finding ground.

Will looked at it.

So did Joel.

The page had opened to the cabin rules, swollen now and blurred by weather and hands:

DO NOT ANSWER. SIGHT. RIGHT WORDS WRONG SOURCE. JOEL KEY. TWO MEN.

Two men.

Will bent for the notebook.

Joel bent too.

From directly behind Will, in Mike's voice, came, "Key's here."

Will did not look back. His good hand closed on one wet corner of the page. Joel put two fingers on the other. Mud squeezed up between them.

For one second Joel thought that meant he had won.

Then Will said, still looking at him, "It put key in Mike's voice because Mike said it."

Joel slid his fingers under the notebook.

Will pressed down.

"Pattern," Will said. "Not meaning. Maybe only close."

His voice had changed. Less wild now. More himself. That was worse. This was the voice Joel had heard over gear spread on garage floors, over maps in bad motel light, over broken stoves and fishing reels and Mike's cheap canoe straps. Will explaining because explanation kept shame out of the room. Will correcting because precision made him useful. For years that habit had saved them small damages: a strap routed right, a stove cleaned before the O-ring failed, a map crease noticed before rain found it. For a while they had all been glad he could say, no, no, no, this part matters.

"Will," Joel said.

The name was out before he could stop it.

Will froze.

So did Joel.

Will's hand came off the page.

Nothing answered immediately.

That delay was almost merciful. Almost intelligent. Or only distance, water, the shape of trees.

Then Joel's voice came from behind them.

"Will."

Not loud. Not a cry. Just the name as Joel had said it, with the small pleading drag he had not known was in it until it came back empty.

Will turned the wrong way.

Joel reached for him and missed the shoulder strap. His fingers caught only loose webbing. It burned through his palm.

"No," Joel said.

From the brush, Joel's voice said, "Will."

Will took one step off the portage.

The pack made the step wrong. Its weight pushed him past the small choice he had made, from edge to drop. One sandal slid into a hidden runnel. The paddles knocked against the barrel inside the lash. Will caught an alder stem with his good hand and stayed upright. The stem bent under him, green and living, and for one absurd second it held.

Beyond the trail the ground fell into a shallow trough hidden by fern and alder. Not steep. Not dramatic. The kind of off-trail dip a man could step into, curse at, climb out of, and mock himself for later. Stones showed through the moss there, flat and dark, their faces slick with water. Beyond them the forest thickened, spruce packed close and gray dead branches crossing at knee and chest height.

Joel went after him.

Branches whipped his face. A dead twig scratched his lower lip and left the taste of blood. The pack on Will's back made a path by force, breaking small limbs and stripping needles. Joel saw the food barrel bulge round under the wet fabric. He saw the paddles swinging loose in their lash. He saw Will's injured hand half-open, fingers useless and white at the tips.

"Say something new," Will called again.

His voice had lifted now. It was no longer experiment only. It was desperation inside experiment's clothes.

"Say something you didn't steal."

From ahead, Will's own voice said, "Say something new."

Will stopped.

Joel nearly ran into him.

The copy came again, slightly to the right.

"Say something you didn't steal."

It was exact enough to hurt and wrong enough to be clear. The first sentence carried Will's crack on new. The second had no understanding of accusation. It made steal a sound, not a charge.

Will laughed once.

"There," he said.

Joel grabbed the back of his vest. "No."

"There," Will said again. "It can repeat the challenge. It can't answer it."

The opening ahead was only a wet place where two old trees had fallen and let in a little more light. Stones showed under moss. Alder grew low at the far edge. The air smelled of rot and cold water. Mosquitoes hung there in a loose dark stitch above the wet. Nothing in it should have held a man.

Something moved there.

Joel did not see a body. He saw a pale angle between trunks, lower than a standing man and higher than a crawling animal. He saw a dark wet shoulder or flank, if shoulder and flank were words that could be trusted. He saw limbs, or branches, crossing where no branch should have crossed. He saw enough for his mind to begin sorting and not enough for any sorted thing to stay.

Bear. Moose. Man.

None of the words held.

Will saw it too.

His face emptied.

For the first time since he had stepped off the trail, he looked less like a man proving a theory than a man who had reached the end of one.

He said, very softly, "Oh."

It was small. It was his last living word.

The motion came low and fast.

Not from the place Joel had fixed on. From the left of it. That was what stayed with him afterward. Sight had not lied. It had been narrowed. A pale flash drew the eye. A voice held the head. The real motion used the gap between.

Will went down without a scream.

The pack hit stone first. The impact drove the breath out of him in a hard animal grunt. His injured hand struck next, bandage bright against black rock. Joel saw the fingers spread and fold wrong. Then alder took the upper half of him. The brush thrashed once, high and violent, as if a canoe had been shoved through it sideways.

Joel had the hatchet in his hand.

He did not remember drawing it.

For one impossible second the world offered him the old shape of courage. Charge. Swing. Get him. Do not leave another man. The shape was so familiar it almost became command, every childhood lesson about rescue and loyalty and the Good Samaritan stripped of church language and made into muscle.

Then Will's legs kicked once in the moss and vanished sideways.

The alder closed.

Joel stood with the hatchet raised. The hand with the hatchet did not go forward. That was what he would have afterward instead of an explanation.

He backed away.

One step.

Then another.

He kept the hatchet up until his wrist began to shake. The opening did not move. The pale angle was gone. The dark wet shoulder was gone. Only alder. Only stones. Only a strip of bandage caught on a low branch, white and then pink where water or blood moved through it.

From the brush came Will's voice.

"No, no, no."

Panicked. Perfect. Empty.

Joel's foot found the runnel. Water filled his sandal. He did not look down.

"No, no, no."

This time it came from the portage behind him, ahead of where living Will had been. Too soon. Too clean. The panic preserved but not lived.

Joel backed to the trail.

The notebook lay in the mud where it had fallen. He wanted it with an intensity that surprised him. Not for the rules. Not only for the rules. It was the last place where two men had still been two men. A shared page. A small roof made of paper, already leaking. Their last argument had weight there. Their last mercy did too, if mercy could be made of block letters and mud.

Will's voice said, from the brush, "Joel."

Joel bent for the notebook.

The name came again from ahead, in Will's voice. Then from behind, in Joel's own.

"Joel."

He picked up the notebook and closed it without reading the open page. Mud smeared under his thumb. He shoved it inside his jacket, against the key.

The canoe waited where he had dropped it, half in brush, its green hull catching weak light. It looked too ordinary to have survived the scene. Scratched, wet, stubborn. A thing made to cross water, now lying on a trail with two dead men's sounds around it.

From the alder: "No, no, no."

Joel put the hatchet back at his belt. His hand did not want to let go of it. He had to open each finger.

The heavy pack was gone with Will. Food barrel, proper paddles, most of the remaining food, the repair kit, some cord, the dense small odds they had kept because weight had not yet become impossible enough. The loss moved through Joel practically before it moved through him morally. That would not forgive him. He had the canoe. He had the small pack. He had the hatchet, the mud-smeared notebook, the key, and the blue dry bag with the emergency paddle still under the front seat. He did not have Will. The sentence tried to become an inventory and failed.

Will laughed from somewhere to the right.

Not his real laugh from before. A cough-laugh from the old days, from before correcting someone. It appeared near the trail, then farther ahead, then behind Joel at shoulder height, as if the sound were being tried in different hollows.

Joel lifted the canoe.

The yoke came down on the bruised place at the base of his neck. Pain steadied him. The hull narrowed the world to mud and roots and the thin view under green Kevlar. He could not see far. He could not afford not to see. He had chosen a tool that blocked sight because leaving it meant losing water.

This was the kind of choice left.

He took one step.

From the brush behind him, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel did not answer.

He took another step.

Mike cleared his throat ahead.

"We're exactly where we ought to be."

Joel stopped.

The phrase moved through the hull and into the small chamber around his head. It filled the canoe's interior like breath. Mike's certainty. Mike's old managerial mercy, the kind that asked evidence to lower its voice. The woods did not have to believe it. It only had to know where to put it.

Joel lowered his head under the hull until his forehead almost touched the yoke.

He did not answer.

Will's voice came from the right side of the trail.

"There is a difference."

Joel moved.

The portage accepted him badly. The canoe struck alder on both sides. Brush scraped the hull in long dry strokes. Twice he had to stop and back up to free the front from young spruce. Each stop opened a place for sound.

Will: "No, no, no."

Alex: "Hold up."

Mike: "Just keep moving."

Joel's own voice: "Wait. Just wait."

The words came from wrong distances, wrong heights, wrong sequences. They made arguments without meaning to. They instructed and pleaded and corrected one another in the damp green air. They were almost human because humans had made them.

Joel kept his mouth shut.

His breath grew loud inside the hull. He hated that too. Fear made a sound of him. Pain did. Cold did. He tried to breathe through his nose, slowly, and failed. His foot slipped on a wet stone and his knee struck root. He stayed upright because dropping the canoe would make a sound large enough to gather everything.

At the next bend, the portage widened enough for him to set the canoe down without crashing it.

He did not set it down.

Behind him, Will said, "Oh."

Joel nearly fell.

The sound was too small. Not the panic phrase. Not the old correction. The last living word, returned before it had cooled. It came once from behind, from the place where Will had seen enough to stop speaking.

Then again from ahead, too clean.

"Oh."

The copy held no discovery. No end of argument. Only the vowel, lifted and placed.

Joel understood then that Will had been right about the limit and wrong about what a limit offered. The thing did not understand. It did not need to. It could fail at meaning and still kill a man who went close enough to prove it. A wrong answer could still move a body. A hollow sound could still put weight in the wrong place.

He walked.

The trail began to slope down. He smelled water before he saw it, cold and mineral, with the faint rotten sweetness of leaves at a lake edge. Spruce thinned. Light widened under the canoe. The final lake lay somewhere ahead. Beyond that, if the map and key and road still belonged to the same world, the bridge and the Highlander waited.

Will said, far behind him, "No, no, no."

Joel put one foot down, then the next, and carried the canoe toward the last lake alone.
