# Chapter 17 - Mike's Authority

Mike died because he was almost right.

That was the part Joel kept returning to when he tried to put the week in an order that would hold. Mike did not run into the dark because a dead friend teased him. He did not forget the rule in a clean, stupid way. He moved ten yards into brush to confirm the things he had spent his life confirming: road, shape, pocket, key.

Joel understood the motion as it happened. Mike had made a life out of checking what other men hoped was fine. Buckles. Straps. Weather. Time. That kind of man was useful until usefulness could be made to answer.

For a few seconds after Mike crossed into the young spruce, no one moved.

The cabin door stood open behind Joel and Will, black in the wet wall. The table they had shoved against it sat crooked from Mike forcing his way out. Inside, the packs were still against the wall, buckles dulled by damp. The black pot under the leak rang once, then once again, thin and domestic.

Outside, dusk thickened the clearing. Every spruce at the edge seemed to stand in front of another spruce, and another behind that. The old road was no longer a road so much as a darker permission between trees.

Mike was still visible.

Not well. Not enough. A darker block between dark young trunks, the orange of his PFD turned brown by rain and shadow. But there. Joel held one hand up as if the gesture itself could keep him counted. Will stood at the cabin threshold with his injured hand pressed against his chest and his good hand on the doorframe. His mouth was open. He knew not to call. Knowing did not give him another tool.

From the old road, Mike's voice said, "It opens up after this."

The living Mike turned his head toward it.

Only his head. Only a little. The smallest possible failure of the rule.

Joel stepped into the grass.

"Mike."

The word left him before he could stop it. It hung below the wet spruce tips, useless and available.

Will made a small sound behind him. Not a word. A swallowed warning, hard enough to hurt.

Mike did not answer. He took another step forward.

Joel went after him.

He went because Mike was still visible. Because sight had to mean something or none of the rules meant anything. Because leaving a man ten yards into brush while his shoulders could still be counted was another kind of murder.

The grass was high and wet. It dragged against Joel's shins and laid cold water into his sandals. The old roadbed made a shallow raised strip through the clearing, but once it entered the trees it broke into suggestion: ditch, moss, alder, gravel under roots. It had been built for wheels and left long enough for the woods to answer.

Mike's shoulders moved ahead of him.

Close enough to see. Close enough to reach if Joel moved faster. Close enough to call if calling were not feeding.

Behind Joel, Will left the doorway.

Joel turned enough to see him and raised one hand hard, palm out.

Stay.

Will stopped at the edge of the cabin shadow. His glasses were smeared. The bandage on his hand had gone dark at the center. In the blue dusk he seemed held in place only by the habit of being seen.

From ahead came Mike's throat-clear.

Not the living one. Joel could see the side of Mike's face now, rain in his beard, jaw set, mouth closed. The sound came from farther up the roadbed, where the young spruce shut into a black wet seam.

Then Mike's voice, calm and low: "We're fine."

Mike stopped.

That was the worst part. He kept stopping. He kept almost understanding.

Joel stepped close enough to touch the back of Mike's PFD.

Mike did not turn around. "Don't grab me."

The sentence was real. Breath in it. Anger in it. A living man's impatience with being handled.

Joel let his fingers hover an inch from the strap. He wanted the notebook, the slow authority of graphite, the way a written word lost tone and gained edge. But the notebook was in his jacket pocket, and taking it out meant looking down.

"Back," Joel said.

Mike's shoulders lifted once with breath. "I'm looking at it."

"At what?"

Mike did not answer at first. Rain ticked off spruce needles. Water moved from one leaf to another in a soft run of drops. The woods ahead had no center.

"There's a gap," Mike said. "Road keeps going."

Joel looked past him. There was a gap because brush made gaps everywhere. A man could look at any dark place long enough and make a route from it. Joel saw two alder stems crossed like bones, a jack pine down with its roots up, and beyond that a pale strip that might have been moss, water, stone, or nothing useful.

"Not tonight."

Mike's head moved once. Not a nod. A refusal he had not yet put into words.

Behind them, Will whispered, "Joel."

Joel turned. Will pointed toward the cabin.

The dark doorway held the shape of their packs. Nothing had moved. Then a dry bag buckle clicked from inside.

Once.

Clean plastic. Familiar as a finger snap.

Mike turned his whole body.

Joel caught the strap.

Mike twisted free. "Key."

"On you."

"I know where it is."

"Then leave it."

The old argument almost found them there. At a landing, in another life, Alex would have made the line funny. Will would have added the technical distinction that annoyed both of them. Mike would have checked the pocket anyway.

Instead Mike pressed his left hand to the PFD pocket.

The zipper was still closed. Joel saw it. The little black pull lay flat against the orange nylon, wet and dark. The key was a hard shape beneath the fabric, no bigger than the last ordinary thing in the world.

"There," Joel said.

Mike looked down at the pocket as if his own body had become evidence entered by someone else. His face changed, not yielding, only losing one layer of certainty.

His thumb stayed on the zipper pull. He rubbed it once, hard, as if the teeth of it could report back.

From the cabin came Will's voice.

"Mike?"

The living Will stood fifteen yards behind them in the clearing, one hand still raised from pointing at the doorway. His mouth was closed.

Mike looked at him.

Good, Joel thought.

Then Will's voice came again from inside the cabin, soft and practical and wrong. "Mike?"

Mike stepped backward, toward the cabin this time.

The sound put duty in both directions. Forward meant false route. Back meant false Will. The only safe thing was stillness, and stillness had never been Mike's gift.

Joel gripped the back of the PFD.

Mike wheeled on him. "Let go."

Joel shook his head.

"Let go of me."

"Look at Will."

"I am."

"Keep looking."

Mike's eyes flicked past him to the living Will in the clearing. For two seconds the three of them made a line of sight: Will at the cabin shadow, Joel in the wet grass, Mike at the first trees. Each man seen by another, each body held in place by attention.

Then from the brush to Joel's right, Alex laughed.

Bright. Exact. Close enough that Joel's body turned before his mind could object.

There was no Alex. Alder. Spruce. Rain-dark leaves. A torn strip of birch bark hanging from a branch. No headlamp. No red jacket. No dead man, only the laugh emptied and used.

When Joel looked back, Mike had moved three steps deeper into the roadbed.

"No," Joel said.

Mike lifted Alex's bear spray.

His voice was lower now. "Come on, then."

The words were not brave in the way a story would make them brave. They were tired. Angry. Humiliated. A man addressing a problem because the alternative was to let it choose the terms.

Joel saw all of Mike in the line of his arm: the canoe dealer who could spot a warped hull from a driveway, the man who checked straps twice and complained about everyone else's knots, the man who had shown Joel how to read a faint current seam and then acted as if the lesson were obvious. Once, on a spring trip years back, Mike had walked knee-deep into black water to free a pinned boat and come back cursing them for letting him be the only adult. Obnoxious, useful, first in.

"Mike," Joel said.

Mike did not turn.

From farther up the old road, Mike's own voice said, "Just keep moving."

The phrase passed through the trees with the steadiness of a hand on the back. It was one of Mike's good sentences. Joel hated that. It had been useful on portages, in mud, when Will talked too much and Alex made complaint into theater. It had kept them from sitting down in bad places.

Now the woods wore it without earning it.

Mike took one step toward it.

Joel lunged and got one hand around the shoulder of the PFD.

Mike shoved him off hard enough that Joel slipped sideways into alder. A wet branch scraped his cheek. He tasted blood or rain and could not tell which.

"I said don't grab me."

"That's not you."

"No shit."

"Then stop."

Mike turned back. His face was red under mud and rain. He looked less like a leader than a man cornered by the failure of every tool he trusted.

"You think I don't know that?"

The words were too loud. Joel could feel them leave Mike and become part of the clearing.

Will made a broken gesture from the clearing. Down. Quiet. Please.

Mike saw him and lowered his voice, but the damage had already become air.

"I'm not following it," Mike said. "I'm checking the line."

"There is no line."

"If the road dies here," Mike said, "then I put us in a box."

The sentence came out stripped of command. It was the closest thing to fear Mike had given them.

"Then it dies here," Joel said.

Mike looked past him into the trees. "You don't know that."

He was right. That was the small knife in it. Joel had a rule and fear and eyes that could not see far enough. Mike had the same eyes and more route sense. On any other day, Joel would have trusted him past the first wall of spruce because Mike could read old cuts on stumps, water choosing grade, gravel under moss, human decisions left in dirt.

The thing had not invented Mike's authority. It had found the working part.

Joel pulled out the notebook at last. He kept his head up and wrote badly against his palm.

NO SCOUTING.

He held it out.

Mike glanced, then looked away.

Joel wrote another line beneath it.

SIGHT IS THE RULE.

Mike looked at that one longer.

Behind them, the dry bag buckle clicked again from the cabin.

Will flinched but did not turn. He held the rule better than either of them for that second, his whole face clenched around the effort.

Then from the trees to Mike's left, Will's voice said, "No, no, no."

The living Will made the same sound half a beat later, quieter, horrified at himself.

The two Wills overlapped and separated. One in the woods. One in the clearing. One stolen. One ashamed.

Mike turned left.

"Visual," Joel said. "You don't have him."

Mike stopped again.

Almost. Always almost.

The brush ahead opened without wind.

Not wide. Not like a door. The alder stems leaned apart in a shallow breathing motion, and behind them something pale moved low to the ground, or high and bent, or neither. Joel saw wet hide, or water on wood. He saw river mud on something that was not a bank. He saw a long blackness where an animal's shoulder might have been, then no shoulder, only spruce shadow closing over the shape it had offered. The smell came next: mud, old meat, wet fur, and the mineral cold of channel water after sunset.

Mike fired Alex's bear spray.

The orange mist burst into the brush and came back on the damp air. Joel's eyes stung at once. His throat closed. Will coughed from the clearing and bent over his injured hand. Mike shouted, not a word but a refusal, and drove another burst into the moving alder. The can hissed in his hand. For half a second he looked like exactly the man they needed: feet set, arm straight, doing the practical thing with the practical tool Alex had never used.

For one wild second the world became almost ordinary. Bear spray. Brush. Men coughing. An animal charge. A bad but known story.

Then Mike's voice came from behind them, at the cabin.

"We're exactly where we ought to be."

Mike looked back.

The impact took him sideways.

Joel did not see the body of the thing. He saw Mike's feet leave the ground. He saw the bear spray canister spin out of his hand and strike stone with a flat metallic crack. He saw alder whip and close. He heard Mike say, "Jesus," in a voice emptied of command, a voice that belonged to pain before pain had language.

Then the woods folded over him.

Will screamed his name.

The sound tore out once and stopped. He clapped his good hand over his mouth as if he could shove the name back inside. His eyes were huge behind his glasses, wet from the spray and from something that had arrived too fast to be grief yet.

Joel moved forward.

He moved two steps because Mike had been there. Because the PFD had been orange. Because a living man did not disappear from ten yards away while two other living men watched.

Something thrashed ahead, lower than where Mike's shoulders should have been. A branch snapped with the clean sound of bone, or a branch. Mike grunted once. Not a word. A wet drag followed, leaves dragged through leaves, weight through alder.

"Mike," Joel said.

Will made a strangled sound behind him.

From the brush, Mike answered, in Mike's exact anger, "Just keep moving."

Joel froze.

The words were too perfect. That was how he knew.

Not because a dying man could not have said them. Men reached for old sentences when new pain gave them no language. But this came from the wrong place, shoulder height, several yards left of the drag, clean through the brush with no blood in it and no breath broken by ribs or mud. It was Mike as instruction, not Mike as body.

Joel backed up.

Every cell in him chose backward. He hated himself while doing it and kept doing it.

Will met him halfway across the clearing with the hatchet in his good hand. Joel did not know when he had picked it up. The blade looked small and wet and useless.

"Where is he?"

Joel shook his head.

"Where is he?"

Joel put one hand on Will's chest and pushed him back.

Will resisted. Not much. Enough to make the hatchet wobble between them.

"That's Mike," Will said.

Joel shook his head again.

"You don't know."

Joel did know. Not enough for police. Not enough for a ranger with a clipboard. Not enough for Mike's sister, or whoever would be called when three men became one report. Enough for the body. Enough to know tone could be pulled over absence.

From the old road came Mike's throat-clear.

Will flinched.

Then Mike's voice, rougher now: "Joel."

It used his name.

Joel's hand tightened on Will's jacket.

There were many ways to be made helpless. One was not knowing. Another was knowing exactly what was being done to you and still feeling the hook enter. Mike had said Joel's name for years in annoyance, in argument, across camp, across water, from truck beds and kitchens and cheap motels. The sound had history in it.

"Joel," Mike said again from the brush.

Will whispered, "Don't."

Joel looked at him.

Will's lips were pressed white. He had given the warning to Joel, maybe to himself. For once, Will had the right word and no explanation after it.

The brush thrashed once more. Farther away this time. Then quiet.

Rain worked through the clearing. A drop gathered on Joel's chin and fell. His eyes burned from the spray drifting back. He wiped them with his sleeve and made the sting worse.

Inside the cabin, the buckle clicked again.

No one turned.

"We have to get him," Will said.

The sentence came out flat. Not argument. Obligation making one last attempt to sound like a thing men could still obey.

Joel took the notebook from his pocket. His hands shook enough that the pencil tore the damp page.

NO VISUAL.

Will stared at it.

"I know we don't have visual."

Joel wrote:

WE GO IN, IT GETS TWO.

Will's face twisted. For a moment Joel thought he would hit him. Maybe he should have. A hit would have belonged to the world of bodies. It would have been an answer a man could understand.

"You don't get to make that math," Will said.

Joel had no defense. The math had made itself. It stood in the clearing with them, ugly and correct.

From the brush, Mike's voice said, "Will?"

Will closed his eyes.

The thing had learned smallness from them too. Not tenderness. Volume.

"Will?" Mike said.

Will bent forward as if the name had struck him low in the stomach.

Joel grabbed his jacket with both hands.

Will did not fight him. That frightened Joel more than resistance would have. Will only stood there, hatchet hanging, his whole body leaned toward the trees.

"He'd come for us," Will said.

He meant the Mike who could poison an evening with certainty and still get up first in cold rain to fix someone else's bad knot. He meant all the years in which coming for each other had been assumed.

Joel wrote:

NOT HIM.

Will read it and made a sound like there was something caught in his throat.

"You don't know."

Joel underlined NOT HIM until the page began to shred.

They stood at the cabin doorway until the light went.

This was not waiting in any useful sense. It was failure given duration. They did not go inside because inside meant admitting they would not run into the brush. They did not go into the brush because going meant giving Mike's death company. They stood where the clearing narrowed into old road and listened to a man who was not there use what remained of a man who was.

Once, from far left, Mike said, "I'm okay."

He had never said it that way in life. That almost helped.

Once, very close to the road, Alex laughed, and Will covered his ears before lowering his hands again because covered ears meant not hearing what might matter.

Once, from inside the cabin, a tent zipper moved. There was no tent set up inside.

Joel kept writing because writing was an action and because the page could hold what his head could not.

MIKE TAKEN OLD ROAD EDGE.

ALEX SPRAY LOST.

FALSE MIKE USED: WE'RE FINE / JUST KEEP MOVING / JOEL / WILL.

REAL MIKE LAST SEEN AT ALDER.

He paused before the last line.

He did not want to write died. Alex had a covered body and a marked place. Mike had brush and sound and a hole where sight had been. But not writing it did not leave Mike living.

ASSUME MIKE DEAD.

Will saw the line and turned away.

Dark came by inches and then all at once. The clearing lost its low green. The cabin became a box of black corners. The old cans in the grass disappeared first, then the road crown, then the nearest spruce trunks. Only the pale ruin of the sky remained between treetops, and that too was closing.

"We call once," Will said.

Joel shook his head.

"Once."

Joel shook his head harder.

Will looked at him with a hatred so clean it almost looked like strength. "I can't sit here and not call once."

Joel wanted to say that calling was not mercy if the thing could wear mercy afterward. He wanted to say Mike had already answered too many things. He wanted to say every name in the group as if names could hold men in place.

Instead he wrote:

IF WE CALL, IT HAS THAT TOO.

Will took the pencil from him.

His bandaged hand fumbled, and the letters came out uneven.

THEN WE CALL BADLY.

Joel looked at him.

Will's mouth trembled once and stopped. "Together," he whispered. "Flat. No names."

It was a terrible idea. It was also the only mercy they could make small enough to survive.

Joel nodded.

They stood shoulder to shoulder at the clearing edge. Will held the hatchet. Joel held the notebook and the broken pencil. The cabin breathed cold behind them. The trees ahead gave them wet dark.

Together, in voices as low and flat as they could make them, they called, "Answer if seen."

The phrase sounded absurd. Bureaucratic. Inhuman. The kind of thing no friend should have to say in woods after dark.

No one answered.

They waited.

Nothing living answered.

Then from far up the old road, Mike's voice said, pleasantly, "Answer if seen."

Will folded at the waist.

Joel put a hand between his shoulder blades and felt the fine shaking there. He did not know whether it was grief or cold. The body did not separate them anymore. Will did not pull away.

Something dragged in the brush beyond the road. Not toward them. Not away in a straight line. Sideways through alder, heavy enough to move stems, slow enough to sound deliberate whether or not anything deliberate stood behind it. Joel listened for Mike in it and heard only weight.

After that, the woods went quiet.

They backed into the cabin before full dark took the threshold. Backed, because turning away from the clearing felt like giving it permission. Will went first, still facing out until his heel struck the table leg. Joel followed, one hand on the doorframe, eyes on the road gap.

They moved the table back against the door. Not fully. Enough. The door did not latch. The table did not make it secure. The act mattered because bodies needed tasks to keep from spending themselves all at once.

Inside smelled of wet wood, mouse, bear spray blown back on their clothes, and the sour salt of fear. The pot rang under the leak. The sound made Joel flinch every time, and every time he forced himself to see the drop fall before he let the sound exist.

Will sank against the back wall.

The hatchet slipped from his hand and struck the floor.

Joel looked at it. Hatchet. Will. Floor. Source confirmed. He picked it up and set it beside Will's knee, handle first, where Will could reach it without looking.

Will's face was wet. He was crying without sound, the tears running straight down into the dirt beside his mouth. He did not cover his face. Maybe he had run out of hands. Maybe he knew that any hand over his eyes was another way to lose sight.

Joel sat across from him and wrote by the last usable light from the window gap.

DOOR BLOCKED.

NO OUTSIDE.

MIKE DEAD VOICE ACTIVE.

KEY UNKNOWN.

The last line stopped him.

He looked at Will.

Will read it and closed his eyes.

The Highlander sat at North Road Bridge in Joel's mind with cruel clarity. Gray or green depending on the light. A cracked dashboard. Mike's canoe straps in the back. An old gas receipt jammed in the console. A vehicle so ordinary that it had become the shape of rescue.

A key made that shape usable. Without it, the Highlander was another cabin: real, human-made, locked against them.

"Maybe it stays on him," Will whispered.

Joel glared.

Will wiped his nose with his sleeve and nodded, ashamed and stubborn both. "I know."

Outside, at the threshold, something small struck wood.

Tick.

Not a buckle. Not a branch. A hard little sound, bright even through the door.

Neither of them moved.

The sound came again. A scrape this time, metal or plastic dragged half an inch over the sill.

Will's eyes went to Joel.

Joel shook his head.

From outside, Mike cleared his throat.

The sound was so close it might have been made by a man standing with his mouth against the door.

Then Mike said, "Key."

Will pressed both hands over his mouth.

Joel wrote:

BAIT.

The pencil point broke on the period he did not need.

The object at the threshold shifted again.

Tick.

Tick.

They waited. The rain softened. The pot rang. A mouse moved in the wall, then stopped.

Joel could not leave the key outside all night if it was the key.

That was the problem. Some bait was real and still bait.

Joel slid the table back an inch.

Will shook his head hard.

Joel held up one finger. Then pointed to his own eyes. Then to Will. Watch me.

Will took the hatchet again and stood. His legs shook. He moved to where he could see the gap beside the door, the room, and Joel's shoulders all at once.

Joel crouched. He moved the table only as far as his forearm needed. The door opened a hand's width. Cold air entered with the smell of wet grass and spruce. The opening made every part of him want to stand, push, run, make a whole action out of the half action. He stayed low.

The key lay just outside the threshold. Not in the room. Not far enough away to excuse leaving it. Close enough that reaching would put his wrist in the dark.

The Toyota fob was mud-slick and absurdly domestic, black plastic with worn buttons and a metal key folded into it. A torn strip of orange nylon clung to the ring. The zipper pull from Mike's left PFD pocket hung there too, bent open, its teeth still holding two threads of orange fabric.

Joel stared at it.

He had the insane thought that Mike would be furious about the zipper. Mike cared about gear even when gear deserved no care. He would complain about waterproof pockets that were not waterproof, about manufacturers making everything worse and charging more for it. The thought was so accurate that Joel almost made a sound.

He reached through the gap and took the key with two fingers.

Nothing grabbed him.

Nothing moved in the clearing that he could see.

That was worse than a grab would have been. The woods had placed the thing they needed at the line and let need do the work.

Joel shut the door and shoved the table back.

Will held out his good hand. Joel put the key in his palm for one second, long enough for both of them to confirm it by sight and touch. The fob left a wet black mark on Will's skin. Then Joel took it back and zipped it inside his inner jacket pocket.

He wrote:

KEY RECOVERED. MIKE LEFT PFD POCKET TORN. JOEL INNER JACKET ZIP.

Will read the line and began to cry harder, still without voice.

Joel wanted to join him. His body wanted it with a force that frightened him. Crying made sound. Grief made sound. Every human thing left in them wanted a mouth.

From beyond the clearing, Mike said, "We're fine."

Joel closed his hand around the key through his jacket until the plastic hurt him.

The voice came again, farther off now, receding into the old road or pretending to.

"It opens up after this."

Will folded down beside the wall, both hands over his ears now, rule or no rule. Joel let him. There were limits to what one body could be asked to hold upright.

Joel stayed by the door.

He watched the black line where the frame failed to meet the floor. Rain worked under it in a thin shine. The key pressed into his palm. The notebook lay open on his knee, the last written lines swelling in the damp.

He added one more line.

MIKE VOICE NOT PROOF.

Outside, Mike cleared his throat.

Not close this time. Somewhere past the cabin, past the road edge, past where sight could do any work.

"Just keep moving," Mike said.

Joel did not move.
