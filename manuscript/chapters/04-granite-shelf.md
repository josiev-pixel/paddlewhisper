# Chapter 4 - Granite Shelf

The granite point was empty in the way a campsite could be empty. No tent. No smoke. No voices carrying over the water. Still, the place had marks before they reached it.

Mike saw it first and lifted his paddle. The gesture was small and certain, blade angled toward the left shore where pale rock came down in shelves through jack pine and black spruce. A few red pines stood higher than the rest, long-trunked and clean-limbed, catching the last good light in their tops. Below them, the point sloped into water dark enough to double the trees.

"Camp," Mike called.

Nobody argued.

They had been paddling long enough that even Alex let the word stand by itself. The afternoon had gone soft and slanted. The lake was not wide, but the wind had found its length and pushed little black wrinkles toward the boats. The work was not hard anymore. It was just constant. Paddle, correct, watch Mike's boat, keep Alex moving, keep the front from wandering toward shore.

Joel felt the day in his shoulders. Not pain yet. A thickening. His hands had gone soft between trips and were relearning friction. The place between thumb and forefinger burned when he shifted his grip. His hiking sandals were wet, then drying, then wet again, and his feet had the swollen, gritty feel they got after a day in water and mud. At home, effort had become calendar work, a thing arranged around calls and dinners and whatever needed signing. Here it had gone back into skin.

Alex turned halfway around.

"That real camp or Mike camp?"

"What's Mike camp?"

"Any place Mike says is fine because he no longer wants to look."

Joel glanced past him toward the point. There was a break in the trees, a flat place above the rock, and a dark line where people had dragged boats before. "Looks real."

"God exists."

"You don't believe that."

"I believe in flat ground."

Ahead, Mike and Will angled in first. Will had his paddle across his lap and his fishing rod already in one hand, as if the day might otherwise forget his priorities. Mike said something Joel could not make out. Will said something back. Mike pointed to the landing with more force than the situation required.

Joel brought their canoe around the lee side of the point. The water quieted near shore. Granite showed under it in gray plates with seams of weed between them. He let the boat glide until the front touched stone.

Alex looked down. "Elegant."

"Out."

"I'm appreciating."

"Appreciate from shore."

Alex stepped carefully, one sandal on slick rock, one foot still in the canoe. He held the position for half a second too long, arms spread.

"Don't do a full performance," Joel said.

"I'm giving the landing what it deserves."

"You're giving me a problem."

Alex shifted his weight, got both feet on rock, and held the canoe while Joel stepped out. The hull bumped once against the granite. Hollow. Familiar. Nothing like the knock from the channel behind them and enough like it that Joel disliked himself for making the comparison.

He pulled the canoe up higher.

Mike was already unloading.

"Boats above the waterline," he said. "Wind shifts tonight, I don't want to hear them banging all night."

"You don't want to hear anything all night," Will said.

"Correct."

Alex dragged a pack onto the rock. "Is this where I ask if we can sit down?"

"No," Mike said.

"Good. I won't."

"You just did."

"I asked if this was where."

Joel lifted his day bag and set it near the first flat place above the landing. The campsite was better than he expected. A granite shelf with enough dirt pockets for tents, two good trees back from the fire ring, a pair of red pines on the water side, and a rough bench made from a log dragged against stone. Someone had left a ring of blackened rocks and a half-burned split log. A faint trail led back into spruce where previous campers had gone for latrine privacy or firewood or both. Small limbs had been cut and stacked under a rock overhang. Old, damp, probably smoky, but still wood.

There were signs of people and not much else. A twist of blue cord tied to a branch. A square of foil half-buried in ash. Boot prints softened by rain and time. One rusted bottle cap wedged in a crack. Out here, human presence did not claim a place. It left small failures and asked the next men to call them proof.

Joel had always found comfort in that, which seemed foolish when named. The ring of stones. The hacked branch. The scrap of cord another party had abandoned because it was too short to be useful and too useful, at the moment, to throw away properly. Someone had stood here cold or hungry or damp and had made these same arrangements. The place had accepted them without promising anything. Still, the arrangements remained.

"Not bad," Will said.

Mike cleared his throat, the small dry preface he used before making a fact out of a preference. "It opens up after this."

Will turned. "What does?"

"Tomorrow. After the next narrow section. More room, better water."

"You said that about today."

"And was I wrong?"

Will looked at the lake, the rock, the trees, the reasonable camp they had reached with enough daylight to unload like civilized men. "Annoyingly, not completely."

Alex raised a finger. "Put that on his memorial shirt."

"Everyone's obsessed with my memorial shirt," Mike said.

"You make people imagine it," Will said.

They unloaded everything before doing anything comfortable. That was Mike's rule and, worse, a good one. Packs came out. Rods came out. Paddles went under the pulled-up canoes, tucked out of the way and off the grit. Mike took off his PFD last, pressed his thumb once against the small yellow pouch clipped inside the left side, then hung it over a low branch where he could see it from the fire ring. The Highlander key belonged to the far end of the route. Mike kept bringing that shore forward by touch.

The food barrel was carried to the middle of camp and then moved because Mike said the middle of camp was where idiots put food. Will opened it anyway to get the dinner bag. The latch made its clean plastic snap, small and final. Joel put the sound with the rest of camp: hull on stone, rope on bark, zipper teeth, water moving below them. Even plastic carried.

He set his pack near the two trees he liked and looked out over the lake instead of opening it. The far shore was darkening. Sun still held in the pine tops, but the water had lost its brightness and now seemed to store the shade coming off the trees. A loon called from the north, long and hollow. Another answered, or the first call came back broken by distance. The notes overlapped for a second and made the lake feel larger than it was.

Then the lake went quiet in the way water could, not silent but refusing to explain the sounds it had just carried.

"You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel turned.

Alex stood with a bundle of tent poles over one shoulder and the tent bag under his arm, smiling at his own usefulness.

"Neither if I can avoid it," Joel said.

"You looked deeply employed."

"I was waiting for you to do something correctly."

Alex handed him the tent bag. "Then your vigil ends."

They pitched the tent before the mosquitoes took full possession of camp. Mike and Alex took it. Joel and Will both had hammocks. Joel had been camping in a hammock almost exclusively the last few seasons, partly because he slept better off the ground and partly because he liked having a system that was his alone. Straps, suspension, tarp, underquilt if weather wanted to be rude. Tree to tree. Line tight. Sag right. Tarp pitched low enough to matter.

The arrangement gave him something close to privacy without requiring a conversation.

Will had brought a hammock too, but mostly for lounging. He still slept in the tent more often than not unless the weather was perfect or he had talked himself into an experiment. Will liked owning options. Joel liked eliminating them.

Mike watched Joel wrap a tree strap around a red pine. "You actually sleeping in that thing?"

"Yes."

"All week?"

"That's the plan."

"Plans are how men experience hope," Alex said.

Will looked over. "That was mine."

"You left it unattended."

Joel clipped the carabiner and pulled the suspension through. The webbing made a dry scrape against bark.

At home, this kind of system annoyed Mara if he talked about it too long. She liked competence fine. She had liked the hammock too, once, on a state-park weekend when rain trapped them under its tarp and they passed a dented thermos back and forth while other campers fled to cars. They had laughed at the steam on each other's faces and at the ridiculous closeness of the rig, two adults pretending a nylon roof was a room. What she disliked was the private satisfaction he took in having one more way not to need a room, a bed, a decision made with another person. Joel had argued that a hammock was only a hammock, and even while saying it had known he was lying by scale, not by kind.

Mike shook his head. "Ground exists for a reason."

"So do rocks," Joel said. "I don't sleep on those either."

Will had his own hammock half out of its sack and was looking at the trees behind camp with a fisherman's version of commitment, which meant one eye was still on the water.

"You setting yours up?" Joel asked.

"Probably for sitting."

"You brought a chair."

"I contain multitudes."

"You contain redundant systems."

"That's preparedness."

The old Will would have made a larger case for it. He would have explained load redundancy or failure points or how most people confused comfort with utility. This Will shook the hammock once and frowned at a tangle in the suspension. He had been quieter since lunch in brief, uneven patches, as if his talk had to climb a small rise before it could get out.

"I keep thinking I forgot to check something," Will said.

"You did," Joel said. "Your judgment."

"No, a real thing."

Will pulled the wrong loop tight and made the knot worse. He looked at it as if it had personally changed the terms of the agreement.

Joel knew about the job because Will had told him in pieces before the trip, over texts and one late call when he had made jokes about severance until the jokes ran out. Quality control for an LED company was not a thing Joel had imagined anyone losing sleep over until Will lost it. Here, in camp, Will still had the shape of a man who knew how things were supposed to work. Filters. Knots. Rod guides. Maps. He could find the failed part in any system small enough to hold in his hands. The larger one had let him go without showing its work.

Mike and Alex argued their tent into position. The ground looked flatter than it was. Every place that seemed level had a root, a knob of rock, or a shallow runnel where rain would collect if the weather changed.

"Door facing water," Alex said.

"Door out of the wind," Mike said.

"I want a view."

"You'll be unconscious."

"I dream visually."

Mike stared at him.

Will, crouched over his hammock sack, said, "Put his side facing the water. If a bear comes, it gets him first."

Alex nodded. "Finally. Logic."

Joel fed one guyline around a trunk and tied it off. The line tightened with a small creak. Behind him, Mike zipped the tent door open, then closed it, testing it the way men tested zippers in the woods: not because they distrusted the zipper exactly, but because a zipper that failed at night became a moral problem.

The zipper ran around the curve with a dry insect sound.

Mike looked at Alex. "Don't yank on it."

"I have used zippers before."

"Not always well."

Alex spread his hands. "Don't babysit the zipper."

Joel glanced over.

The line got the little laugh it wanted, then disappeared into the general work of camp. That was how most of Alex moved through the world: given away fast, trusting there would be more.

They staked the tent, tightened lines, and set sleeping pads inside. The pads squeaked and sighed as they unrolled. Joel shook out his hammock, clipped it, sat once to set the sag, and stood again. He pitched the tarp above it even though the sky was clear. Early September could change its mind without warning, and the first night of any trip was not the night to trust a blue sky.

Will wandered toward the water with his rod before deciding whether he was sleeping in nylon or in the air.

Mike saw him. "Water first."

"I'm at the water."

"For drinking."

Will stopped, considered whether he could argue it, and came back for the filter.

They divided chores without formally dividing them. Mike and Will handled water because Will had opinions about filtering and Mike had opinions about where the bag should hang. Joel handled dinner because he usually did. He had a dehydrator at home and had become the kind of man who could talk about sauce leather without shame. Jerky at a hundred sixty for five or six hours. Sauce lower and longer. Sweet potatoes if he remembered to slice them evenly.

The others made fun of him until they ate what he brought. Then they asked how much was left.

Tonight was dried tortellini with Joel's dehydrated red sauce, plus parmesan packets and a little oil. Easy first-night food. No fish required, no coals, no timing beyond boil, soak, simmer, stir. The more ambitious meals could wait: quesadillas, lentils and yams, fish fry if the lake gave them enough, mac and cheese with bacon when morale needed to be purchased cheaply.

Joel set the sauce pieces in a pot with water to soften. They looked like dark red bark.

Alex came over and peered in. "That food?"

"Eventually."

"It looks like something peeled off a canoe."

"You'll like it."

"You always say that."

"I'm often right."

Will called from the water, "The jerky's already open, by the way."

Joel turned. "It was sealed."

"Was."

Mike came up from shore chewing. "Good batch."

"You opened my dinner-adjacent morale system?"

"It was labeled jerky."

"That is not consent."

Alex walked to the food bag with sudden interest. "Is this the Doc's stuff?"

"Doc's-ish," Joel said. "I changed it."

Mike took another piece. "Don't change it more."

Will appeared with the gravity bag, water dripping from one elbow. "Agreed. This is one of your useful traits."

Joel looked at him. "My useful trait is meat dehydration?"

"One of them."

"I'll update the wedding vows."

Alex bit into a piece of jerky and nodded with genuine seriousness. "Okay. That's good. That has appropriate intensity."

"Food can have opinions now?" Joel said.

"Jerky is allowed. Tuna is not."

Alex went to the rock overhang and came back with an armload of the cut sticks, some damp, some dry enough to matter. He made a show of staggering under them, but he had sorted the good pieces to the top without saying so. The performance hid work. It did not cancel it.

The stove came out when the water was ready. Mike set it on a flat patch of granite near the fire ring, screwed the fuel canister in, and turned the valve. The first click did nothing. The second click did nothing. The third click gave a blue flutter of flame.

"Modern civilization," Alex said.

"Don't get attached," Mike said.

Will hung the gravity bag from a low limb. Water ticked through the filter into the clean bag, one patient drop after another. "That limb is sketchy."

"It's holding," Mike said.

"Lots of things hold until they don't."

"Put that on your own memorial shirt," Alex said.

Dinner took its time coming back to life. Joel stirred the sauce as it softened and reddened the water. The smell came up slowly: tomato, garlic, dried herbs, a faint sweetness from hours in the dehydrator. Not home exactly. A camp version of home, thinner and smokier and more valuable because it had crossed water in a sealed bag.

The mosquitoes came hard at dusk.

They came out of the spruce and up from the damp places between rocks, first a few, then a whining net around ears and wrists. The air had been comfortable and then it was not. Everyone put on long sleeves. Will found a head net and wore it like a man proving a point. Alex mocked him until one mosquito got him on the eyelid.

"Head net me," Alex said.

Will looked at him through mesh. "No."

"I apologize to the technology."

"Too late."

Joel poured the sauce over the tortellini and stirred. The pot got passed around with bowls. Mike ate first because he had cooked the noodles and because he was holding the spoon when it became food. Alex approached his bowl with caution and then ate quickly, which was the highest compliment he gave anything with visible seasoning.

"Too much?" Will asked.

Alex shook his head. "No. This has discipline."

"Food review accepted," Joel said.

Mike scraped his bowl. "Make more next time."

"I made enough."

"You made enough for the men you imagined us to be."

Will said, "We also have tuna and Triscuits if you want to ruin your own evening."

"Lunch food stays lunch food," Mike said.

Alex took another piece of jerky. "Unless society collapses."

"Day one," Joel said.

"Plenty of time."

They cleaned bowls with hot water and scraps of tortilla because Will claimed that was efficient and Mike claimed it was disgusting until he did it too. The stove cooled on granite. Someone set a mug down beside Joel and it made a small ceramic tick, bright against the lower water sounds.

"Careful," Joel said. "That one's mine."

Alex glanced at it. "I know. It looks judgmental."

"Everything I own can't be judgmental."

"I have evidence."

After dinner, Will went back to the shore with his rod. Nobody told him not to. Everyone knew by now that Will would fish if there was water, light, and enough motor function left in his arms. It was less a choice than a tide.

The lake darkened by degrees. Across the water, the opposite shore went from green to black. The first stars appeared between the pine tops before the sky was fully night. The chill came up from the stone. It entered through damp sandal straps and wet cuffs and the places where sweat had dried into salt.

Will cast from the edge of the granite shelf. The lure made a small whip in the air and a softer sound when it touched water. He reeled slowly, patiently, his head net pushed back now that the bugs had thinned in the open breeze.

Alex sat near the fire ring with his fleece zipped to his chin. "You actually enjoy that?"

"Yes."

"You know there are fish at stores."

"They're not surprised to see you."

"That's the appeal?"

Will cast again. "Part of it."

Mike had built a small fire because the ring existed, the wood was there, and the evening had dropped cold enough to justify flame. It was not much. Thumb-thick sticks, pine curls, a few split pieces from the dry stack. The hatchet bit twice into one stubborn piece before Mike gave up and fed it half-burned ends from the old ring. The fire took with a low crackle, then settled into itself.

Alex came alive in firelight.

He could not help it. The day's fatigue left him, or else the fire gave him a place to spend it. He told the story of a backpacking trip in the Cascades where a friend had packed six pounds of cheese and no rain jacket. He told it too long and too well, with voices, pauses, and a whole middle section about the cheese sweating through a stuff sack. It should have been cut. Nobody cut it because everyone was tired and the fire was warm and Will, despite himself, kept asking questions.

"Six pounds?" Will said.

"Minimum."

"That's not physically plausible."

"It was a brick. A dairy ingot."

"What kind of cheese?"

"Orange."

"That's not a kind."

"It was by day three."

Joel watched Alex hold them there. Even Mike listened, though he pretended not to by using a stick to worry the fire. Alex had a talent for making annoyance indistinguishable from attention. He required a room, a landing, a fire ring. He made you give it to him, and sometimes the gift became real after you had surrendered it.

Mike sat back against a log. "People don't know how to be uncomfortable anymore."

The line went into the small space around the fire and stayed there.

Will looked at him. "People who pack six pounds of cheese know discomfort intimately."

"That's not what I mean."

"I know what you mean."

"You don't."

"I do. You mean everybody's soft except whoever happens to be talking."

Mike's jaw moved once. "I mean people want the story without the part where things suck."

Alex opened his mouth, then closed it. For once, the story had been taken from him and used for something heavier.

Joel fed a stick into the fire and let the end catch. The remark was not about cheese. It was about the emails required to arrange four men in one place. Jobs. Partners. Houses. Knees. Money. The little negotiations by which adulthood took a man back one weekend at a time. It was also about Mike, whose life still fit in other people's basements, spare rooms, and borrowed driveways when the teardrop camper was cold. Hardness gave need a better posture.

Will said, quieter, "There's plenty of discomfort."

Mike snorted. "Sure."

The word found Will more directly than Mike seemed to intend, or exactly as directly. Will looked at the fire. In the orange light the hollows under his eyes showed darker. Joel thought of the late call again, Will laughing too hard about being between opportunities, about LinkedIn turning everyone into a motivational hostage, about knowing every failed diode on a board no one wanted anymore. The fire made his face look briefly older, then briefly like the boy Joel remembered from church basements and bad coffee, correcting adults under his breath because nobody else had noticed the thing that was wrong.

Joel waited for Will to answer. Will did not.

The fire settled, and one piece of pine clicked as sap heated inside it.

Alex leaned forward before the silence hardened.

"Speaking as a man who voluntarily packed powdered eggs after the Incident," he said, "I have known the valley of suffering."

Will coughed a laugh.

"We all agreed never again," Joel said.

"They were not eggs," Mike said.

"They were egg-adjacent," Will said.

"They were yellow punishment."

Alex nodded gravely. "We learned. We grew. We became oatmeal men."

"Pancakes tomorrow if morale requires civilization," Will said.

Mike looked at him. "Pancakes take time."

"Joy takes time."

"Breakfast bars take thirty seconds."

"Despair also takes thirty seconds."

Joel smiled and looked out toward the water.

The canoes were pulled high above shore, turned slightly on the slope so rain would not sit in them if weather came. The paddles lay underneath, blades tucked out of the open, shafts parallel in the shadows. One painter rope hung loose and touched the hull when the breeze moved it. Tap. Pause. Tap. Ordinary enough to vanish unless a man had already begun keeping track.

Will came back from the shore without a fish and pretended not to care.

"Nothing?" Mike said.

"I had one follow."

"Of course you did."

"It's a real phenomenon."

"So is lying."

Will set the rod against a tree and took his mug from the rock. Ceramic on granite. A bright little sound. He drank and winced. "Cold."

"Drink while it's hot," Mike said.

"I can't drink it while it's hot if I'm fishing."

"That sounds like a you problem."

"You can't just reassign physics to character flaws."

"I can do whatever I want in camp."

Alex stood and stretched. His joints popped. "I'm going to offend a tree. Nobody make it literature."

Joel looked up. The line was aimed at him, so he let it miss.

"Take a headlamp," Mike said.

"It's ten feet."

"Take a headlamp."

Alex patted his pockets, found the lamp, and put it on without turning it on. "Quit mothering me."

He walked to the edge of camp, past the tent and toward the darker line of spruce. His sandals scraped once on rock, then went quiet on duff. A moment later his headlamp clicked on, throwing a white band through low branches.

From the trees came his laugh. Not loud. More private than usual. Maybe he had nearly tripped. Maybe he had amused himself. Then the light shifted, and he said something they could not make out.

Will looked toward him. "You fall in?"

"No," Alex called. "I'm communing."

"With what?" Mike asked.

"None of your business."

"Don't wander."

"Quit-"

"We heard it," Will said.

Alex laughed again. The laugh came through spruce this time, broken by trunks and needles, not by motel wall or bridge concrete or bar noise. It sounded like him and less like him for the way the woods handled it. A familiar thing could survive distance and still come back thinned.

Joel turned his mug in his hands.

The lake gave a soft slap against the rocks below camp. Somewhere far off, a loon called once. Then again, too soon or only seeming too soon because the first note had not fully left Joel's ear. The second call bent thinner and lower, not wrong, only badly returned.

Will stopped with his mug halfway to his mouth.

Mike said, "Loon."

"I know," Will said.

Nobody said anything else.

Alex came back a minute later, headlamp still on, beam hitting their faces until Mike told him to point it down. The ordinary world returned with him: fleece, sandals, bad peanut-butter breath, the need to brush teeth and secure food and keep sparks out of nylon. His body in the light gave the laugh back its owner.

They cleaned camp.

The food barrel was latched and carried back from the tent and hammocks, not far enough to satisfy a ranger and far enough to satisfy tired men. Mike wanted it wedged between two rocks where it could not roll. Will wanted it farther downwind. Joel helped move it once and then stopped helping the argument. The latch clicked again when Mike checked it.

Dry bags went under the tent vestibule or under Joel's hammock tarp. Paddles stayed stacked under the canoes. The stove cooled on granite. Someone's mug tipped and rolled until Joel caught it with his foot. Toothpaste spit flashed pale on the edge of the firelight and vanished into dirt.

The fire burned low. Mike poured a little water over one edge, then stopped because there was still heat enough to sit with. Steam rose and carried the smell of wet ash. The night pressed in around the small ring of men and gear.

When Joel finally went to his hammock, the zipper on his bug net sounded louder than it had in daylight. He slid in carefully, felt the fabric take his weight, and lay still while the hammock found its shape around him. The tarp above him was a low dark roof. Beyond its edge he could see a strip of sky between pine branches.

Will came over and stood near his own half-hung hammock, looking between it and the tent.

"You actually committing?" Joel asked.

Will looked at the trees. Then at the tent. Then at the lake, where a ring spread and vanished. "No."

"Coward."

"Strategist."

Will gathered his quilt and went to the tent. Mike, without looking up from the fire, said, "Left side's flatter."

Will paused with one hand on the zipper. "That an order?"

"It's a fact. Do what you want with it."

Will ducked inside, bringing cold air and the smell of lake water when he opened the door. Mike and Alex were still talking in low voices near the fire. Mike said something about tomorrow's first narrow section. Alex answered with a wordless sound of disbelief.

Will whispered from inside the tent, "He's going to push early."

"Mike?" Joel said.

"No, the loon."

Joel smiled in the dark.

"He wants to make the big lake before wind," Will said.

"That bad?"

"No. Maybe. It's fine if we get there clean."

"Fine according to you or fine according to Mike?"

Will was quiet a moment. "Exactly."

Joel listened. Fire ticking. Water against rock. The faint scrape of Mike's log shifting under weight. Alex's voice, muffled by nylon and distance, telling the end of something he had already told once.

Then silence came down, not all at once, but by layers.

Will stopped talking. Mike and Alex put out the fire. Their tent zipper opened and closed. A headlamp beam crossed Joel's tarp and disappeared. The food barrel latch clicked once more, or Joel thought it did, though it could have been a buckle on Mike's pack. Mike's PFD hung dark on its branch, the yellow pouch invisible now but still fixed in Joel's mind because he had seen Mike touch it.

He lay awake long enough for the first night to become itself. His body did not yet trust the hammock. His shoulder wanted to be elsewhere. His feet stayed cold in the damp socks he had meant to change earlier and had not. From the tent came Mike's heavier breathing, then Alex turning on a sleeping pad, then Will's small dry catch every few breaths. Each sound had a source. That was the comfort. A body, a zipper, a buckle, a rope, a hull. Joel could return a sound to its object and let it go.

He woke later without knowing why.

The hammock held him in a shallow curve. His shoulders ached. His feet were still cold. For a moment Joel did not move.

Outside, the lake was quiet. The camp had the dense stillness of first night, when every object was still in the place men had put it and had not yet become familiar enough to ignore.

From the shoreline came a soft knock.

One sound. Hollow. Wood or hull against stone. Low enough to belong to the canoes, the water below them, or a branch the lake had been worrying against rock all evening.

Joel opened his eyes fully.

He waited.

Nothing followed. No voice. No footstep. No second knock. Only the lake moving lightly below the granite and wind in the top of one pine.

He could not see the shoreline from the hammock. The tarp edge cut off the canoes and the darker slope below them, so every explanation had to be assembled from memory. He pictured the boats pulled high, angled on rock, paddles tucked beneath them, painter rope loose enough to touch. Wind could do it. Cooling hull could do it. Water lifting a branch could do it. There were a dozen ways for the night to make one ordinary sound, and one reliable way for a tired man to lose the rest of the night.

From the tent, Will breathed in and out. The dry catch came again, and then the cleaner breath after it.

Joel rolled slightly in the hammock, careful not to shift too hard and make the straps talk, and closed his eyes again. He did not sit up. If he sat up, the sound became an event. If he stayed still, it could remain weather, rope, branch.

The camp did not answer. Below it, the lake kept working lightly at the stone.

After a while, Joel slept.
