# Chapter 16 - The Cabin

No one moved toward the voice.

For a few seconds that counted as discipline. Then Joel realized none of them had breathed.

The false Mike did not repeat itself. It had come from outside the cabin, from the far side of the clearing or the old road beyond it, and then it had left the sentence hanging in the room. Map says there's a cabin. The words were still ordinary. Their ordinariness did the damage.

Rainwater dripped through the roof into a black pot on the floor. One drop, then another, each one making a thin metallic note. Somewhere in the wall a mouse worked at paper. The real Mike stood with the old map half folded in both hands, looking more exposed than Joel had ever seen him.

Will had one hand against the doorframe. His bandaged palm had bled through again, not dramatically, just enough red stippling to show through the dirty gauze. He looked at Mike, then at the opening behind Joel, then at Mike again.

"It heard me say that," Mike said.

His voice was low. Not denial. Inventory.

Will nodded too fast. "Yes."

"At the gas station."

"Yes."

"Or on the second day."

"Yes."

"Stop saying yes."

Will's face hardened. The old Will would have met that tone with something sharp enough to make the others laugh and make Mike mad. This Will only looked at him with blood in his gauze and mud dried in the lines beside his mouth. "I'm agreeing with you, you miserable--"

Joel lifted a hand between them.

The argument died because the cabin was too small to hold it. It had room for three men, one dead hope, and the sound of water collecting in a pot. There was no room for the kind of argument they used to have in a kitchen or on a gravel landing, the kind that could flare, spend itself, and leave everyone still alive.

Joel took out the notebook. The pages had begun to swell at the corners. The pencil dragged over damp paper.

FALSE MIKE: MAP SAYS THERE'S A CABIN.

He paused, then added:

REAL MIKE SEEN INSIDE.

Mike read it. His eyes stayed on the last line longer than the first. Joel thought of the motel room, of Mike standing over the maps with his throat-clear and certainty, turning a wilderness into a problem of lines and timing. It was not that Mike had been useless. That would have been easier. He had read water. He had found lost trail. He had kept them moving when stopping would have become another form of collapse. His competence had brought them here, and here was nothing.

Will looked around the cabin as if the room itself might now be shamed into producing a radio.

"Okay," he said. "So we search."

No one argued.

They searched because not searching would have been another kind of answer. Mike folded the map and put it in the cloudy case with unnecessary care. Joel saw his fingers not quite work on the snap. Cold, exhaustion, anger. The snap closed on the second try.

They began with the obvious places.

There was a shelf above the little stove. On it sat a rusted coffee can with no lid, three mouse-chewed packets of something that had once been instant soup, and a spool of picture wire gone green at the core. Will tipped the can with the back of his knuckle. Mouse droppings slid out, small and black and dry. He made a face but did not speak.

The stove was a square iron thing on four short legs, its door warped open a finger's width. Joel crouched and swung it carefully with the cracked paddle. A nest had been built inside from shredded paper, pink insulation, and needles. No ashes fresher than old gray dust. No matches. No dry kindling. A spider moved from the door hinge into shadow.

Under the table they found a plastic jug collapsed in on itself, a bent fork, an oil-stiff rag, and a cigarette lighter with the wheel fused in place. Mike tried it anyway. Once. Twice. The tiny scrape of flintless metal sounded obscene in the room.

Will flinched at the second scrape.

Mike saw him. "It's me."

Will held up his good hand. I know.

Joel wrote:

LIGHTER DEAD.

It looked stupidly formal on the page. As if this were an inspection. As if they could report the cabin for failing.

The names cut into the wall drew Joel's eye again. RAY 1998. STUMP CREW 04. KELLY. A heart carved too deep around three initials. Men and women had stood here with knives and time, believing a mark could hold them in a place. Or not believing it, only being young enough to cut wood because the wood was there. Joel imagined them entering wet and laughing, shaking rain from jackets, setting coffee on the table, complaining about blackflies and bad pay and somebody else's chainsaw work. The cabin had held their noise once. Now it held theirs.

Will moved to the back corner, where a warped plywood box lay under a fallen strip of ceiling. He pulled the strip aside with his foot. The plywood had swollen and delaminated, its layers opening like pages ruined by water.

"Could be a logbook," he said.

Mike looked at him.

Will's jaw tightened. "I'm not answering anything. I'm telling you what I'm seeing."

Joel almost said that telling was answering if there was something outside to hear. He did not. There were too many rules now and too little mercy in enforcing them all.

Will pried at the box. His cut hand made him clumsy. Joel crossed the room to help, and for a moment they worked shoulder to shoulder as they had in another life: a stuck cooler latch, a tire chain, a tent pole sleeve in rain. Will smelled of wet wool, old sweat, iodine, and the sourness of fear kept too long in the body. The lid came up with a soft tearing sound.

Inside were papers, but not a logbook. Permits, maybe. Carbon forms. A map brittle with mildew and eaten through at the folds. A printed sheet of radio frequencies so old the paper had browned at the edges. Columns of numbers. Call signs. The ghost of contact.

Mike took the sheet from Will.

For one second Joel saw the old current enter him. Numbers meant systems. Systems meant someone on the other end. Someone had once written this because communication had existed here. A human chain had run from this wet square to another room, a road, a desk, an agency that had once cared enough to nail a cabin onto the map.

Then Mike turned the page over.

The back was blank except for mouse stains.

"No radio," Will said.

Mike did not answer.

Joel wrote:

OLD FREQ SHEET. NO RADIO.

Will pointed at the map, or what remained of it. Joel laid it on the table. The paper came apart where his damp fingers touched the fold. It showed the old road in a thicker line than Mike's map did, a confident slash through contour and marsh, then a spur toward the cabin, then another line continuing north and east.

At the edge of the sheet, where the continuation mattered, the paper had gone soft and black. The road left the map into rot.

Will stared at it.

Joel knew what he wanted to say. The road existed here. The road might not exist there. There is a difference. Will's mouth worked around the distinction, the whole machinery of him straining toward speech because speech was how he made fear take shape.

He took the notebook instead.

ROAD ON OLD MAP CONTINUES.

Then beneath it, after a long pause:

PAPER FAILS WHERE NEEDED.

He pushed the notebook to Joel as if angry at the sentence for being true.

Mike read it. A muscle moved once in his cheek.

"We don't know it fails outside," he said.

Joel waited.

Mike heard himself. The sentence had crossed the room and become material. His hand closed around the frequency sheet until it buckled.

Will looked away first, which was almost kindness.

They finished the search with the same thoroughness they had brought to every camp chore because thoroughness was what was left. Joel checked behind the stove and under the table. Will lifted the broken chair and found only mouse pellets and a bottle cap. Mike worked along the back wall, testing loose boards with the paddle, as if a maintenance crew might have hidden a working hand radio under a panel chewed by mice.

The cabin gave up nothing.

No radio. No batteries. No first-aid kit. No flare. No tarp worth taking. No tool but a rusted file with the handle missing. No rope that would hold weight. No food. No recent tracks in the dirt floor beyond theirs. No ash new enough to hold shape. No sign that anyone had slept there this season or last season or in any season close enough to matter.

Outside, water dripped from the roof at the back corner into something metal. The same note as the pot inside, but lower. Two drips. Two little bells. Joel watched Mike hear it and look toward the wall.

Not now, Joel thought. Don't let even water become another message.

Mike stepped to the broken window and peered through the gap where the board had shrunk away from the frame. Late afternoon light sat low in the clearing. The grass had gone a flat green, each blade holding water. The old road beyond the cabin was a darker opening between young spruce, not a path so much as the idea of one.

"We need to see the road," Mike said.

Will laughed once.

It came out wrong. Not humor. The body making a sound because the mind had found no useful action.

Mike turned on him.

"What?"

Will rubbed his forehead with the heel of his good hand. "Nothing."

"Say it."

"No."

"You already made the noise."

"I said no."

The word sat between them. Small. Dangerous.

Joel wrote:

CHECK ROAD BY SIGHT ONLY. ALL THREE.

He held the notebook up before the argument could collect itself.

Mike read it and shook his head. "Will's hand is bleeding."

Will's face tightened. "My hand is attached."

"Someone stays here with packs."

Joel wrote over the empty lower half of the page, larger:

ALL THREE.

Mike stared at the words. "This isn't a vote."

Will said, "Good, because you're losing."

It would have been funny two days ago. Maybe even yesterday morning, if anything could have been funny then. Now the old rhythm surfaced, recognizable and useless. It could not carry them.

Mike opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked at the door, the clearing, the old road. He looked at the map case under his arm. Joel could see the argument happening in him: speed against rule, control against evidence, the contempt he had always had for committee decisions against the fact that every voice had become suspect except the ones attached to faces.

At last he pointed to himself, then to the road, then held up three fingers.

All three.

They left the packs inside except for water, bear spray, the notebook, Mike's map, and the small first-aid kit. Leaving gear in the cabin felt wrong. Carrying everything felt worse. The choice had no good shape. Joel watched Mike set his pack against the wall and then touch the left zip pocket of his PFD where the Highlander key sat. The gesture had become involuntary now, a little proof he could perform with his hand.

Joel wrote:

KEY STILL MIKE PFD LEFT ZIP.

Mike saw it and gave him a look.

"I know where my key is."

Joel did not answer. The page answered enough.

Outside, the clearing received them without change. No voice. No branch crack. No sudden movement at the edge. Grass dragged wet over Joel's shins. A blackfly worried at his ear, and he let it. The cabin behind them looked smaller from ten steps away, more like an abandoned object than a place. Its open door made a dark vertical shape in the wall. Their packs sat just inside, visible if he turned his head, which he did every few seconds.

Mike led, but slower than usual. Will walked in the middle with his injured hand tucked against his chest. Joel stayed close enough to touch Will's pack, close enough to see Mike's shoulder, close enough to see the cabin door behind them when the road bent only a little.

The old road north of the clearing had once been real. There was comfort in that and insult in it. Two shallow ditches ran on either side. A crown rose in the middle under moss and grass. Here and there gravel showed through, rounded and sunk deep. Then the young spruce began.

They had grown in the roadbed itself, not beside it. Knee-high first, then waist-high, then taller than Joel. Poplar saplings leaned across at face level. A jack pine had fallen lengthwise along one rut and become a nursery for moss. No tire had passed here in years. No four-wheeler. No maintenance crew. No human traffic heavy enough to keep light on the ground.

Mike stopped at the first wall of spruce and pushed through with the paddle.

"It continues," he said.

Joel looked at the trunk in the roadbed, the moss over old gravel, the darkening sky beyond the branches.

"Not today," he said.

Mike turned.

Joel regretted the sound as soon as he made it, but the words were not theory. They were weather and daylight and bodies. He took the notebook and wrote:

ROAD REAL / NOT PASSABLE BEFORE DARK.

Mike read it, then looked past the page as if distance might change out of embarrassment.

"You don't know that."

Joel wrote:

YOUNG TREES IN ROAD. LIGHT DROPPING. WILL HAND. NO SCOUTING.

Will leaned in to read. "Concise."

The word was too close to a joke. He winced after it.

From the spruce ahead, Mike's throat cleared.

Not words. Just the small rough sound Mike made before turning uncertainty into decision. It came from ten yards beyond the living Mike, from the road that could not be read past the young trees.

The living Mike stood beside Joel, close enough that Joel could see rain caught in his beard and a pale crescent of old sunscreen at the edge of one ear. His throat had not moved.

Will's eyes went wide behind his smeared glasses.

Then, from ahead, in Mike's voice, low and practical: "Just keep moving."

The phrase entered Joel's body before thought. It was an old instruction, useful on portages, useful in rain, useful when someone wanted to sit down in a place that would only make standing harder later. It had gotten them across wind and mud and beaver work. It was one of Mike's better tools, and the thing had taken it whole.

Mike took one step forward.

Joel caught the back strap of his PFD.

Mike swung around with real fury. His hand came up, not to strike, but hard enough that Will shifted back.

Joel held on.

He shook his head once.

Mike's face went red under the dirt. For a second Joel thought he would rip free out of pride alone. Then the false throat-clear came again from ahead. Softer this time. Almost thoughtful.

Mike heard it. Hearing himself stopped him more completely than Joel's hand had.

Joel let go.

He wrote:

FALSE MIKE AHEAD: JUST KEEP MOVING.

REAL MIKE SEEN.

DO NOT FOLLOW MIKE WITHOUT SEEING MIKE.

Mike read it. The last line looked absurd and necessary. A child's rule written for grown men: a man is not his voice. A friend is not his sentence. A map is not a place. A cabin is not help.

Will added beneath it:

MIKE VOICE NOT MIKE.

He pressed the pencil so hard the point tore the damp page.

Mike read that and looked at Will with something worse than anger. Hurt, maybe. Shame with its skin off.

Will looked like he wanted to take the sentence back. The page would not give it back.

They retreated to the clearing.

No one said retreat. Mike walked first because the path was too narrow to rearrange without ceremony. Joel watched his back. The PFD made him broad and orange, dulled by mud, ridiculous on land and indispensable because of one key in one pocket. Beyond the PFD, beyond the old road and cabin failure, was the Highlander at North Road Bridge, ordinary and locked, windshield probably filmed with road dust, stale coffee smell trapped in the upholstery. The thought of it hurt. Help had become not a person but a key shape in a pocket.

At the cabin door, Will stopped.

"Packs," he said.

Joel looked in. The packs were where they had left them. Mike's blue pack slumped against the wall. Will's green one beside it. Joel's gray one near the table. They had not moved. Nothing had opened them. Nothing had dragged them into the clearing. That should have comforted him more than it did.

From inside the cabin came a dry bag buckle click.

Once.

Then again.

All three men stood at the threshold and looked at the packs.

No buckle moved. The sound had come from inside, or from under the floor, or from the back wall, or from memory using the room's stale air. Joel's eyes went to Mike's pack. A black buckle hung loose from the top roll, motionless. Will's sleeping bag stuff sack bulged under its straps. Joel's own side pouch was open where he had taken the first-aid kit out and not closed it properly.

The click came again, this time from behind the cabin.

Mike's hand went to his PFD pocket.

Joel saw the thought arrive. Gear. Key. Pouch. Something misplaced. Something critical. The whole trip had trained them to answer such sounds with action. A buckle meant a bag not sealed. A latch meant food exposed. A shift in gear meant water inside, something lost, something that would matter later when later was colder.

Mike pressed the pocket flat. The key shape was there. He closed his hand over it through the fabric.

"It's on me," he said.

Joel wrote:

KEY FELT THROUGH POCKET.

Will read it and nodded. He had begun to tremble, not violently, only a fine shaking in his shoulders that might have been cold or rage or the effort of swallowing every sentence that tried to rise.

Mike stepped into the cabin and grabbed his pack. Too fast.

Joel put a hand on his arm.

Mike looked down at the hand.

"Don't," Joel said.

The word came out before he could make it paper. It was small, but the cabin took it. The room received the sound as if sound were weather, as if every syllable had weight and could soak into wood.

Joel released him and wrote:

JOEL SAID DON'T - TO MIKE.

The inventory had become humiliating. They were cataloging not only mistakes now but care. Every warning was a future weapon if spoken aloud. Every small human reflex had to be laid out like contraband.

Mike watched him write. Some of the anger went out of his face and left only fatigue.

"I know," he said.

Then, because he knew, he added nothing.

They checked the packs by sight. Buckles closed. Roll-tops tight enough. Food not there; food had been cached with the boats except for the small ration bag in Mike's pack. Water bottles half full. Map case present. First-aid kit on the table. Bear spray with Mike. Alex's absence was everywhere, even here, in the way no one had a fourth pack to count, no fourth complaint about the smell, no one making the dead cabin into a bit about rustic accommodations.

Will leaned against the table and closed his eyes.

"We stay?" he asked.

It was a real question. The kind they could not avoid.

Joel looked up at the roof. Three holes showed pale sky through blackened boards and moss. One was narrow and might be covered with a pack liner. Another spread along a rotten seam above the back wall. The third was large enough that a hard rain would own the room. The floor had soft places. Mouse droppings everywhere. The door did not latch. One window was open to the size of a human shoulder if a human were determined or desperate enough to enter sideways, and whatever followed them did not need to enter by rules Joel understood.

Outside, the clearing was open enough to see movement but too open to feel protected. The woods stood around it in a dark ring. If they stayed in the cabin, they would have walls, a bad roof, and one door. If they returned toward the water, they would have distance, fading light, the trace, and the problem of traveling tired through sound.

There was no correct answer. There was only the answer that failed less immediately.

Mike spread the map on the table again. The page had softened under the damp air. He put one finger on the cabin square. Then he traced backward with the other hand along the maintenance trace they had taken from Emergency Camp.

"Back to the boats is too far before dark," he said.

Will did not say yes. He watched the map.

Mike continued, more carefully now, as if each sentence had to pass through a gate. "Road forward is not passable tonight. Cabin is bad shelter. Clearing gives sightlines. We can make one night here and go at first light."

The plan sounded like Mike because it was Mike. It also sounded like the only plan available. Joel hated that. He had wanted the cabin's failure to disqualify Mike's authority cleanly, but failure did not do that. A wrong man could still say the needed thing. A damaged map could still tell them where they stood. Competence did not become false because it had limits.

Will took the notebook.

CABIN BAD BUT BETTER THAN DARK TRACE.

He looked at Mike before adding the second line.

NO ONE OUT ALONE.

Mike read it and nodded once.

Joel added:

DOOR BLOCKED. PACKS TO WALL. WATCHES BY SIGHT. NO ANSWERING OUTSIDE.

The practical work steadied them because practical work asked less of language. They moved the table to brace the door but left a gap they could open if the roof failed or smoke somehow filled the room. They dragged the broken chairs under the worst leak and set the black pot and collapsed jug to catch water. Mike cut a square from a ruined pack liner and wedged it with the rusted file over one roof gap, a repair too poor to trust and too useful to ignore. Will found a place along the wall where the floor held his weight and spread his damp sleeping pad there without inflating it fully. Joel hung their wet socks and bandage wrappers from old nails because anything lifted off the floor felt like progress.

The work made noise. Table legs scraping. Pack buckles. The pot's dull clank. The soft thud of gear against wall. Each sound had to be matched to a visible source before it was allowed to pass into the background. Joel found himself doing it silently, automatically. Table. Mike. Pot. Will. Buckle. Joel. Mouse. Wall. Drip. Roof. Unlabeled sound could call itself anything.

Near dusk, Will let Joel redo the bandage.

The cut had opened at the edge where damp skin had softened. It looked less serious than it smelled. Dirt had worked into the gauze. Joel cleaned it with the last of a sting packet and a squeeze from a water bottle. Will's fingers flexed against the table.

"Sorry," Joel almost said.

He stopped in time.

Will saw him stop. His mouth twitched once.

"Appreciated," he whispered.

Joel glared at him.

Will closed his eyes. "Written appreciated."

Joel wrote:

WILL WHISPERED APPRECIATED.

Will looked at the page and gave a silent little laugh through his nose. It was the saddest version of himself Joel had ever seen, still correcting the social record at the edge of whatever was hunting them.

They ate without cooking. Bars, the last of the cheese sweating oil into its wrapper, two handfuls of trail mix divided by sight. Mike gave Will the larger share without comment. Will noticed and did not comment either. Joel watched them not speak kindness because kindness had become another thing to ration.

The light left the clearing in layers. First the grass lost color. Then the old cans became dark mouths in the weeds. Then the road opening beyond the cabin thickened until it no longer looked like a way through.

They had the door mostly shut by then, table against it, a gap at the side wide enough for a blade of evening. The cabin smelled worse as it cooled. Wet wood. Mouse. Old ash. Human breath. The metal pot under the leak rang at longer intervals now as the roof emptied itself.

Mike sat nearest the door with the bear spray across his knees. Will sat against the back wall, injured hand elevated on his pack because Joel had pointed until he did it. Joel sat where he could see both men and the window gap. The notebook rested open on his thigh.

Outside, something clicked.

A dry bag buckle. Once.

No one moved.

Again.

Mike's head tilted. Not much. Enough.

Joel wrote without looking down:

BUCKLE OUTSIDE. NO VISUAL.

He held the page toward Mike.

Mike did not look at it. His eyes were on the door gap.

From the same place outside, Will's voice said, "Mike?"

The living Will sat against the back wall, visible, his mouth closed, his face gone slack with fear. The false voice had used him softly. Not panic. Not argument. A simple call for help or attention, the way Will had said Mike's name a thousand times before beginning a correction, a complaint, a joke, a question about where the hell the filter had gone.

Mike looked at the living Will.

Good, Joel thought.

Then the false Will said, closer, "Mike?"

Mike stood.

Joel stood with him.

Mike did not reach for the table. He did not open the door. He only stood, bear spray in one hand, the other hand going to the pocket with the key. But standing had changed the room. It had put motion into him.

Joel shook his head.

Mike whispered, "I know."

Outside, something brushed the wall under the broken window.

Will pressed his good hand over his own mouth. His eyes watered behind the glasses, and Joel could not tell if it was fear or fury at hearing himself reduced to bait.

The brush came again. Then a buckle click. Then Alex, from just beyond the wall, said, "Quit mothering me."

The words were almost playful.

Mike moved toward the window.

Not fast. That was the worst part. Not a lunge, not stupidity, not forgetting the rule. He moved like a man trying to confirm a threat at the only opening in the room. The wall had been touched. The window was a vulnerability. The thing had brought Alex into a place where no dead man should have a voice. Capable men moved toward problems they could define.

Joel caught his sleeve.

Mike pulled free.

His face when he turned was not angry now. It was stripped down to something Joel could not bear. "I can't just sit here."

Joel wanted to tell him that sitting here was the work. That not answering was work. That not fixing a thing could be the only way to survive it. He wanted to say that this was what the thing had learned about him: not his mistakes, not only his phrases, but the shape of his usefulness. It had found the part of Mike that could not leave a problem unhandled if the problem made a sound like gear or friend or authority failing.

He wrote instead:

SITTING IS ACTION.

Mike read it.

For one second, maybe two, it held him.

Then from beyond the wall, farther away now, Mike's own voice said, "We're fine."

It was not loud. It did not need to be. It had his old impatience with fear, his old dismissal of hesitation. It came from outside the cabin, from the left side where the grass met the trees, and it sounded almost kind because certainty can resemble kindness when men are tired enough.

Mike's face changed.

Will made a sound behind his hand. Not a word. A broken little intake.

Joel wrote:

FALSE MIKE: WE'RE FINE.

He looked up.

Mike was at the door.

Not outside. Not yet. One hand on the table, shifting it just enough to make the legs scrape against the floor.

"Key," Mike said.

Joel shook his head.

"If something gets the pack--"

Joel pointed to Mike's PFD.

"I know it's on me." His whisper was harsh enough to scrape. "I know. I want to check the road edge. Ten feet."

Will shook his head hard.

Mike looked at him. "I'm not following a voice."

That was true in the narrowest possible way. It was not enough truth.

Joel stepped between Mike and the table. He held up the notebook.

NO OUTSIDE.

Mike looked past the page toward the door gap.

From outside, beyond the cabin, beyond the clearing's wet grass, Alex laughed.

Bright. Wrong. Perfect.

Then, from a different place, Will's voice said, "You guys hear that?"

The living Will flinched as if struck.

The room broke into directions. Alex at the road. Will near the back wall outside. Mike's old reassurance in the trees. A buckle at the threshold. Water in the pot. Mouse in the wall. Three living men breathing too hard in a room built by people who had left no help behind.

Mike shoved the table.

Joel grabbed for him and caught only the slick shoulder of the PFD. Mike twisted sideways, still controlled, still trying not to make it a fight. That restraint made him stronger. The table moved enough for the door to open a foot. Cold air entered. Wet grass smell. Spruce. Old ash from the clearing.

"Mike," Joel said.

Mike looked back.

For that moment Joel had him. The living Mike was in the gap, one boot inside, one boot on the soft threshold, bear spray in his hand, face white with the effort of being still.

From the trees behind him, Mike's throat cleared.

Mike turned his head.

Only his head. Only a little.

Joel saw the rule fail by an inch.

He reached again, but Will was moving too, coming off the back wall too late, knocking his shoulder against the table, making the pot ring under the leak. For half a second Joel looked toward that sound because every sound demanded accounting now.

When he looked back, Mike was outside.

Still visible.

Three feet from the door. Then five.

Joel pushed through after him.

Will caught the back of Joel's jacket. Joel shook him off without meaning to. The clearing had gone blue with dusk. The cabin threw one crooked bar of shadow over the grass. Mike stood at the edge of it, facing the old road, bear spray raised but not extended. His PFD pocket was still zipped. The key was still with him. His body was there. Seen. Seen. Seen.

"Mike," Joel said.

The real Mike did not answer.

From the old road, very softly, the false Mike said, "It opens up after this."

Mike took one step toward it.

Joel moved after him.

Behind Joel, Will said, "Visual."

It was the right word. Spoken too late. Spoken because speech was the fastest tool they had and the worst.

Mike stopped.

For one clean second all three living men were visible: Will in the doorway, Joel in the wet grass, Mike at the clearing edge. The cabin behind them useless and real. The old road ahead closed and dark. Daylight almost gone.

Then a dry bag buckle clicked inside the cabin.

Will turned.

Joel turned with him.

One glance. Less than a second. Long enough to see the dark doorway and the packs still in place. Long enough for the woods to take what it had been waiting for.

When Joel looked back, Mike had moved into the trees.

Not far. Not gone. A darker body between dark spruce, still visible if Joel chose the right gap and did not blink.

Then the gap closed around him.

Joel understood too late that visual confirmation was not a rule you possessed. It was an act you had to keep performing.
