# Chapter 26 - Alone

Alone simplified the arithmetic and ruined everything else.

Joel had one canoe, one small pack cut down to what could still be called useful, one map going soft at the folds, one Highlander key zipped against his chest, one hatchet, one filter, one weak headlamp, two crushed bars, one wet sleeping bag he did not plan to unroll, and one blue dry bag wedged under the front seat with the emergency paddle inside it.

He had daylight draining through low cloud.

He had three dead men behind him who would not stop speaking.

They did not come as a chorus. A chorus would have become weather. The woods spent them one at a time, which left each voice alone long enough to remain a person.

Mike said, "Just keep moving."

Alex asked if he was coming or writing a poem.

Will coughed from ahead, then from behind, then from so close that Joel felt the old expectation of breath on the back of his neck. No breath came with it.

Joel kept the canoe on his shoulders and did not turn.

The portage bent through black spruce and alder, then dropped and climbed in small useless humps. With two men under load, three before that, four before the world began spending them, the rises had been annoyances. Now each one made him stop and set his feet. The yoke pressed into the bruised base of his neck. The hull narrowed his sight to mud, root, branch, his own hands on the gunwales, and the thin strip of trail under green Kevlar.

The canoe had become rescue and blindfold. He needed it for the last water. He hated it for taking the sky away.

At a fallen cedar the trail widened enough for him to set the canoe down.

Quietly was not silent.

The hull touched wet duff with a soft, broad sigh. A branch sprang loose and ticked along the side. Water trapped inside the boat shifted toward one end and clicked against a rib.

From the brush, the same water-click answered.

Joel stood over the canoe and waited. Not because waiting was safe. Movement without knowing what had moved was worse.

The click came again from ten yards off the trail. Then farther away. Then behind him, quick and small, as if a hand had learned only the last inch of the motion.

Not a voice. Not a command. Only the boat making itself somewhere the boat was not.

That was the new pressure. A man could teach himself not to answer his dead friend. He still had to know whether the boat had shifted.

He opened the notebook against his thigh. Mud had dried along the spine and cracked when he bent it. The swollen pages near the middle clung together. Earlier rules showed through later paper like bruises: DO NOT ANSWER. SIGHT. RIGHT WORDS WRONG SOURCE. TWO MEN.

He did not let himself look long at TWO MEN.

On a cleaner space at the bottom of the page he wrote:

NO NAMES.

NO ANSWERS.

ONLY SEEN IS REAL.

SOUND IS BAIT.

He started to tear the page loose, then stopped with the paper strained under his thumb. Tearing was sound. Everything was sound. He held the notebook open long enough to learn the page as an object: four black blocks, bottom right, mud thumbprint at the margin. When the boat was on his shoulders he would have only memory. He closed the notebook and put it back inside his jacket.

The pencil shook in his fingers. He put it away before he wrote a name by accident.

Names opened the air. A name made a shape a body was supposed to fill.

He wanted to say Will's name.

He wanted it with the stupid part of him that still expected Will to come stomping up behind him, angry and filthy and triumphant, carrying the heavy pack wrong because he had found some technical reason the obvious way was not best. He wanted the cough before a correction. He wanted no, no, no from a living mouth, irritated at Joel's assumptions and not emptied by brush.

He closed his teeth until his jaw hurt.

From the portage behind him, Will said, "There's a difference."

Joel let the sentence pass through him like cold water through a sleeve. The living Will had been right about differences. That was part of the damage. Right source, wrong source. Sound, sight. Repetition, understanding. A man could be right all the way to the wrong place.

Joel put one palm on the canoe hull. Its surface was cold and slick with old rain. Real because it resisted him. Real because when he pressed harder his hand hurt.

He lifted the canoe.

The next quarter mile taught him what a man could leave.

At first the pile was practical: the damp tarp folded once under spruce, the extra pot set inside it, the stove wrapped in a sock because loose metal ticked. Ten minutes ago those things had still been gear. Now they were weight. Joel stood too long looking at the stove. It had been ordinary as a hinge all week. Click, fuel, flame. Coffee, oats, soup, something hot enough to tell the body it had not been discarded yet.

He left it.

There was no one to witness the choice, which made it feel less like sacrifice than littering. He hated that. He had been raised to pack out wrappers, orange peels, fishing line, the smallest human evidence. Now he was leaving a stove under spruce because the living part of him had become lighter than the etiquette of the woods.

Ten minutes later he left the wet sleeping bag.

That was harder. Not because he believed he would sleep. Sleep had become a thing that belonged to another species. But a sleeping bag meant a plan extending past dark. It meant a man was not only moving but expected to stop and still continue. It was a promise of a body worth warming.

Mara would have asked about it if he reached her. Not first. First would be where are you, are you hurt, who is with you, words with their ordinary claims on answer and body. Later, after the facts had been divided into what could be said and what had to stay in the notebook, she would ask if he had kept warm. He would have to tell her no in a language that did not include this hollow under balsam.

He unstrapped the bag from the small pack, water heavy in the insulation, and pushed it under the low limbs. He did not hide it well. He did not have enough hope to hide it well.

Alex laughed somewhere above him, wrong height, wrong place, caught in spruce like a thrown object.

"Quit mothering me," Alex said.

Joel's hands paused on the strap.

He saw Alex at First Camp holding a mug like a microphone, making everyone look at him, making Joel resent the need to laugh and then laugh anyway. He saw Alex at the beaver dam with mud up his shins, strong and complaining, one arm out to steady a pack that was not his. He saw the cracked headlamp and the red place in the alder.

The sleeping bag lay under the tree like something already finished.

Joel tightened the remaining strap and moved.

Weight had its own blunt judgment now. Anything kept meant another minute under the yoke, another stumble, another opening for sound. Anything left meant a future crossed out.

The map failed next, though he could not leave it. Grit had worked into the case seal. Water had entered through a corner days ago and softened the paper. Contours blurred where his thumb had pressed them too often. The blue shapes of lakes still held. The black portage line still appeared in pieces. The final lake was there: narrow at its south end, spreading north, then pinching toward the outlet and North Road Bridge.

The map said the take-out was beyond the lake.

The map had also said there was a cabin.

That was what maps did badly. They did not lie. They kept old agreements after the world stopped honoring them.

Joel folded it along a crease that no longer wanted to fold and slid it back under the cord. The Highlander key pressed cold against his sternum in the inner pocket of his jacket. It had become more intimate than a key should be. A little cut piece of another man's confidence, carried against Joel's heart.

He touched the pocket once to confirm it.

From ahead, Mike cleared his throat.

Joel stopped before the reflex completed. He had almost looked for Mike. Not because he believed. Because a body learns before the mind can forbid it. Mike cleared his throat before a decision. Men who had followed him for years turned toward that sound.

"We're exactly where we ought to be," Mike said.

The words moved through the tunnel of the canoe and filled the space around Joel's head. Mike's route certainty, preserved without Mike. The trip reduced to one sentence the woods did not have to believe.

Joel looked at the mud under his feet.

One sandal strap had torn at the side. He had tied it with cord, and the knot pressed into the soft place above his arch. His left knee had begun to swell from the root he struck after Will died. His hands were pale and dirty around the gunwales. Blood had dried in the cracks of his knuckles and turned almost black.

Those were facts. His feet. His hands. The trail he could see.

He moved.

When the voices did not turn him, the portage used smaller sounds.

A boot scraped stone behind him. One scrape, then another, spaced like a tired man following. Joel kept walking until the scrape came again, closer. He stopped and turned with the whole canoe, slow, because the hull would catch if he spun.

Nothing stood in the trail.

The mud held his own tracks, deep at the heel where fatigue made him careless. A strip of alder leaf trembled. Water fell from spruce tips. The portage behind him curved out of sight as if the world had been folded there.

A boot scraped stone ahead.

Joel faced forward again.

He did not write a new rule. The old one covered it. SOUND IS BAIT. He repeated the words without moving his mouth.

The portage dropped into a wet section where old planks had once crossed the mud. Only three remained, black, slick, and half sunk. He set the canoe down before them and made himself plan the passage. One man under a canoe could not see where the back end swung. One man could not have another voice say left, easy, hold up, your end's catching. He had to become both ends and still remain one body.

He dragged the canoe sideways around the worst mud, lifted the front over a fallen branch, stepped into muck to mid-calf, and pulled. Mud closed cold around his sandal. The sucking sound was obscene in its familiarity. It had been funny on Day 2: Alex making a performance of losing a sandal, Will explaining that peat behaved differently from clay, Mike telling everyone to keep moving because mosquitoes did not care about footwear.

Now the mud made the same wet mouth around Joel's foot, and the woods answered with another pull, another release, somewhere out of sight.

He leaned his weight on the canoe and freed himself slowly.

The sandal came up full of muck. He scraped it against the edge of a plank before he could seat the strap under his heel. His fingers would not do small work easily anymore. They pinched and missed. The cord knot at the side of the sandal held, slid, then held again.

He wanted Will's voice to tell him it was a bad knot.

He wanted the correction so sharply that wanting became almost audible inside him.

From the right, Will coughed.

Not words. Worse, maybe, because the cough had no argument in it. It was Will before speech. Will about to explain. Will alive in the half second before language armed him.

Joel closed his eyes.

Only briefly. Too briefly for the dark behind his lids to become anything. Then he opened them and looked at the canoe, the planks, the mud, the line of the trail. He had no room for reverence. No room for farewell. Ceremony wanted words.

He lifted the canoe again.

The emergency paddle was a joke of a thing by canoe-trip standards: aluminum shaft, black plastic blade, two pieces that screwed together with a collar. Mike had mocked it when he saw it packed under the front seat, then kept it because even mockery had limits around water. The proper paddles had been lashed to Will's heavy pack. They were gone with him into the alder, with the food barrel, most of the remaining food, the repair kit, and the coil of cord Joel had meant to keep.

The emergency paddle clicked under the seat each time the canoe tilted.

Click.

From the left, another click.

Joel set his jaw and carried it anyway.

He thought of the group before sound had become poisonous. Four men took up space differently than one. Four men made ordinary noise around an object until the object almost disappeared inside use: jokes over lashings, argument over load order, a paddle knocked loose and cursed at, Alex asking if anyone had seen his sunglasses while the sunglasses sat on his hat. The canoe had belonged to all of them then. It had been lifted badly, corrected, balanced, shoved into water, bumped against rock, cursed into usefulness.

Now it was weight and green hull and the fact of the last lake.

At the next rise, the trail narrowed under young spruce. Branches clawed both sides of the canoe. He had to turn sideways, lower the front, back up, lift again, and push through by inches. Each touch made the hull speak. Scratch, knock, scrape. The portage repeated the sounds ahead of him and behind him until the whole trail seemed to be carrying a canoe badly.

Then his own voice said, from a bend he had not reached, "Stop."

The word was small and plain. It did not have the pleading drag that had been in Will's name. It was the word he had given the woods with his hands on Will's vest, breath hot and useless between them.

Joel stood under the canoe until the sound thinned out.

He had already failed that once. He would not fail it for himself.

He stopped once to drink.

The filter squeaked when he loosened it from the side pocket. He had half a bottle left, cloudy with grit, warm from his body. He took three swallows. His throat wanted more. He capped it and put it away.

The filter squeaked from the trees.

Then Alex said, "Jesus, listen to this guy."

It was almost funny. The timing, the old line dropped into a moment with no guy left to listen to. Joel felt laughter rise in him as nausea. He bent over with both hands on his knees and kept his mouth shut.

If he laughed, it would have his laugh.

He had not thought of that with enough force. The entity had Joel's voice. It had "Wait. Just wait." It had "Stop." It had Will's name, said with pleading in it. It might not have his laugh. Joel had not laughed much after the first day. He could not remember the last time. The possibility made silence possessive.

He stood.

The trail changed near late afternoon. He knew it by light before map. Spruce thinned. The ground began a long, steady drop. Air moved differently ahead, cooler and wider, carrying the mineral smell of open water and wet stone instead of only rot and needles. A raven called once from somewhere high. He waited for the call to repeat wrong, but the sky gave him nothing.

The ordinary world had not stopped. Some sounds were still only sounds. Water still dripped from branches. Birds still moved through black spruce. Wind rubbed the lake ahead, indifferent to whether one man reached it.

The final stretch of portage was rockier. Granite showed through the moss in gray backs. His sandals slipped twice. The canoe's weight had become a fact beyond pain, a number too large to use. His shoulders went numb and then sharpened again. The yoke seemed to settle into bone.

Will said from behind him, "I can make it contradict itself."

Joel nearly answered in his head.

No.

He bit the inside of his cheek and tasted blood.

"No, no, no," Will said.

The phrase came lower this time, more private. Not the panic from the brush after he died, but the old Will before panic had taken him: correcting a gear mistake, resisting a bad explanation, trying to keep the world from being stupid. Joel saw him in Will's garage years ago with a headlamp strap around his forehead in daylight, both of them half-drunk, Will taking apart a cheap lantern because the battery contact offended him.

No, no, no, that's not how that works.

Joel had laughed then. Will had looked pleased and pretended not to be.

Joel stopped. The canoe rocked on his shoulders.

He did not know how long he stood there.

The woods waited with him. Or did not. It was impossible to tell patience from absence.

From the brush at his left, close enough that the alders trembled after the sound, Will whispered, "Joel."

The name did what a hand would have done. It touched him.

He almost turned.

The canoe saved him by being stupid and heavy. To turn, he would have to commit the whole hull. He would have to step back, pivot, let the front swing into spruce, make a sound large enough to call the dead from every side. The physical difficulty gave the rule time to catch up.

ONLY SEEN IS REAL.

He looked at the trail ahead until the desire to look left passed from command into ache.

"Joel," Will said again.

This time it came from ahead.

Joel walked through it.

The final portage ended without ceremony. No sign. No blaze. No sudden widening into safety. The trail simply gave up into a sloped mix of gravel, mud, and flat stones where the last lake took the land in small dark licks. Alder crowded the left side. Low black spruce ran down the right shore. The water stretched north under dusk-colored cloud, a long narrow reach between dark banks, wrinkled by wind moving along it rather than across.

For a minute he could not step out from under the trees. The lake looked too open. Not safe. Only exposed in a different way. On the portage every sound had a trunk, a bend, a clump of alder to hide behind. Water had no walls. It would carry a voice cleanly. It would make distance behave like a lie.

Wind across the lake would have ended him or made him wait for dark, which was another way to end. Wind along it was trouble but not refusal. He could paddle that if his hands kept working and if the short emergency paddle held and if the map meant anything and if he did not fall asleep sitting up.

He lowered the canoe carefully at the landing.

The woods behind him assembled the group.

Mike cleared his throat.

Will said, "I can make it contradict itself."

Alex laughed.

The sounds did not overlap. They took turns like men around a fire, each waiting for the old rhythm to receive him. The arrangement hurt more than noise would have.

Joel crouched beside the canoe and inventoried what could go forward.

Small pack: map, filter, bottle, headlamp with weak batteries, knife, two energy bars crushed into meal, first-aid roll reduced to tape and dirty gauze, lighter in a plastic sleeve, pencil, notebook when it was not in his jacket. Hatchet at belt. Key in inner pocket. Blue dry bag under the front seat, with the emergency paddle, a pair of socks, the torn corner of the old repair sleeve, and the little orange whistle nobody had used because a whistle was only a mouth made of plastic.

He clicked the headlamp on against his palm. The light came up yellow and weak, then steadied. He clicked it off before the small plastic switch could become an invitation. The lamp would be for the map only. Not for searching trees. Not for proving voices wrong. Light was not the same as sight if fear chose where to point it.

He took the whistle out and held it.

It was almost weightless, molded orange plastic, practical in exactly the way the old world had been practical. Three blasts for distress. A small signal that assumed anyone hearing it would want to help. It had not done anything wrong. It was still impossible to keep.

Joel threw it into the lake.

Not far. His throw was bad with fatigue. It struck water five yards out and floated for a moment, bright and ridiculous, before a ripple turned it over. He watched until it drifted beyond reach.

Alex said, "Hold up."

Joel loaded the canoe.

He did it without hurry because hurry made noise and mistakes. The pack went behind the middle thwart, close enough to reach, low enough not to shift. He pulled the blue dry bag out, took the emergency paddle from it, screwed the two sections together, and tightened the collar until his fingers slipped. The joint flexed. He tightened it again. The bag went back under the front seat where he could see it. He turned the canoe parallel to the water and checked the painter knot. The shortened rope was stiff with dried mud.

Each action arrived with the ghost of the man who should have argued about it.

Mike would have told him the trim was wrong.

Will would have said there was no point calling it trim when it was one exhausted idiot in one ruined canoe.

Alex would have asked if this was the part where Joel became rugged and insufferable.

Joel put one hand over his mouth until the almost-smile died there.

He needed direction. That was the hard fact beneath all the others. The map showed the lake running north, outlet at the far end, bridge beyond, Highlander waiting if the key still mattered and the road had not swallowed itself in rain. But the shoreline near the landing split into two dark possibilities. A left-hand channel tucked behind a reed point. A broader right-hand reach held the wind and looked more like the main lake. In daylight with Will and Mike and time, they would have read the shore, checked the map against slope and contour, argued, corrected, chosen. Alone at dusk, with voices in the trees, every direction could be made to sound like help.

He could not ask the map to speak.

He could not ask the dead.

He needed a test that was not a question.

The thought came without drama. It was not bravery. It was procedure. The thing used what the men had given it. So give it one new thing. A stone with no blood on it. A fact flat enough that its use would show only placement.

He looked for an object.

The blue dry bag sat under the front seat. It was visible. Its color had no memory except inventory. Not Alex's food complaints. Not Mike's route certainty. Not Will's correction habit. Not Joel's pleading. Bag. Blue. Under the front.

He opened the notebook to the wet rules page and wrote under SOUND IS BAIT:

GIVE IT ONE THING.

Then, on the torn corner of the map margin, he wrote:

BLUE BAG UNDER THE FRONT.

He stared at the words until they flattened.

The woods stayed quiet.

Quiet was not consent. Quiet was only the absence of a sound he had noticed.

He tucked the torn margin under the thwart cord, facing up. He wanted the words where his eyes could find them after his mind began to slip. Then he stood at the edge of the landing, facing the lake, not the woods.

His throat did not want to open. It had learned too late.

He waited until the wind moved along the water and covered the tremor in his breath. He looked at the bag. He looked at the right-hand reach. He looked at the left-hand channel. He held both possibilities in his mind and gave the woods nothing from either one.

Then he said clearly, once, "Blue bag under the front."

The sentence went out over the lake and came back smaller, thinned by open water.

Behind him, nothing answered.

He waited until waiting became another way to answer.

Mike said, very softly, "Just keep moving."

Joel did not obey Mike. He obeyed the daylight leaving the water.

He set both hands on the canoe and pushed it toward the lake.
