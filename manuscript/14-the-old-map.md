# Chapter 14 - The Old Map

In the morning Mike spread the old map on a wet rock and put stones on the corners.

The cabin square sat inland from a portage they had already missed by landing wrong. A faint line ran from it toward an old fire road. On paper it looked reasonable. Paper was good at that.

"We leave one canoe hidden," Mike said. "Take the repair kit, food for two days, sleeping bags, first aid, map, water filter. Find the cabin. If there's a road, we follow it. If not, we come back."

Will stared at the map. "That's a lot of if."

"Everything is if."

"No. Some things are distances. Some things are contours. Some things are false hope printed in black."

Mike's throat cleared. "You want to paddle the big water with a cracked boat and voices?"

Will had no answer.

Joel wanted to say that voices were in the woods too. He did not because saying it did not improve the world.

They cached the damaged canoe under spruce boughs and tied it off above the high-water line. The other they pulled farther into brush, in case they needed both again and in case whatever followed them understood value. That last thought made Joel stop. Understand value. Did it? Did it know why the boats mattered, or did it only learn the sounds men made around things they feared losing?

He wrote in the notebook:

ONLY WHAT IT HEARD.

Will read over his shoulder and whispered, "Maybe."

The trace began as a path old enough to have humility. Grass grew in the center. Alder narrowed it. Boot prints appeared and vanished in mud. They walked single file: Mike, Will, Joel. Mike insisted on front because Mike insisted on front.

No one spoke for the first half hour. Silence made every practical thing harder. A raised hand meant stop, or maybe watch the branch, or maybe listen. Will kept starting to say something and biting it back. Joel could hear his teeth click once.

From the brush ahead came Mike's throat clear.

Mike stopped walking.

The sound had come from twenty yards in front of him.

Will looked back at Joel. Joel lifted one hand: wait.

Then Mike's voice, from ahead, said, "Just keep moving."

The living Mike turned slowly. His face had lost color under the grime.

Will mouthed something. Joel shook his head hard.

They did not move until the woods resumed being woods: rain ticking from old leaves, a distant raven, the small release of water from moss. Then Mike stepped forward with the careful fury of a man refusing to be handled by his own voice.

By afternoon the trace worsened. Blowdown forced them around a black tangle of trunks. Will fell once and split his palm on a rock. Joel bandaged it while Mike watched the trees.

From somewhere off trail, Alex said, "You coming or writing a poem?"

Joel closed the first-aid kit.

"No," he said before he could stop himself.

The woods gave no answer. That was worse.
